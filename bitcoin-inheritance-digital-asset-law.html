Bitcoin Inheritance & Digital Asset Law: What Bitcoin-Wealthy Families Need to Know Right Now

The legal framework for Bitcoin inheritance is still being written — and most estate plans don't include Bitcoin at all. Here's the definitive guide to protecting your family's Bitcoin before it's too late.

By Hal Turner · Bitcoin Wealth Strategist · February 25, 2026 · ✓ Last Verified: February 2026

Three things happened in recent months that every Bitcoin-wealthy family should pay attention to.

First: sovereign wealth funds quietly accumulated over $462 million in Bitcoin ETFs. The implication isn't just institutional validation — it's that estate planners, attorneys, and trustees are now being handed Bitcoin positions of institutional scale with no institutional-grade estate planning infrastructure to support them.

Second: the Daily Mail and other mainstream outlets covered a surge in divorce cases where one spouse hid cryptocurrency worth millions from the other. Blockchain analytics firms are being retained as expert witnesses. Forensic accountants are learning to trace wallet flows. Digital asset discovery is a real legal battleground.

Third: H&R Block, PCMag, and mainstream tax publications are finally covering crypto tax reporting — but the estate planning angle is being missed entirely. The conversation stops at capital gains. It never reaches what happens when you die.

This is that article. Bitcoin inheritance law is still being written. Most states have no clear framework for digital asset transfer at death. Most estate plans don't include Bitcoin. Most families have no idea how their Bitcoin will be found, accessed, or distributed when they die. This is the definitive guide for Bitcoin-wealthy families.

The core problem: A brokerage account can be transferred to your heirs with a death certificate and some paperwork. Bitcoin held in self-custody requires the private key or seed phrase. Without it, the Bitcoin is permanently inaccessible — no court order, no government agency, no probate proceeding changes that. The Bitcoin simply ceases to be usable. It becomes a monument to the failure to plan.

The good news: This is entirely solvable. With the right three documents, the right custody architecture, and a clear understanding of what the law currently says, Bitcoin-wealthy families can pass their holdings to the next generation with less friction, lower taxes, and more certainty than almost any other asset class.

Who this is for: Bitcoin holders with meaningful holdings — whether $500,000 or $50 million — who have not yet integrated Bitcoin into a formal estate plan. Section 1: The Unique Inheritance Challenge Bitcoin Creates

Bitcoin is not like any other asset you have ever owned. It is not held at a custodian that knows your name. It is not recoverable through a call to customer service. It is not subject to a court order that can compel a third party to hand it over to your executor. Bitcoin is mathematical proof of ownership, and ownership is defined entirely by knowledge of a private key.

If you die with Bitcoin in self-custody — on a hardware wallet, in a paper wallet, or secured by a seed phrase — and no one in your estate plan has documented access to that key material, the Bitcoin is permanently inaccessible. The coins sit on the blockchain, visible to anyone, owned by no one. They are gone.

How Much Bitcoin Has Been Lost This Way?

Chainalysis and other blockchain analytics firms estimate that between 3 and 4 million BTC — roughly 15–20% of the total 21 million supply — is permanently lost. This includes coins from Satoshi's earliest mining era that have never moved, coins sent to invalid addresses, and a significant share attributed to early Bitcoin holders who held their keys informally and died without any succession plan in place.

In dollar terms at today's prices, this is hundreds of billions of dollars that will never be accessible to anyone — not heirs, not governments, not creditors. It is a cautionary statistic with a clear message: the Bitcoin community has a generational wealth destruction problem, and it is entirely preventable.

Why Self-Custody Compounds the Estate Problem

For Bitcoin holders who moved from exchanges to self-custody — the right move for security — the estate planning challenge is significantly more complex. Exchange accounts are recoverable through identity verification. Self-custody is not. This creates a tension: the custody arrangement that is most secure during your lifetime is the least estate-planning-friendly if not properly documented.

There is a spectrum of custody arrangements and their estate implications:

  • Exchange-only holdings: Recoverable by executor with court order, but subject to exchange terms of service, counterparty risk, and no step-up in basis complexity. Best for smaller allocations or less sophisticated holders.
  • Single-sig hardware wallet: Recoverable if seed phrase is documented and accessible to executor. Inaccessible if seed phrase is lost or unknown.
  • Multisig (2-of-3, 3-of-5, etc.): More resilient to single-point-of-failure loss, but requires careful documentation of quorum, co-signer contacts, and recovery procedures. See our guide on Bitcoin custody architecture.
  • Institutional/regulated custodian: Most estate-friendly. Provides a named custodian, account statements, and a defined beneficiary designation process — similar to a brokerage account.

The estate planning framework you build must match your actual custody arrangement. A plan written for exchange holdings fails a hardware wallet holder. A plan written for single-sig fails a multisig user. Most generic estate plans — written by attorneys without Bitcoin-specific experience — fail all three.

Section 2: RUFADAA — What the Law Actually Says

The Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) is model legislation drafted by the Uniform Law Commission and adopted, in some form, by 47+ states as of early 2026. It is the legal foundation for everything else in digital asset estate planning — and most Bitcoin holders have never heard of it.

What RUFADAA Actually Does

RUFADAA does one essential thing: it grants fiduciaries — executors, trustees, and attorneys-in-fact under a power of attorney — the legal authority to access, manage, and transfer digital assets after an owner's death or incapacity.

Before RUFADAA, fiduciaries existed in legal limbo. They had a duty to administer the estate but no clear statutory right to access digital accounts. Exchange platforms routinely refused access, citing their terms of service. Courts were inconsistent. Executors sometimes faced criminal exposure for accessing accounts — even when acting in good faith under a valid will.

RUFADAA resolved this by creating a layered priority system:

  1. Layer 1 — Your own documented directions: If you have left explicit digital asset access instructions using an online tool provided by the platform, or in a will, trust, or power of attorney that expressly authorizes fiduciary access, those directions govern. This is the highest priority layer.
  2. Layer 2 — Terms of service: If you have left no instructions, the platform's terms of service control what the fiduciary may access. Most terms of service are quite restrictive.
  3. Layer 3 — Default fiduciary rights: If layers 1 and 2 don't resolve the question, RUFADAA grants baseline statutory rights — but these are limited and may not cover all digital assets.

The practical implication for Bitcoin holders: RUFADAA gives your executor legal authority — but it does not give them the technical ability to access your Bitcoin. Even with full legal authority under RUFADAA, your executor cannot unlock your hardware wallet without the PIN. They cannot use your seed phrase they don't know. Legal authority is necessary but not sufficient. You must pair RUFADAA-compliant legal documents with a clear, documented, secure key access protocol.

Does RUFADAA Cover Hardware Wallets?

This is the question most estate attorneys get wrong, and the answer requires precision. RUFADAA defines "digital assets" broadly to include "electronic records in which an individual has a right or interest" — which covers Bitcoin wallet addresses, private keys stored in digital form, and exchange accounts. However, RUFADAA's fiduciary access provisions were primarily designed around accounts held at service providers, not self-custody hardware.

The honest legal answer: RUFADAA creates the legal framework for fiduciary access, but the physical seed phrase or hardware wallet PIN must be accessible through other means. Your estate plan must address both the legal authority (RUFADAA-compliant trust or will) and the physical/technical access (where the keys are, how to use them, who is the first call when something doesn't work).

What Two States Still Haven't Adopted RUFADAA?

Louisiana and, to a limited degree, certain provisions in Oklahoma remain outside the RUFADAA framework. Louisiana operates under a civil law system derived from French and Spanish tradition — fundamentally different from the common law base of all other U.S. states. Louisiana's forced heirship rules (which mandate inheritance shares for children under 24) create additional complexity for Bitcoin holders. If you live in Louisiana or have significant Louisiana ties, your digital asset estate planning requires specialized local counsel.

Section 3: Bitcoin in Divorce — Why Documentation Protects You

The news stories about wealthy spouses hiding crypto during divorce tell a real story about a real problem. But the coverage frames it backwards for the Bitcoin-wealthy family that is actually trying to do the right thing.

The mainstream narrative: crypto is a tool for hiding assets. Spouses can move millions offshore on a blockchain that no one can trace.

The reality: Bitcoin is the most auditable monetary system ever created. Every transaction is permanently recorded on a public ledger. Blockchain analytics firms like Chainalysis and Elliptic can trace wallet flows with high precision. Forensic accountants trained in digital asset discovery can — and do — reconstruct entire Bitcoin transaction histories in litigation. Courts are beginning to understand this.

The Real Risk for Honest Families

For the Bitcoin family that has done nothing wrong, the risk isn't that they're hiding assets. The risk is that undocumented holdings look like hidden assets. If your spouse (or their attorney) discovers a hardware wallet with 10 BTC during a divorce proceeding, and you have no purchase records, no cost basis documentation, no prior disclosure in financial statements, and no reference to those holdings in your estate plan — you are creating a problem that didn't need to exist.

Proper estate planning documentation is also asset discovery documentation. When your Bitcoin holdings are:

  • Recorded in a net worth statement referenced in your trust documents
  • Disclosed on financial affidavits in any prior proceeding
  • Listed (with wallet addresses, not seed phrases) in a Letter of Instruction accessible to your attorney
  • Held in a titled entity (LLC, trust) that appears on your balance sheet

...there is no ambiguity. There is nothing to discover. You have documented what you own, when you acquired it, and at what cost basis. This is not just good estate planning — it is the best protection available against any allegation that you have hidden or transferred assets.

Prenuptial Agreements and Bitcoin

For high-net-worth Bitcoin holders entering marriage, a prenuptial agreement with explicit Bitcoin provisions is increasingly important. This means:

  • Identifying Bitcoin held as separate property at the time of marriage (with wallet addresses and acquisition records)
  • Specifying how post-marriage Bitcoin appreciation will be characterized (separate or marital)
  • Addressing how Bitcoin acquired with joint funds will be titled and divided
  • Determining dispute resolution procedures if Bitcoin valuation is contested

Bitcoin's price volatility makes valuation in divorce proceedings genuinely complex — what is worth $5M at filing may be worth $8M or $2M by the time a decree is entered. A well-drafted prenup specifies how valuation will be handled in this scenario. See our dedicated guide to Bitcoin prenuptial agreements for more on structuring these provisions.

Section 4: State Law Gaps — Where You Die Matters

Federal estate tax applies equally to every American. But the state where you are domiciled at death can determine more about your estate's final outcome than almost any other single factor.

The variation is dramatic. Consider two Bitcoin holders with identical $5M estates:

  • Wyoming holder: No state estate tax. Wyoming-specific digital asset property statutes. RUFADAA adopted. Dynasty trust infrastructure. No state income tax. Estate passes to heirs with zero state-level transfer tax and clear legal authority for digital asset administration.
  • Oregon holder: State estate tax kicks in at $1M — the lowest exemption in the country. A $5M estate owes significant state estate tax before the federal exemption even becomes relevant. Oregon's estate tax rates reach up to 16% on amounts above the exemption. A $5M Bitcoin estate in Oregon could face $400,000 or more in state estate tax that a Wyoming holder pays nothing on.

Wyoming: Why It Leads

Wyoming has systematically built the most Bitcoin-friendly legal infrastructure in the United States. Key provisions:

  • Digital asset property law: Wyoming Stat. § 34-29-101 et seq. explicitly recognizes digital assets as property under Wyoming law — a foundational legal clarity that most states still lack.
  • DAO LLC: Wyoming was the first state to allow Decentralized Autonomous Organization LLCs — giving Bitcoin-holding entities a legal structure designed for digital assets.
  • Digital asset custodian licenses: Wyoming created a regulatory framework for entities that custody digital assets, similar to a bank charter but purpose-built for cryptocurrency.
  • No rule against perpetuities: Wyoming trusts can last forever — enabling true dynasty trust planning for Bitcoin.
  • No state estate tax or income tax.
  • Robust DAPT (Domestic Asset Protection Trust) laws.

You do not need to be a Wyoming resident to establish a Wyoming trust. A qualified Wyoming trustee is sufficient to establish nexus. For the full analysis of Wyoming vs. Nevada LLC structures for Bitcoin holders, see our guide to Wyoming vs. Nevada LLCs.

Section 5: The Three Documents Every Bitcoin Holder Needs

Most estate plans are missing all three. Some have one. The complete Bitcoin estate plan requires all three working together — each serving a distinct purpose, each complementing the others. None of them alone is sufficient.

Document 01

The Bitcoin Letter of Instruction

This is the operational document — the one that actually gets your Bitcoin into your heirs' hands. It is not part of your will. It is not filed with any court. It is private, secure, and updated regularly as your holdings change.

  • Location of all hardware wallets and seed phrase backups
  • PIN codes and passphrases (stored separately from the seeds themselves)
  • Multisig quorum details and co-signer contact information
  • Exchange account login recovery procedures
  • Step-by-step instructions for transferring Bitcoin to beneficiaries
  • Contact information for your Bitcoin-knowledgeable attorney and/or technical advisor
  • Current wallet addresses and balance summary (not seed phrases)

The Letter of Instruction is referenced in your will and trust — "see the attached Letter of Instruction for digital asset access information" — but never included in those documents. It is stored in a fireproof safe, with a trusted attorney under seal, or using a secure key management service.

Document 02

A Bitcoin-Specific Trust with Digital Asset Provisions

A standard revocable living trust is not enough for Bitcoin-wealthy families. Your trust needs explicit provisions for digital assets, including:

  • Express authorization for the trustee to hold, manage, and transfer digital assets
  • RUFADAA-compliant language granting fiduciary access to all digital accounts and wallets
  • Trustee powers specific to digital assets (authority to use private keys, interact with blockchain protocols, engage custodians)
  • Successor trustee selection criteria that includes technical competency or a co-trustee with technical expertise
  • Reference to the Letter of Instruction without reproducing sensitive information
  • Dynasty provisions if generational wealth transfer is the goal (best achieved in Wyoming or South Dakota situs)

For the full framework on trust structures for Bitcoin, see the Bitcoin Estate Planning Guide and our guide to Bitcoin irrevocable trusts.

Document 03

A Key Access Protocol (Multisig, Timelock, or Trusted Custodian)

The legal documents tell your fiduciary they can access your Bitcoin. The key access protocol tells them how. This is the technical layer of your estate plan, and it determines whether your family actually gets the Bitcoin after everything else is in place.

  • Multisig inheritance: A 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multisig scheme where one key is held by the owner, one by a trusted family member or attorney, and one in a secure backup location. At death, the fiduciary can reconstruct the quorum without any single point of failure. See our guide on multisig estate planning.
  • Timelock arrangements: Scripted Bitcoin transactions that become broadcastable after a defined period — enabling automated inheritance without relying on any third party to take action.
  • Regulated Bitcoin custodian: Institutions like Unchained Capital, Casa, or Fidelity Digital Assets offer qualified custody with explicit inheritance and succession procedures built into the account structure. Most estate-friendly option for holders who prefer institutional infrastructure.

The key access protocol must be documented in your Letter of Instruction and must be understood (at least procedurally) by your chosen executor or trustee before you die. Test it. Walk through the access procedure with your fiduciary while you're alive and can answer questions.

⚠ Critical Warning

Never put your seed phrase or private key in your will. A will becomes a public document when it enters probate. Any person with access to the probate court — which is public in every state — can read your will and extract your seed phrase. By the time your heirs realize what happened, the Bitcoin is gone. Permanently.

This is the single most common catastrophic mistake Bitcoin holders make in estate planning. It converts a secure custody arrangement into a public broadcast of your keys. Use a private Letter of Instruction for all key material, kept entirely outside the public record.

Section 6: Tax Implications at Inheritance

The tax treatment of Bitcoin at death is one of the most favorable of any asset class — if structured correctly. Understanding the mechanics before you die (and before you make decisions about how to hold Bitcoin) is essential.

The Step-Up in Basis: Bitcoin's Most Powerful Estate Planning Tool

Under IRC § 1014, assets in a taxable estate receive a stepped-up cost basis to fair market value on the date of death. For Bitcoin, this means:

  • You bought 5 BTC in 2018 at an average cost of $8,000 per BTC ($40,000 total)
  • Bitcoin is worth $90,000 per BTC at your death ($450,000 total)
  • Your heirs inherit the 5 BTC with a cost basis of $450,000 — not $40,000
  • The $410,000 of capital appreciation that occurred during your lifetime disappears for income tax purposes
  • Your heirs can sell immediately and owe zero capital gains tax on that appreciation

This step-up applies to Bitcoin held in direct ownership or through a grantor trust. It does not apply to Bitcoin held in an IRA or other tax-deferred account (where all distributions are ordinary income to the heirs). For most high-net-worth Bitcoin holders, this creates a strong argument for direct ownership or grantor trust structures over IRA holdings.

Estate Tax if Your Estate Exceeds the Exemption

For Bitcoin holders with estates above the exemption threshold — expected to be approximately $7M per person after TCJA sunset — the federal estate tax is 40% on all amounts above the exemption. A $20M Bitcoin estate with no planning faces a potential $5.2M federal estate tax bill ($20M minus $7M exemption × 40%).

The primary tools for reducing this exposure are: irrevocable trusts (GRATs, SLATs, IDGTs), annual gifting (up to $18,000 per recipient in 2024, $36,000 for couples), charitable structures (CRTs, donor-advised funds), and strategic timing of transfers before Bitcoin appreciates further. Section 7: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

These are the most expensive and most preventable errors in Bitcoin estate planning. We see variants of all of them regularly.

  • ✕
    Putting a seed phrase in your will. Already covered above — but worth repeating because it is the single most catastrophic mistake. Wills are public records. Seed phrases in a will are publicly broadcast private keys. Don't do it under any circumstances. Use a private Letter of Instruction stored outside the probate record.
  • ✕
    Relying on exchange accounts alone. Exchange accounts are subject to counterparty risk — the exchange can freeze, fail, or be hacked. They are also subject to exchange terms of service that may make estate access difficult even with RUFADAA authority. A well-structured estate plan combines direct custody with a documented access protocol. Don't build a generational wealth plan on a platform you don't control.
  • ✕
    Assuming your spouse knows where the keys are. This assumption has cost families their entire Bitcoin holdings. In a survey of Bitcoin holders, a significant majority reported that their spouses did not know the location of their private keys or seed phrases. If you become incapacitated or die suddenly, your family needs to find the keys without your assistance. Document the location — separately from the keys themselves — and make sure your executor or spouse knows where to look.
  • ✕
    Using a generic estate plan not updated for Bitcoin. The vast majority of estate attorneys do not have specific training in digital asset law, RUFADAA implementation, or custody architecture. A standard revocable trust written five years ago almost certainly does not contain the provisions needed for Bitcoin. Have your existing documents reviewed and updated by an attorney with demonstrated digital asset experience.
  • ✕
    Failing to account for state estate tax. Many Bitcoin holders focus exclusively on federal estate tax — where the exemption is high and immediate exposure may be low. But state estate taxes can start at $1M (Oregon) and apply at rates up to 20% (Washington State). If you live in a high-risk state, this is often the first and most immediate planning priority, regardless of where you are relative to the federal exemption.
  • ✕
    Naming a technically unsophisticated trustee without technical support. If your trustee has never interacted with a hardware wallet, does not understand how to verify a Bitcoin address, and has no protocol for who to call when the hardware wallet returns an error — your Bitcoin is at serious risk even with perfect legal documentation. Name a co-trustee with technical competency, or establish a relationship with a technical advisor who will be on call for your estate administration.
  • ✕
    Never testing the access protocol while alive. The best time to discover that your key management scheme doesn't work is not during estate administration under time pressure. Test your access protocol annually. Walk your executor through the procedure at least once while you are alive and can answer questions. Treat it like a fire drill — the goal is to discover problems before the emergency.
  • ✕
    Ignoring the TCJA sunset. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act estate tax exemption is currently $13.61M per person — but it is scheduled to sunset at the end of 2025, reverting to approximately $7M per person. Transfers made under the current high exemption are protected under current IRS regulations even after sunset. For Bitcoin holders near or above the expected post-sunset exemption, the window to make large gifts or trust transfers at the current exemption level is closing. Don't miss it. See our analysis of the 2026 estate tax exemption changes.
✓ What Good Planning Looks Like

A Bitcoin-wealthy family with a complete estate plan has: a RUFADAA-compliant trust with digital asset provisions, a private Letter of Instruction stored securely with their attorney, a multisig custody arrangement with documented recovery procedures, a named technical advisor their executor can call, an annual review process to update all documents as holdings change, and a clear understanding of their state estate tax exposure with a documented mitigation strategy. Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to Bitcoin when someone dies without a key access plan? +
If a Bitcoin holder dies without documenting how to access their private keys or seed phrases, the Bitcoin is effectively lost forever. Unlike a brokerage account where a custodian can verify identity and release assets to an executor, Bitcoin held in self-custody has no custodian. Without the private key or seed phrase, no court order, no probate proceeding, and no government entity can recover the coins. Researchers estimate 3–4 million BTC — roughly 15–20% of the total supply — is permanently lost, much of it from early holders who died or became incapacitated without succession plans. The first step in any Bitcoin estate plan is a secure, documented key access protocol.
What is RUFADAA and how does it apply to Bitcoin? +
RUFADAA — the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act — is model legislation adopted in 47+ states that grants fiduciaries (executors, trustees, attorneys-in-fact) legal authority to access, manage, and transfer digital assets including Bitcoin after an owner's death or incapacity. Without RUFADAA, your executor may have no legal right to access your Bitcoin wallets, private keys, or exchange accounts. The Act creates a layered priority system: your own documented directions come first (an online tool or estate-planning document), then the platform's terms of service, then default fiduciary rights. For Bitcoin holders, this means expressly authorizing fiduciary access in your estate plan documents and providing the technical means to actually exercise that authority.
Should I put my Bitcoin seed phrase in my will? +
No — never put a seed phrase or private key in your will. A will becomes a public record when it enters probate. Any person with access to the probate court — which is public in every state — can read your will and extract your seed phrase, sweeping your Bitcoin before your heirs receive a single satoshi. Instead, document key access information in a private Bitcoin Letter of Instruction — a separate, secure document stored outside the will and accessible only to your designated fiduciary. The Letter of Instruction is referenced in your will and trust but not filed with any court.
Does Bitcoin get a step-up in basis at death? +
Yes — Bitcoin held in direct ownership (not through an IRA or other tax-deferred account) receives a stepped-up cost basis to the fair market value on the date of death under IRC § 1014. This means if you purchased Bitcoin at $10,000 and it is worth $90,000 at your death, your heirs inherit at the $90,000 basis and owe zero capital gains tax on the $80,000 of appreciation that occurred during your lifetime. This is one of the most powerful wealth transfer mechanisms available for Bitcoin holders, and it argues strongly for holding Bitcoin in direct ownership (or a grantor trust) rather than an IRA for estate planning purposes. Estate tax may still apply if the estate exceeds the applicable exemption.
Which states have the best laws for Bitcoin inheritance planning? +
Wyoming leads for Bitcoin inheritance law. It was the first state to legally recognize digital assets as property, has enacted specific digital asset custodian statutes, allows DAO LLCs, has abolished the rule against perpetuities (enabling perpetual dynasty trusts), has no state estate or inheritance tax, and offers robust directed trust and domestic asset protection trust (DAPT) frameworks. South Dakota and Nevada are close seconds. The worst jurisdictions for Bitcoin holders include Oregon ($1M estate tax exemption — the lowest in the US), Massachusetts (a cliff-effect $2M exemption), Washington State (rates to 20%), and states without full RUFADAA adoption where fiduciary access to digital assets lacks statutory clarity.
How can proper estate planning protect against Bitcoin hidden asset claims in divorce? +
Thorough documentation of Bitcoin holdings — purchase records, wallet addresses, exchange account statements, and a current net worth schedule — is the best protection against hidden asset allegations in any divorce or estate dispute. Blockchain transactions are permanently recorded and fully auditable; forensic accountants and blockchain analytics firms can trace Bitcoin movements with high precision. If you have properly documented your Bitcoin as a marital or separate asset in your estate plan, prenuptial agreement, or financial disclosure, you are protected. The families most vulnerable to hidden-asset litigation are those with undocumented or informal Bitcoin holdings — not those with a formal, transparent estate plan.
What is a Bitcoin Letter of Instruction? +
A Bitcoin Letter of Instruction is a private document — not filed with any court — that provides your executor or trustee with the practical information needed to access and manage your Bitcoin. It typically includes: the location of all hardware wallets and seed phrase backups, the access protocol (PIN codes, passphrases, multisig quorum details), the names and contacts of any co-signers or custodians, a list of exchange accounts with login recovery procedures, and step-by-step instructions for transferring Bitcoin to designated beneficiaries. The Letter of Instruction is referenced in your will and trust as the authorized source for digital asset access but is stored securely — in a fireproof safe, with a trusted attorney, or in a sealed envelope with a co-signatory — rather than in the will itself.
What is the estate tax on a $5 million Bitcoin estate? +
The answer depends heavily on your domicile, marital status, and prior planning. At the federal level, if the TCJA exemption has sunset to approximately $7M per person, a $5M Bitcoin estate is below the federal threshold — meaning zero federal estate tax. However, if you live in Oregon, a $5M Bitcoin estate already faces substantial state estate tax on everything above the $1M exemption, potentially amounting to $300,000–$450,000 in state-level tax. A Wyoming resident with the same $5M estate pays nothing at the state level. Capital gains tax on the estate is eliminated through the step-up in basis — heirs inherit at date-of-death fair market value. Proper planning (trust structures, domicile optimization, gifting strategies) can bring the total tax bill on a $5M Bitcoin estate to zero in many scenarios.
