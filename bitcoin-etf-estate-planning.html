Bitcoin ETF vs Direct Custody: The Estate Planning Implications Every Wealthy Family Must Understand

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  18 min read  ·  February 25, 2026

Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund, Mubadala Investment Company, quietly disclosed a $462 million position in BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) in early 2025. Truist Wealth, a major US bank advisory firm, added spot Bitcoin ETFs to its managed portfolio offerings. State Street is now custodying Bitcoin ETF assets. The institutional infrastructure that did not exist three years ago has been built, and institutional capital is flowing through it.

The financial press has covered these developments extensively, almost entirely through the lens of adoption, legitimacy, and price impact. What nobody is writing about is the estate planning question: when a sovereign wealth fund, family office, or high-net-worth individual buys a Bitcoin ETF instead of holding Bitcoin directly, what are they actually choosing — and what are they giving up?

This is not an academic exercise. For a family with a $5 million Bitcoin position, the structural choice between an ETF and direct custody can affect tens of millions of dollars in estate outcomes over a 20-year horizon. The analysis requires understanding what an ETF actually is at the legal and structural level, how the step-up in basis rules apply, what estate planning strategies are only available with direct custody, and when the ETF is genuinely the right tool.

Let's work through it carefully.

Section 1: What You Actually Own When You Buy a Bitcoin ETF

When you purchase shares in the iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT), the Fidelity Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC), or any spot Bitcoin ETF, you are not buying Bitcoin. You are buying a security — a share in a Delaware statutory trust — that is backstopped by Bitcoin held by a qualified custodian (Coinbase Custody, for most of the major products).

The legal chain of ownership looks like this:

  1. You hold shares in the ETF trust, held at your brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, Interactive Brokers, etc.)
  2. The ETF trust holds Bitcoin, custodied at a regulated custodian
  3. The custodian (e.g., Coinbase Custody Trust Company) holds the private keys

At no point do you hold Bitcoin. You hold a claim on a trust that holds Bitcoin. That distinction matters enormously for estate planning, and it matters in ways that the financial industry has largely failed to communicate.

As a security, ETF shares are:

  • Registered with the SEC and FINRA-regulated
  • Subject to brokerage custodianship (SIPC-protected up to $500,000)
  • Transferable only through broker-dealer channels
  • Subject to probate if not properly titled (TOD designations, trust ownership, etc.)
  • Disclosed on Form 13F if held by institutional managers with ≥$100M AUM
  • Governed by the terms of the trust indenture and SEC regulations — not by you

Bitcoin held directly, by contrast, is a bearer asset. Whoever controls the private keys controls the Bitcoin. There is no issuer, no custodian, no brokerage, no intermediary standing between you and the asset. That property — which many investors treat as a technical curiosity — turns out to be extraordinarily significant for estate planning.

Key Structural Distinction

An ETF share is a security with a custodian chain. Direct Bitcoin is a bearer asset with no intermediary. Every estate planning implication in this article flows from that single difference.

Section 2: The Step-Up in Basis Question

The step-up in basis is one of the most powerful wealth transfer mechanisms in the US tax code. Under IRC §1014, when an heir inherits an asset, their cost basis is "stepped up" to the fair market value of the asset at the date of the decedent's death. Any appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime evaporates for income tax purposes.

Example: You bought Bitcoin at $30,000 per coin in 2021. You die in 2030 when Bitcoin is worth $300,000 per coin. Your heir inherits the Bitcoin with a cost basis of $300,000 per coin — not $30,000. The $270,000 per-coin gain is never taxed as income. It was subject to estate tax (if the estate exceeded the exemption), but it avoids capital gains tax entirely upon inheritance.

Do Bitcoin ETF Shares Receive a Step-Up?

Yes — ETF shares held in a taxable brokerage account do receive a step-up in basis at death, just like stocks, mutual funds, and other securities. On this narrow question, Bitcoin ETF shares are not worse than directly held Bitcoin. Both can receive a step-up if held in a taxable account.

However, there are two important caveats:

Caveat 1: Retirement accounts. Bitcoin ETF shares held in a Traditional IRA, 401(k), or other pre-tax retirement account do not receive a step-up in basis. Heirs who inherit a pre-tax retirement account must take distributions — and pay ordinary income tax on every dollar — within 10 years of the original account holder's death (under the SECURE Act 2.0 for most non-spouse beneficiaries). This means that $5 million in Bitcoin ETF held in a Traditional IRA could produce a $1.85 million+ tax bill for heirs simply from distributions, on top of any estate tax exposure. Directly held Bitcoin cannot be held in a standard IRA, so this particular trap applies exclusively to the ETF vehicle.

Caveat 2: The step-up is only the beginning of the estate planning story. For a high-net-worth family, the goal is not merely to get a step-up in basis at death — it is to move as much appreciation as possible out of the taxable estate before death. That is where the structural differences between ETF shares and directly held Bitcoin become decisive.

The step-up is a floor, not a ceiling. Sophisticated estate planning is about removing appreciation from the estate entirely — through GRATs, irrevocable trusts, charitable vehicles, and other structures. The step-up only helps heirs with the income tax. It does nothing about the estate tax, which can reach 40% on estates above the federal exemption.

Section 3: Advanced Estate Planning Strategies Only Available With Direct Custody

This is where the analysis becomes material for wealthy families. There is a set of estate planning strategies that are theoretically available for both ETF shares and directly held Bitcoin — but practically, the unique properties of Bitcoin as a bearer asset create advantages in direct custody structures that ETF shares cannot replicate.

3.1 The Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

A GRAT is an estate planning vehicle in which you transfer assets into an irrevocable trust, receive annuity payments back for a defined term, and at the end of the term, any appreciation above the IRS Section 7520 rate passes to heirs gift-tax free. GRATs work best for volatile assets with high appreciation potential — which is precisely what Bitcoin is.

You can fund a GRAT with either Bitcoin ETF shares or directly held Bitcoin. Both structures allow you to freeze the estate value at the time of contribution and pass appreciation to heirs. However, there are practical differences:

  • Valuation: ETF shares have a published daily NAV, making valuation straightforward. Direct Bitcoin has a spot price from multiple exchanges, which requires a documented valuation methodology. This can actually create planning opportunities — if you can argue for a discount based on liquidity constraints of a large direct position, you may be able to get assets into the GRAT at a lower taxable value.
  • Custodianship: A GRAT funded with ETF shares still requires a brokerage account in the trust's name. A GRAT funded with directly held Bitcoin can be structured with a multi-signature custody arrangement where the trustee controls keys — potentially more flexible and less broker-dependent.
  • Volatility risk: Both structures are exposed to Bitcoin price risk during the GRAT term. A zeroed-out GRAT (where you structure the annuity to equal the §7520 rate) passes all upside to heirs with no gift tax cost. The direct Bitcoin version may allow more creative structuring around timing and valuation.

3.2 Irrevocable Trusts and Dynasty Trusts

For families looking to pass Bitcoin wealth across multiple generations, a dynasty trust — an irrevocable trust designed to hold assets for 100+ years — is a powerful vehicle. Some states (Nevada, South Dakota, Delaware, Alaska) allow perpetual trusts with no rule against perpetuities limitations.

Bitcoin ETF shares can be held in a dynasty trust. But directly held Bitcoin in a dynasty trust may carry advantages that ETF shares cannot match:

  • No brokerage dependency: A dynasty trust holding ETF shares will always be dependent on a brokerage account — subject to that brokerage's continued existence, policies, and fee structure over 100+ years. A dynasty trust holding Bitcoin directly can be structured with multi-signature keys where different trustees or beneficiaries hold key shards, requiring no financial intermediary for the underlying asset to exist.
  • Self-sovereign custody across generations: The multi-signature structure can be designed so that any two of three designated family members can authorize transactions — enabling generational transfer without any third party. This is not possible with ETF shares, which always require a brokerage.
  • No counterparty risk: ETF shares are backed by a custodian (Coinbase Custody, BNY Mellon, etc.) — introducing counterparty risk over multi-decade timeframes. Directly held Bitcoin has no custodian.

3.3 Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) and Appreciated Assets

A Charitable Remainder Trust allows you to contribute appreciated assets, receive an income stream, take a partial charitable deduction, and pass the remainder to charity at death — all while avoiding immediate capital gains tax on the contribution.

For Bitcoin, the CRT is particularly powerful for long-term holders with a very low cost basis. Contributing $5 million in Bitcoin with a $100,000 basis to a CRT allows the trust to sell Bitcoin without triggering capital gains, then reinvest in a diversified income-producing portfolio. The grantor receives an annuity stream and a charitable deduction. The remaining assets pass to charity at death.

ETF shares work the same way mechanically. The distinction with direct Bitcoin comes in the contribution: transferring directly held Bitcoin to a CRT is a direct transfer of a bearer asset — potentially simpler and more flexible than transferring brokerage-custodied securities, which involves broker-dealer coordination. More importantly, if you intend to contribute Bitcoin specifically (rather than selling it and contributing cash), direct custody gives you complete control over the timing and mechanics of the contribution.

3.4 Privacy and the 13F Problem

Any investment manager with $100 million or more in US equities and ETFs under management is required to file Form 13F with the SEC within 45 days of each calendar quarter end. These filings are public. Abu Dhabi's Mubadala holding was disclosed precisely because of this rule — $462 million in IBIT triggered mandatory public disclosure.

For family offices that have crossed the $100M threshold, Bitcoin ETF holdings are therefore public information. Any analysis of your Bitcoin position — your cost basis (if you've disclosed it elsewhere), your concentration risk, your portfolio construction — is available to anyone who reads your 13F filings.

Directly held Bitcoin in a private trust structure is not subject to 13F requirements. It does not appear in any public filing. This is not about hiding anything illegal — it is about basic financial privacy for wealthy families who reasonably prefer not to publicly disclose the extent and nature of their Bitcoin holdings.

3.5 Generational Transfer via Multisig Key Ceremony

Perhaps the most practically distinctive advantage of directly held Bitcoin for estate planning is the ability to conduct a multisig key ceremony — a structured, documented process for establishing and distributing control of a Bitcoin wallet across multiple parties.

Here is what this looks like in practice:

  1. A Bitcoin attorney and a qualified custody architect design a 3-of-5 multisig wallet (any 3 of 5 keys can authorize transactions)
  2. The wallet is created in a structured ceremony, documented and witnessed
  3. Key shards are distributed among: the trustee, two adult children (contingent beneficiaries), a professional co-trustee, and a hardware key held in a bank vault
  4. Instructions are documented for surviving key holders to reconstruct access if two key holders become unavailable
  5. At the grantor's death, the three surviving key holders authorize the transfer directly — no brokerage, no probate, no intermediary

This is structurally impossible with ETF shares. ETF shares require a brokerage transfer process — which may require letters testamentary, a death certificate, account transfer paperwork, and potentially probate proceedings if the account is not properly titled. The multisig key ceremony compresses the generational transfer to a structured process under the family's own control.

The Bearer Asset Advantage

Directly held Bitcoin can be transferred at death through a pre-planned multisig key ceremony — with no brokerage, no probate delay, no intermediary. This capability does not exist for ETF shares, which always require broker-dealer involvement in every transfer.

Section 4: When the Bitcoin ETF IS the Right Tool

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the cases where the ETF is the appropriate vehicle. There are several.

Retirement Accounts (With Important Caveats)

Bitcoin ETFs can be held in Self-Directed IRAs, standard IRAs (at brokerages that allow ETF holdings), and some 401(k) plans. For tax-deferred accumulation, this is the primary vehicle available. Direct Bitcoin cannot be held in a standard IRA without a Self-Directed IRA structure with a specialized custodian.

The estate planning caveat is significant: pre-tax retirement accounts holding Bitcoin ETFs create a potentially brutal inheritance tax scenario — estate tax on the gross value, plus ordinary income tax on distributions, with a 10-year distribution window for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Roth conversions should be seriously evaluated for Bitcoin ETF positions in pre-tax accounts, particularly as Bitcoin appreciates.

Institutional Mandates That Require Securities

Many institutional investors — pension funds, endowments, registered investment advisors — have investment policy statements that restrict holdings to securities. Bitcoin ETFs are the only vehicle that allows these institutions to gain Bitcoin exposure within their existing regulatory and governance frameworks. For these investors, the ETF is not a choice between "good" and "better" — it is the only viable path.

Smaller Positions and Liquidity Needs

For Bitcoin positions under $500,000, the complexity, cost, and operational burden of multi-signature custody architecture may not be justified. The ETF provides instant liquidity, brokerage-level simplicity, and SIPC protection. For families who want Bitcoin exposure without the operational overhead of self-custody — and where the estate planning implications are less material due to position size — the ETF may be genuinely appropriate.

Tax-Loss Harvesting Flexibility

ETF shares may create more flexible tax-loss harvesting opportunities than directly held Bitcoin, because multiple ETFs track the same underlying asset — allowing investors to sell IBIT and buy FBTC (or vice versa) without triggering wash sale rules that apply to securities... with the important caveat that the IRS has not definitively ruled on whether wash sale rules apply to Bitcoin or Bitcoin ETFs. An attorney's guidance on current IRS guidance is essential here.

Section 5: The Hybrid Approach

The most sophisticated family offices are not choosing between ETF and direct custody. They are using both — deliberately, for different purposes.

A structure that has emerged in practice among HNWI Bitcoin holders:

  • ETF layer (20–30% of position): Held at a brokerage for liquidity, simplicity, and easy partial liquidation. Used for rebalancing, charitable giving (DAF contributions of appreciated ETF shares are straightforward), and positions inside IRAs or taxable accounts where the primary goal is liquidity.
  • Direct custody core (70–80% of position): Held in a properly structured irrevocable trust or dynasty trust with multi-signature custody architecture. This is the estate planning core — structured for generational transfer, privacy, and advanced planning strategies.

This bifurcation acknowledges that the ETF is genuinely useful for certain functions while ensuring that the bulk of the position is structured optimally for estate planning. It also provides operational flexibility: if liquidity is needed quickly, the ETF layer can be liquidated immediately, while the direct custody core remains intact in its estate planning structure.

The Trust Structure Caveat

The Option B outcome — $0 estate tax on the appreciating Bitcoin — requires proper execution. Specifically:

  1. The Bitcoin must be transferred into the irrevocable trust when the trust is established — contributions later in life may trigger gift tax
  2. The trust must be properly structured (irrevocable, with appropriate provisions for beneficiaries, trustees, and investment powers over digital assets)
  3. The trust must be funded before the Bitcoin appreciates significantly — otherwise, gift tax applies on the full fair market value at the time of contribution
  4. State law matters: some states are more favorable for Bitcoin trust structures (Nevada, South Dakota, Delaware, Wyoming)
  5. The trustee must have authority to hold and manage digital assets — most standard trust documents did not contemplate Bitcoin and may need to be drafted or amended specifically

None of these steps can be accomplished with an ETF position that has not been restructured. A family holding Bitcoin ETF shares in a taxable brokerage account has, by default, chosen Option A — with the full $81.8M potentially in their taxable estate at Year 20.

The Cost of Inertia

For a family with a $5M Bitcoin position today, every year of delay in restructuring increases the cost of estate planning — because the trust must be funded at fair market value, and as Bitcoin appreciates, the gift tax cost of funding the trust rises. The optimal time to structure is when Bitcoin is at a lower value — which is always, in retrospect, in the past.

Frequently Asked Questions: Bitcoin ETF Estate Planning

Do Bitcoin ETF shares get a step-up in basis at death?

Yes — like most securities, Bitcoin ETF shares held in a taxable account can receive a step-up in cost basis to fair market value at the date of death under IRC §1014. This eliminates embedded capital gains for heirs. However, ETF shares held inside an IRA or 401(k) do not receive a step-up and will be taxed as ordinary income when distributed to beneficiaries.

Can a Bitcoin ETF be placed in a trust for estate planning?

Yes, Bitcoin ETF shares can be held in revocable or irrevocable trusts, GRATs, CRTs, and other trust vehicles. However, they remain securities — subject to brokerage custodianship, broker-dealer involvement in every transfer, and potential 13F disclosure for institutional holders. They do not carry the unique bearer asset properties of directly held Bitcoin, which can be transferred via multisig key ceremony without any intermediary.

What is the estate tax difference between a Bitcoin ETF and direct Bitcoin?

For estate tax purposes, both Bitcoin ETF shares and directly held Bitcoin are included in the gross estate at fair market value — there is no automatic estate tax advantage to one structure over the other. The difference emerges in what planning strategies are accessible. Direct Bitcoin custody enables: transfer via multisig key ceremony (no broker), greater trust structuring flexibility, privacy from 13F disclosure, and the ability to use certain charitable and family limited partnership strategies that work differently for bearer assets than for brokerage-custodied securities.

Is a Bitcoin ETF appropriate for retirement account estate planning?

Bitcoin ETFs in IRAs and 401(k)s can be appropriate for tax-deferred accumulation, but from an estate planning perspective they carry significant disadvantages: no step-up in basis (heirs pay ordinary income tax on distributions), subject to the 10-year distribution rule under the SECURE Act for most non-spouse beneficiaries, and still subject to estate tax on the gross value. For large retirement accounts, Roth conversions or strategic spending down of pre-tax Bitcoin ETF accounts may be a consideration worth exploring with a tax professional.

What is a multisig key ceremony and why does it matter for inheritance?

A multisig key ceremony is a structured process for generating, distributing, and documenting the private keys that control a Bitcoin wallet. For inheritance planning, it allows families to pre-authorize heirs as co-signers on a wallet — so that at death, the heir can access Bitcoin directly without involving any brokerage, probate court, or financial intermediary. This is not possible with ETF shares, which require a brokerage account transfer process and potentially probate if not properly titled.

What is the 13F disclosure risk for institutional Bitcoin ETF holders?

Institutional investment managers with more than $100 million in assets under management are required to file Form 13F with the SEC quarterly, disclosing all equity and ETF holdings by position. Bitcoin ETF shares held at this scale are therefore publicly disclosed. Directly held Bitcoin in a private trust structure is not subject to 13F requirements — it remains confidential. For family offices and wealthy families who value financial privacy, this distinction is material.

Should wealthy families choose ETF or direct Bitcoin for estate planning?

There is no universal answer. For Bitcoin in retirement accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s), the ETF is typically the only viable vehicle. For taxable accounts where advanced estate planning is a priority — particularly for families holding significant Bitcoin wealth — direct custody in a properly structured trust generally unlocks more planning flexibility, more privacy, and more control over generational transfer. Many sophisticated families use both: ETF for liquidity and simplicity in smaller positions, direct custody for their estate planning core. Consult a qualified estate planning attorney who understands both Bitcoin and advanced trust strategies.

The Bottom Line

The Bitcoin ETF has been one of the most important financial product innovations of the past decade. It has made Bitcoin accessible to institutional capital that could not previously hold it, provided regulatory clarity, and created enormous liquidity. These are real benefits.

But the ETF is a wrapper — a security issued by a trust, custodied at a broker, governed by SEC regulations. It is fundamentally different from Bitcoin itself, and those differences matter profoundly for estate planning. The sovereign wealth funds buying Bitcoin ETFs today are making a legitimate choice for their mandates. But they are also, implicitly, accepting a set of structural constraints that a properly designed direct custody arrangement would not impose.

For HNWI families with meaningful Bitcoin positions — say, $1 million and above — the choice between ETF and direct custody is not primarily an investment question. It is a legal, structural, and estate planning question. And the analysis leads to the same conclusion that applies across Bitcoin wealth management generally: structure determines outcome, and structure must be designed deliberately, early, and with qualified advisors who understand both the asset and the legal architecture.

The families who will benefit most from Bitcoin's appreciation over the next 20 years are not necessarily those who accumulated the most — they are those who structured their holdings correctly so that appreciation flowed to heirs instead of to the IRS.
