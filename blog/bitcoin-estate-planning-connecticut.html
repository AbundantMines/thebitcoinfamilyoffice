Bitcoin Estate Planning in Connecticut: No State Estate Tax Since 2023

By Hal Turner  ·  February 25, 2026  ·  1,800 words

Connecticut made a quiet but consequential change to its tax law that every CT resident with significant Bitcoin holdings should understand: the state eliminated its estate and gift tax entirely, effective January 1, 2023.

For Connecticut Bitcoin holders, this represents a major shift in planning priorities. Where CT residents once faced both a state estate tax — with one of the lowest exemptions in the country — and the federal estate tax, they now face only the federal tax. The state layer is gone.

What remains is still consequential. Connecticut's income tax treatment of capital gains, combined with the uncertainty around federal estate tax exemptions, means that CT Bitcoin holders still have significant planning work to do. But the framework has changed, and the strategies that made sense in 2022 need to be revisited in light of the 2023 elimination.

What Connecticut Changed — and Why It Matters

Connecticut's estate tax had long been an outlier. In its final years, the CT estate tax applied to estates above a $9.1 million exemption — one of the lowest state exemptions in the country at the time. Many states with estate taxes have exemptions that match the federal level or higher. Connecticut's was deliberately lower, catching middle-market estates that would have owed nothing under federal rules alone.

The CT estate tax was also applied on a graduated basis with rates up to 12% — on top of the federal estate tax. For a Connecticut Bitcoin holder with a moderately appreciated position, the combined federal and state estate tax could represent a significant portion of the estate's value being paid to two separate taxing authorities.

Connecticut's 2023 elimination of the estate tax — included in the state's budget legislation — means that residents who die on or after January 1, 2023 owe no Connecticut estate tax regardless of estate size. The $9.1 million exemption, the graduated rates, the filing requirements — all gone.

TCJA and Federal Exemption Uncertainty: The federal estate tax exemption was significantly increased by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Those provisions have been subject to sunset and legislative action. Verify the current federal exemption amount with your estate planning attorney — this is an active area of law, and the applicable exemption for your planning may differ from figures published in older resources.

Connecticut Has No Inheritance Tax Either

It is worth being explicit: Connecticut has never had a state inheritance tax, and that remains true today. The distinction matters because these are two different taxes that people frequently conflate.

An estate tax is paid by the decedent's estate before assets are distributed to heirs. Connecticut had this tax and eliminated it in 2023.

An inheritance tax is paid by the heir upon receiving assets. Several states — Pennsylvania, Iowa, Kentucky, Maryland, Nebraska, and New Jersey among them — impose inheritance taxes. Connecticut does not and never has.

For Connecticut Bitcoin holders, this means neither the estate nor the heirs owe any Connecticut tax at the moment of inheritance. The only state tax exposure for Bitcoin in Connecticut relates to income taxes on dispositions during life — which, as discussed below, remain significant.

Connecticut as a Common Law State

Connecticut is a common law equitable distribution state, not a community property state. This has important implications for Bitcoin planning, particularly for married couples.

In community property states (California, Texas, Arizona, and several others), assets acquired during marriage are generally owned 50/50 by both spouses by default — which carries specific tax advantages, including a double step-up in basis on community property assets at the death of one spouse.

In Connecticut, assets are owned by whoever holds title. Bitcoin purchased and held in one spouse's name is that spouse's separate property. This means:

  • A surviving spouse in Connecticut receives a step-up in basis only on the deceased spouse's share of any jointly-titled asset, or on assets held solely in the deceased's name
  • Married CT couples should be intentional about titling — either joint tenancy, trust ownership, or other structures — rather than defaulting to individual ownership
  • Marital deduction planning (unlimited transfers to a surviving US citizen spouse, estate-tax free) remains available at the federal level
"With Connecticut's state estate tax gone, the planning conversation for CT Bitcoin holders shifts entirely to federal estate tax strategy and income tax management."

Connecticut Income Tax — The Planning Priority That Remains

While Connecticut eliminated its estate tax, it has not eliminated its income tax — and for Bitcoin holders, Connecticut's treatment of capital gains is meaningfully unfavorable compared to federal rules.

Connecticut taxes capital gains as ordinary income. There is no preferential long-term capital gains rate at the state level. Connecticut's top marginal income tax rate is 6.99%, which applies to income above $500,000 (single) or $1,000,000 (married filing jointly).

This means a Connecticut Bitcoin holder who sells $1 million in appreciated Bitcoin faces:

  • Federal long-term capital gains tax (20% at the top bracket, plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax)
  • Connecticut income tax at up to 6.99% on the same gain — with no rate preference for long-term holding

The income tax dimension makes CT Bitcoin planning meaningfully different from a state like Florida or Texas, where no state income tax applies. A CT resident considering a large Bitcoin disposition should model the full state and federal tax cost, ideally before executing.

The Step-Up in Basis — Still the Most Powerful CT Tool

Under current federal law, Bitcoin (and other capital assets) received by inheritance get a step-up in cost basis to the fair market value at the date of death. This means an heir who sells inherited Bitcoin immediately after receiving it owes no capital gains tax — state or federal — on the appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime.

For Connecticut Bitcoin holders with large unrealized gains, the step-up in basis remains the most powerful tax-reducing mechanism available. Because Connecticut now has no estate tax, the step-up can eliminate decades of accumulated gain without triggering a state estate tax liability in return — a combination that was not available before 2023.

Bitcoin Mining & Connecticut Tax Planning

For Connecticut Bitcoin holders subject to the state's 6.99% capital gains rate, Bitcoin mining offers a compelling tax planning alternative: rather than selling appreciated Bitcoin (triggering 6.99% CT state tax plus federal capital gains), mining generates new Bitcoin with deductible operating expenses and equipment depreciation. This can meaningfully reduce overall tax exposure for holders building long-term positions.

Planning Considerations for Connecticut Bitcoin Holders

With the state estate tax eliminated, what does CT Bitcoin estate planning actually look like in 2026?

Trusts Are Still Valuable — For Federal Reasons

Connecticut trusts are no longer primarily about avoiding the state estate tax — that tax is gone. But they remain essential for two purposes: federal estate tax planning for larger estates, and probate avoidance for all estates.

A revocable living trust keeps Bitcoin out of the probate process (which is public and can be slow) and allows for seamless transfer to heirs without court involvement. This is a compelling reason to use trust structures regardless of estate tax exposure.

For estates large enough to approach the federal estate tax threshold, irrevocable trust structures — grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs), spousal lifetime access trusts (SLATs), charitable remainder trusts (CRTs) — remain the primary planning tools for reducing federal taxable estate. Connecticut's elimination of its state tax simplifies the analysis but does not reduce the value of federal estate tax planning for larger Bitcoin holders.

South Dakota and Wyoming Dynasty Trusts Remain Relevant

Even with no Connecticut state estate tax, the use of South Dakota or Wyoming as a trust situs remains highly relevant for CT residents with significant Bitcoin holdings.

South Dakota and Wyoming offer several trust-law advantages unavailable in Connecticut: perpetual dynasty trust statutes (no rule against perpetuities), no state income tax on trust income, strong asset protection provisions, and directed trust statutes that allow the investment and distribution functions to be separated from the administrative trustee.

A Connecticut resident can establish a South Dakota or Wyoming situs trust, fund it with Bitcoin, and have trust income grow free of state income tax — a meaningful benefit given Connecticut's 6.99% rate on capital gains from trust distributions. The trust is created under CT law but administered and taxed under SD or WY law. This strategy is appropriate primarily at the $1M+ Bitcoin threshold where the infrastructure cost is justified by the tax savings.

Annual Gifting Remains Core Federal Strategy

The federal annual gift tax exclusion — which allows you to transfer assets free of gift tax up to a certain amount per recipient per year — remains one of the most accessible and consistent estate planning tools for CT Bitcoin holders. By gifting Bitcoin (or interests in a Bitcoin-holding trust) to heirs annually, a holder can systematically reduce their taxable estate over time.

Annual gifting works in concert with trust structures and is particularly effective when combined with a Bitcoin-specific entity (like an LLC holding the Bitcoin) that allows for valuation discounts on minority interests transferred as gifts. Consult with qualified estate counsel before executing any gifting strategy involving discounts — the IRS scrutinizes these carefully.

Income Tax Planning Drives CT Bitcoin Dispositions

For Connecticut Bitcoin holders not yet at the federal estate tax threshold, the most significant tax planning issue is not estate planning at all — it's income tax management on dispositions during life. The 6.99% state capital gains rate means CT residents have a meaningful incentive to:

  • Hold Bitcoin to death to benefit from the step-up in basis (eliminating the CT capital gain entirely)
  • Consider installment-sale structures or charitable vehicles if a disposition is planned
  • Model domicile change to a no-income-tax state if the magnitude of planned dispositions justifies it (a genuine move, with proper domicile change documentation)
  • Utilize tax-loss harvesting and opportunity zone investments to offset capital gain in years of Bitcoin disposition

Finding a Connecticut Bitcoin Estate Attorney

Bitcoin estate planning at any meaningful scale requires a qualified estate planning attorney — not because the documents are unusual, but because the interaction between Bitcoin custody mechanics, trust structures, and the evolving legal landscape requires counsel who understands both estate law and digital assets.

When evaluating a Connecticut estate attorney for Bitcoin planning, look for:

  • Familiarity with Connecticut's Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (CT Gen. Stat. § 45a-334a et seq.) — which governs fiduciary access to digital accounts and assets
  • Experience drafting trust documents with explicit digital asset provisions, not just generic asset language
  • Comfort with custody concepts — seed phrases, hardware wallets, multisig — even if the attorney is not technically expert
  • Understanding of the tax interaction between estate plans and Bitcoin's cost basis rules

If your current estate attorney has never heard the words "seed phrase," that is not automatically disqualifying — but it does mean you will need to educate them, or find co-counsel who handles the digital asset layer while your existing attorney handles the broader estate structure.
