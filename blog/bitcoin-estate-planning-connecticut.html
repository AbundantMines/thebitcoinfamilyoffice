Bitcoin Estate Planning in Connecticut

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Connecticut Est. 9 min read

Connecticut presents a distinctive estate planning environment for Bitcoin families — one that is more complex than most states in two important respects. First, Connecticut imposes a state estate tax, though recent legislation has aligned the Connecticut exemption with the federal exemption (approximately $13.61 million per individual in 2024, indexed annually). Second, and uniquely among nearly all states, Connecticut imposes a state-level gift tax on lifetime transfers above the exemption threshold. Connecticut is one of only a handful of states in the country that taxes both transfers at death and transfers during life — a combination that demands careful coordination of lifetime gifting strategies and estate planning.

For Connecticut Bitcoin families with estates approaching or above the exemption threshold, the state gift tax creates a planning constraint that simply does not exist in most other states. Any lifetime transfer strategy — GRATs, SLATs, intra-family sales — must be analyzed for Connecticut gift tax consequences, not just federal ones. Bitcoin estate planning in Connecticut requires advisors who understand the interaction between the Connecticut estate and gift tax regime, the state's Uniform Trust Code, and the full spectrum of federal and state planning strategies.

The Connecticut Estate and Gift Tax: What Bitcoin Families Must Know

Connecticut's estate tax is codified at Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-391 et seq. Since January 1, 2023, the Connecticut estate tax exemption has been equal to the federal estate tax exemption (approximately $12.92 million in 2023, $13.61 million in 2024). This means the Connecticut estate tax only applies to very large estates — those exceeding the federal exemption — at a flat rate of 12% on amounts above the exemption. For most Bitcoin families, the Connecticut estate tax is no longer the primary state planning concern it was when the exemption was lower.

However, Connecticut's gift tax remains a live issue. The Connecticut gift tax (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 12-642 et seq.) applies to taxable gifts above the Connecticut exemption amount, which is also aligned with the federal gift tax exemption. The Connecticut gift tax rate is 12%, applied on the same unified lifetime basis as the estate tax. Practically, this means Connecticut gift tax is triggered only on gifts above the lifetime exemption — but the structure of the unified system means Connecticut gift tax analysis cannot be omitted from any large lifetime transfer planning.

Additionally, Connecticut imposes a cap on the combined estate and gift tax — currently $20 million — meaning the maximum Connecticut transfer tax any estate can pay is capped, regardless of estate size. For extremely large Bitcoin estates, this cap becomes a relevant planning ceiling.

Connecticut is one of the only states in the country with both an estate tax and a gift tax. Any lifetime transfer strategy for Connecticut Bitcoin families must be analyzed for state gift tax consequences — a step that is unnecessary in most other states.

Connecticut's Uniform Trust Code Framework

Connecticut adopted a version of the Uniform Trust Code, codified at Conn. Gen. Stat. § 45a-499 et seq. (effective 2020). The Connecticut UTC modernizes trust law and provides important tools for Bitcoin trust design:

  • Directed trusts: Connecticut's UTC permits trust instruments to separate investment direction from administrative trustee functions. For Bitcoin trusts, this allows a family member or Bitcoin custody specialist to control private key management and custody architecture decisions, while a corporate trustee manages distributions, accounting, and compliance — with neither role bearing liability for the other's actions when properly structured.
  • Trust protectors: The Connecticut UTC authorizes trust protectors with enumerated powers in the trust instrument, including the ability to modify trust terms, remove and replace trustees, and adapt the trust structure to changed legal or tax circumstances. For Bitcoin trusts where regulatory and technical landscapes evolve rapidly, a trust protector with broad amendment authority provides valuable long-term flexibility.
  • Decanting: Connecticut law provides statutory authority for trust decanting under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 45a-499pp, allowing trustees to transfer assets to a new trust with updated terms. Bitcoin trusts drafted without adequate digital asset succession provisions can be decanted into a modernized instrument.
  • Perpetual trusts: Connecticut permits trusts to continue for up to 800 years — effectively allowing multigenerational dynasty trusts for Bitcoin holdings. A Connecticut dynasty trust using the federal generation-skipping transfer (GST) exemption can hold Bitcoin for multiple generations without estate tax at each generational transfer.

Trust Strategies for Connecticut Bitcoin Families

Given Connecticut's unified estate and gift tax structure, trust strategies must be coordinated across both lifetime and death-time transfers:

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs): GRATs are among the most efficient strategies for transferring Bitcoin appreciation to heirs with minimal transfer tax. Importantly for Connecticut, GRATs are structured to produce zero taxable gifts — the actuarial value of the retained annuity equals the initial contribution, resulting in no gift on funding. Connecticut gift tax is therefore not triggered on GRAT funding. Appreciation above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate passes to heirs free of both Connecticut and federal estate tax. Rolling GRAT programs can systematically transfer Bitcoin appreciation over time.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs): A SLAT is funded using lifetime exemption — which, in Connecticut, means the transfer is subject to Connecticut gift tax analysis. For Connecticut families with remaining lifetime exemption, a SLAT transfer removes Bitcoin from the taxable estate at the cost of consuming exemption that would otherwise offset estate tax at death. The calculation requires careful modeling given Connecticut's $20 million tax cap and the unified exemption structure.

Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs) — Installment Sales: A sale of Bitcoin to an IDGT for a promissory note — rather than a gift — avoids gift tax on the principal transferred. The structure moves future appreciation out of the grantor's estate without triggering Connecticut gift tax on the sale proceeds, since an arms-length sale for fair market value is not a taxable gift. This is one of the most tax-efficient large-scale wealth transfer strategies for Connecticut Bitcoin families.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs): For Connecticut Bitcoin holders with appreciated low-basis positions, a CRT allows transfer of Bitcoin, deferral of capital gains recognition, and receipt of an annuity stream — while simultaneously generating a charitable deduction. The CRT remainder passes to charity, which is deductible for both federal and Connecticut estate tax purposes.

Digital Asset Succession in Connecticut

Connecticut enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) at Conn. Gen. Stat. § 45a-334a et seq. RUFADAA authorizes fiduciaries — executors, trustees, and attorneys-in-fact — to access digital assets, including Bitcoin, after death or incapacity. The priority hierarchy: online tool designations control first; governing instrument authority controls second; RUFADAA statutory defaults apply last.

Connecticut RUFADAA provides the legal authorization framework, not the technical solution. Connecticut Bitcoin estate plans must pair explicit RUFADAA authority in governing documents with a separate, secured technical succession protocol that specifies the Bitcoin custody architecture — hardware wallets, multi-signature arrangements, passphrase locations, and recovery procedures — stored and maintained separately from the estate plan documents themselves.

Connecticut LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Connecticut LLCs (governed by the Connecticut Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act, Conn. Gen. Stat. § 34-243 et seq.) provide effective holding vehicles for Bitcoin with valuation and asset protection benefits. Bitcoin held in a properly structured family LLC can qualify for minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts — typically 20–35% — reducing the taxable transfer value of LLC interests transferred to heirs or trusts. The operating agreement for a Connecticut Bitcoin LLC should specify custody protocols, transaction authorization thresholds, succession provisions, and restrictions on transfers of membership interests, creating formal governance around Bitcoin custody decisions.

Practical Priorities for Connecticut Bitcoin Families

  1. Understand the Connecticut unified estate and gift tax — any large lifetime transfer strategy must be modeled for Connecticut gift tax consequences, not just federal implications.
  2. Prioritize GRAT and installment sale strategies that minimize or eliminate Connecticut gift tax while still moving Bitcoin appreciation out of the taxable estate efficiently.
  3. Establish a Connecticut LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle, with an operating agreement governing custody, succession, and governance — and gift or sell LLC interests at discounted valuations to trusts or heirs.
  4. Consider a dynasty trust using Connecticut's 800-year perpetual trust authorization and the federal GST exemption for long-horizon Bitcoin positions intended to compound across generations.
  5. Implement RUFADAA authority explicitly in all governing instruments, and maintain a separate, secured technical succession protocol for Bitcoin custody access and private key management.
