Bitcoin Estate Planning for Surviving Spouses: Portability, QTIP Trusts & the Complete Post-Loss Guide

Your spouse held Bitcoin. Now they're gone. Here is every deadline, election, and decision you need to navigate — with compassion and precision.

If you are reading this, you have likely lost someone you love — and along with the grief, you have inherited a set of financial and legal decisions that feel impossibly complex. Bitcoin adds layers that most estate attorneys have never encountered: private keys, custodial accounts, volatile valuation, and tax rules that interact in ways few advisors fully understand.

Take a breath. You do not need to solve everything today. But you do need to understand the timeline, because some of these deadlines are unforgiving. This guide walks you through every critical decision the surviving spouse faces after a Bitcoin-holding spouse passes — from the portability election to QTIP trust administration, from inherited IRA distributions to rebuilding your own estate plan.

We are writing this in March 2026, with the estate and gift tax exemption at $15 million per person under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), the annual gift exclusion at $19,000, and Bitcoin trading near $74,000. The numbers in this guide reflect that reality.

The Portability Election: Capturing Your Spouse's Unused Exemption

Portability is the single most valuable election available to a surviving spouse, and missing it is one of the most expensive mistakes in estate planning. Here is what it means and why it matters — even if your spouse's estate was well below the exemption threshold.

What Is the DSUE Amount?

The Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion (DSUE) is the portion of your deceased spouse's federal estate and gift tax exemption that they did not use during their lifetime or at death. Under current law, the executor of your spouse's estate can elect to transfer that unused exemption to you by filing IRS Form 706 — the federal estate tax return — even if no estate tax is owed.

With the 2026 exemption at $15 million, if your spouse's taxable estate was $14.2 million, the DSUE amount would be $800,000. That $800,000 gets added to your own $15 million exemption, giving you a combined $15.8 million of transfer tax shelter. At a 40% estate tax rate, that is worth up to $320,000 in tax savings.

For Bitcoin holders, this matters enormously. Bitcoin appreciates. If you inherit 120 BTC worth $8.9 million today, and Bitcoin reaches $200,000 in a decade, that same stack is worth $24 million. Every dollar of additional exemption you capture now could shield hundreds of thousands from the estate tax later.

The 9-Month Deadline — and the 2-Year Safety Net

The portability election must generally be made on a timely filed Form 706, which is due nine months after the date of death, with a possible six-month extension. Many families — especially those dealing with grief, probate, and the complexity of Bitcoin custody — miss this deadline.

If you miss the nine-month window, Rev. Proc. 2022-32 provides a critical safety net. This IRS guidance allows the estate to file a late portability-only Form 706 within two years of the date of death without requesting a private letter ruling. Before this revenue procedure, obtaining late portability relief required expensive and uncertain PLR applications. Now, if the estate was not otherwise required to file a 706 (because the gross estate was below the filing threshold), you can simply file within two years and make the election.

But here is the catch for Bitcoin holders: the Form 706 filing threshold for 2026 deaths is $15 million. If your spouse's gross estate — including all Bitcoin, real estate, retirement accounts, life insurance, and other assets — exceeds $15 million, the estate was required to file a 706 regardless. In that case, the two-year relief under Rev. Proc. 2022-32 does not apply, and you would need to pursue a private letter ruling for late filing. Do not let this happen. File on time.

Valuing Bitcoin for the 706

The Form 706 requires that all assets be valued at their fair market value on the date of death — or, if elected, on the alternate valuation date (six months after death). For Bitcoin, this means establishing the price on the exact date of death using a recognized exchange or pricing index. Document the source thoroughly. The IRS has no official guidance on which exchange to use, but consistency and documentation are your best defenses.

If Bitcoin has declined significantly since the date of death, the alternate valuation date election can reduce the taxable estate and increase the DSUE amount — but it can only be used if it actually reduces both the gross estate value and the estate tax liability. Discuss this with your estate attorney and CPA before the six-month mark.

QTIP Trust Administration After the First Death

If your spouse's estate plan included a Qualified Terminable Interest Property (QTIP) trust, the dynamics of your financial life change significantly after their death. Understanding your rights and obligations as the income beneficiary of a QTIP trust is essential.

How a QTIP Trust Works After the First Spouse Dies

A QTIP trust qualifies for the unlimited marital deduction, meaning the assets placed in it are not taxed at the first spouse's death. In exchange, the trust must distribute all income to the surviving spouse at least annually, and no one other than the surviving spouse can receive distributions during their lifetime. The deceased spouse's estate plan controls who receives the trust assets after the surviving spouse dies — typically children or other family members.

For Bitcoin held in a QTIP trust, "income" creates an immediate question: Bitcoin does not generate traditional income. There are no dividends, no interest payments. Under the Uniform Principal and Income Act (and its successor, the Uniform Fiduciary Income and Principal Act), the trustee may need to adopt a unitrust conversion or exercise an equitable adjustment power to provide the surviving spouse with a reasonable income stream, typically 3-5% of trust value annually.

This is where Bitcoin-specific trust drafting matters. If the trust instrument does not address digital assets or provide the trustee with a power to adjust or convert to a unitrust, the surviving spouse may be entitled to "income" from an asset that produces none. This creates conflict, litigation risk, and potential fiduciary liability. If you find yourself in this situation, retain a trust administration attorney experienced with digital assets immediately.

The QTIP Election Itself

The executor makes the QTIP election on the Form 706 by checking a box on Schedule M. This election is irrevocable once made. The executor can make a partial QTIP election, placing some assets in the QTIP (qualifying for the marital deduction) and allowing the remainder to use the deceased spouse's exemption directly. This is a powerful planning tool — especially when Bitcoin values are volatile and the optimal split between marital and credit shelter trusts is uncertain at the time of filing.

Disclaimer Strategies: A Powerful Tool with a Tight Window

A qualified disclaimer under IRC § 2518 allows the surviving spouse to refuse an inheritance, causing the disclaimed assets to pass as if the surviving spouse predeceased the decedent. When the estate plan is properly drafted, disclaimed assets typically flow into a credit shelter (bypass) trust — which is not included in the surviving spouse's taxable estate at their subsequent death.

Why Disclaim Bitcoin?

Consider this scenario: your spouse left everything to you outright. With Bitcoin at $74,000, the 120 BTC stack is worth $8.9 million. Combined with your other assets, your estate might approach or exceed the $15 million exemption. By disclaiming a portion of the Bitcoin into a credit shelter trust, you remove those assets from your taxable estate while still potentially benefiting from them during your lifetime (if the trust allows discretionary distributions for your health, education, maintenance, and support).

The disclaimed Bitcoin also receives a stepped-up basis to date-of-death value, then grows inside the credit shelter trust outside your estate. If Bitcoin quadruples, that growth is entirely estate-tax-free.

The 9-Month Disclaimer Deadline

A qualified disclaimer must be made within nine months of the date of death (or, for a minor, within nine months of reaching age 21). The surviving spouse must not have accepted any benefit from the disclaimed property. For Bitcoin, this means you cannot have sold, transferred, staked, or otherwise exercised dominion over the specific BTC you intend to disclaim.

This deadline overlaps with the Form 706 due date, which is not a coincidence — the estate plan should be evaluated holistically at that point. But the disclaimer decision should be made before the 706 is filed, because the disclaimer changes the estate tax picture.

Step-Up in Basis: Community Property vs. Separate Property Bitcoin

The step-up in basis at death is one of the most valuable tax benefits in the Internal Revenue Code. When your spouse dies, assets included in their gross estate receive a new cost basis equal to their fair market value on the date of death. If your spouse bought Bitcoin at $500 and it is worth $74,000 at death, the new basis is $74,000. The $73,500 of unrealized gain vanishes.

The Community Property Advantage

In community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin), both halves of community property Bitcoin receive a full step-up in basis when one spouse dies — not just the decedent's half. This is IRC § 1014(b)(6), and it is extraordinarily powerful.

If a married couple in California acquired 120 BTC as community property at a basis of $5,000 per coin ($600,000 total), and one spouse dies when BTC is at $74,000, the entire 120 BTC receives a new basis of $8,880,000. If the surviving spouse then sells all 120 BTC at $74,000, the capital gains tax is zero.

Separate Property: Only the Decedent's Half Steps Up

In common law (separate property) states, only the decedent's share of jointly held Bitcoin receives the step-up. If the same couple held 120 BTC as joint tenants in New York, only the decedent's 60 BTC would step up. The surviving spouse's 60 BTC retains the original $5,000 basis. Selling the surviving spouse's half at $74,000 would trigger capital gains on $69,000 per coin — roughly $4.14 million of taxable gain.

This distinction alone can mean a difference of over $1 million in capital gains taxes. If you live in a common law state and hold significant Bitcoin jointly, consult with an estate planning attorney about strategies to maximize the step-up, including community property trusts recognized by some states and retitling strategies.

Inherited Bitcoin IRA: Required Minimum Distributions

If your deceased spouse held Bitcoin in a self-directed IRA, the rules for inherited IRAs apply — and the SECURE Act of 2019 (as modified by SECURE 2.0) has made these rules more complex than ever. As a surviving spouse, you have options that no other beneficiary enjoys.

Surviving Spouse Options

Unlike non-spouse beneficiaries who are generally subject to the 10-year distribution rule, a surviving spouse can:

  1. Roll the inherited IRA into their own IRA. This is often the best choice if you do not need the funds immediately. You treat the IRA as your own, delay RMDs until your own required beginning date (age 73 under current law), and can name new beneficiaries. The Bitcoin continues to grow tax-deferred.
  2. Remain as beneficiary of the inherited IRA. This can be advantageous if you are under 59½ and may need distributions before then (no 10% early withdrawal penalty on inherited IRA distributions).
  3. Elect the 10-year rule. Rarely beneficial for a surviving spouse, but available if the decedent died before their required beginning date.

For Bitcoin IRAs, the unique consideration is that distributions are in kind (the actual Bitcoin) or in cash after liquidation, and each distribution is taxed as ordinary income. If Bitcoin has appreciated substantially inside the IRA, large distributions can push you into higher tax brackets. Strategic distribution planning — spreading withdrawals across multiple years and coordinating with Roth conversions — is essential.

RMD Calculations for Bitcoin

Required minimum distributions are based on the account balance on December 31 of the prior year, divided by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table. For Bitcoin, this means the RMD amount fluctuates with Bitcoin's price. A year when Bitcoin surges to new highs could create an unexpectedly large RMD — and an unexpectedly large tax bill.

Work with your CPA to model different Bitcoin price scenarios and plan quarterly estimated tax payments accordingly. The penalty for under-withholding is avoidable with proper planning.

The Roth Conversion Opportunity

This is a silver lining that most grieving spouses miss — and that many advisors fail to mention in the critical first year after a spouse's death.

When one earner passes, the surviving spouse's income often drops significantly. If your deceased spouse was the higher earner, you may find yourself in a substantially lower federal income tax bracket in the year of death and subsequent years. This creates a window for Roth conversions at reduced tax rates.

Converting traditional IRA assets (including Bitcoin IRA assets) to a Roth IRA triggers ordinary income tax on the converted amount — but if you are in the 24% bracket instead of the 37% bracket, you are paying 13 cents less per dollar converted. For a $500,000 conversion, that is $65,000 in tax savings compared to converting at the higher bracket.

Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime, grow tax-free, and pass to beneficiaries with no income tax on distributions. For Bitcoin — an asset that many holders expect to appreciate dramatically — the combination of lower current income and long-term growth potential makes Roth conversion one of the most impactful post-death planning strategies available.

Note: you file a joint return in the year of your spouse's death, which preserves the married filing jointly brackets and standard deduction for that year. Starting the following year, you file as single (or head of household if you have dependents), which compresses the tax brackets. Plan your Roth conversions across the year of death and subsequent years accordingly.

The Widow's Penalty: Tax Bracket Compression

Estate planning professionals call it the "widow's penalty" — and it catches surviving spouses off guard every year. When you shift from married filing jointly to single filing status, the tax brackets compress dramatically:

  • The 37% bracket begins at approximately $626,350 for single filers vs. $751,600 for married filing jointly in 2026.
  • The standard deduction drops from approximately $32,300 (MFJ) to $16,150 (single).
  • Income thresholds for the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, capital gains brackets, and Medicare surcharges all shift downward.

For a surviving spouse who inherits significant Bitcoin — and especially one who must take RMDs from an inherited traditional IRA — this bracket compression can increase the effective tax rate by 10-15 percentage points on the same income. On a $400,000 income, that can mean $40,000-$60,000 more in annual federal taxes.

How to Address the Widow's Penalty

Strategic use of trusts, timed Roth conversions, charitable giving (including charitable remainder trusts funded with appreciated Bitcoin), and careful distribution planning from inherited accounts can smooth income across years and minimize the bracket compression effect. This is not a one-time calculation — it requires annual tax projection and adjustment for the rest of your life.

Updating Everything: Beneficiary Designations, Titles & Your Own Estate Plan

After your spouse dies, your own estate plan needs a complete overhaul. The plan you created together was designed for a two-person household with shared goals. You are now operating solo, and the legal structures must reflect that.

Beneficiary Designations

Review and update every beneficiary designation — IRAs, 401(k)s, life insurance policies, Bitcoin custodial accounts, and any transfer-on-death registrations. Your deceased spouse may have been listed as the primary beneficiary on your accounts. If those designations are not updated and you die unexpectedly, assets could pass to your spouse's estate, triggering probate and potentially unintended distributions. This is one of the most commonly overlooked tasks. Do it within the first 30 days. See our guide on when to update your Bitcoin estate plan for a complete checklist.

Reregistering Bitcoin in Your Name

Bitcoin held in custodial accounts (exchanges, qualified custodians, Bitcoin IRA platforms) must be retitled to the surviving spouse or to the applicable trust. This typically requires a death certificate, letters testamentary or letters of administration, and the custodian's specific transfer forms.

Bitcoin held in self-custody (hardware wallets, multisig arrangements) presents different challenges. If your spouse was the sole keyholder, you need access to seed phrases, PINs, and any multisig co-signers. This is where proper Bitcoin estate planning pays dividends — if your spouse documented custody access in a letter of intent or with a trusted third party, recovery should be straightforward. If not, you may need professional recovery assistance. Do not attempt to brute-force access or use unvetted third-party recovery services. The risk of permanent loss or theft is real.

Your Own Estate Plan

With your spouse gone, several documents need updating:

  • Revocable trust: Your joint revocable trust likely converted to an irrevocable trust (the survivor's trust and the decedent's trust/bypass trust) at your spouse's death. You need to understand which assets are in which sub-trust, what your powers are over each, and whether the trust terms still align with your goals.
  • Pour-over will: Update to reflect the removal of your spouse as executor and beneficiary.
  • Power of attorney and healthcare directive: Your spouse was likely your primary agent. Name new agents immediately.
  • Digital asset access: If your spouse managed the Bitcoin custody infrastructure, you need to document your own access plan and designate a new digital asset executor or advisor.
  • Successor trustee: With your spouse gone, who serves as trustee if you become incapacitated? This decision is urgent — not theoretical.

Remarriage Considerations: Protecting Children from a Prior Marriage

This is a conversation no one wants to have while grieving, but it is one your estate plan must address — because life continues, and people do remarry.

If your deceased spouse's estate plan included a QTIP trust, one of the primary reasons was likely to protect children from the current marriage. The QTIP ensures that after you (the surviving spouse) pass away, the remaining trust assets go to the deceased spouse's designated beneficiaries — typically the children — regardless of whether you remarry.

Without a QTIP, if you inherit everything outright, remarry, and commingle assets with your new spouse, the children from your first marriage may receive nothing. In a blended family situation, the QTIP trust is the primary guardrail that ensures the deceased spouse's wishes are honored.

Practical Remarriage Planning

If you do remarry, consider a prenuptial agreement that clearly identifies the Bitcoin and other inherited assets as separate property. In community property states, income generated by separate property during marriage can become community property — creating a commingling risk with appreciated Bitcoin held outside the QTIP trust.

Also be aware that remarriage can affect the portability election. If you remarry and your new spouse predeceases you, the DSUE amount from your first spouse is replaced by the DSUE amount from your second spouse (which may be zero if they used their full exemption). Portability is a "last deceased spouse" rule — it does not stack.

Surviving Spouse as Trustee: Conflicts and Co-Trustee Solutions

Many estate plans name the surviving spouse as trustee of the decedent's trusts — including the bypass/credit shelter trust and sometimes even the QTIP trust. This creates inherent conflicts of interest.

The Conflict

As income beneficiary of the QTIP trust and discretionary beneficiary of the bypass trust, you have a personal interest in distributions. As trustee, you have a fiduciary duty to balance your interests against those of the remainder beneficiaries (usually your children). These duties are legally in tension, and breach-of-fiduciary-duty claims by remainder beneficiaries are more common than most families expect.

For Bitcoin trusts, the conflict intensifies around investment decisions. Should the trustee sell Bitcoin to diversify? Hold for further appreciation? The surviving spouse may prefer to hold (expecting growth that benefits them during their lifetime), while remainder beneficiaries may prefer a more diversified portfolio that protects the value they will eventually receive. Every investment decision carries potential fiduciary liability.

The Co-Trustee Recommendation

For trusts holding significant Bitcoin, we strongly recommend a co-trustee arrangement — typically the surviving spouse and an independent trustee (a corporate trustee, trust company, or trusted professional advisor). The independent co-trustee provides:

  • Protection against claims of self-dealing or breach of fiduciary duty
  • Professional investment oversight and documentation
  • A buffer between the surviving spouse and remainder beneficiaries
  • Digital asset custody expertise (if using a trust company with Bitcoin capabilities)

If the trust document names you as sole trustee, you may have the power to appoint a co-trustee or you may petition the court for appointment of one. Review the trust terms with your attorney. The cost of a co-trustee is far less than the cost of fiduciary litigation.

Case Study: Margaret Chen — 120 BTC, Widowed at 67

Margaret Chen is 67 years old. Her husband David passed unexpectedly on January 15, 2026. He held 120 BTC — worth approximately $8.9 million at $74,000 per coin — in a combination of self-custody (hardware wallet, 80 BTC) and a self-directed Bitcoin IRA (40 BTC). Their other assets include a home worth $1.2 million, traditional investment accounts of $2.1 million, and David's $500,000 life insurance policy payable to Margaret. They live in Oregon, a separate property state.

David and Margaret had a joint revocable trust. David's total gross estate: approximately $14.2 million (including his half of jointly held assets, the full Bitcoin IRA, the self-custodied BTC titled in his name, and the life insurance). With the 2026 exemption at $15 million, his estate is not required to file a Form 706 — but Margaret's attorney recommends filing one anyway to elect portability.

The DSUE Calculation

David's taxable estate, after the marital deduction (for assets passing to Margaret or the QTIP trust), is approximately $14.2 million. His unused exemption — the DSUE — is approximately $800,000 ($15M exemption minus $14.2M taxable estate). By filing the 706, Margaret captures that $800,000 and adds it to her own $15 million exemption, giving her $15.8 million of shelter.

Days 1-30: Immediate Actions

  • Secure Bitcoin custody. Margaret works with David's designated digital asset advisor to access the hardware wallet using the seed phrase documented in David's letter of intent (stored with their estate attorney). The 80 BTC in self-custody is transferred to a new multisig arrangement with Margaret, her eldest son, and a Bitcoin-savvy trust company as co-signers.
  • Notify the Bitcoin IRA custodian. File death certificate and begin the inherited IRA transfer process. Margaret elects to roll 40 BTC from David's IRA into her own IRA.
  • Update all beneficiary designations. Margaret removes David as beneficiary on her own accounts and names her children as primary beneficiaries with proper per stirpes designations.
  • File life insurance claim. The $500,000 policy is income-tax-free and provides liquidity for administrative costs and living expenses during the estate settlement period.
  • Engage the estate administration team. Estate attorney, CPA, financial advisor, and digital asset specialist. Margaret does not try to navigate this alone.

Days 30-60: Strategic Analysis

  • Trust administration review. The joint revocable trust has split into the survivor's trust (Margaret's assets, revocable) and the decedent's trust (David's share, now irrevocable). The estate attorney maps every asset to the correct sub-trust.
  • Disclaimer analysis. Margaret and her advisors evaluate whether she should disclaim a portion of the outright inheritance into the credit shelter trust. Given that her combined estate (including the DSUE) is comfortably below $15.8 million, they determine that a disclaimer is not necessary at current Bitcoin prices — but they document the analysis in case Bitcoin appreciates dramatically before the nine-month deadline.
  • Roth conversion modeling. David earned $350,000 annually. Margaret's income from her part-time consulting is $60,000. Her CPA models Roth conversions of $200,000-$400,000 per year for the next several years, taking advantage of the lower brackets. They begin with a $300,000 conversion in 2026 (the year of death, filing jointly).

Days 60-90: Estate Tax Return Preparation

  • Bitcoin valuation. The CPA documents Bitcoin's price on January 15, 2026, using the CoinDesk Bitcoin Price Index as the primary reference, with Coinbase and Kraken spot prices as corroboration. They also evaluate the alternate valuation date (July 15, 2026) to determine whether it would reduce the gross estate.
  • QTIP election analysis. The attorney and CPA determine the optimal split between the marital (QTIP) and bypass portions of the decedent's trust, balancing estate tax efficiency with Margaret's income needs and the children's remainder interests.
  • Healthcare directive and power of attorney. Margaret executes new documents naming her daughter as primary agent and her son as backup.

Days 90-180: Filing and Forward Planning

  • File Form 706. The estate attorney files the return with the portability election, capturing the $800,000 DSUE. The QTIP election is made for the marital trust portion.
  • Margaret's new estate plan. With $15.8 million of combined exemption and approximately $14 million of assets (including appreciated Bitcoin), Margaret works with her attorney to create an updated revocable trust that includes: a credit shelter trust funded at the second death (using the DSUE), specific Bitcoin custody provisions, successor trustee designations, and charitable giving provisions that address the widow's penalty through income smoothing.
  • Ongoing Roth conversion strategy. Margaret's advisor creates a 5-year Roth conversion schedule, converting approximately $300,000 per year from the rolled-over traditional IRA, targeting the top of the 24% bracket each year.
  • RMD planning. Margaret will begin RMDs from the inherited IRA (if not fully rolled over) and from her own IRA at age 73. Her CPA projects future RMDs under different Bitcoin price scenarios and coordinates with the Roth conversion strategy.

Margaret's outcome: she preserved $800,000 of DSUE ($320,000 of potential estate tax savings), secured all 120 BTC in a professionally managed custody arrangement, began a Roth conversion strategy that will save approximately $200,000 in income taxes over five years, updated her estate plan to protect her children, and established a co-trustee arrangement for the irrevocable decedent's trust that protects her from fiduciary liability.

She did all of this within six months, despite grief, despite complexity, because she had the right team and understood the deadlines.

Moving Forward

Losing a spouse is devastating. Losing a spouse who held Bitcoin adds layers of urgency and complexity that few families are prepared for. But the tools exist — portability elections, QTIP trusts, disclaimer strategies, Roth conversions, co-trustee arrangements — to protect both the surviving spouse and the next generation.

The most important step is the first one: assembling a team that understands both estate law and Bitcoin. An attorney who has never dealt with digital asset custody will miss critical issues. A CPA who does not understand basis step-up on community property will leave money on the table. A financial advisor who is not fluent in inherited IRA rules for surviving spouses will give you suboptimal guidance.

You do not need to become an expert in all of this. You need to understand the deadlines, ask the right questions, and surround yourself with people who have done this before. This guide is your roadmap. Your advisory team is your vehicle. And the destination — financial security and peace of mind — is absolutely reachable.
