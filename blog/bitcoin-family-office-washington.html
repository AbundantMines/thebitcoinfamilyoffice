Washington State occupies a peculiar position in the Bitcoin wealth planning landscape. On the surface, the Evergreen State looks attractive: no state income tax, a thriving tech economy anchored by Amazon, Microsoft, and a constellation of well-funded startups, and community property laws that allow married couples to benefit from a full double step-up in basis on appreciated assets at death. For Bitcoin holders who accumulated their stacks while building careers at Redmond or Seattle's South Lake Union campus, this sounds like a reasonable starting point.

But scratch beneath the surface and Washington reveals a planning hazard that has blindsided more than a few affluent tech families: a state estate tax that kicks in at just $2.193 million — one of the lowest exemption thresholds in the United States. At a Bitcoin price of $100,000 per coin, a holder with just 22 BTC could find their estate exposed to Washington's progressive estate tax. At $200,000 per coin — a price level many analysts have forecast for 2025–2026 — just 11 BTC triggers Washington exposure.

This guide is for Washington State Bitcoin families: Amazon and Microsoft employees with meaningful Bitcoin positions, tech founders who converted equity into BTC, and long-term holders who have built their lives in the Pacific Northwest and want to protect what they've built without necessarily uprooting their families. We cover the estate tax danger in granular detail, walk through tech-worker specific planning considerations, explain how out-of-state trust strategies can shelter appreciation, and provide a practical setup guide you can bring to your attorney.

Section 1: Washington's Estate Tax — The Hidden Danger for Bitcoin Families

Most wealthy Washingtonians are aware that the state has no income tax. Far fewer fully grasp the implications of Washington's estate tax — a tax that is, by many measures, the most aggressive in the western United States.

The Exemption That Isn't What You Think

Washington's estate tax exemption is indexed for inflation but remains far below the federal threshold. In 2026, the Washington exemption sits at approximately $2.193 million per individual. To put this in perspective: the federal estate tax exemption for 2026 (adjusted under TCJA rules before potential sunsetting) is $13.61 million per individual. The gap is staggering.

What this means in practice: a Washington resident with an estate valued at $3 million — well below the federal radar — owes Washington estate tax. A Bitcoin family with $2.5 million in BTC and a paid-off home in Bellevue could easily breach the threshold. This isn't theoretical. As Bitcoin has appreciated, tens of thousands of Washington tech workers have entered this danger zone without any planning in place.

The Progressive Rate Structure

Washington's estate tax is progressive, ranging from 10% on the first taxable dollar to 20% on estates above $9 million. This is a statewide tax on the taxable estate — the amount above the exemption. The rate schedule breaks down roughly as follows:

  • First $1M above exemption: approximately 10%
  • $1M–$2M above exemption: approximately 14%
  • $2M–$3M above exemption: approximately 15–16%
  • $9M+ above exemption: 20% (top rate)

For a Washington Bitcoin holder with an estate worth $5 million, the liabBitcoin Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusty could exceed $500,000 in state estate taxes alone — before federal estate taxes come into play for those above the federal threshold.

No Portability: A Critical Distinction

At the federal level, the estate tax exemption is "portable" — if a spouse dies without using their full exemption, the surviving spouse can "port" the unused exemption to their own estate. Washington provides no such portability. Each spouse has one exemption of $2.193M and it is use-it-or-lose-it. A married couple that fails to plan — relying on the simple "everything passes to the surviving spouse" approach — can lose one $2.193M exemption entirely.

Planning Implication: A Washington married couple with a $5M Bitcoin estate that passes everything outright to the surviving spouse wastes the deceased spouse's $2.193M exemption. The surviving spouse now has a $5M estate with only a $2.193M exemption — exposing roughly $2.8M to estate tax. The solution is a Credit Shelter Trust (also called a Bypass Trust) that captures the deceased spouse's exemption and keeps those assets outside the surviving spouse's taxable estate.

Federal vs. Washington Estate Tax: Two Separate Systems

Washington and federal estate taxes are completely independent calculations. An estate may owe Washington estate tax with no federal liability whatsoever. The Washington estate tax return (Form REV 85 0050) is filed separately, with a nine-month deadline from the date of death. Washington's tax does not provide a deduction for itself when calculating federal tax. The systems are parallel, not linked.

Married Couple Strategies Under Washington Law

Given the no-portability rule, married Washington couples with meaningful Bitcoin holdings should consider two foundational structures:

Credit Shelter Trust: At the first spouse's death, assets up to the exemption amount ($2.193M) pour into an irrevocable Credit Shelter Trust. These assets are excluded from the surviving spouse's estate. The trust can benefit the surviving spouse and/or descendants while remaining outside the taxable estate.

QTIP Trust: A Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust allows the estate to take a marital deduction for assets passing to the surviving spouse while still controlling the ultimate disposition. At the surviving spouse's death, the QTIP assets are included in their estate but taxed at that point, with the benefit that the trust terms can protect the assets from a second spouse or other unintended beneficiaries.

Section 2: Washington Amazon/Microsoft Employee Bitcoin Planning

Washington State is home to Amazon's global headquarters and Microsoft's campus in Redmond, plus major operations for Google, Meta, T-Mobile, Costco, and Boeing. This concentration of tech wealth has created a unique planning challenge: employees who hold unvested RSUs, exercisable stock options, and accumulated Bitcoin positions simultaneously — three separate wealth layers that interact in complex ways with Washington's estate tax.

The Amazon/Microsoft Equity-to-Bitcoin Conversion Path

A common pattern among Pacific Northwest tech workers: they receive Amazon RSUs or Microsoft ESPP shares over a multi-year vesting schedule, sell a portion each year (paying income tax), and convert a portion of the proceeds into Bitcoin. After five to ten years at a major tech firm, the Bitcoin position can dwarf the remaining RSU balance. This creates a concentrated Bitcoin position layered on top of unvested equity — a planning situation most estate attorneys have rarely encountered.

The key planning considerations for tech workers in this position:

  • Unvested RSUs: These are not part of the taxable estate at death if they lapse or are subject to forfeiture. But if unvested RSUs are "substantial risk of forfeiture" under IRC Section 83, they have no estate tax value. Vested but unpaid RSUs do carry estate tax exposure.
  • NQSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options): Options with a spread — the difference between exercise price and fair market value — are included in the estate at death. The estate then receives an income tax deduction (IRD deduction) for the taxes the beneficiary will pay when they exercise. This requires careful coordination between estate and income tax counsel.
  • Bitcoin Holdings: Unlike unvested equity, Bitcoin has no forfeiture risk and is fully includable in the estate at fair market value on the date of death.

The Timing Challenge

For a Microsoft principal engineer in year three of a four-year vest cycle with $800K in unvested RSUs, $500K in Bitcoin, and a $900K Kirkland home, the total estate is already approaching or exceeding Washington's $2.193M exemption. By the time that vest cycle completes — without any Bitcoin appreciation — the estate could be $2.5–3M+. Any Bitcoin appreciation between now and death is a direct tax liability.

This is exactly the scenario where proactive planning creates outsized value. The strategy: move Bitcoin appreciation out of the Washington taxable estate now, before it happens, using irrevocable trust structures funded at today's values.

When to Move Domicile from Washington

For some high-net-worth tech workers — particularly those with $5M+ estates and significant flexibility in where they live — relocating from Washington to Bitcoin family office in Nevada, Wyoming, or Texas may be the highest-value planning decision available. A $500K state estate tax bill at death justifies significant lifestyle adjustments.

What domicile change requires in Washington: Washington is a community property state and takes domicile seriously. To establish domicile elsewhere, you need to: (1) register to vote in the new state, (2) obtain a driver's license in the new state, (3) file tax returns from the new domicile, (4) spend a majority of time in the new state, and (5) update your estate planning documents. Working remotely for Amazon or Microsoft while living in Nevada is increasingly common and legally straightforward from a tax perspective.

Section 3: Out-of-State Trust Strategy for WA Residents

A Washington resident does not need to move out of state to benefit from out-of-state trust laws. The strategy of establishing a dynasty trust in Wyoming or South Dakota — with a professional trustee in that state — is fully legal and well-established under both state and federal law.

Wyoming Dynasty Trust for Washington Residents

Wyoming's dynasty trust statute (Wyo. Stat. § 4-10-510) allows perpetual trusts with no rule against perpetuities. A Washington resident who transfers Bitcoin to a properly structured Wyoming dynasty trust removes those assets — and all future appreciation — from their Washington taxable estate.

The mechanics: the Washington resident (as grantor) creates an irrevocable trust governed by Wyoming law, with a Wyoming-domiciled corporate trustee. The grantor can name themselves as a beneficiary during their lifetime (though doing so has asset protection implications), or exclude themselves and name only family members. Once the transfer is complete and the gift tax return is filed, the assets inside the trust are outside the Washington estate.

Key Point: Washington courts generally respect the choice of law provisions in out-of-state trusts, provided the trust has a genuine nexus with the chosen state (e.g., a Wyoming trustee with meaningful Wyoming contact). The Washington Uniform Trust Code (RCW 11.96A) does not override a valid Wyoming trust choice of law for trusts with proper Wyoming connections.

Washington UTC and Directed Trusts

Washington adopted the Uniform Trust Code in 2011 (RCW 11.96A). Washington's UTC allows directed trusts — trusts where investment decisions can be delegated to a separate "trust protector" or "investment advisor" — but Washington's directed trust statute is less flexible than Wyoming's. In Washington, the liability shield for the trustee following an investment advisor's direction is somewhat limited compared to Wyoming's more comprehensive protection.

For Bitcoin families who want maximum flexibility in managing their trust's Bitcoin holdings — including the ability to use self-custody, hardware wallets, or multisig arrangements — Wyoming's directed trust statute (Wyo. Stat. § 4-10-710) provides a clearer framework for separating the custodial trustee from the investment decision-maker.

Funding the Out-of-State Trust

Transferring Bitcoin to a Wyoming dynasty trust from Washington involves a taxable gift. If the transfer exceeds the annual exclusion ($18,000 per beneficiary in 2026), a federal gift tax return (Form 709) must be filed. The transfer uses federal lifetime exemption but creates no Washington gift tax (Washington has no gift tax). The key benefit: you're "locking in" the current Bitcoin value for gift tax purposes, and all future appreciation escapes the taxable estate entirely.

Section 4: Washington-Specific Planning Strategies

Community Property Double Step-Up

One of Washington's hidden benefits is its community property treatment of appreciated assets. In Washington, all assets acquired during marriage are presumed community property, including Bitcoin purchased with marital earnings. At the death of the first spouse, both halves of community property receive a stepped-up basis — not just the decedent's half.

This means a Washington married couple who bought 10 BTC at $5,000 each ($50,000 total basis) and holds them as community property at the time of the first spouse's death with BTC at $150,000 per coin ($1.5M total value) gets a step-up to $1.5M basis for the entire 10 BTC. The surviving spouse could sell at that price with zero capital gains tax. Compare this to a non-community-property state where only half the BTC gets a step-up.

This advantage, however, must be weighed against the Washington estate tax exposure. The $1.5M Bitcoin position in our example would contribute toward Washington's $2.193M exemption — and if the total estate exceeds that threshold, Washington estate tax applies regardless of the step-up benefit.

Annual Gifting Strategy

The federal annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per recipient, per year in 2026; $36,000 for married couples splitting gifts) allows Bitcoin families to transfer wealth outside the estate tax system without touching the lifetime exemption. A Washington Bitcoin family with three adult children can transfer $108,000 in Bitcoin per year ($36,000 × 3) completely tax-free, reducing the taxable estate incrementally.

Irrevocable Trust Structures

Beyond dynasty trusts, Washington Bitcoin families can consider Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs), Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusts (ILITs), and Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs). GRATs are particularly attractive in rising-asset environments: the grantor transfers Bitcoin to the GRAT, retains an annuity stream, and at the end of the GRAT term (often 2–5 years), the remaining appreciation passes to heirs with no additional gift tax — provided the Bitcoin appreciates faster than the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate.

Washington No-Contest Clauses

Washington recognizes in terrorem (no-contest) clauses in wills and trusts under RCW 11.12.930. These clauses discourage estate challenges by providing that a beneficiary who contests the estate plan forfeits their share. For Bitcoin families with complex structures and multiple heirs, properly drafted no-contest provisions add an important layer of protection against costly litigation.

Section 5: Practical Setup Guide for WA Bitcoin Families

Step 1: Washington LLC for Bitcoin Holdings

A Washington limited liability company (LLC) provides a first layer of organization and liability separation for Bitcoin holdings. Washington LLC formation is straightforward: file Articles of Organization with the Secretary of State ($180 filing fee), create a detailed Operating Agreement specifying Bitcoin custody procedures, and maintain separate records.

Important: a Washington LLC does not provide the same charging order protection as a Wyoming LLC. Washington's charging order statute (RCW 25.15.261) allows a court to enter a charging order against the LLC interest, but the exclusivity of this remedy as a creditor's sole recourse is less clear under Washington law than under Wyoming law. For maximum asset protection, a Wyoming LLC may be preferable even for Washington residents.

Step 2: Out-of-State Dynasty Trust

The centerpiece of a Washington Bitcoin family's estate plan is typically an irrevocable dynasty trust sited in Wyoming or South Dakota. Work with a Wyoming-licensed trust attorney (or a national firm with Wyoming trust expertise) to draft the trust instrument. The trust should include:

  • Perpetual duration (no rule against perpetuities)
  • Directed trust provisions separating investment and distribution functions
  • Bitcoin-specific custody provisions (multisig, hardware wallet protocols)
  • Trust protector with amendment powers (for law changes)
  • Decanting authority (to pour trust into new trust if law changes)

Step 3: Credit Shelter Trust in Washington Will

Washington residents with estates that could exceed the $2.193M exemption should include a Credit Shelter Trust provision in their will. At the first spouse's death, assets up to the exemption fund the Credit Shelter Trust, sheltering them from the surviving spouse's estate. This requires careful drafting — the trust must be irrevocable at the first death to achieve the estate tax exclusion.

Step 4: Annual Gifting Program

Establish a systematic annual gifting program targeting the federal annual exclusion. Gifts of Bitcoin should be made in kind (transferring fractional BTC) rather than as cash after selling, to avoid triggering capital gains at the grantor level. Document all gifts properly with contemporaneous records.

Step 5: Custody Architecture

For Washington Bitcoin families, custody architecture should account for: (1) Pacific Northwest weather risks (flooding in low-lying areas, wildfire risk east of the Cascades), (2) geographic distribution of hardware wallets and seed backups across multiple locations, and (3) a third backup location outside Washington (e.g., with a family member in a different state) to protect against regional catastrophes.

WA vs. Oregon

Both Washington and Oregon have estate taxes, but Oregon's is arguably more punishing: a $1 million exemption (even lower than Washington's), with rates from 10% to 16%. Oregon also has income tax of up to 9.9%. Washington is substantially better than Oregon for Bitcoin families, but still far from ideal. Oregon residents with significant Bitcoin holdings who have flexibility on location should seriously consider moving to Washington (for the no-income-tax benefit) or farther afield to Nevada or Wyoming.

WA vs. Nevada

Nevada offers no income tax and no estate tax — a clear advantage over Washington. Nevada also has a mature Domestic Asset Protection Trust statute and one of the strongest LLC charging order protections in the country. For Washington Bitcoin families who don't want to move to Wyoming, Nevada is a compelling alternative domicile. The lifestyle transition from Seattle to Las Vegas or Reno is significant but increasingly common among tech workers seeking tax optimization.

WA vs. Wyoming

Wyoming is the gold standard for Bitcoin family office planning: no income tax, no estate tax, best-in-class dynasty trust law, DAPT statute, and a Bitcoin-friendly legal framework explicitly codified in state law. The Wyoming Legislature has consistently passed Bitcoin-friendly legislation, and the state has a growing community of Bitcoin-specialized trust attorneys and corporate trustees. Moving from Washington to Wyoming is a major lifestyle change — but for a Bitcoin family with a $10M+ estate, the estate tax savings can exceed $1 million per generation.

Section 7: Frequently Asked Questions

My estate is only $1.5 million now. Do I need to worry about Washington's estate tax?
Not today, but planning should start now if you hold Bitcoin. At $150,000 per BTC, just 10 coins pushes a single person to the $1.5M mark. A price of $200K/BTC with 11 BTC crosses the $2.193M threshold. Estate plans take time to implement, and some strategies (like GRATs or dynasty trusts) work best when started early. Don't wait until you're over the exemption to begin planning.
Can I have a Wyoming trust if I live in Washington?
Yes. Washington residents can establish irrevocable trusts governed by Wyoming law with a Wyoming professional trustee. This is a common and well-established estate planning strategy. The key requirements are: (1) the trust must be genuinely governed by Wyoming law, (2) there must be a Wyoming trustee with meaningful Wyoming nexus, and (3) the trust must be properly funded with a documented gift.
Does Washington have a gift tax that could catch me if I transfer Bitcoin to a trust?
No. Washington has no gift tax. Gifts from Washington residents are subject only to federal gift tax rules. Transfers within the federal annual exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2026) are tax-free. Transfers above that amount use federal lifetime exemption (approximately $13.61M in 2026) and require a Form 709 filing but no immediate tax payment for most families.
I'm an Amazon employee with unvested RSUs and Bitcoin. Should I prioritize protecting Bitcoin or the RSUs in my estate plan?
Unvested RSUs that are subject to a "substantial risk of forfeiture" are generally not included in your taxable estate if you die before vesting. Bitcoin, by contrast, has no forfeiture condition — it's always includable at full FMV. Bitcoin appreciation also doesn't cap out (unlike a stock price that may be more predictable). Focus your irrevocable trust strategies on Bitcoin first, where the unlimited appreciation potential creates the greatest estate tax risk.
What is a Credit Shelter Trust and does my Washington will need one?
A Credit Shelter Trust (also called a Bypass Trust or Family Trust) is an irrevocable trust that captures the deceased spouse's Washington estate tax exemption. Without it, a Washington married couple that leaves everything outright to the surviving spouse wastes the first spouse's exemption. If your combined estate is above $2.193M, your Washington estate plan almost certainly should include a Credit Shelter Trust provision.
How long does it take to change domicile from Washington to another state?
There's no fixed waiting period, but Washington (like most states) looks for clear, unambiguous actions establishing the new domicile: new driver's license, new voter registration, updating estate planning documents, filing taxes from the new address, and spending the majority of time in the new state. Most tax attorneys recommend completing these steps and documenting them thoroughly to withstand scrutiny in the event of a challenge by the Washington Department of Revenue.
If my Bitcoin goes to my spouse at death, does it avoid Washington estate tax?
Yes — the unlimited marital deduction eliminates Washington estate tax on assets passing outright to a surviving U.S.-citizen spouse. But this only defers the tax; it doesn't eliminate it. At the surviving spouse's death, the entire combined estate faces Washington estate tax with only one $2.193M exemption (not portable). A Credit Shelter Trust at the first death is usually the better strategy for estates above the exemption.
