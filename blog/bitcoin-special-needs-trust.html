Bitcoin and Special Needs Trusts: Protecting Disabled Beneficiaries Without Losing Government Benefits

By Hal Turner, Bitcoin Estate Planning Analyst — February 26, 2026 — 18 min read

You have spent years accumulating Bitcoin. You have also spent years caring for a child — or an adult family member — with a disability. You want your Bitcoin to provide for them long after you are gone. And you are terrified of getting this wrong.

Your fear is justified. Leaving Bitcoin directly to a disabled beneficiary who receives Supplemental Security Income (SSI) or Medicaid could be one of the most expensive estate planning mistakes you ever make. Not because of taxes. Because of government benefits eligibility rules that most estate planning attorneys barely understand — and that virtually no Bitcoin-focused advisor has written about.

This article closes that gap. We cover exactly how Special Needs Trusts work, why Bitcoin introduces unique complications, how to properly fund and structure an SNT for a Bitcoin holder's estate, and which complementary tools — including ABLE accounts — can work alongside the trust to maximize your beneficiary's quality of life.

The core danger in one sentence: If a disabled person who receives SSI or Medicaid inherits Bitcoin — or any other asset — directly, the value of that inheritance counts as a "countable resource," which can immediately disqualify them from their benefits until the asset is spent down to the applicable limit (typically $2,000 for an individual).

Why Government Benefits Are So Fragile

To understand the planning problem, you first have to understand the programs at stake. SSI and Medicaid are means-tested — eligibility depends on having resources below strict thresholds. These programs are fundamentally different from Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI), which is based on work history and is not means-tested.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

SSI is the federal program that provides monthly cash payments — currently up to $943 per month for an individual in 2026 — to people who are aged, blind, or disabled and who have limited income and resources. The resource limit is $2,000 for an individual and $3,000 for a couple. These limits have not meaningfully increased in decades despite inflation. Bitcoin at even the most modest valuation can blow through these limits in an instant.

Any countable resource above the limit triggers disqualification. The beneficiary has a narrow window to "spend down" the excess to regain eligibility, but during the period of disqualification, they lose both their cash payment and — more importantly — their Medicaid linkage.

Medicaid

Medicaid provides health coverage and, critically, long-term care services (group homes, supported living, day programs, in-home support) that can cost tens of thousands of dollars per year — services that private pay would exhaust most estates. Medicaid eligibility is also means-tested. Many states tie SSI eligibility directly to Medicaid: lose SSI, lose Medicaid automatically. For a disabled person with significant support needs, losing Medicaid can be catastrophic.

The lifetime cost of care for someone with a significant intellectual or developmental disability often exceeds $2–3 million. Medicaid coverage of that care is the single most valuable asset most disabled individuals will ever have access to. An inheritance that disqualifies them from Medicaid can be worth far less than it appears on paper.

What Counts as a "Countable Resource"?

Under SSI rules, a countable resource is any asset the individual owns and can convert to cash to use for food or shelter. Bitcoin is unambiguously a countable resource. It has market value, it is liquid, and it can be sold. If your disabled heir inherits even a fraction of a Bitcoin — worth, at time of writing, tens of thousands of dollars — they will be over the resource limit the moment the asset is legally theirs.

The SSA has issued guidance confirming that virtual currency is treated as a financial instrument and counted as a resource based on its fair market value. There is no exception for cryptocurrency volatility, no averaging period, and no grace window. The moment of transfer is the moment of disqualification risk.

Countable vs. Excluded Resources Under SSI

  • Countable: Bitcoin, bank accounts, stocks, bonds, second real estate, vehicles beyond one, cash
  • Excluded: Primary home, one vehicle, household goods, burial funds up to limits, and — crucially — assets held in a properly structured Special Needs Trust
  • Key rule: Resources held in a third-party SNT are not counted as the beneficiary's resources because the beneficiary does not own or control them
  • ABLE accounts: Excluded up to $100,000 from SSI resource counting (with conditions)

The Special Needs Trust: The Core Tool

A Special Needs Trust — sometimes called a supplemental needs trust — is a legal structure designed specifically to hold assets for the benefit of a disabled individual without those assets counting as the beneficiary's resources for Medicaid and SSI purposes. The legal basis is 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4) and related federal regulations, and every state has implementing law.

The fundamental mechanism is that the trust owns the assets — not the beneficiary. The trustee controls distributions. Because the beneficiary does not have legal ownership or control over the trust assets, those assets are not counted in the beneficiary's resource calculation. The trust supplements government benefits; it does not replace them.

"A properly structured Special Needs Trust lets Bitcoin's appreciation work for your disabled heir over decades — without ever threatening the Medicaid or SSI eligibility that makes their daily life possible."

What Can the SNT Pay For?

The SNT trustee can make distributions for "supplemental" expenses — things that Medicaid and SSI do not cover. This is where the trust's value becomes clear. With proper management, SNT funds can pay for:

  • Private therapy, specialists, and medical treatments not covered by Medicaid
  • Adaptive technology, communication devices, wheelchairs, and equipment
  • Education, vocational training, and job coaching
  • Recreation, entertainment, travel, and vacations
  • Personal care attendants beyond what Medicaid covers
  • Housing modifications, transportation, and accessible vehicles
  • Legal and financial advocacy services
  • Funeral and burial costs

What the SNT generally should not pay for, without careful analysis, is cash directly to the beneficiary or items classified as "food and shelter." These can trigger an "in-kind support and maintenance" (ISM) reduction in SSI payments. A knowledgeable SNT trustee navigates these rules precisely — ideally with ongoing guidance from a benefits counselor.

First-Party vs. Third-Party Special Needs Trusts

There are two fundamentally different types of SNTs, and understanding the distinction is critical for Bitcoin estate planning. The type you need depends on whose money is funding the trust.

Third-Party Special Needs Trusts

A third-party SNT is funded with assets that belong to someone other than the beneficiary — a parent, grandparent, sibling, or any other person. This is the primary tool for Bitcoin estate planning. When you, the Bitcoin holder, create and fund a trust for your disabled heir using your Bitcoin, that is a third-party SNT.

The critical advantage of a third-party SNT: there is no Medicaid payback requirement. When the disabled beneficiary dies, the remaining trust assets pass to whoever the trust document designates — other family members, other heirs, charitable organizations — without the state having any claim to reimbursement for Medicaid costs paid during the beneficiary's lifetime.

A third-party SNT can be set up as a standalone trust document, or it can be a sub-trust within your revocable living trust that "springs into existence" and becomes irrevocable when you die. For Bitcoin holders, the standalone or sub-trust approach in your revocable trust is the most common and flexible structure.

First-Party Special Needs Trusts (d4A Trusts)

A first-party SNT — formally called a "(d)(4)(A) trust" — is funded with assets belonging to the disabled person themselves. This arises when a disabled individual receives assets directly: an inheritance left without planning, a personal injury settlement, or other windfall. The trust "rescues" those assets from counting as resources.

The critical restriction: first-party SNTs require a Medicaid payback provision. When the beneficiary dies, the state must be reimbursed for all Medicaid expenditures made on the beneficiary's behalf before any remaining assets pass to other heirs. In many cases — particularly for individuals with decades of Medicaid support — this payback obligation can consume the entire trust.

First-party SNTs also have restrictions on who can establish them: the disabled individual, a parent, grandparent, legal guardian, or court. And the beneficiary must be under 65 when the trust is established (with some exceptions for pooled trusts).

First-Party vs. Third-Party SNT — Quick Comparison

  • Funding source: First-party = beneficiary's own assets | Third-party = other person's assets
  • Medicaid payback: First-party = required | Third-party = none
  • Who can create: First-party = limited | Third-party = anyone (typically parents/grandparents)
  • Age limit: First-party = must be under 65 at creation | Third-party = no age limit
  • Remainder beneficiaries: First-party = state first, then family | Third-party = family/charity directly
  • Estate planning tool: Third-party is the primary tool for Bitcoin holders planning ahead

The Bitcoin-Specific Complications

Standard SNT drafting and administration practice was built around traditional assets: brokerage accounts, real estate, cash. Bitcoin introduces several layers of complexity that demand careful attention.

Custody and Key Management

When Bitcoin is held in a trust, the trust entity owns it — but Bitcoin ownership is controlled by private keys, not by legal title documents. The trustee must be capable of taking actual custody of the Bitcoin in a way that reflects the trust's legal ownership. This means the trustee needs to either:

  • Hold keys on hardware wallets maintained in the trustee's name in a secure facility, or
  • Use a qualified custodian that can hold Bitcoin in a custody account titled to the trust, or
  • Establish a multi-signature arrangement where the trustee controls the required signing threshold

Critically: the seed phrase for any Bitcoin held in a trust should never be recorded in the trust document itself, in a will, or in any probate filing. These documents become public record. Seed phrases belong in a separately secured, encrypted format — a hardware wallet, a fireproof vault, or a properly structured inheritance protocol — with instructions for the trustee on how to access the custody arrangement, not the keys themselves.

Volatility and Trustee Duty

A trustee has a fiduciary duty to administer the trust prudently for the beneficiary's benefit. Holding 100% of trust assets in a single volatile asset — Bitcoin — may create tension with the Uniform Prudent Investor Act, which governs trustee investment duties in most states. Some states have adopted digital asset trust statutes that clarify this (Wyoming is the leading example). The trust document should explicitly authorize Bitcoin as a permitted investment and address the trustee's authority to hold, convert, or hedge the position.

An SNT trustee who diversifies Bitcoin into a more conservative portfolio may be acting prudently — but may also be doing the beneficiary a disservice if Bitcoin continues its long-run appreciation trajectory. These competing considerations should be addressed explicitly in the trust document's investment policy provisions, and the grantor (you) should memorialize your intent regarding the trustee's discretion over the Bitcoin position.

Valuation for SSI Purposes

Even properly structured trust assets are not literally invisible to the SSA. If an SNT is drafted or administered improperly, the SSA can re-characterize the assets as available to the beneficiary. Annual documentation that the trust complies with applicable law, that distributions are consistent with supplemental purposes, and that the beneficiary has no direct access or control over the trust assets is essential. The trustee should maintain detailed records and work with an SNT-specialist attorney to ensure ongoing compliance.

Liquidation for Distributions

When the trustee needs to make a distribution — pay for therapy, a vacation, or specialized equipment — if the trust holds only Bitcoin, the trustee must sell Bitcoin to generate the necessary cash. This creates a taxable event at the trust level. SNTs pay taxes at the trust rate, which reaches the top bracket quickly. Many SNT trustees maintain a cash reserve (equivalent to one to two years of projected distributions) so that Bitcoin need not be liquidated in a down market or for small routine expenses. The investment policy should address this explicitly.

How to Fund Your SNT with Bitcoin

The timing and method of funding a third-party SNT with Bitcoin matters significantly.

Funding at Death: Testamentary SNT

The most common approach for Bitcoin holders who are not yet ready to give up control of their Bitcoin is a testamentary SNT — a trust created through your will or pour-over will that receives Bitcoin after your death. Your Bitcoin passes to the trust rather than directly to your disabled heir.

For self-custodied Bitcoin, this requires an inheritance plan that allows the trustee to access the Bitcoin after your death. This typically involves a multi-signature wallet where the trustee holds a key, or a secure inheritance protocol (such as a custodial arrangement with a co-signing institution) that releases control to the trustee upon verified death. Merely writing the seed phrase on a piece of paper inside an envelope in your safe is not a sufficient plan — it relies on physical access in a specific window and creates security risks.

For Bitcoin held in an exchange or custody account, the account can be designated to transfer to the trust at death through a Transfer on Death (TOD) designation or through beneficiary designation to the trust itself (where the institution allows). This is simpler but introduces exchange custody risk.

Funding During Life: Inter Vivos SNT

Some parents prefer to fund the SNT while alive — either to remove assets from their taxable estate, to test the trust administration, or to begin supplementing their disabled family member's life now. Transferring Bitcoin to a third-party SNT during your lifetime is a completed gift for gift tax purposes. The transfer uses your lifetime gift and estate tax exemption.

In 2026, the federal lifetime estate and gift tax exemption is currently $13.99 million per individual (indexed for inflation). Transfers within this exemption incur no gift or estate tax. Transfers above it are taxed at 40%. If your Bitcoin position is significant, a lifetime SNT funding strategy should be evaluated alongside other wealth transfer tools (GRATs, irrevocable trusts, dynasty trusts) with qualified tax counsel.

Bitcoin transferred to an inter vivos SNT carries over your original cost basis — there is no step-up in basis on lifetime gifts. The trust will owe capital gains tax (at trust rates) when it eventually sells Bitcoin. This is a meaningful disadvantage compared to testamentary transfers, which receive a stepped-up basis at death. The decision to fund the trust during life versus at death involves weighing estate tax exposure against basis step-up opportunity.

Choosing the Right Trustee

The trustee of an SNT carrying significant Bitcoin holdings must bring together capabilities that rarely coexist: deep familiarity with SSI and Medicaid compliance rules, investment management competence, digital asset custody capability, and genuine commitment to the beneficiary's quality of life. This is a demanding combination.

Individual Trustees

Many families appoint a sibling, other family member, or trusted friend as trustee. The advantages are personal knowledge of the beneficiary's needs and lower cost. The risks are that individual trustees may lack SSI/Medicaid expertise, may have conflicts of interest, and — for Bitcoin — may not be equipped to handle custody and ongoing compliance. An individual trustee for a Bitcoin-heavy SNT should be required by the trust document to engage an SNT-specialist attorney and a qualified digital asset custodian, at trust expense.

Corporate Trustees

Corporate trustees (trust companies and bank trust departments) offer professional fiduciary service, but most have limited Bitcoin capability. A handful of trust companies — primarily in Wyoming, South Dakota, and Nevada, states with favorable digital asset trust laws — can hold Bitcoin in custody for trusts. This is a rapidly evolving space. If Bitcoin is a material portion of the trust, a corporate trustee with demonstrated digital asset custody infrastructure is worth the higher cost.

Co-Trustee Structure

A common solution is a co-trustee structure: a family member who knows the beneficiary serves as distribution co-trustee (making day-to-day benefit decisions), while a professional or corporate co-trustee handles investments and compliance. For Bitcoin specifically, a digital asset custodian can hold the Bitcoin on behalf of the trust without being a full trustee, freeing the human trustee to focus on the beneficiary's life while the custody infrastructure handles key management.

ABLE Accounts: The Complementary Tool

ABLE accounts — Achieving a Better Life Experience accounts, authorized under 26 U.S.C. § 529A — are tax-advantaged savings accounts for individuals with disabilities whose disability began before age 26 (expanded from age 26 to birth-before-age-46 beginning in 2026 under ABLE Age Adjustment Act provisions). They are simpler than SNTs and can work alongside a trust as a complementary tool.

Key ABLE Account Features

  • Contribution limit: $19,000 per year in 2026 (the annual gift exclusion amount), with additional contributions allowed for working beneficiaries
  • SSI exclusion: ABLE account balances up to $100,000 are excluded from SSI resource counting; balances above $100,000 suspend (but do not terminate) SSI
  • Medicaid payback: Required at death (unlike third-party SNTs), but only for state Medicaid expenditures after the account was opened
  • Investment growth: Tax-free if used for "qualified disability expenses" (a broad category including housing, transportation, education, healthcare, and more)
  • Control: Unlike an SNT, the beneficiary can often control their own ABLE account, enabling more autonomy

Bitcoin in an ABLE Account?

ABLE accounts are administered through state programs that offer limited investment options — typically mutual funds and similar vehicles. Bitcoin is not currently a direct investment option in any state ABLE program. However, Bitcoin-related ETFs or funds may be available as investment options in some programs. The ABLE account and the SNT serve different functions: the ABLE account can offer the beneficiary more spending autonomy for day-to-day expenses, while the SNT holds larger capital and is trustee-managed for major supplemental needs.

SNT-to-ABLE Transfers

Third-party SNTs can transfer funds directly to a beneficiary's ABLE account. This is a useful mechanism for getting smaller amounts into the beneficiary's control without triggering ISM concerns or SSI cash payment reductions. The transfer counts against the ABLE annual contribution limit. Many well-designed SNTs include an explicit provision authorizing ABLE account contributions.

Common Planning Mistakes That Bitcoin Holders Make

Understanding what not to do is as important as knowing the right structure.

1. Leaving Bitcoin Directly to a Disabled Heir in a Will

The most common and most damaging mistake. A will bequest to a disabled beneficiary — even with instructions to "use it for their care" — transfers legal ownership of the asset to that person. They inherit the Bitcoin, it becomes a countable resource, and they can lose SSI and Medicaid until the asset is spent down. No amount of well-meaning language in a will fixes this.

2. Naming a Disabled Person as Beneficiary on Accounts

Transfer-on-death designations and beneficiary designations on exchange accounts that name a disabled SSI/Medicaid recipient directly create the same problem as a will bequest — a direct transfer that bypasses any protective structure. Beneficiary designations for Bitcoin holdings should name the SNT, not the disabled individual personally.

3. Relying on "Sibling Will Handle It"

Some families plan to leave everything to a non-disabled sibling with an informal understanding that the sibling will "take care of" the disabled family member. This arrangement has no legal force, depends entirely on the sibling's goodwill and financial health, creates gift tax issues for the sibling when they make transfers, and means the disabled person has no enforceable rights to the funds. A properly drafted SNT is legally binding, enforceable, and durable across decades.

4. Funding an SNT with Bitcoin but Ignoring Custody

An SNT that legally "owns" Bitcoin but has no clear mechanism for the trustee to actually access and control the private keys is an SNT that may not function when it needs to. The legal ownership on paper must be matched by practical, secure custody infrastructure. This requires planning before the trustee ever needs to act.

5. Failing to Update the SNT as Bitcoin Value Changes

An SNT that held $50,000 of Bitcoin at funding may now hold $500,000. The investment policy, trustee compensation, co-trustee arrangements, and distribution strategy that made sense at $50,000 may need revision at $500,000. Review your SNT and its administration annually or whenever Bitcoin's value changes materially.

The "Letter of Intent" for Bitcoin SNT Beneficiaries

Alongside the legal documents, one of the most valuable things a Bitcoin-holding parent can create for their disabled beneficiary is a detailed Letter of Intent — sometimes called a Letter of Instruction — that communicates to future trustees who the beneficiary is, what their life looks like, and how you want the Bitcoin to support them.

This document has no legal force but provides invaluable guidance. For a Bitcoin-heavy SNT, the Letter of Intent should cover:

  • The beneficiary's daily routine, preferences, and support needs
  • Your intent regarding the Bitcoin position (hold long-term? diversify? liquidate to fund specific life needs?)
  • The custody infrastructure and who holds keys or manages the custodial relationship
  • The names of trusted advisors (attorneys, benefits counselors, financial advisors)
  • A clear description of what supplemental distributions you would prioritize
  • Your philosophy about quality of life vs. asset preservation

The Letter of Intent should be stored alongside trust documents and updated regularly. Unlike the trust itself, it can be amended without legal formality.

State Law Variations and Jurisdiction Considerations

Federal law establishes the framework for SSI and Medicaid, but SNT law also involves significant state-level variation. States differ in how they implement Medicaid payback rules, pooled trust regulations, and trustee duties. Wyoming, South Dakota, Nevada, and Delaware have particularly favorable trust and digital asset statutes. If your Bitcoin holdings are significant and you have flexibility in trust siting, the choice of governing law — often separate from where you live — can matter.

A Wyoming trust, for example, benefits from Wyoming's comprehensive digital asset statute (Wyoming Stat. § 34-29-101 et seq.) that explicitly addresses trustees' authority and duties with respect to digital assets, and from Wyoming's favorable self-settled trust and dynasty trust laws. The trust can be governed by Wyoming law even if you and your beneficiary live in another state, subject to proper structuring.

Medicaid Planning and the SNT Interaction

For beneficiaries who will need significant Medicaid-funded long-term care over their lifetime, the interaction between the SNT and Medicaid planning rules requires careful ongoing management. Distributions from an SNT that cover Medicaid-coverable services can reduce Medicaid's obligation — which might seem helpful but can actually be counterproductive if it depletes trust assets that could have covered truly supplemental needs. An experienced SNT administrator knows the line between supplemental and replacement care funding and stays on the right side of it.

The Estate Tax Overlay

For Bitcoin holders with estates that approach or exceed the federal exemption ($13.99 million per individual in 2026), the SNT interacts with estate planning in ways worth noting. Assets that pass into a third-party SNT at your death are included in your gross estate for estate tax purposes — they were yours when you died. The marital deduction, charitable deduction, and lifetime exemption apply in the usual way. If you fund the SNT during your lifetime, the gift uses your lifetime exemption and removes future appreciation from your estate.

For very large Bitcoin positions — where significant estate tax exposure is a real concern — an SNT can be combined with other structures: an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) to fund estate taxes without depleting the SNT, or a dynasty trust for non-disabled heirs alongside the SNT for the disabled beneficiary. These structures require coordination among your estate attorney, tax advisor, and SNT specialist.
