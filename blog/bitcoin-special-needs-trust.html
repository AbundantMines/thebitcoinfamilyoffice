Bitcoin and Special Needs Trusts: Protecting a Disabled Beneficiary's Inheritance

Leaving Bitcoin directly to a disabled family member can instantly disqualify them from Medicaid, SSI, and other means-tested benefits they depend on for daily care. A properly structured Special Needs Trust solves this — but Bitcoin adds layers of complexity that most SNT attorneys have never encountered.

By Hal Turner
February 25, 2026
~14 min read

Most Bitcoin estate planning advice is written for wealthy, healthy heirs. This article is for families with a different situation: a beloved family member with a disability who depends on government benefit programs for medical care and daily support — and who stands to receive a Bitcoin inheritance that could eliminate that safety net in an instant.

At $95,000 per Bitcoin, it takes 0.021 BTC — less than $2,000 worth even at that price a few years ago — to exceed the $2,000 asset limit that governs Supplemental Security Income (SSI) and Medicaid eligibility. A direct Bitcoin inheritance of any meaningful size will end those benefits. And losing Medicaid doesn't just mean paying out-of-pocket for prescriptions — for many disabled individuals, it means losing access to residential care, in-home support, and life-sustaining medical services that may cost tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars per year.

A Special Needs Trust (SNT) is the legal instrument designed to solve this problem. But making an SNT work with Bitcoin requires careful attention to custody, investment policy, trustee selection, and distribution standards that most SNT attorneys have not yet confronted. This guide walks through every layer.

The Core Problem: Benefits Cliffs and Bitcoin

The federal benefit programs that serve people with disabilities — primarily Supplemental Security Income (SSI) and Medicaid — are needs-based. They impose strict asset limits that count most property a recipient owns. For SSI, the limit is $2,000 for an individual ($3,000 for a couple). Many Medicaid programs use the same threshold, though details vary by state.

At today's Bitcoin price of approximately $95,000/BTC:

  • 0.021 BTC ≈ $2,000 — the SSI asset limit. Any holding above this disqualifies the beneficiary from SSI.
  • 0.1 BTC ≈ $9,500 — roughly five times the asset limit. Received directly, this eliminates SSI and potentially Medicaid in most states.
  • 1 BTC ≈ $95,000 — a life-changing inheritance that, received outright by an SSI/Medicaid recipient, would eliminate their benefits entirely and potentially cost far more in lost care than it provides in cash.

The cruel math: a disabled individual who loses Medicaid due to an inheritance may need to spend down virtually all of those inherited funds on care costs before re-qualifying for benefits. The inheritance that was meant to improve their life effectively becomes a temporary bridge to spending down — not a lasting improvement.

Bitcoin's price volatility adds a layer of urgency. Even a position that appears modest today can breach the asset limit quickly if prices rise. A parent who leaves their disabled child 0.02 BTC today, thinking it's under the limit, may inadvertently create a benefits crisis if Bitcoin appreciates between the time the estate plan is drafted and when the inheritance is received.

Critical Warning

Never leave Bitcoin directly to a disabled beneficiary who receives SSI or Medicaid. Even a small Bitcoin inheritance received outright can immediately disqualify them from the benefits they depend on for care. A Special Needs Trust must be in place before the inheritance is received.

What a Special Needs Trust Is — and How It Works

A Special Needs Trust (SNT) is a specific type of irrevocable trust designed to hold assets for a disabled beneficiary without those assets being counted as the beneficiary's own resources for purposes of means-tested benefit eligibility. Assets held in a properly drafted SNT are owned by the trust — not by the beneficiary — and therefore do not count against their SSI or Medicaid asset limits.

The foundational legal authority is 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(A) for first-party SNTs and § 1396p(d)(4)(C) for pooled trusts. Third-party SNTs are governed by state trust law and federal benefit regulations. When properly structured and administered, an SNT allows a disabled beneficiary to receive supplemental support — technology, recreation, education, personal care beyond what government programs cover — without losing access to those programs.

How the trust distributes: The trustee makes discretionary distributions from the trust for the beneficiary's "supplemental and extra care" — items and services that are not provided by government programs. The trustee must be careful not to make distributions that would be deemed to "replace" government benefits (called "in-kind support and maintenance" or ISM), as those can reduce SSI payments. Distributions must be structured to supplement, never substitute.

Third-Party SNT vs. First-Party SNT: A Critical Distinction

There are two fundamentally different types of Special Needs Trusts, and the distinction matters enormously for Bitcoin families.

Third-Party Special Needs Trust

A third-party SNT is funded with assets belonging to someone other than the beneficiary — typically a parent, grandparent, or sibling. This is the instrument most relevant for Bitcoin estate planning: you create and fund the trust during your lifetime or at death, for the benefit of your disabled family member.

Key features of a third-party SNT:

  • No Medicaid payback requirement. When the beneficiary dies, the remaining trust assets pass to other named remainder beneficiaries (other children, charity, etc.) — not to Medicaid to reimburse the state for care provided. This is the most important distinction from a first-party SNT.
  • No contribution limits. You can fund it with as much Bitcoin as you wish — through annual exclusion gifts during your lifetime, through your will at death, or by naming the trust as beneficiary on life insurance or retirement accounts.
  • Tax treatment: Contributions are treated as gifts by the donor; the trust itself pays income tax as a complex trust (or the grantor may be treated as the owner under grantor trust rules if living). Capital gains inside the trust are generally taxed at trust rates, which can be punitive at high income levels — a reason to plan distributions carefully.
  • Flexibility in design: You control the trust terms — trustee selection, investment policy, distribution standards, and successor provisions — within the legal parameters for SNT qualification.

First-Party Special Needs Trust (Self-Settled)

A first-party SNT is funded with assets that belong to the disabled individual themselves — for example, a personal injury settlement, an inheritance already received outright, or the individual's own savings. This type is established under 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4)(A) and must be established by a parent, grandparent, legal guardian, or court.

Critical limitations of a first-party SNT:

  • Medicaid payback on death. At the beneficiary's death, the state Medicaid program must be repaid for all benefits paid during the beneficiary's lifetime. Remaining assets only pass to other beneficiaries after Medicaid is reimbursed. For a disabled individual who has received Medicaid for decades, this payback obligation can be substantial.
  • Must be established while beneficiary is under 65. First-party SNTs cannot be established for individuals 65 or older — which matters if a disability develops in later adulthood.
  • Complex administration: The Medicaid agency in the state of residence must typically be notified, and distributions are subject to more intense scrutiny than third-party SNTs.
Feature Third-Party SNT First-Party SNT
Who funds it? Parent, grandparent, anyone except the beneficiary The beneficiary themselves (their own assets)
Medicaid payback at death? No — assets pass to remainder beneficiaries Yes — state Medicaid reimbursed first
Age limit for creation? None Beneficiary must be under 65
Best for Bitcoin planning? Yes — primary vehicle for Bitcoin inheritance Only if beneficiary already holds Bitcoin directly
Remainder at death? Named beneficiaries (children, charity, etc.) Medicaid payback, then named beneficiaries

For the vast majority of Bitcoin estate planning situations — a parent or grandparent who holds Bitcoin and wants to provide for a disabled child or grandchild — the third-party SNT is the right vehicle. The rest of this article focuses primarily on the third-party structure.

Bitcoin-Specific SNT Design: The Four Hard Questions

A conventional SNT holds stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and cash. Adding Bitcoin to the SNT ecosystem creates four design challenges that require deliberate answers before the trust is drafted.

1. Who Holds the Keys?

The trustee of an SNT has a fiduciary duty to manage trust assets prudently. For Bitcoin, this means the trustee must be capable of either: (a) directly safeguarding the private keys in a secure, recoverable manner, or (b) delegating that function to a qualified sub-custodian.

Most individual trustees — even sophisticated, well-intentioned family members — are not equipped to custody significant Bitcoin holdings on behalf of a trust with an indefinite life span and a vulnerable beneficiary. A hardware wallet held by an individual trustee creates a single point of failure: if the trustee dies, becomes incapacitated, or simply loses the seed phrase, the trust's Bitcoin may be permanently inaccessible.

Better options for SNT Bitcoin custody:

  • Qualified Bitcoin custodian as sub-custodian: The trustee delegates custody to a regulated Bitcoin custodian (such as Anchorage Digital, BitGo, or Coinbase Custody) that can hold Bitcoin on behalf of trusts with proper titling. The trustee retains investment authority; the custodian holds the keys.
  • Directed trust structure: The trust document separates custodial authority from distribution authority. A Bitcoin-literate investment advisor or directed trustee handles custody and investment decisions; a separate administrative trustee handles distributions, record-keeping, and benefits compliance. This division of labor is well-established in directed trust states (South Dakota, Nevada, Delaware, Alaska).
  • Multi-signature structure: For larger positions, a 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multisig arrangement with keys held by different trusted parties (trustee, successor trustee, custodian) ensures no single point of failure and no single individual can unilaterally move funds.

2. Can the SNT HODL Bitcoin? The Prudent Investor Duty

Every trustee in the United States is subject to the Uniform Prudent Investor Act (or its state equivalent), which requires trustees to invest and manage trust assets with the care, skill, prudence, and diligence that a prudent investor would exercise. The default standard includes a diversification requirement — a trustee who concentrates 100% of trust assets in a single volatile asset is exposed to personal liability if the trust suffers losses.

For a Bitcoin SNT, this creates a tension: the grantor may intend for the trust to hold Bitcoin as its primary asset indefinitely, but the trustee's fiduciary duty could be read to require diversification. The resolution is a written Investment Policy Statement (IPS) that explicitly:

  • Authorizes the trustee to hold a concentrated Bitcoin position
  • Articulates the rationale (long-term appreciation potential, inflation hedge, the grantor's intent)
  • Defines acceptable volatility parameters and liquidity requirements for distributions
  • Relieves the trustee of personal liability for maintaining the Bitcoin allocation as directed, provided the trustee acts in good faith

This IPS should be incorporated by reference into the trust document, so it has legal force. The grantor — who presumably understands Bitcoin's risk profile — should draft it in consultation with the estate planning attorney and ensure the trustee's acceptance is documented.

Some states allow a trust instrument to override the default prudent investor diversification requirement with an explicit direction from the grantor. South Dakota, Nevada, and Delaware are particularly favorable. If the family's SNT is sited in a directed trust state, the grantor's Bitcoin concentration instruction carries greater legal weight.

3. Distribution Standards: What Can the Trust Pay For?

An SNT's distributions must supplement government benefits, not replace them. The trustee must avoid making distributions for items or services that would be counted as "in-kind support and maintenance" (ISM) under SSI rules — primarily food and shelter. ISM distributions reduce SSI payments dollar-for-dollar (up to one-third of the SSI benefit plus $20).

Distributions from a Bitcoin SNT that are generally permissible:

  • Education, vocational training, and technology (computers, tablets, communication devices)
  • Transportation (vehicle, ride services, adapted vehicles)
  • Recreation, entertainment, and cultural experiences
  • Medical and dental services not covered by Medicaid
  • Personal care attendants beyond what Medicaid provides
  • Home modifications not funded by state programs
  • Legal fees and advocacy services
  • Pre-paid funeral and burial arrangements

The trustee must document every distribution carefully — both the purpose and the relationship to the beneficiary's government benefit eligibility. This record-keeping discipline is especially important when distributions are funded by Bitcoin liquidations, which generate capital gains inside the trust.

4. Bitcoin Liquidation and Tax Inside the Trust

When the trustee liquidates Bitcoin to fund a distribution, the trust recognizes a capital gain (assuming the Bitcoin has appreciated). Trusts reach the top capital gains tax rate (23.8%, including NIIT) at very low income thresholds — approximately $15,000 of taxable income in 2024. A trust holding appreciated Bitcoin that makes regular distributions may face substantial annual tax liability.

Strategies to manage this:

  • Distribute income to the beneficiary when appropriate — trust income distributed to the beneficiary is taxed at their individual rate (often 0% if they have no other income), not the trust rate. However, SNT distributions require careful analysis to ensure they don't trigger ISM rules.
  • Plan liquidations strategically — use years of lower Bitcoin price appreciation (or temporary price dips) for planned liquidations, reducing the embedded gain recognized in any single year.
  • Maintain a cash reserve within the trust for near-term distributions, reducing the frequency of Bitcoin liquidations and improving the trustee's ability to choose favorable liquidation timing.

Naming an SNT as Beneficiary on Life Insurance and Retirement Accounts

One of the most practical ways to fund a Bitcoin SNT — especially for families who hold Bitcoin in taxable accounts or cold storage rather than IRAs — is to use the SNT as the beneficiary on life insurance policies or retirement accounts, while holding the Bitcoin directly in the trust during the grantor's lifetime.

Life insurance → SNT: Name the SNT as the beneficiary on a life insurance policy. The death benefit passes to the trust income-tax-free and immediately available for the beneficiary's supplemental needs. This is a clean structure with no income tax complications. The trust receives the policy proceeds as principal (not income), which avoids the trust income tax issue entirely.

Retirement account → SNT: Naming an SNT as beneficiary on an IRA or 401(k) creates complexity. Most SNTs are not "see-through" trusts under IRS rules because they have multiple potential beneficiaries with different life expectancies. This may mean the retirement account must be distributed (and taxed) within 10 years under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule — generating significant trust income that is taxed at punitive trust rates.

For this reason, most Bitcoin estate planning attorneys recommend that Bitcoin holders with disabled beneficiaries:

  • Leave retirement accounts to other beneficiaries (healthy heirs who can use the 10-year rule efficiently at their own tax rates)
  • Leave Bitcoin, real property, and other assets with favorable tax treatment (step-up in basis) to the SNT
  • Fund the SNT with life insurance proceeds or lifetime contributions for the liquidity and distribution needs it serves

The ABLE Account: A Partial Alternative for Smaller Amounts

Congress created ABLE accounts (Achieving a Better Life Experience, 26 U.S.C. § 529A) in 2014 as a simpler, lower-cost alternative to SNTs for disabled individuals. An ABLE account is a tax-advantaged savings account that does not count against the beneficiary's SSI or Medicaid eligibility — up to $100,000 in the account.

ABLE accounts have meaningful limitations compared to a Bitcoin SNT:

  • $100,000 SSI protection cap. ABLE account balances above $100,000 count against the SSI asset limit. For a Bitcoin inheritance of any significant size, this cap is quickly reached — particularly as Bitcoin appreciates.
  • Annual contribution limits. Contributions are capped at the annual gift tax exclusion amount ($18,000 in 2024) per year. You cannot fund an ABLE account with a large lump sum.
  • Cannot hold Bitcoin directly. ABLE accounts are administered through state-sponsored programs similar to 529 plans. They hold cash and investment options offered by the program — not direct Bitcoin. There is no mechanism to hold self-custodied BTC in an ABLE account.
  • Medicaid payback on death. Unlike a third-party SNT, remaining ABLE account funds at death are subject to Medicaid payback in most states.
  • Disability onset requirement. The beneficiary's disability must have begun before age 26 (changing to age 46 beginning in 2026 under SECURE 2.0).

The practical role of an ABLE account in a Bitcoin estate plan: ABLE accounts can work alongside a Bitcoin SNT — the SNT holds the Bitcoin position and larger assets, while an ABLE account holds cash for day-to-day supplemental expenses. ABLE accounts offer easier administration and fewer distribution restrictions than a full SNT for routine, smaller purchases. Think of the ABLE account as the operating checking account and the Bitcoin SNT as the long-term wealth vehicle.

Common Mistakes: How Bitcoin Families Accidentally Harm Disabled Heirs

The consequences of getting this wrong are severe and often irreversible. Here are the most common mistakes Bitcoin families make:

Mistake 1: Leaving Bitcoin Directly to a Disabled Heir

Naming a disabled beneficiary directly in a will or trust distribution means the Bitcoin passes to them outright at your death. If they receive SSI or Medicaid, this immediately eliminates their benefit eligibility. Depending on their care needs, they may spend down most of the inheritance on care before requalifying for benefits — and the process of requalifying can take months, during which essential services may be interrupted.

Fix: Name the Special Needs Trust as the beneficiary in your will, revocable living trust, and any beneficiary designations. The Bitcoin never touches the beneficiary's hands — it passes directly into the SNT.

Mistake 2: The "Letter of Intent" That Isn't a Trust

Some parents write detailed instructions in a letter of intent or side letter explaining how they want their disabled child cared for. This letter has enormous emotional value and practical utility for trustees — but it has no legal effect. A letter of intent is not an SNT. Bitcoin left without a formal trust structure will pass outright, regardless of what a letter says.

Mistake 3: Funding a First-Party SNT Without Understanding Payback

If a disabled beneficiary directly receives Bitcoin (perhaps before an SNT is in place) and then transfers it to a first-party SNT to preserve benefits, that SNT is subject to Medicaid payback on death. Families sometimes assume that "putting it in a trust" protects the full inheritance for other heirs — but a first-party SNT payback obligation can consume the entire trust balance. Get competent counsel before funding a first-party SNT with appreciated Bitcoin.

Mistake 4: Naming the Disabled Beneficiary as Trustee of Their Own SNT

This is a disqualifying structure. The SSI regulations deem assets held in a trust where the beneficiary is also the trustee to be available resources — meaning they count against the $2,000 limit. The trustee of a Special Needs Trust must be someone other than the beneficiary. Options include a parent, sibling, professional trustee, corporate trustee, or nonprofit organization.

Mistake 5: Using a Generic Trust Form

An SNT must contain specific language to qualify under federal and state regulations. A generic irrevocable trust form, even one drafted by a competent estate attorney, may not include the SNT-specific provisions required. The cost of using incorrect trust language — benefits disqualification — far exceeds the cost of retaining a qualified special needs planning attorney.

Do Not DIY This

Special Needs Trusts involve the intersection of trust law, federal benefits law, and Bitcoin custody mechanics. This is not a document that should be drafted from a template or by a general practice attorney without SNT experience. The stakes — loss of Medicaid for a vulnerable family member — are too high.

Finding an SNT Attorney with Bitcoin Competency

This is one of the rarest skill combinations in the legal profession today: a special needs trust attorney who also understands Bitcoin custody, basis tracking, and the technical aspects of digital asset ownership. You need both skill sets.

What to look for when interviewing attorneys:

  • Active SNT practice. Ask how many SNTs they have drafted in the last 12 months. This should be a regular part of their practice, not an occasional occurrence. Membership in the Academy of Special Needs Planners (ASNP) is a useful credential.
  • Bitcoin literacy (not just "digital assets" generally). Ask specifically about Bitcoin custody mechanics — hardware wallets, multisig, on-chain transfers, UTXO management. An attorney who conflates Bitcoin with crypto tokens or who defers entirely to "whatever the custodian recommends" may not have the depth you need.
  • Directed trust experience. If you want to segregate Bitcoin custody from distribution decisions (the recommended approach), the attorney should be familiar with directed trust structures and the relevant state laws.
  • Experience with trustee instruction documents. A good SNT attorney will prepare not just the trust instrument but a Letter of Instruction for the trustee covering custody procedures, distribution standards, and benefit compliance protocols.
  • Familiarity with your state's Medicaid rules. SNT compliance is partly federal and partly state-specific. The attorney should be current on your state's specific Medicaid policies for SNTs — particularly around distribution standards and payback exceptions.

Expect to work with at least two professionals: the SNT attorney for trust drafting and benefits compliance, and a Bitcoin-competent estate planning advisor (or a firm like The Bitcoin family office) for custody architecture, investment policy, and the technical aspects of Bitcoin integration into the trust structure.

State-by-State Considerations for Bitcoin SNTs

While the core SNT framework is federal, state law significantly affects how a Bitcoin SNT should be structured and administered. Key state-level considerations:

State Medicaid Programs and Supplemental Benefits

Many states operate supplemental benefit programs for disabled individuals that layer on top of federal SSI and Medicaid. Some of these state programs — such as California's In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS) or New York's OPWDD programs — have their own asset limits, look-through provisions, and distribution rules that interact differently with SNT assets than the federal programs do. An SNT that is perfectly compliant for federal SSI purposes may create complications for a state supplemental program.

Always engage an attorney licensed in the state where the beneficiary resides — not just where the trust is sited.

Directed Trust Siting

States like South Dakota, Nevada, Delaware, and Alaska have particularly favorable directed trust statutes that allow clean separation of investment, custody, and distribution functions. For a Bitcoin SNT with a large position, siting the trust in one of these states (using a directed trust structure with an in-state directed trustee) can provide both better Bitcoin custody flexibility and reduced trustee liability exposure.

Siting a trust in a non-resident state requires compliance with that state's jurisdictional requirements (typically at least one in-state trustee or trust company), but for significant Bitcoin positions, the planning flexibility may be worth the administrative complexity.

State Income Tax on Trust Income

Some states impose income tax on trust income based on the residence of the grantor, trustee, or beneficiary — regardless of where the trust is sited. For a Bitcoin SNT that will recognize capital gains as it liquidates Bitcoin for distributions, the state income tax treatment of trust capital gains is a meaningful planning variable. Work with a CPA who understands both state trust taxation and Bitcoin capital gains to model the tax impact.

Letter of Instruction for SNT Trustees: What Bitcoin Information They Need

A Letter of Instruction (LOI) for an SNT trustee handling Bitcoin is one of the most important documents in a Bitcoin estate plan involving a disabled beneficiary. It should contain practical, operational information that the trustee cannot derive from the trust document alone — and it may be the difference between the trustee managing the Bitcoin competently and accidentally losing access to the funds.

The Bitcoin SNT Letter of Instruction should cover:

  1. Custody architecture: Where is the Bitcoin held? (Custodian name and account number, hardware wallet location and make/model, multisig configuration if applicable.) Who holds keys, seed phrases, or custodian access credentials? Where are the backup seed phrases stored?
  2. Access and succession: Step-by-step instructions for a trustee who has never used the custody system to access and transfer Bitcoin. Contact information for the custodian, sub-custodian, or Bitcoin advisor who can assist the trustee. Successor access procedures if the primary trustee becomes incapacitated.
  3. Investment policy summary: Reminder that the trust is designed to hold Bitcoin as its primary asset; reference to the IPS incorporated into the trust document; instructions for when and how to liquidate for distributions.
  4. Distribution compliance guidelines: A plain-language summary of what distributions are permissible under the SNT without jeopardizing the beneficiary's benefits. Examples of compliant vs. non-compliant distributions. Contact information for the SNT attorney to consult when uncertain.
  5. Benefit program contacts: The Social Security Administration contacts for the beneficiary's SSI case, the state Medicaid agency contacts, and the beneficiary's benefits coordinator or case manager.
  6. Cost basis records: The complete basis history for every Bitcoin lot in the trust — acquisition dates, prices paid, and any adjustments. This information is critical for tax reporting when the trustee liquidates Bitcoin for distributions.
  7. Annual review reminder: A note that the SNT trustee should consult with the SNT attorney annually to confirm that distribution practices remain compliant with any changes in SSI/Medicaid rules, and that Bitcoin custody arrangements remain secure and appropriate.

This Letter of Instruction should be updated whenever the trust's custody arrangements change, whenever Bitcoin prices move materially (so context for distribution planning stays current), and at least annually. It should be stored securely but accessibly — the trustee needs to find it when they need it, which may be in an emergency.

Summary: Protecting a Disabled Beneficiary's Bitcoin Inheritance

The stakes in Bitcoin Special Needs Trust planning are higher than in almost any other area of Bitcoin estate planning. A mistake doesn't just mean your heirs pay more tax — it can eliminate a vulnerable family member's access to the medical care and support services they depend on to live.

The framework:

  • Never leave Bitcoin directly to a disabled beneficiary who receives means-tested benefits. At $95,000/BTC, it takes 0.021 BTC to breach the SSI asset limit. Use a properly drafted third-party SNT.
  • Structure the trust for Bitcoin custody. Use a directed trust structure that separates Bitcoin custody (sub-custodian or investment advisor) from administrative duties (distributions, benefits compliance). Don't burden a single trustee with both technical custody and benefits administration.
  • Write an Investment Policy Statement. Authorizing the trustee to maintain a concentrated Bitcoin position in the face of the prudent investor rule requires documented justification. The IPS provides that protection and should be incorporated into the trust document.
  • Understand the Medicaid payback rules. Third-party SNTs have no payback. First-party SNTs do. If the beneficiary's own Bitcoin must go into a trust, understand exactly what the payback obligation means for the heirs.
  • Don't name the beneficiary as trustee. A self-trusteed SNT is not an SNT — it's a countable resource that eliminates benefit eligibility.
  • Use an ABLE account for supplemental day-to-day expenses alongside the SNT for larger, long-term Bitcoin holdings. The ABLE account reduces friction for small distributions; the SNT preserves the Bitcoin position.
  • Find attorneys with both SNT and Bitcoin competency. They exist. The cost of their expertise is trivially small compared to the lifetime value of the benefits they'll help preserve.
  • Write a thorough Letter of Instruction for the trustee. Your trustee needs to be able to access the Bitcoin without you present. Give them the information they need before they need it.
