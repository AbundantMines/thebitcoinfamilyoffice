What the One Big Beautiful Bill Act Actually Changed

On July 4, 2025 — with timing that was almost certainly deliberate — President Trump signed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) into law. The sprawling legislation touched immigration, energy, defense, and a dozen other policy areas. But for Bitcoin-wealthy families, one provision towered above everything else:

The federal estate and gift tax exemption is now permanently set at $15 million per individual ($30 million per married couple), indexed for inflation. There is no sunset. The feared reversion to ~$7 million is dead.

To appreciate why this matters, you need to understand what almost happened. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 had roughly doubled the estate exemption from $5.49 million to over $13 million — but that increase was temporary. It was scheduled to sunset on December 31, 2025, reverting the exemption to approximately $7 million per person.

For two years, the estate planning world operated in a state of controlled panic. Attorneys urged clients to use their exemption before it disappeared. Families rushed to fund trusts. The uncertainty was a tax on time itself — every month of delay was a month closer to a drastically lower exemption.

The OBBBA ended that uncertainty. Here's what the law actually provides:

  • $15 million per individual in lifetime estate and gift tax exemption (up from the 2025 TCJA level of ~$13.99 million)
  • $30 million per married couple with portability (the surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's unused exemption)
  • Indexed for inflation — the exemption will grow annually, not erode
  • No sunset clause — this is permanent legislation, not a temporary provision
  • Annual gift exclusion remains at $19,000 per recipient ($38,000 per couple splitting gifts)

The OBBBA also made permanent the individual income tax rate cuts from the TCJA, including the 37% top marginal rate. According to Britannica Money's analysis, the combined impact represents the largest permanent tax reduction in a generation.

But here's the part most coverage misses: permanence doesn't mean invulnerability. Future Congresses can — and historically do — modify tax law. What permanence gives you is certainty today and the IRS anti-clawback protection that makes acting on that certainty rational. Gifts made under the $15 million exemption are protected even if a future Congress reduces the amount.

That protection is what makes the next section matter.

Three OBBBA Landmines Nobody's Talking About

The $15 million headline is the story most advisors are telling. It's a good story — and it's incomplete. As WealthManagement.com reported on April 7, 2026, in their analysis "The One Big Beautiful Bill Act Reshapes Wealth Planning," the OBBBA introduced several provisions that create meaningful complications for high-net-worth families.

Three in particular deserve attention.

Landmine #1: The 35-Cent Deduction Cap

The OBBBA introduced a new limitation on itemized deductions for taxpayers in the top bracket: a 35-cent ceiling on the deduction value of each dollar contributed. According to Britannica Money, this cap applies broadly — affecting charitable deductions, state and local tax (SALT) deductions, and potentially other itemized deductions.

For Bitcoin families, this has direct implications:

  • Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) that split between charity and family become less tax-efficient at the top bracket
  • Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs) funded with appreciated Bitcoin may provide less immediate tax benefit than expected
  • Charitable Lead Annuity Trusts (CLATs) that rely on the present value of the charitable deduction need recalculation under the new cap

The deduction cap doesn't eliminate these strategies — it changes their math. Families in the top bracket need to model the after-cap benefit rather than assuming the full 37% deduction rate applies. Savant Wealth Management's analysis of OBBBA's impact on high-income earners emphasizes that the interplay between the higher exemption and the new deduction cap creates a more complex planning landscape than either provision alone would suggest.

Landmine #2: Medicaid Work Requirements

This one is less obvious but potentially significant. The OBBBA introduced Medicaid work requirements that could affect family members involved in trust administration — particularly adult children who serve as trust protectors, distribution advisors, or who are actively involved in managing family Bitcoin custody infrastructure.

If a family caregiver's role in trust administration doesn't meet the new work requirement definitions, their Medicaid eligibility could be affected. For multigenerational planning where younger family members are being trained as future trustees, this creates a compliance overlay that didn't previously exist.

The practical advice: ensure that family members involved in trust governance have clearly documented roles that satisfy employment or equivalent activity requirements under the new OBBBA framework.

Landmine #3: State-Level Clawback Risk

Perhaps the most underreported risk: several states are exploring clawback mechanisms for unused federal exemption amounts. The theory is straightforward — if a resident uses only $5 million of their $15 million federal exemption, the unused $10 million represents "phantom" estate capacity that some states want to recapture through their own estate tax systems.

This is particularly relevant in the twelve states plus DC that impose their own estate taxes with exemptions far lower than the federal $15 million. Oregon and Massachusetts, for instance, have state estate tax exemptions of just $1 million.

Planning note: The state-level clawback risk is evolving. Some states may implement these provisions through new legislation in 2026-2027. Families domiciled in estate-tax states should monitor this closely and consider whether a trust in a no-estate-tax jurisdiction (Wyoming, South Dakota, Nevada) provides better long-term protection. Consult with a qualified estate planning attorney in your state of domicile.

GRATs Under OBBBA: Simplified or Obsolete?

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts have been the workhorse of estate planning for appreciating assets for decades. The basic mechanics: you transfer assets to a GRAT, retain an annuity payment stream for a term of years, and if the assets outperform the IRS Section 7520 rate, the excess passes to heirs transfer-tax-free.

GRATs were particularly popular during the TCJA uncertainty era because they allowed wealthy families to transfer appreciation without using any lifetime exemption — critical when nobody knew if the exemption would drop to $7 million.

The OBBBA changes this calculation significantly.

When GRATs Are No Longer Necessary

For a married couple with a combined estate under $30 million in Bitcoin: direct trust funding is now simpler and more efficient than GRAT mechanics.

Why? A GRAT requires:

  • Actuarial calculations based on the 7520 rate
  • Annual annuity payments back to the grantor (which re-enter the estate)
  • Mortality risk — if you die during the GRAT term, the assets snap back into the estate
  • Short-term rolling GRATs to manage mortality risk, adding complexity
  • No GST exemption allocation until the GRAT term ends

With a $30 million exemption, a couple can simply gift Bitcoin directly to an irrevocable dynasty trust and allocate GST exemption immediately. No annuity payments. No mortality risk. No rolling GRAT complexity. The trust owns the Bitcoin outright from day one, and every satoshi of appreciation is permanently outside both the estate and the generation-skipping transfer tax system.

When GRATs Still Make Sense

GRATs remain valuable in two scenarios:

  1. Estates significantly above $30 million — If a couple holds 1,000+ BTC, even the $30 million exemption doesn't cover the full position. GRATs can transfer the excess appreciation without additional gift tax.
  2. Income retention — The annuity payments from a GRAT provide the grantor with income during the term. For families who need cash flow from their Bitcoin position while still achieving transfer goals, the GRAT structure serves a dual purpose.

For most Bitcoin families in the $5-30 million range, the OBBBA made GRATs an unnecessary complication. Direct gifting to a dynasty trust, using the permanent $15M/$30M exemption, is the cleaner path.

Dynasty Trust Positioning: The Multi-Generational Bitcoin Compound Machine

If the OBBBA exemption is the fuel, the dynasty trust is the engine. And the right jurisdiction is the track.

A dynasty trust is an irrevocable trust designed to last for multiple generations — in some jurisdictions, perpetually. When funded with Bitcoin and structured correctly, it creates a compounding machine that removes wealth from the transfer tax system permanently.

The $30 Million Dynasty Trust Blueprint

Here's what optimal positioning looks like for a married couple under OBBBA:

  1. Establish the trust in a favorable jurisdiction. Wyoming, South Dakota, and Nevada are the top three for Bitcoin dynasty trusts. Wyoming is particularly strong because it:
    • Has no state income tax (trust income is not taxed at the state level)
    • Allows perpetual trusts — no rule against perpetuities
    • Has a robust directed trust statute
    • Recognizes the Private Family Trust Company (PFTC) structure
    • Has Bitcoin-favorable digital asset legislation
  2. Use a Wyoming Private Family Trust Company (PFTC) as the institutional trustee. A PFTC lets the family maintain investment control over the trust assets — including Bitcoin custody decisions — without causing estate tax inclusion. The family effectively runs its own trust company.
  3. Fund with ~420 BTC (couple) using the full $30 million exemption at today's prices. Allocate GST exemption to the entire transfer, making the trust exempt from generation-skipping transfer tax for every future generation.
  4. Structure custody with institutional-grade security. Multi-signature arrangements, geographic key distribution, and clear succession protocols for key holders across generations.
  5. Define distribution standards that allow discretionary distributions for health, education, maintenance, and support (HEMS) while keeping principal compounding inside the trust.

The compound math: 420 BTC inside a perpetual dynasty trust. No estate tax at generation one. No GST tax at generation two. No transfer tax at generation three, four, five, or ever. If Bitcoin reaches $1 million per coin — a scenario that a growing number of institutional models project within 15-25 years — that trust holds $420 million. Tax-free across every generational transfer. The OBBBA made this structure fully funded at today's prices for the first time.

A dynasty trust that will hold Bitcoin across generations needs governance flexibility. Best practices under OBBBA:

  • Appoint a Trust Protector with power to modify administrative provisions (but not beneficial interests) as tax law, technology, and family circumstances evolve

Your 5-Step Action Plan Under OBBBA

Theory is useful. Action is what captures the window. Here are the five concrete steps for Bitcoin families operating under the OBBBA framework in 2026.

Step 1: Get a Qualified Bitcoin Valuation

Gift tax reporting requires a defensible valuation of the Bitcoin transferred. For publicly traded assets, the IRS accepts the mean between the high and low trading prices on the date of the gift. Bitcoin trades 24/7, so the valuation methodology needs to be documented clearly.

Engage a qualified appraiser or establish a written valuation policy that specifies:

  • Which exchange(s) provide the reference price
  • The time window for determining the daily high/low
  • Treatment of any block discount for large transfers (typically minimal for Bitcoin given market liquidity)

The valuation date matters: you want to gift on a date when BTC is at or near a local low. Monitor prices and work with your attorney to execute the transfer on the optimal date.

Step 2: Establish the Trust Structure

If you don't already have an irrevocable dynasty trust in a favorable jurisdiction, this is step one. The key decisions:

  • Jurisdiction: Wyoming, South Dakota, or Nevada for perpetual trusts with no state income tax
  • Trust type: Irrevocable grantor trust (for income tax purposes during your lifetime) with dynasty provisions
  • Trustee: Independent trustee for tax purposes, with PFTC if family control is desired
  • GST allocation: Allocate GST exemption to the transfer on Form 709
  • Trust protector: Independent party with power to modify administrative (not beneficial) provisions

Allow 4-8 weeks for trust drafting and establishment. If you're targeting a Q2 2026 transfer — while BTC remains in this price range — start the legal work now.

Step 3: Fund While BTC Is Below ATH

The single most important tactical decision: transfer Bitcoin to the trust while prices are depressed.

At $71,500, you move 210 BTC per $15M of exemption. At $126,000, you move 119 BTC. The 91-coin difference per individual (182 per couple) is worth $18.2 million at $100K BTC, $36.4 million at $200K BTC. This is not theoretical — it's the mathematical consequence of gifting a volatile asset at a low versus a high.

Execute the transfer:

  • Move Bitcoin to trust-owned wallet addresses (multi-sig recommended)
  • Document the transfer with blockchain transaction records
  • File Form 709 (Gift Tax Return) reporting the transfer and allocating exemption
  • Retain copies of all valuation documentation

Step 4: Review State Estate Tax Exposure

The OBBBA raised the federal exemption. It did nothing for state estate taxes. If you're domiciled in one of the twelve states (plus DC) that impose their own estate tax, you may have state-level exposure even with a $15 million federal exemption.

States with estate taxes and their exemptions (2026):

  • Oregon: $1 million
  • Massachusetts: $1 million
  • Connecticut: $13.61 million (roughly matches old federal)
  • New York: $7.16 million (with the "cliff" — estates exceeding 105% of the exemption get no exemption at all)
  • Washington: $2.193 million
  • Minnesota, Illinois, Maryland, Vermont, Maine, Hawaii, Rhode Island, DC: varying thresholds

With the new state-level clawback risk for unused federal exemption, a situs trust in a no-estate-tax state may provide better protection than domicile-based planning alone.

Step 5: Coordinate on OBBBA Deduction Caps

Before year-end planning, sit down with your tax advisor to model the impact of the new 35-cent deduction cap on your overall strategy. If you've been using charitable strategies (CRTs, CLATs, DAFs) as part of your estate plan, the after-cap benefit may differ significantly from pre-OBBBA projections.

Key questions for your advisor:

  • Does the deduction cap affect the optimal split between charitable and dynasty trust funding?
  • Should charitable strategies shift from income-tax-driven to estate-tax-driven under the new cap?
  • Are there timing strategies (bunching charitable contributions into lower-income years) that mitigate the cap's impact?

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the estate tax exemption under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act?

The OBBBA permanently set the federal estate and gift tax exemption at $15 million per individual ($30 million per married couple using portability), indexed for inflation. This was signed into law on July 4, 2025, replacing the TCJA provision that was scheduled to sunset to approximately $7 million.

How much Bitcoin can I transfer to a trust tax-free under OBBBA?

At today's price of approximately $71,500 per BTC, a single individual can gift roughly 210 BTC using the full $15 million exemption. A married couple can transfer approximately 420 BTC — about $30 million — into an irrevocable dynasty trust with zero federal gift or estate tax. All future appreciation occurs inside the trust, permanently outside the taxable estate.

Did OBBBA introduce any downsides for wealthy families?

Yes. The $15 million exemption headline is accompanied by a new 35-cent deduction cap for the top income bracket (affecting charitable and other deductions), Medicaid work requirements that may affect family caregivers involved in trust administration, and potential state-level clawback of unused federal exemption amounts. These require careful planning with qualified advisors.

Is the $15 million exemption truly permanent?

The OBBBA made it permanent — no sunset date, unlike the original TCJA provision. However, future Congresses can always modify tax law. The critical protection: IRS anti-clawback regulations ensure that gifts already made under the current exemption are protected even if a future Congress reduces the amount. This creates a strong argument for acting now while the full exemption is available.

Why does Bitcoin's current price matter for estate planning?

Bitcoin at $71,500 is roughly 44% below its all-time high of ~$126,000. This means more BTC fits under the $15 million exemption at today's price. At $71,500, you move 210 BTC per $15M; at $126,000, you move only 119 BTC. The 91-coin difference per individual (182 per couple) represents millions in potential tax-free appreciation if Bitcoin recovers.

Do I still need a GRAT after OBBBA?

For estates under $30 million (couple), GRATs are likely unnecessary. Direct gifting to a dynasty trust using the $15M/$30M exemption is simpler and avoids GRAT complexity — no annuity payments, no mortality risk, immediate GST allocation. GRATs remain useful for estates significantly above $30 million or where the grantor needs retained income.

What's the best trust jurisdiction for Bitcoin under OBBBA?

Wyoming, South Dakota, and Nevada are the top three. Wyoming stands out for Bitcoin-specific reasons: no state income tax, perpetual trust duration, directed trust statutes, and a Private Family Trust Company (PFTC) framework that lets families maintain investment control over Bitcoin custody without estate tax inclusion.

Can future legislation reduce the $15 million exemption?

Technically, yes — a future Congress can change any tax law. But the anti-clawback protection is powerful: gifts made under the current $15 million exemption are not retroactively taxed even if the exemption is later reduced. This is one of the strongest arguments for funding trusts now rather than waiting.
