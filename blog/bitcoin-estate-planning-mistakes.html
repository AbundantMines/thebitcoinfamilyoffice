The 12 Most Dangerous Bitcoin Estate Planning Mistakes (And How to Fix Each One)

Most Bitcoin holders have done zero estate planning. Those who have often made one of these twelve mistakes — any of which can leave their heirs locked out forever.

By Hal Turner · February 25, 2026 · ~2,500 words · ⬡ Bitcoin Estate Planning
What You'll Learn

Bitcoin estate planning mistakes are not just legal oversights — they are technical failures with permanent consequences. Unlike a forgotten bank account that can be recovered with enough paperwork, Bitcoin locked out of an inaccessible wallet is gone forever. There is no customer service number. There is no probate court process that generates a private key.

These 12 mistakes cover both the operational failures (no access pathway for heirs) and the financial ones (unexpected estate tax exposure). Each comes with a concrete fix. Address even one of these today and you significantly improve your family's outcome.

Mistake 01
No Estate Plan at All

This is by far the most common bitcoin estate planning mistake — the "I'll do it later" problem. The uncomfortable reality is that most Bitcoin holders, even those with significant positions, have done zero estate planning specific to their Bitcoin. They have not documented where their Bitcoin is held, how to access it, or who should receive it. They assume the process is complicated and defer it indefinitely.

The consequence is brutal. If you die without a plan, your Bitcoin may be permanently inaccessible. Not just legally in dispute — cryptographically inaccessible. If your heirs don't know you held Bitcoin, don't know which wallet or exchange it's on, and don't have the credentials to access it, those coins are effectively lost. Chainalysis estimates that between 17% and 23% of all Bitcoin in existence is already permanently inaccessible — much of it from exactly this scenario. Every day you delay is a day your heirs' risk grows.

✓ The Fix

Start today, even if imperfectly. Write down where your Bitcoin is held — every exchange, every wallet, every custody arrangement — and store that document securely with instructions for your executor. Then build toward a complete estate plan with a Bitcoin-competent attorney. A formal plan takes time; an interim Letter of Instruction takes an afternoon and can be the difference between access and permanent loss.

Mistake 02
Putting Seed Phrases in Your Will

This is the single most dangerous document error in Bitcoin estate planning, and it is more common than it should be — sometimes even suggested by well-meaning but Bitcoin-illiterate attorneys. The logic seems sound: put the critical information in a legal document that your heirs will receive. But it is catastrophically flawed for one reason: wills are public record upon probate.

When a will enters probate, it becomes a public court document that anyone can access. A 24-word seed phrase in a probated will is a public announcement to every bad actor with access to court records that a specific Bitcoin wallet exists, that it was not already emptied, and here is the key to take it. The Bitcoin can be swept within minutes of the document becoming accessible — before your heirs ever receive it. This is not a theoretical risk; Bitcoin inheritance theft is a documented and growing threat vector.

✓ The Fix

Your will should reference your Bitcoin and direct it to beneficiaries — but it should never contain access credentials. Seed phrases, private keys, and hardware wallet PINs belong in a separate, securely stored Letter of Instruction that is not part of the public probate record. That document should be held by a trusted custodian, a Bitcoin-native attorney, or split across secure storage locations using a protocol your executor understands. Your will points to the plan; it is not the plan itself.

Mistake 03
No Letter of Instruction

A will gives your executor legal authority. A trust gives your trustee legal control over assets. Neither document tells them how to actually access your Bitcoin. Legal authority and operational access are entirely different things — and in Bitcoin, the gap between them can make your estate plan functionally useless. Your executor can have the most beautifully drafted legal documents in existence and still be completely unable to move a single satoshi if they don't know where to look or how to access it.

The Letter of Instruction is the operational document: where the Bitcoin is held, which hardware wallet or exchange, how to initiate access, who to contact, what credentials are needed. Without it, your executor must reverse-engineer your entire custody setup under emotional duress, often without the technical knowledge to do so. Critical information is regularly discovered too late — or not at all.

✓ The Fix

Create a comprehensive Bitcoin Letter of Instruction that documents every custody arrangement, every wallet, every exchange account, and the specific steps your executor needs to take. Keep it current and store it securely outside of your will and any public-record documents. Mistake 04
Storing Bitcoin on an Exchange Without Succession Provisions

Exchange-held Bitcoin is often easier to accumulate and easier to manage during your lifetime. But exchanges are built for individual account holders, not for succession. When an account holder dies, most exchanges require a probate process that can take months to years, requires original documentation your executor may struggle to obtain, and is not designed to accommodate heirs efficiently. Many heirs cannot even verify the account exists without the original email address or phone number tied to the account — both of which may be inaccessible.

The operational reality is grim: your executor contacts the exchange's support team, is routed through multiple departments over weeks, is asked for documentation that may conflict with your state's probate process, and may ultimately receive a partial or denied access decision. Meanwhile, the Bitcoin is frozen and may be earning nothing. In some cases, if the process stalls long enough, exchanges have sunset accounts or moved assets to unclaimed property divisions in ways that complicate heir access further.

✓ The Fix

For significant Bitcoin positions, self-custody with documented access protocols is far more estate-friendly than exchange custody. If you maintain exchange holdings, document the account, the email address tied to it, the KYC documentation used to open it, and the recovery process specific to that exchange in your Letter of Instruction. Verify the exchange's death-of-account-holder policy and document the exact steps required — before you need to. Do not assume your executor can figure it out.

Mistake 05
Naming a Bitcoin-Illiterate Trustee or Executor

Most estate attorneys don't know what a seed phrase is. Most bank trust departments have never handled hardware wallet custody. Most family members named as executors — even highly intelligent, responsible people — have no idea what to do when they're handed a Ledger device and told it contains your Bitcoin. Legal authority over a Bitcoin estate and the technical ability to execute that authority are completely different competencies, and the gap between them can permanently strand your estate.

A Bitcoin-illiterate executor faces specific risks: they may accidentally wipe a hardware wallet during the wrong sequence of actions; they may be vulnerable to social engineering attacks that result in being scammed out of the Bitcoin; they may fail to act quickly enough on time-sensitive decisions (like moving Bitcoin off an exchange that is closing or in financial distress); or they may simply be unable to verify what they have access to, leaving part of the estate permanently undiscovered.

✓ The Fix

Name at least one executor or co-trustee with genuine Bitcoin technical competency, or engage a qualified Bitcoin custodian with fiduciary experience as part of your estate plan. If your preferred executor is someone you trust personally but lacks Bitcoin knowledge, supplement them with a technical advisor role in your estate documents — someone who provides technical guidance while the executor maintains legal authority. And prepare them in advance: the worst time for your executor to learn how Bitcoin works is after you're gone.

Mistake 06
Not Updating the Estate Plan After Custody Changes

Your estate plan — especially your Letter of Instruction — is only as good as its last update. Bitcoin custody arrangements change constantly: you move from an exchange to self-custody; you set up a multisig arrangement; you change hardware wallets; you open a new exchange account or close an old one; you migrate to a different custody structure after a price appreciation event. Every one of these changes creates a gap between your documented plan and reality.

An outdated Letter of Instruction can be worse than no Letter at all — it creates false confidence in an executor who follows the documented steps faithfully and arrives at wallet addresses or exchange accounts that no longer hold any Bitcoin. Meanwhile, the actual custody arrangement — the one with the real assets — may be entirely undocumented. This scenario plays out regularly in Bitcoin estates.

✓ The Fix

Establish a formal review cadence — at minimum once per year, and immediately after any custody change. Every time you move Bitcoin, open a new wallet, change a multisig configuration, or modify any custody arrangement, update your Letter of Instruction the same day. Treat it as a living document with version control, not a one-time task. Calendar a recurring annual estate plan review every January — treat it the same way you treat reviewing your homeowners insurance.

Mistake 07
Underestimating Estate Tax Exposure

Most Bitcoin holders dramatically underestimate their estate tax exposure because they think of themselves as "not wealthy enough" to worry about estate taxes. The math tells a different story. At approximately $95,000 per Bitcoin, a 15 BTC position is worth approximately $1.4 million — and that's before accounting for a home, retirement accounts, life insurance death benefits, or any other assets. Many Bitcoin holders who accumulated during the 2020 or 2024 cycles are well above the federal estate tax exemption threshold without fully realizing it.

The federal estate tax applies to estates above the applicable exemption threshold (verify the current amount with your estate attorney, as this figure is subject to legislative change). Amounts above that threshold face a 40% marginal estate tax rate. If your combined estate — Bitcoin plus all other assets — exceeds that exemption, your heirs could owe significant tax on assets that have already been taxed during your lifetime. Bitcoin's historical appreciation pattern means this exposure can change dramatically year over year, making annual review essential.

✓ The Fix

Calculate your total estate value — not just your Bitcoin — and compare it to the current federal exemption and your state's applicable exemption. If you are approaching or above either threshold, begin exploring estate tax reduction strategies immediately: irrevocable trusts, GRATs, CRTs, charitable strategies, and annual gifting programs. Don't guess at your exposure — model it precisely.

Mistake 08
Not Using an Irrevocable Trust When Above the Exemption

Many Bitcoin holders who have done some estate planning have placed their Bitcoin in a revocable living trust. That's a reasonable first step for avoiding probate and maintaining control during your lifetime — but it does nothing to reduce estate taxes. Assets in a revocable trust are still part of your taxable estate because you retain control over them. If your estate is above the applicable exemption threshold, a revocable trust is the wrong tool for the estate tax problem.

Every year your Bitcoin appreciates inside a revocable trust, the taxable estate value grows — and the eventual estate tax liability grows with it. Bitcoin's historical appreciation trajectory means this is not an academic concern. A $1.5 million Bitcoin position today could be a $4 million position in five years. If it stays in a revocable trust throughout, the entire appreciation accrues inside the taxable estate. Irrevocable structures — properly executed with a Bitcoin-competent attorney — can move appreciation outside the estate, reducing the eventual tax exposure significantly.

✓ The Fix

If your estate is above or approaching the exemption threshold, work with a Bitcoin-competent estate attorney to evaluate irrevocable trust structures appropriate for your situation. Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusts (ILITs), Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs), Bitcoin GRATs, and Charitable Remainder Trusts are among the structures that can remove appreciation from the taxable estate. Each has different tradeoffs around access, control, and reversibility — understanding those tradeoffs requires expert guidance.

Mistake 09
Living in a High-Tax State and Ignoring State Estate Tax

Twelve U.S. states and the District of Columbia impose their own estate tax — often with exemptions far lower than the federal threshold. Oregon taxes estates above $1 million. Massachusetts taxes above $2 million. Washington State taxes above approximately $2.193 million. If you live in one of these states, your Bitcoin position alone may put you well above the state exemption even if you're comfortably below the federal exemption — and you owe state estate tax whether or not you owe federal tax.

State estate tax rates typically range from 10% to 20%, and unlike the federal threshold, state exemptions are rarely indexed to inflation. A holder with 25 BTC at current prices has approximately $2.4 million in Bitcoin alone — which exceeds the exemption in Massachusetts and is approaching the threshold in Washington State. Add a home, retirement accounts, and life insurance, and many Bitcoin holders in these states are significantly exposed without realizing it.

✓ The Fix

Know your state's estate tax exemption and rate schedule — and verify it annually, as state laws change. If you live in a state with a low exemption, your planning strategy must account for both the federal and state levels, which are calculated separately and require different strategies. In some cases, domicile planning (establishing legal residence in a non-estate-tax state) is worth evaluating for families with large Bitcoin positions. Consult a Bitcoin-competent estate attorney who is licensed in your state and familiar with your state's specific rules.

Mistake 10
No Heir Education

The worst time for a family member to learn how Bitcoin works is at inheritance. When your spouse or adult child receives custody of your hardware wallet and has no prior experience with Bitcoin, they face a perfect storm: they're grieving, they're under time pressure, they may have scammers already targeting them (Bitcoin inheritance scams are a documented and growing threat), and they have no baseline competency to evaluate whether the help they're receiving is legitimate.

Unprepared heirs are the number one target for Bitcoin inheritance scams. These scams are sophisticated: bad actors monitor obituaries, probate filings, and social media for indications of Bitcoin wealth at estate. They contact heirs posing as Bitcoin recovery specialists, estate technology advisors, or even attorneys. A grieving family member with no Bitcoin education is extraordinarily vulnerable. The result can be a complete and permanent loss of the estate's Bitcoin — not to the IRS, but to criminals.

✓ The Fix

Start your heirs' Bitcoin education before they need it. This doesn't mean turning your family into Bitcoin experts — it means ensuring they understand what Bitcoin is, what self-custody means, what a seed phrase is and why it must never be shared, and who legitimate contacts are in the event of inheritance. A dedicated family conversation and a curated reading list can dramatically reduce their vulnerability to scams and ensure they know the first steps to take.

Mistake 11
Failing to Account for Incapacity (Not Just Death)

Most Bitcoin estate planning focuses exclusively on what happens after death — and entirely ignores the scenario of incapacity during your lifetime. A stroke, a serious accident, dementia, or any other condition that leaves you alive but unable to manage your own affairs creates an immediate Bitcoin access crisis. Your Bitcoin is locked inside a wallet that only you can access, and there is no mechanism for your spouse or family to use it, pay bills with it, or manage it on your behalf.

Standard durable power of attorney documents often fail for Bitcoin because they do not specifically authorize the agent to access digital assets, do not include the technical information needed to actually access the wallet, and may conflict with the RUFADAA framework in ways that leave the agent's authority unclear. Your attorney may have drafted a perfectly valid POA that still leaves your Bitcoin inaccessible during your incapacity — because neither the attorney nor you thought to address Bitcoin specifically.

✓ The Fix

Ensure your durable power of attorney explicitly authorizes your agent to access and manage digital assets and cryptocurrency holdings. Verify your state's implementation of RUFADAA and confirm your POA complies with its provisions. Your Letter of Instruction should address incapacity scenarios specifically — including who has authority, what they have access to, and what the protocols are for managing your Bitcoin during a period of incapacity. Don't leave this until you're planning only for death.

Mistake 12
Treating the Estate Plan as a One-Time Event

A Bitcoin estate plan drafted once and filed away is not an estate plan — it is a time capsule that grows increasingly obsolete with each passing year. Bitcoin's price appreciation alone changes your estate tax exposure dramatically year over year. A position that was comfortably below the federal exemption at $40,000 per Bitcoin may be well above it at $95,000 per Bitcoin. A holder who was in no hurry to plan at $30,000 Bitcoin may have a genuine estate tax emergency at $150,000 Bitcoin — and may not realize it.

Beyond price appreciation, estate laws change. Trust and estate rules are modified by Congress, by state legislatures, and by IRS guidance. The post-TCJA estate tax landscape has been evolving since 2017 and will continue to evolve. Custody arrangements change — new wallets, new exchanges, new multisig configurations. Each change creates a potential gap between your documented plan and your actual estate. An estate plan reviewed once is almost certainly out of date.

✓ The Fix

Build a formal annual review into your financial calendar — every year in January or whenever you complete your tax filing. That review should include: your total estate value vs. current exemptions, your custody documentation vs. your actual custody arrangements, any applicable changes in state or federal law, and any life changes (marriage, divorce, new children or grandchildren, deaths in the family) that affect your plan. Set a calendar reminder right now. The review takes less than a day; the consequences of skipping it can last generations.

The Common Thread: Bitcoin Demands Specialized Planning

These twelve mistakes share a common thread: they all stem from treating Bitcoin like a traditional asset in an estate plan designed for traditional assets. Bitcoin is not a bank account. It is not a brokerage. It is not real estate. It is a cryptographic bearer instrument that requires both legal authority and technical access to transfer — and the standard tools of estate planning address only one of those two requirements.

The good news is that these are all solvable problems. None of them require exotic structures or extraordinary expense to begin addressing. What they require is intentionality: a conscious decision to treat Bitcoin estate planning as a distinct discipline with its own requirements, its own documents, and its own ongoing maintenance cadence.

Estate taxes are one side of the Bitcoin wealth equation. Income taxes — on gains, on mining income, on trust distributions — are the other.
