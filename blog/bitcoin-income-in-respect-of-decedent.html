Bitcoin IRD: Why Retirement Account Bitcoin Doesn't Get the Step-Up — and What to Do About It

By The Bitcoin Family Office  |  Updated March 2026  |  Estate Planning  •  Tax Strategy

Every Bitcoin holder learns about the step-up in basis: at death, inherited Bitcoin resets to fair market value, wiping out decades of capital gains. What far fewer understand is that this rule has a critical exception — one that affects millions of Bitcoin holders who bought through an IRA, 401(k), or other qualified retirement account. Bitcoin inside a retirement account does not get the step-up. It is "income in respect of a decedent" — and heirs will pay ordinary income tax on every dollar they withdraw, regardless of what the original cost was.

If you bought Bitcoin at $500 in a traditional IRA and it's now worth $200,000, your heirs don't inherit $200,000 tax-free. They inherit a future ordinary income tax liability of $60,000–$74,000 (at 30–37% rates) that must be paid as they take distributions. The step-up in basis — the crown jewel of Bitcoin estate planning — does not apply here.

Understanding IRD, the §691(c) deduction that partially offsets it, and the strategies to minimize it before death is one of the most important and least-discussed topics in Bitcoin estate planning.

What Is Income in Respect of a Decedent?

Income in respect of a decedent (IRD) is income that a deceased person had earned or was entitled to but had not yet received for income tax purposes at the time of death. The distinguishing feature of IRD: it does not receive a step-up in basis under §1014. Instead, when the beneficiary receives or collects the income, they pay income tax on it — just as the decedent would have.

The most common categories of IRD are:

  • Distributions from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and other pre-tax retirement accounts — the entire balance is IRD because the decedent never paid income tax on the contributions or earnings
  • Unpaid wages, salary, bonuses, or commissions earned before death but paid after
  • Installment sale receivables — the gain portion of installment payments received by the estate or heirs after the seller's death
  • Accrued interest on savings bonds, CDs, or other instruments at date of death
  • S-corporation or partnership income allocable to the period before death but paid after

For Bitcoin holders, the primary IRD concern is the retirement account. Bitcoin held in a traditional IRA or 401(k) — either directly (via a self-directed IRA) or through a Bitcoin ETF — carries a full IRD liability at death.

⚠️ The step-up rule has an explicit exception for IRD. IRC §1014(c) states that the step-up in basis does not apply to "property which constitutes a right to receive an item of income in respect of a decedent." Retirement account assets are the paradigm case. No matter how much your Bitcoin appreciates inside an IRA, your heirs start with a cost basis of zero and pay ordinary income tax on every dollar they take out.

The §691(c) IRD Deduction: A Partial Offset

Congress recognized the potential for double taxation when IRD assets are also subject to estate tax. A $1M IRA balance already included in the decedent's gross estate is taxed once at 40% estate tax, then taxed again at up to 37% income tax when the heir withdraws it. Without relief, the combined tax burden would be catastrophic.

IRC §691(c) provides a partial solution: the heir who receives IRD can deduct, as an income tax deduction, the estate tax attributable to the IRD item. This reduces the income tax on the inherited retirement account by a portion of the estate tax already paid on the same assets.

How the §691(c) Deduction Works

  1. Calculate the "net value of IRD" in the estate: total IRD items minus any deductions directly connected to them (e.g., income tax deductions that would have been available to the decedent).
  2. Calculate the "excess estate tax": the additional estate tax generated by the IRD items — the difference between actual estate tax and what estate tax would have been if the IRD items had zero value.
  3. Each time an IRD item is collected, the heir deducts a pro-rata portion of the excess estate tax as a miscellaneous itemized deduction (not subject to the 2%-of-AGI floor under current law).

Example: A $2M traditional IRA (all IRD) is included in a $20M estate paying $2M in estate tax. The incremental estate tax attributable to the IRA (using the formula): approximately $400,000. As the heir withdraws the $2M over 10 years, they can deduct a pro-rata portion of $400,000 — effectively reducing the income tax rate on each withdrawal by about 20 cents per dollar. The heir still pays income tax, but on a reduced effective amount. This is not a full offset — it reduces, but does not eliminate, the double taxation.

The §691(c) deduction only helps when the estate also pays estate tax. For estates under the $15M exemption (2026), there is no estate tax — and therefore no §691(c) deduction available. The heir simply pays full income tax on all IRD distributions with no offset. This is the situation for the vast majority of Bitcoin IRA holders whose estates are under the exemption: they get no §691(c) relief, and the full IRD tax burden falls on the heir.

The 10-Year Rule Makes It Worse

Before the SECURE Act (2019), non-spouse beneficiaries who inherited an IRA could "stretch" distributions over their own lifetime — spreading the IRD tax across decades at low annual amounts. That option is largely gone.

Under the SECURE Act and SECURE 2.0 Act, most non-spouse beneficiaries of inherited IRAs must take all distributions within 10 years of the original owner's death. For beneficiaries who are not "eligible designated beneficiaries" (minor children, disabled beneficiaries, chronically ill beneficiaries, and beneficiaries not more than 10 years younger than the decedent), the 10-year rule is mandatory.

The practical effect for Bitcoin IRA inheritance: a $1M IRA distributed over 10 years at $100,000/year. At a 32% marginal rate, that's $32,000 in tax every year for 10 years — $320,000 total. If the Bitcoin inside the IRA continues to appreciate during the distribution period, the withdrawals become even larger (and the taxes even higher) in later years.

Combine the 10-year forced distribution rule with the absence of step-up basis, and a Bitcoin IRA can generate an extraordinary tax burden for heirs — all while directly held Bitcoin passes to the same heirs completely free of income tax.

Planning to Minimize IRA Bitcoin IRD

Strategy 1: Roth Conversion During Lifetime

The most powerful solution: convert the traditional IRA (with its IRD liability) to a Roth IRA during the original owner's lifetime. Pay income tax now; heirs receive tax-free distributions forever. The conversion is especially valuable when:

  • Bitcoin in the IRA has dropped significantly from its high — converting at a lower value reduces the tax cost
  • The account owner has a lower-income year (retirement, business loss, large deductions)
  • The account owner has many years until required minimum distributions begin (maximizes the tax-free compounding runway)
  • The heir's expected tax rate is higher than the owner's current rate — paying tax now at a lower rate beats the heir paying later at a higher rate

Strategy 2: Charitable Remainder Trust as IRA Beneficiary

A Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) named as IRA beneficiary is one of the most effective strategies for eliminating IRD tax on a large inherited IRA. Here's why:

  • The CRT receives the IRA distribution tax-free (the CRT is tax-exempt under §501(c)(3) as a split-interest charitable trust)
  • The CRT distributes an income stream to family beneficiaries over the trust term (typically 15–20 years)
  • Family beneficiaries pay income tax on distributions as they receive them — spreading the tax over the CRT term at potentially lower annual brackets
  • At the CRT's termination, the remainder passes to charity
  • The estate receives a charitable estate tax deduction for the present value of the remainder interest

The CRT does not eliminate IRD entirely — the family beneficiaries still pay income tax on distributions, which carry the IRD character from the IRA. But it dramatically extends the distribution period beyond the 10-year mandatory rule, reduces the annual IRD burden, and converts some of the tax liability into a charitable deduction.

Strategy 3: Asset Location — Keep Bitcoin Out of the Traditional IRA

The most direct solution is prevention: don't put Bitcoin in a traditional IRA in the first place, or move it out before it appreciates further.

The optimal asset location for Bitcoin is:

  1. Direct personal holding — full step-up at death, long-term capital gains rates during lifetime
  2. Roth IRA (self-directed or via conversion) — no step-up needed (distributions are tax-free); qualified distributions to heirs are tax-free forever
  3. Irrevocable trust (dynasty, IDGT, SLAT) — no step-up for the trust's assets (the trust is a separate taxpayer), but future appreciation escapes estate tax; grantor trust structure allows income tax to be paid by the grantor, effectively gifting additional assets tax-free
  4. Traditional IRA or 401(k) — worst location for Bitcoin from an estate planning perspective; avoid for assets with large appreciation potential

The asset location rule for Bitcoin: Hold high-appreciation assets (Bitcoin) in accounts that provide the best after-tax outcome at death. That means direct holding (for the step-up) or Roth accounts (for tax-free distributions). Traditional pre-tax retirement accounts are ideal for assets with lower appreciation potential — bonds, stable value funds, dividend-paying equities — where the ordinary income tax treatment at distribution is less punishing because less appreciation has accumulated.

Strategy 4: Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs) from the IRA

If you are age 70½ or older, you can make a Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) directly from your IRA to a qualified charity — up to $105,000 per year (2026, indexed). QCDs satisfy your required minimum distribution obligation and are excluded from income entirely. They carry no IRD character when they pass to the charity.

For Bitcoin IRA holders with charitable intent, QCDs are an elegant way to drain the IRD-tainted traditional IRA over time, reducing the future tax burden for heirs. The Bitcoin inside the IRA is sold to fund the QCD cash distribution — the capital gain inside the IRA is never taxed (it's subsumed in the QCD exclusion), and the charity receives unrestricted funds.

Strategy 5: Name the Right Beneficiary

Even if you cannot convert the IRA, the choice of beneficiary affects how badly the 10-year rule and ordinary income tax applies:

  • Surviving spouse: Can roll inherited IRA into their own IRA; subject only to their own lifetime RMD schedule; can delay distributions for decades. Best outcome for a non-Roth IRA among all beneficiary choices.
  • Minor children: "Eligible designated beneficiary" — can stretch distributions until they reach age 21, then the 10-year clock starts. Better than naming adult children directly, if child is young.
  • Disabled or chronically ill beneficiary: Eligible designated beneficiary — can stretch over their lifetime. If you have a family member who qualifies, naming them may significantly extend the distribution period and reduce annual IRD burden.
  • Adult children: 10-year rule applies. Distributions must clear the account within 10 years of the parent's death.
  • Charity (outright): Pays no income tax on IRA distributions. Zero IRD burden. The most tax-efficient beneficiary for a traditional IRA — but the family gets nothing.
  • CRT (split-interest): Hybrid — family gets income stream; charity gets remainder; see Strategy 2 above.

Self-Directed IRA Bitcoin: A Special Case

Some Bitcoin holders have used self-directed IRAs (SDIRAs) to hold direct Bitcoin — not ETFs, but actual BTC with private keys managed by the SDIRA custodian or (in some structures) the account holder via checkbook control LLC.

The IRD rules apply identically whether the IRA holds Bitcoin ETFs, direct Bitcoin, or any other asset. The IRA wrapper is what matters for IRD — not what's inside it. A self-directed IRA holding $2M in direct Bitcoin passes to heirs with the same full IRD tax exposure as a conventional IRA holding the same value in a Bitcoin ETF.

SDIRA structures add one additional complication: at the owner's death, the SDIRA custodian must transfer the actual Bitcoin to the beneficiary's IRA account (or distribute it). This requires the custodian to handle the key management transition — a process that can take weeks or months and may require beneficiary-initiated probate or estate administration steps. Direct Bitcoin in an SDIRA is harder to inherit than Bitcoin ETF shares in a conventional IRA custodian.

Roth IRA and Roth 401(k): The Clean Solution

Roth accounts do not generate IRD. Qualified distributions from a Roth IRA or Roth 401(k) are excluded from the beneficiary's gross income entirely — not just capital-gains-rated, but completely tax-free. The basis rules for Roth accounts are different: contributions are after-tax, and qualified distributions (after 5 years and age 59½, or at death) are tax-free regardless of growth.

The 10-year rule still applies to inherited Roth IRAs for non-spouse beneficiaries — but the distributions within those 10 years are tax-free. The time pressure remains; the tax cost does not.

For Bitcoin specifically, the Roth IRA is the superior retirement account structure in almost every estate planning scenario:

  • No RMDs during the owner's lifetime — Bitcoin can compound indefinitely without forced liquidation
  • Tax-free distributions to heirs, even if Bitcoin reaches $1M per coin
  • No step-up needed — the Roth structure already eliminates income tax on the appreciation
  • No IRD burden — heirs take distributions tax-free, even over a 10-year accelerated schedule

Mining Income Creates the Perfect Roth Conversion Window

Bitcoin mining generates depreciation deductions that can offset taxable income in high-expenditure years — creating lower-bracket windows ideal for Roth conversions of IRA Bitcoin. The two strategies work together to eliminate IRD exposure over time.

Key Takeaways

  • Bitcoin held in a traditional IRA, 401(k), or other pre-tax retirement account is "income in respect of a decedent" (IRD) — it does not receive a step-up in basis at death under §1014(c).
  • Heirs pay ordinary income tax (up to 37%) on all distributions from an inherited traditional IRA — even if the Bitcoin's original cost was $500 and its current value is $200,000.
  • The SECURE Act 10-year mandatory distribution rule compresses the IRD burden: most non-spouse heirs must drain the entire inherited IRA within 10 years, potentially generating large annual income tax bills.
  • The §691(c) deduction provides a partial offset for estates that also pay estate tax — but provides no relief for the majority of estates under the $15M exemption.
  • The primary solutions are: Roth conversion during lifetime (especially in low-income or Bitcoin dip years), CRT as IRA beneficiary (for families with charitable intent), and asset location (keep Bitcoin in direct holding or Roth accounts; use traditional IRAs for lower-appreciation assets).
  • A Roth IRA holding Bitcoin is equally tax-efficient as directly held Bitcoin at death — zero income tax for heirs — while also eliminating RMDs during the owner's lifetime.
