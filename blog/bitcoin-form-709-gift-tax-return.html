Bitcoin Gift Tax Return: Form 709 Complete Guide for Bitcoin Holders

Every Bitcoin gift above the annual exclusion, every GRAT funded, every seed gift to an IDGT — all require Form 709. Most Bitcoin holders don't know when to file, how to value Bitcoin on the gift date, or what happens when they don't. Here's the complete filing framework.

📅 March 14, 2026 ⏱ 14 min read 🏷 Form 709 · Gift Tax · Annual Exclusion · GST

Bitcoin holders who actively transfer wealth to family members, fund trusts, or execute estate planning strategies will encounter Form 709 — the United States Gift (and Generation-Skipping Transfer) Tax Return — repeatedly throughout their planning lives. Yet it is among the least understood compliance obligations in the estate planning toolkit.

Many Bitcoin holders skip it entirely, not realizing that a Form 709 is required even when no gift tax is owed. Others file it incorrectly, misvaluing Bitcoin on the gift date or failing to report trust transactions that technically constitute taxable gifts. The consequences of non-filing compound silently: the statute of limitations never runs on unreported gifts, and the IRS can revisit decades-old transfers during an estate tax audit.

This guide covers everything a Bitcoin holder needs to know about Form 709: when to file, how to value Bitcoin, what planning transactions require reporting, gift splitting election mechanics, GST exemption allocation, and the most common filing mistakes.

1. When Form 709 Is Required

Form 709 is required when a US citizen or resident makes a taxable gift during the calendar year. For Bitcoin holders, the filing triggers:

Transaction Form 709 Required? Notes
Bitcoin gift to any person ≤ $19,000 (2025 annual exclusion) No — unless gift splitting elected Annual exclusion per recipient; no 709 needed if under limit
Bitcoin gift to any person > $19,000 Yes Reports taxable gift; reduces lifetime exemption
Funding an irrevocable trust (IDGT, SLAT, dynasty trust) Yes Completed gift; taxable if > annual exclusion
Funding a GRAT Yes Reports remainder gift (often $0 for zeroed-out GRAT)
Installment sale to IDGT (note = adequate consideration) No — for the sale itself Sale is not a gift; seed gift reportable if > annual exclusion
Gift to US citizen spouse No (unlimited marital deduction) No limit; no 709 unless gift splitting elected
Gift to non-citizen spouse > $185,000 (2026 indexed) Yes Elevated exclusion for non-citizen spouses; excess is taxable gift
Direct payment of tuition or medical expenses No §2503(e) exclusion — no 709 needed for qualifying direct payments
Gift to 529 plan (5-year election) Yes 5-year front-loading election requires Form 709 reporting
Charitable gift No Qualifying charitable gifts are not taxable gifts

⚠️ File Even When No Tax Is Owed

The most common Form 709 mistake: not filing because no gift tax is actually due. A return is required to report any taxable gift — even if the gift is fully covered by the lifetime exemption and zero tax is paid. Filing starts the three-year statute of limitations. Not filing means the IRS can challenge that gift indefinitely — including decades later during an estate tax audit when the gift is added back to the taxable estate.

2. The Annual Exclusion: $19,000 per Recipient (2025)

The annual exclusion under §2503(b) allows each donor to give up to $19,000 (2025, indexed for inflation) per recipient per year with no gift tax and no Form 709 filing requirement. Key points for Bitcoin:

  • The exclusion is per recipient, not per donor or per year total — a married couple can give $38,000 to each of their children annually with no 709 (via gift splitting)
  • Bitcoin gifts to a single recipient must stay under $19,000 annually to avoid filing
  • The exclusion applies separately to each recipient — gifts to three children = $57,000 tax-free without filing (2025)
  • The gift must be a present interest — a gift of Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust generally does not qualify for the annual exclusion unless the trust has Crummey withdrawal rights

Present Interest Requirement

The annual exclusion requires the gift to be a "present interest" — an immediate, unrestricted right to use, possess, or enjoy the property. Bitcoin transferred to an irrevocable trust typically fails this test because the beneficiaries don't have immediate access. The solution: Crummey withdrawal rights — a limited window (usually 30 days) during which beneficiaries may withdraw the contribution. This converts the trust contribution into a present interest, qualifying for the annual exclusion.

3. Valuing Bitcoin on Form 709

Bitcoin on Form 709 must be reported at its fair market value on the date of the gift — the date the donor irrevocably relinquished dominion and control.

What Counts as the "Date of Gift"

  • On-chain transfer: For Bitcoin held in self-custody or at an exchange, the gift date is when the Bitcoin is sent on-chain to the recipient's address — confirmed on the blockchain
  • Exchange transfer: If Bitcoin is gifted via exchange account transfer, the gift date is the date the transfer is executed and received
  • Trust funding: The gift date is when Bitcoin is transferred to the trust's custody — not when the trust document is signed

Valuation Methodology

The IRS has not issued Bitcoin-specific Form 709 valuation guidance beyond the general FMV standard. Best practice, consistent with Notice 2014-21 and the treatment on Schedule D:

  • Use the closing price on a major US exchange (Coinbase, Kraken) on the gift date
  • For very large gifts where the amount itself would move the market, a discount for blocksize may be arguable — but this is rarely applicable and requires qualified appraisal support
  • Document the exchange, the specific price used, and the source in the return workpapers
  • For fractional Bitcoin, apply the per-coin price proportionally

4. Reporting Estate Planning Transactions on Form 709

GRAT Funding

When a grantor funds a GRAT, they make a taxable gift equal to the present value of the remainder interest — what passes to the trust after the annuity term. For a zeroed-out GRAT (annuity set so the present value of the annuity equals the contributed Bitcoin value at the §7520 rate), the taxable gift is $0 or near $0.

Even with a $0 taxable gift, Form 709 must be filed to:

  • Report the GRAT transaction
  • Establish the GRAT's existence in IRS records
  • Start the three-year statute of limitations
  • Document the §7520 rate used and the annuity calculation

💡 GRAT Statute of Limitations

A properly reported GRAT on Form 709 starts a three-year clock during which the IRS can challenge the valuation. After three years, the gift valuation is generally final. A GRAT that is not reported on Form 709 has no statute of limitations — the IRS can challenge the transfer during estate tax audit at any time, potentially arguing that the annuity payments were inadequate or the valuation was wrong, generating a taxable gift with interest and penalties decades later.

IDGT Seed Gift

When establishing an IDGT for a Bitcoin installment sale, the grantor typically makes an initial "seed gift" — a completed gift of cash or Bitcoin equal to approximately 10% of the total intended sale value. This gives the trust sufficient equity to support the promissory note (a trust with no equity cannot give a creditworthy note).

The seed gift is reportable on Form 709 if it exceeds the annual exclusion. The installment sale itself — Bitcoin transferred to the trust in exchange for a note bearing at least AFR — is not a gift and is not reported on Form 709 (it's a sale). The distinction matters for lifetime exemption usage: only the seed gift reduces the grantor's lifetime exemption, not the full value of the installment sale.

SLAT Funding

Funding a SLAT is a completed taxable gift of the full FMV of assets contributed. Form 709 is required. The gift is typically sheltered entirely by the lifetime exemption (no actual gift tax owed), but the return must be filed to report the exemption usage.

Dynasty Trust Funding

Contributions to a dynasty trust are completed taxable gifts reported on Form 709. The GST exemption allocation (discussed below) is also made on Form 709.

5. GST Exemption Allocation on Form 709

The generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax applies to transfers that skip a generation — gifts or bequests to grandchildren, great-grandchildren, or trusts for their benefit. The GST tax rate is a flat 40%.

Each person has a GST exemption equal to the estate tax exemption (approximately $13.99 million in 2025 for individuals; confirm with your advisor as this amount is subject to legislative change). The GST exemption must be proactively allocated to gifts that are subject to GST — it is not automatic.

Allocating GST Exemption on Form 709

Form 709 includes Schedule C for GST exemption allocation. For a Bitcoin gift to a dynasty trust (which can benefit grandchildren and beyond), the grantor should allocate GST exemption equal to the gift value to prevent future GST tax on the trust's distributions and terminations.

Automatic allocation rules under §2632(c) apply to certain "indirect skip" transfers — but automatic allocation may not always be optimal. Affirmative elections to allocate or not allocate GST exemption are made on Form 709.

⚠️ Don't Waste GST Exemption

GST exemption allocated to a trust that doesn't ultimately benefit skip persons is wasted — it cannot be recovered. For Bitcoin trusts that may eventually benefit skip persons (grandchildren) but primarily benefit children first, careful timing of GST allocation maximizes the exemption's value. Allocating when Bitcoin's value is depressed leverages the exemption most efficiently: $1M of GST exemption covers $1M of Bitcoin today, but if Bitcoin triples, that same exemption covers $3M of trust value going forward.

6. Gift Splitting Election (§2513)

Married couples can split gifts under §2513 — treating any gift made by one spouse as made equally by both. For Bitcoin gifting programs, this doubles the annual exclusion ($38,000 per recipient in 2025) and splits any taxable gift between both spouses' lifetime exemptions.

Election Mechanics

  • Both spouses must consent to split all gifts made during the calendar year (the election applies to all gifts made in the year, not individual gifts)
  • Both spouses must file Form 709 — including the non-donor spouse
  • The non-donor spouse's Form 709 reports their half of the split gifts
  • Gift splitting requires both spouses to be US citizens or residents throughout the calendar year
  • Cannot split gifts made after a divorce becomes final

7. Lifetime Exemption: How Form 709 Tracks It

The unified credit against gift tax is equivalent to the lifetime exemption — approximately $13.99 million per person in 2025 (confirm current amount with your advisor). Every taxable gift reduces this exemption. Form 709 tracks cumulative lifetime gifts through:

  • Part 2, Schedule A: All taxable gifts made in the current year
  • Part 2, Line 7: Total taxable gifts from prior years (pulled from prior year Form 709s)
  • Part 2, Applicable Credit: Unified credit against cumulative lifetime gifts

Every Form 709 you file adds to the cumulative gift history that will be reported on Form 706 (estate tax return) at death. The estate tax is computed on the combined value of taxable lifetime gifts plus the taxable estate — all gifts reported on Form 709 add to this calculation.

8. Filing Deadline and Extensions

  • Annual deadline: Form 709 is due April 15 of the year following the calendar year in which the gift was made
  • Extension: An automatic 6-month extension (to October 15) is available by filing Form 8892 or by timely extending the income tax return (Form 4868 automatically extends Form 709 as well)
  • Extension does not extend time to pay gift tax: Any gift tax owed is due April 15 regardless of extension
  • Special election for GRAT and installment sale: The §7520 rate used for valuing GRAT annuities must be from the month of the gift or either of the two preceding months — the election is made on Form 709

9. Common Form 709 Mistakes for Bitcoin Holders

  1. Not filing when no tax is owed. The most common error. A zeroed-out GRAT, seed gift covered by annual exclusion, or gift fully sheltered by lifetime exemption still requires a return. Non-filing leaves the transfer permanently open to IRS challenge.
  2. Wrong valuation date. Using the date the trust agreement was signed (rather than the date Bitcoin was actually transferred on-chain) can misstate the FMV by thousands of dollars in a volatile market.
  3. Failing to report GRAT as a gift. Some preparers treat a zeroed-out GRAT as not needing a 709 because the taxable gift is $0. Wrong — the return is required even for a $0 gift to report the structure.
  4. Not allocating GST exemption to dynasty trusts. Funding a dynasty trust without allocating GST exemption on Form 709 means the trust will be subject to GST tax on distributions to grandchildren and beyond. The exemption must be proactively allocated.
  5. Forgetting the non-donor spouse's return. When gift splitting is elected, both spouses must file Form 709. Preparers sometimes file only the donor spouse's return. The non-donor's 709 is required for a valid split election.
  6. Not reporting prior year gifts. Form 709 requires cumulative reporting of all prior taxable gifts since 1977. Preparers must obtain prior year returns or reconstruct the history. Missing prior gifts understates taxable estate at death.
  7. Reporting installment sales as gifts. A bona fide installment sale to an IDGT is not a gift — only the seed gift is reportable. Reporting the full sale value as a gift consumes lifetime exemption unnecessarily.

Frequently Asked Questions

When does a Bitcoin gift require filing Form 709?

You must file Form 709 if you make a Bitcoin gift to any single recipient exceeding the annual exclusion ($19,000 in 2025), if you elect gift splitting with your spouse, if you fund a trust with a taxable gift, if you fund a GRAT (even a zeroed-out one), or if you make any gift subject to GST tax. The return is required even when no gift tax is owed — it's how the IRS tracks lifetime exemption usage and the statute of limitations begins running.

How is Bitcoin valued on Form 709?

Bitcoin is valued at its FMV on the date of the gift — the date the donor irrevocably relinquishes dominion and control. For on-chain Bitcoin transfers, the gift date is the confirmation date on the blockchain. Use the closing price on a major US exchange (Coinbase, Kraken) on that date. Document the source in your workpapers. For fractional Bitcoin, apply the per-coin price proportionally.

Does an installment sale of Bitcoin to an IDGT require Form 709?

No — the sale itself is not a gift and does not require Form 709. Only the initial seed gift (typically 10% of the sale value) is reportable if it exceeds the annual exclusion. The installment sale is a transaction for adequate consideration (a promissory note at AFR), not a gratuitous transfer.

Does funding a GRAT require Form 709?

Yes, always — even for a zeroed-out GRAT with a $0 taxable gift. The return is required to report the GRAT transaction, establish it in IRS records, and start the three-year statute of limitations. A GRAT not reported on Form 709 has no limitation period and can be challenged by the IRS indefinitely.

What is the gift splitting election and how does it affect Form 709?

§2513 gift splitting allows married couples to treat any gift by one spouse as made equally by both — doubling the annual exclusion per recipient to $38,000 in 2025. To split gifts, both spouses must file Form 709 for the year. The election covers all gifts made during the calendar year. The non-donor spouse files their own Form 709 reporting their half of all split gifts.

What happens if I don't file Form 709 for a Bitcoin gift?

The statute of limitations never runs on unreported gifts. The IRS can assess gift tax — including interest and penalties — at any time on unreported gifts, even decades later during an estate tax audit. Unreported gifts are added back to the taxable estate and may generate additional estate tax at death. Penalties for failure to file when required can reach 25% of the gift tax owed.
