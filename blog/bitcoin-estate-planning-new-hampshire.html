Bitcoin Estate Planning in New Hampshire

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in New Hampshire Est. 9 min read

New Hampshire stands apart from virtually every other state in the Northeast — and arguably in the country — as a natural home for Bitcoin families. The Granite State imposes no state estate tax, no inheritance tax, and no income tax on wages or capital gains. The state's governing philosophy, captured in its famous motto Live Free or Die, aligns more closely with Bitcoin's ethos than any other state east of the Mississippi. The Free State Project — a coordinated migration of libertarian-minded individuals to New Hampshire to achieve a critical mass of liberty-oriented voters and residents — has brought thousands of Bitcoin enthusiasts and advocates to the state over the past two decades.

This guide covers New Hampshire's tax environment for Bitcoin families, the NH trust law framework, LLC structures appropriate for Bitcoin holding, the state's strong privacy culture and its intersection with Bitcoin self-custody, and the federal strategies that remain necessary even when state-level death taxes are absent.

New Hampshire's Tax Environment for Bitcoin Families

New Hampshire's tax profile for Bitcoin families is exceptional:

  • No estate tax. New Hampshire does not impose a state-level estate tax on decedents' estates, regardless of size.
  • No inheritance tax. Transfers to heirs at death are not subject to New Hampshire inheritance tax.
  • No income tax on wages or capital gains. New Hampshire does not tax earned income or investment gains — including Bitcoin capital gains realized during the owner's lifetime.
  • Interest and Dividends Tax — now phased out. New Hampshire historically taxed interest and dividend income at 5% under the "I&D Tax." The legislature voted to phase out this tax, with the rate reduced to 3% in 2023, 1% in 2024, and complete elimination effective January 1, 2025. For Bitcoin families, this tax rarely applied directly (Bitcoin does not generate dividends or traditional interest), but it affected income from trust investments and dividend-paying assets that might coexist with Bitcoin in a family portfolio.
  • No general sales tax. New Hampshire is one of five states with no general sales tax.

The practical result: a New Hampshire-domiciled Bitcoin holder who sells Bitcoin, earns wages, receives distributions from trusts, and dies leaving Bitcoin to heirs faces no state-level tax at any stage of that sequence. Federal taxes apply — capital gains, income tax, and estate tax — but the state layer is largely absent. This makes New Hampshire's primary estate planning focus federal: managing the federal estate tax exemption, maximizing stepped-up basis at death for large positions, and building trust structures that protect Bitcoin for multiple generations under federal GST tax rules.

New Hampshire's fiscal profile — no estate tax, no income tax on wages or gains, no sales tax — makes it one of the most Bitcoin-aligned states in the country for building and transferring generational wealth.

The Free State Project and Bitcoin Culture in New Hampshire

The Free State Project, founded in 2001 and headquartered in Manchester, has achieved its goal of attracting 20,000 signatories who pledged to move to New Hampshire to participate in its political and civic culture. The resulting concentration of libertarian-minded residents — including a disproportionately high representation of Bitcoin advocates, developers, and long-term holders — has shaped New Hampshire's policy environment in ways that matter for Bitcoin estate planning.

New Hampshire has been an early mover in cryptocurrency-friendly legislation. The state has generally resisted onerous regulations on digital asset transactions, and its political culture makes future hostile Bitcoin legislation less likely than in more regulatory-oriented states. For Bitcoin families considering domicile change from high-tax northeastern states — particularly Massachusetts, Connecticut, or New York — New Hampshire's cultural alignment with Bitcoin values is a meaningful consideration beyond the raw tax numbers.

Privacy is a deeply held value in New Hampshire's culture. Bitcoin's self-custody model — holding your own keys, transacting without intermediaries — resonates powerfully in this environment. Estate planning for New Hampshire Bitcoin families frequently involves explicit, principled decisions about custody architecture: not just what is technically optimal, but what preserves the sovereignty and privacy that motivated the Bitcoin holding in the first place. Succession planning that involves third-party custodians or financial institutions may conflict with the values of New Hampshire Bitcoin holders in ways that must be navigated carefully.

New Hampshire Trust Law for Bitcoin Families

New Hampshire adopted the Uniform Trust Code (UTC) and has subsequently enhanced it with several modernizing provisions. The state permits directed trusts, perpetual trusts (dynasty trusts with no rule-against-perpetuities limitation), and trust decanting — giving New Hampshire trust law a competitive profile, though Wyoming and South Dakota remain the gold standard for offshore-competitive trust situs.

Directed trusts: New Hampshire's directed trust statute allows separation of investment direction from administrative trustee functions. A Bitcoin-specialized custodian or family member can direct custody decisions while a NH corporate trustee handles administrative and compliance functions. This is particularly valuable for Bitcoin trusts where the family insists on self-custody or multi-signature control but needs a professional trustee for tax reporting and distribution administration.

Dynasty trusts: New Hampshire permits perpetual trusts, allowing Bitcoin to remain in trust indefinitely for multiple generations without mandatory distribution. A dynasty trust funded with Bitcoin and allocated generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax exemption can compound for descendants across centuries — entirely outside the federal estate tax system after the initial transfer.

Privacy and trust registration: New Hampshire does not require public disclosure of trust terms. This aligns with the privacy preferences of many Bitcoin holders who prefer minimal public records of their holdings and estate planning structures. The trust instrument itself remains a private document, and trust administration does not become part of the public record absent litigation.

LLC Structures for New Hampshire Bitcoin Holding

New Hampshire LLCs (governed by RSA Chapter 304-C) provide a fully capable holding vehicle for Bitcoin. The NH LLC offers charging order protection, flexible operating agreement provisions, and disregarded entity treatment for federal tax purposes when single-member.

Operating agreement and custody governance: A New Hampshire LLC holding Bitcoin should have an operating agreement that explicitly addresses: multi-signature custody requirements and threshold signing counts; succession of key management authority if the primary manager is incapacitated or dies; restrictions on transferring Bitcoin to external wallets without multi-member approval; and technical documentation protocols specifying how and where seed phrases, hardware wallets, and access information are stored and updated. The operating agreement transforms the LLC from a tax vehicle into a governance framework for Bitcoin custody.

Valuation discounts on gifting: When minority LLC interests holding Bitcoin are transferred to heirs or irrevocable trusts, qualified appraisers can support minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts of 20–35% off the pro-rata Bitcoin value. This reduces the federal gift and estate tax value of transferred interests while the economic benefit of the underlying Bitcoin position is fully preserved. For New Hampshire families focused purely on federal estate tax, this discount is valuable when estates exceed the federal exemption.

Federal Estate Planning Priorities for NH Bitcoin Families

With no state estate tax, New Hampshire Bitcoin families focus entirely on federal planning. The TCJA sunset after 2025 may reduce the federal estate tax exemption from approximately $14 million to approximately $7 million per individual — bringing many NH Bitcoin families into the federal estate tax scope for the first time. Planning before the sunset is critical:

  1. Use the elevated federal exemption before it sunsets — transfers completed before a statutory reduction are grandfathered; the IRS has confirmed there is no clawback for gifts made under the higher exemption.
  2. Fund a dynasty trust with Bitcoin and allocate GST exemption to remove the position from the estate tax system for all future generations.
  3. Consider GRAT structures for large positions — transferring Bitcoin to a GRAT during price appreciation can shift substantial value to heirs with zero gift tax cost.
  4. Document Bitcoin custody succession explicitly — technical key succession must be addressed separately from the legal trust document, with a tested protocol coordinated with your multi-signature architecture.
  5. Review beneficiary designations and LLC operating agreements annually — New Hampshire's favorable environment makes these structures long-lived, but they need updating as the family, the Bitcoin position, and the law evolve.
