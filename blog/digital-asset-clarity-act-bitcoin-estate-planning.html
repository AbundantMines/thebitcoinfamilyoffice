The Digital Asset Clarity Act: What It Means for Bitcoin Estate Planning

H.R.3633 passed the House in July 2025. JPMorgan analysts called it a potential "major catalyst" today. Here's what every HNWI Bitcoin family needs to understand about the CLARITY Act before their estate plan is obsolete.

Published March 1, 2026  ·  BFO Editorial Team  ·  ~18 min read

1. The CLARITY Act: What It Is and Why It Matters Now

On March 1, 2026, JPMorgan analysts published a note calling the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025 a potential "major catalyst" for Bitcoin and crypto markets in the second half of the year. The timing is notable: Bitcoin is range-bound near $67,000, and the market is searching for the regulatory development that could break the range. JPMorgan thinks the CLARITY Act is it.

But this isn't a price piece. The JPMorgan note matters to this audience for a different reason. Regulatory certainty isn't just a trading catalyst — it is the structural precondition for confident, long-duration Bitcoin estate planning. When the legal framework around Bitcoin custody, classification, and fiduciary obligation becomes settled law, the estate planning implications are profound.

To understand why, start with why the U.S. has lacked a workable digital asset regulatory framework for over a decade.

The Regulatory Vacuum, 2014–2025

The fundamental problem was jurisdictional ambiguity. The Commodity Exchange Act gave the CFTC authority over commodities. The Securities Exchange Act gave the SEC authority over securities. Bitcoin emerged in a gap between these frameworks — one written long before decentralized digital assets existed. Neither agency had a clear statutory mandate to regulate Bitcoin spot markets. Both claimed enforcement authority when convenient. Neither provided meaningful guidance for institutional holders, trustees, or fiduciaries.

For over a decade, the result was regulatory whack-a-mole. The SEC pursued enforcement actions against exchanges, token issuers, and custodians under elastic theories of securities law. The CFTC regulated Bitcoin futures markets but had limited authority over spot markets. Congress introduced and abandoned digital asset legislation multiple times — the DCCPA, the FIT21 precursors, the Lummis-Gillibrand framework — without passing anything into law. Meanwhile, institutional adoption accelerated, and families holding seven- and eight-figure Bitcoin positions were structuring estates, trusts, and retirement accounts under a legal framework that simply didn't account for what they were holding.

SAB 121 — the SEC's staff accounting bulletin requiring banks to treat custodied crypto as a liability on their balance sheets — crystallized how bad the situation had become. Major banks wanted to custody Bitcoin for clients. Regulators made it financially prohibitive. Congress voted to overturn SAB 121 in 2024. The White House initially vetoed, then relented. SAB 121's reversal was a signal that the political winds had shifted — but it was not comprehensive reform. It was a patch.

What H.R.3633 Does

The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025 (H.R.3633) is the most comprehensive attempt at federal digital asset legislation in U.S. history. Passed by the House in July 2025, it does four things that matter for estate planning purposes:

First, it establishes a digital asset taxonomy. The CLARITY Act creates a statutory classification system distinguishing between "digital commodity assets" — those with decentralized networks where no single entity controls issuance — and "digital asset securities" — those with centralized issuers or where purchasers reasonably expect profits from the efforts of a promoter. This is not a novelty. It maps to the Howey test framework the SEC has used for decades, but finally codifies it in statute for digital assets.

Second, it assigns regulatory jurisdiction clearly. Digital commodity assets — Bitcoin being the paradigmatic example — fall under CFTC jurisdiction for spot market oversight. Digital asset securities remain under SEC authority. The years of jurisdictional uncertainty, where the SEC could claim Bitcoin was a security in one enforcement context and the CFTC could assert commodity jurisdiction in another, end under this framework.

Third, it establishes custody standards for digital assets. The CLARITY Act mandates that qualified custodians holding digital commodity assets meet defined capital, technical, and operational standards. For the first time, there is a federal statutory basis for what it means to be a "qualified custodian" of Bitcoin — a concept that currently exists in patchwork state law and informal regulatory guidance.

Fourth, it creates a regulatory framework for digital asset exchanges — requiring registration, disclosure, and operational requirements analogous to regulated commodity exchanges. This matters less directly for estate planning, but it matters for the liquidity and price discovery environment in which Bitcoin holdings are valued and transacted.

2. What Changes for Bitcoin Specifically

Bitcoin's classification under the CLARITY Act is as close to settled as anything in this bill. No reasonable reading of the commodity/security distinction the bill establishes leaves Bitcoin anywhere other than the commodity column.

Bitcoin has no central issuer. No promoter controls the protocol. No team of developers can unilaterally alter the monetary policy. The Howey test — which asks whether an investment contract involves an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profits from the efforts of others — fails to apply to Bitcoin in any coherent way. Bitcoin is bought and held. Its value derives from the network, from energy security, from adoption. No company profits from your Bitcoin purchase.

The CLARITY Act essentially codifies what regulators have been saying informally for years — that Bitcoin is a commodity — and gives it statutory force. Gary Gensler famously refused to say Bitcoin was a security. The CLARITY Act makes it a commodity in law.

The practical implication for holders: Bitcoin classified as a commodity is structurally simpler to hold at every level of the estate planning stack. Trusts can hold commodities under existing trustee investment standards. IRAs can hold commodities with less regulatory ambiguity. Family offices need not grapple with whether their Bitcoin exposure requires registered investment adviser treatment as a securities portfolio.

The Grandfather Clause and Altcoins

The CLARITY Act includes provisions treating certain tokens as commodities if they were tied to spot ETFs approved before January 1, 2026 — a framework that would cover XRP, Solana (SOL), and Litecoin (LTC), among others, given their spot ETF applications or approvals in the late 2025 window. This is significant for those with diversified digital asset portfolios, but it creates a class of "grandfather commodity" tokens whose commodity treatment is by legislative fiat rather than structural decentralization.

Bitcoin's commodity classification doesn't depend on a grandfather clause. It is commodity-native by every structural analysis. This distinction matters for estate planning: Bitcoin's legal footing is categorical and durable. The commodity treatment of grandfathered altcoins could be politically revisited in future legislation. Plan accordingly.

3. Estate Planning Implications: Trusts Holding Bitcoin

For trustees, estate attorneys, and the families they serve, the CLARITY Act changes the framework in ways that are both practical and fundamental.

The Custodian Problem — Solved

The most immediate operational problem the CLARITY Act resolves for trustees is the custodian question. Today, when a trustee wants to hold Bitcoin on behalf of a trust, they face a fractured landscape: no federal standard defining what a "qualified custodian" of Bitcoin means, state-by-state licensing frameworks that vary dramatically, and a practical market where a handful of specialized firms — Coinbase Custody, BitGo, Anchorage Digital, Fidelity Digital Assets — have self-appointed as institutional-grade custodians without a federal framework validating their status.

A trustee holding Bitcoin through Fidelity Digital Assets today is making a good-faith fiduciary judgment with no federal statutory backup. If a beneficiary challenges the custody arrangement, the trustee must defend the choice under general fiduciary prudence standards — standards not designed with digital assets in mind. The CLARITY Act's CFTC-defined custodian standards create a safe harbor. A trustee who uses a CLARITY Act-compliant qualified custodian has federal statutory backing for that decision.

This isn't theoretical. Fiduciary challenges to Bitcoin custody arrangements are coming as Bitcoin enters the mainstream estate. The families that structure their trusts to use CLARITY Act-compliant custodians before passage are the ones who will have the cleanest audit trail when challenged.

Fiduciary Standards and the Uniform Trust Code

The Uniform Trust Code (UTC) and the Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA) govern trustee investment decisions in most states. Both are principles-based frameworks: trustees must act prudently, diversify, and consider the purposes and circumstances of the trust. Neither framework explicitly addresses digital assets. The CLARITY Act's commodity classification of Bitcoin gives trustees a cleaner statutory hook for including Bitcoin in a trust portfolio under the UPIA.

Specifically, Bitcoin as a CFTC-regulated commodity asset has more in common with gold, oil futures, or other alternative investments already treated as appropriate trust assets in institutional portfolios than with speculative assets or unregulated tokens. Estate attorneys drafting investment policy statements (IPS) for trusts can reference the CLARITY Act commodity framework directly — providing trustees with a documented legal basis for Bitcoin exposure that didn't exist before.

Trust Document Language — Update Now

Legacy trust documents often contain either no language about digital assets or boilerplate prohibiting "speculative investments" in ways that arguably sweep in Bitcoin. In the pre-CLARITY Act environment, an estate attorney conservatively drafting trust language might restrict trustees to "investment-grade securities, government obligations, and listed commodities." Post-CLARITY Act, Bitcoin is a listed commodity in every relevant sense.

Trust documents should be reviewed and updated to:

1. Explicitly authorize holding "digital commodity assets as defined under applicable federal commodity law" — language that captures Bitcoin and any CLARITY Act-recognized commodity without requiring repeated amendment as the law evolves.

2. Reference qualified custodians compliant with CFTC standards for digital commodity assets, rather than simply requiring "FDIC-insured institutions" or "SEC-registered custodians" — language that inadvertently excluded Bitcoin custodians.

3. Address private key management — whether self-custody, institutional custody, or multi-signature arrangements — as part of the trustee's administrative authority.

Wyoming + CLARITY Act: The Strongest Jurisdiction

Wyoming has been the most forward-thinking U.S. state for digital asset law since 2019. Its Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI) framework created the first state-chartered banks specifically designed to hold digital assets. Wyoming's trust law explicitly authorizes trustees to hold digital assets, defines fiduciary duties in that context, and permits directed trusts where beneficiaries can direct the Bitcoin custody arrangement.

The CLARITY Act federal framework layered on top of Wyoming's existing state law creates the strongest available jurisdiction for Bitcoin trust structures. Wyoming-sited trusts with CLARITY Act-compliant custodians, governed by Wyoming directed trust law, represent the current gold standard for families holding significant Bitcoin in trust.

How the CLARITY Act Affects Trust Investment Policy Statements

Investment Policy Statements for trusts holding Bitcoin — or considering it — should be updated to reflect the CLARITY Act framework before the bill passes. The reason: a trustee who has documented a forward-looking IPS that anticipated and aligned with the CLARITY Act framework is in a stronger position than one who scrambles to update after passage. Proactive alignment signals the fiduciary competence that trust law demands.

Key IPS elements to update include: the asset class classification of Bitcoin (commodity, not speculative investment), the custody policy (CFTC-compliant qualified custodians), the liquidity framework (Bitcoin spot markets vs. futures), and the concentration limits (most institutional frameworks allow 5–15% alternative commodity exposure; Bitcoin warrants a dedicated sub-policy).

4. IRA and Retirement Account Implications

The retirement account landscape for Bitcoin has been shaped by improvisation. Self-directed IRAs, checkbook IRA LLC structures, and a handful of specialized Bitcoin IRA custodians have provided the infrastructure — not because federal law sanctioned Bitcoin in IRAs, but because nothing explicitly prohibited it, and the tax advantages were too compelling to leave on the table.

The CLARITY Act changes that calculus in meaningful ways.

Self-Directed IRAs: From Gray Zone to Clearer Ground

Currently, self-directed IRA custodians holding Bitcoin do so under general IRS "alternative investment" provisions, with no specific guidance on digital asset custody standards. When a self-directed IRA custodian like Kingdom Trust or Equity Trust Company holds Bitcoin on your behalf, they operate under their own compliance frameworks — not federal digital asset custody standards, because none exist.

The CLARITY Act's CFTC-defined custodian standards create a baseline. Self-directed IRA custodians that hold Bitcoin will need to meet — or partner with entities that meet — CLARITY Act custody standards. This is not bad news for investors; it's a quality floor. The custodians that can't meet the standard will exit the market. The ones that can will have a federal imprimatur that makes their services more defensible to IRA holders and their beneficiaries.

Mainstream Custodians Enter the IRA Market

The more significant change is downstream. Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard have watched the self-directed Bitcoin IRA market from the sidelines, uncomfortable with the regulatory ambiguity that comes with offering Bitcoin in retirement accounts under current law. The CLARITY Act removes the single largest regulatory barrier to mainstream IRA custodians offering direct Bitcoin exposure.

If CFTC oversight of Bitcoin custodians provides a clear compliance framework, and if spot Bitcoin ETFs are already available through standard brokerage IRAs (which they are), the question becomes: why maintain a separate self-directed IRA structure at all? For some holders, the answer remains self-custody and control. For others, the CLARITY Act may make a mainstream custodian's Bitcoin IRA offering — with lower fees and simpler administration — the superior option.

The strategic question for HNWI holders: if the CLARITY Act passes as written, is your current self-directed Bitcoin IRA structure still optimal, or does the changed landscape warrant a repositioning?

Checkbook IRA Structures

Checkbook IRA LLC structures — where the IRA owns a single-member LLC that holds Bitcoin directly — introduce self-custody flexibility at the cost of complexity and ongoing compliance risk. The IRS has audited these structures; the prohibited transaction rules under IRC Section 4975 are exacting. The CLARITY Act does not change prohibited transaction rules. If anything, as Bitcoin custody becomes more institutionally mature under the CLARITY Act framework, the incremental advantage of checkbook IRA self-custody diminishes while the compliance burden remains.

This is not an argument against checkbook structures where they're appropriate — for holders who require self-custody or cold storage control at the IRA level, the structure remains viable. But for holders using checkbook IRAs primarily because institutional custody options were limited, that rationale may weaken post-CLARITY Act.

The Inherited IRA Problem

The SECURE 2.0 Act's 10-year rule for inherited IRAs creates a specific pressure point for Bitcoin IRA holders. Non-spouse beneficiaries inheriting a Bitcoin IRA must distribute the funds within 10 years of the original owner's death — meaning they bear both income tax exposure and Bitcoin price volatility over that window. If the CLARITY Act catalyzes a Bitcoin price increase (as JPMorgan implies it might), inherited Bitcoin IRA beneficiaries who aren't planning may find themselves distributing highly appreciated Bitcoin with large RMD tax bills compressed into a 10-year window.

The planning answer is not to avoid Bitcoin IRAs, but to size them with the inherited distribution horizon in mind — and to ensure beneficiary designations align with who can best absorb taxable distributions from a significantly appreciated Bitcoin position.

5. Family Office Implications

Family offices operating outside the registered investment adviser (RIA) framework — single-family offices, specifically — have held Bitcoin without the regulatory compliance overhead that broker-dealers or RIAs face. The CLARITY Act's commodity classification of Bitcoin doesn't change that core exemption. But it changes the operating environment in ways that make running a Bitcoin-holding family office cleaner and more defensible.

Regulatory Reporting — Cleaner Under CFTC

Family offices that hold significant Bitcoin positions currently navigate ambiguous reporting requirements. The CFTC's large trader reporting thresholds apply to commodity futures. The SEC's Form 13F applies to securities. Bitcoin spot holdings fall in a gap. Post-CLARITY Act, Bitcoin as a regulated commodity will come with defined reporting frameworks. Family office compliance teams — and the attorneys and CPAs supporting them — will have clear guidance on when and how to report Bitcoin holdings. Ambiguity has its own cost; compliance teams price it in through conservative over-reporting or expensive legal opinions. Clarity reduces that cost.

Investment Policy Statement Language

Family office IPSs today that address Bitcoin typically include hedging language about "regulatory uncertainty" as a risk factor. Post-CLARITY Act, that language should be updated to reflect the changed framework. The IPS can reference Bitcoin's CFTC commodity classification, define it as an "alternative commodity" consistent with the CLARITY Act framework, and establish risk management parameters that align with how institutional commodity portfolios are managed — not how speculative digital asset portfolios were previously described.

More importantly, family office governance documents should explicitly authorize Bitcoin exposure at the board or trustee level with reference to the regulatory framework — ensuring that decisions made today survive the scrutiny of beneficiaries, successor trustees, or regulatory reviewers years from now.

RIA Clarity for Bitcoin-Focused Advisers

Multi-family office RIAs managing Bitcoin for multiple client families have faced an uncomfortable question: is their Bitcoin advisory activity covered by their investment adviser registration, or does managing commodity exposure require CFTC-side registration as a commodity trading adviser (CTA)? The CLARITY Act's commodity classification of Bitcoin pushes this question toward the CFTC. Bitcoin-focused RIAs may need to evaluate whether their existing registration covers their CLARITY Act commodity activity — a question for their compliance counsel, but one that should be answered before, not after, passage.

The Morgan Stanley Moment

Morgan Stanley obtained OCC trust charter approval to operate as a federal savings association — part of its infrastructure buildout for institutional digital asset custody. That move, combined with the CLARITY Act's federal custodian standards, represents the convergence of institutional infrastructure that the sophisticated family office market has been waiting for. A family office that holds Bitcoin through a Morgan Stanley-level institutional custodian operating under CLARITY Act-compliant standards — with CFTC oversight — is operating in a structurally different regulatory environment than the 2021-era "buy Bitcoin on Coinbase and hope" approach. The infrastructure is ready. The law is nearly there.

6. What Passes vs. What's at Risk in the Senate

The House passed H.R.3633 with meaningful bipartisan support. The Senate is a different institution, and several provisions are under active pressure.

NASAA's Investor Protection Concerns

The North American Securities Administrators Association (NASAA), representing state securities regulators, has publicly opposed aspects of the CLARITY Act's investor protection provisions. Their central concern: that the bill's preemption of certain state-level investor protection rules for digital commodity assets creates a gap in retail investor protection. NASAA's opposition is historically significant — state securities regulators have been effective backstops against financial fraud, and their concerns carry weight with Senate Democrats.

The most likely Senate amendment: strengthened anti-fraud provisions and clearer anti-manipulation authority for the CFTC in digital commodity markets. This would not undermine the core commodity classification framework that matters for estate planning. It would add enforcement teeth — which is not a negative outcome for legitimate Bitcoin holders.

What Might Change in Senate Markup

Three areas are most likely to be amended in Senate markup: (1) the grandfather clause for altcoin commodity treatment — NASAA and some Democratic senators view this as a back-door legitimization of tokens that haven't earned it; (2) custody standards — some senators want FDIC-equivalent insurance for digital asset custodians, which could impose significant capital requirements on smaller CFTC-registered custodians; (3) DeFi provisions — the CLARITY Act's treatment of decentralized exchanges is contested; Senate markup may tighten or remove DeFi carve-outs.

None of these changes would materially alter Bitcoin's commodity classification or the core custody standards framework. For Bitcoin holders, the most estate-planning-relevant provisions are also the most bipartisan and the least contested.

Timeline: What Has to Happen

For JPMorgan's H2 2026 estimate to materialize, the Senate Banking Committee needs to mark up the bill in Q2, floor debate and amendments need to clear in Q3, and any House-Senate conference on amendments needs to resolve before year-end. This is aggressive but not impossible given the White House's stated support for digital asset legislation. The Senate calendar is the primary constraint, not political will.

Planning Under Uncertainty

The right planning posture is not to wait. The CLARITY Act's core framework — Bitcoin as a commodity, CFTC oversight, qualified custodian standards — is the direction U.S. digital asset law is headed regardless of whether H.R.3633 passes in 2026 or a successor bill passes in 2027. The families that structure now — updating trust documents, reviewing custodian arrangements, updating IPS language — will be positioned regardless of the exact legislative path. The families that wait for legal certainty will scramble when it arrives.

"Planning under regulatory uncertainty isn't waiting for the rules to change. It's architecting structures durable enough to survive the change when it comes."

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025 (H.R.3633)?

H.R.3633, known as the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act of 2025 or the CLARITY Act, is federal legislation passed by the U.S. House of Representatives in July 2025. It establishes a clear taxonomy for digital assets — classifying them as either commodities (regulated by the CFTC) or securities (regulated by the SEC) — and creates federal standards for digital asset custody, exchanges, and market structure. As of March 2026, the bill is pending Senate action, with JPMorgan analysts projecting passage in H2 2026.

Is Bitcoin classified as a commodity or a security under the CLARITY Act?

Under the CLARITY Act, Bitcoin is almost certainly classified as a commodity digital asset, placing it under CFTC jurisdiction — analogous to how gold is regulated. Bitcoin's decentralized, proof-of-work network, lack of a central issuing entity, and long regulatory history make it the clearest candidate for commodity classification. This is significant for estate planning because it means Bitcoin held in trusts, IRAs, and family offices would be governed by commodity regulations rather than securities laws, with simpler and more established fiduciary frameworks.

How does the CLARITY Act affect Bitcoin held in a trust?

The CLARITY Act would establish federal custodian standards for commodity digital assets like Bitcoin, giving trustees clearer authority to hold Bitcoin with qualified institutional custodians. This resolves a significant ambiguity in current fiduciary law where trustees faced uncertainty about which custodian standards applied. Trust documents should be updated now to authorize "digital commodity assets under applicable federal commodity law" — language that captures the CLARITY Act framework. Wyoming's existing digital asset trust law combined with CLARITY Act commodity classification creates the strongest jurisdiction for Bitcoin trust structures.

What does the CLARITY Act mean for Bitcoin IRAs and self-directed IRAs?

The CLARITY Act's establishment of CFTC oversight and custody standards for Bitcoin may enable mainstream IRA custodians to offer direct Bitcoin exposure, reducing the need for specialized self-directed IRA structures. Existing Bitcoin IRA holders should review whether their structures remain optimal post-passage. The inherited IRA interaction with Bitcoin — particularly the 10-year rule under SECURE 2.0 — becomes more important to plan around as Bitcoin valuations may benefit from regulatory clarity. Checkbook IRA structures should also be evaluated against the cleaner institutional custody options the CLARITY Act will enable.

Should HNWI Bitcoin holders wait for the CLARITY Act to pass before updating estate plans?

No. Waiting for passage is a planning mistake. The CLARITY Act's passage is anticipated in H2 2026 by JPMorgan analysts, but Senate markup could take months and alter specific provisions. Bitcoin estate plans — trust documents, IRA structures, custodian arrangements, and family office investment policy statements — should be reviewed now and drafted to align with the framework the CLARITY Act creates. Planning ahead of passage, rather than reacting after, gives families the most structural flexibility and the strongest fiduciary documentation trail.
