Bitcoin and Real Estate Investor Estate Planning: The Complete 2026 Guide

Two concentrated, illiquid asset classes. Completely different tax rules. One unified estate plan — if you structure it correctly.

Published March 16, 2026 · 22 min read

You've spent decades building a real estate portfolio. Eight figures in rental properties, maybe some commercial assets, a few 1031 exchanges stacked on top of each other. You understand depreciation, passive activity losses, and cost segregation studies the way most people understand their checking account.

Then at some point — maybe 2017, maybe 2020, maybe last year — you bought Bitcoin. And kept buying. Now you're sitting on two massive concentrated positions with completely different tax treatment, completely different liquidity profiles, and completely different estate planning tools available to each.

This is the most complex estate planning crossover that exists. Real estate investors who also hold significant Bitcoin face a tax and transfer puzzle that no single professional — not your real estate attorney, not your CPA, not the crypto-native estate planner — fully understands alone. The intersection is where the complexity lives, and it's where most advisors miss critical optimization opportunities.

This guide covers the real estate + Bitcoin estate planning crossover in full. We assume you already know what a 1031 exchange is, understand depreciation schedules, and don't need the basics of Bitcoin estate planning explained from scratch. This is the advanced playbook for families holding both asset classes.

The Dual Concentration Problem

Most real estate investors already have a concentration problem. Seventy to eighty percent of net worth locked in illiquid property is standard for a successful portfolio holder. The conventional estate planning wisdom has always been: diversify away from concentration, create liquidity through life insurance, and use trusts to manage the transfer of hard-to-divide assets.

Adding Bitcoin changes the equation in ways that the traditional framework doesn't handle well.

Real estate is illiquid, income-producing, depreciable, and eligible for 1031 exchange. Bitcoin is highly liquid (in theory), non-income-producing (in traditional terms), not depreciable, and explicitly ineligible for 1031 exchange since the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 restricted like-kind exchanges to real property only.

So you now have two concentrated positions, but the estate planning tools that work for one don't necessarily work for the other. A 1031 exchange defers real estate gains beautifully — but does nothing for your Bitcoin. A dynasty trust can hold Bitcoin indefinitely across generations — but managing rental properties inside a dynasty trust creates operational headaches that most trustees aren't equipped to handle.

The dual concentration problem demands a dual-track estate plan: one strategy optimized for real estate's unique tax advantages, and a parallel strategy optimized for Bitcoin's unique characteristics. The art is in how these two tracks interact, where they can share structures (like a family limited partnership), and where they absolutely must remain separate.

Here's the critical insight most advisors miss: the two asset classes actually complement each other in estate planning when properly structured. Real estate generates tax losses through depreciation that can offset Bitcoin gains. Bitcoin provides the liquidity that real estate portfolios chronically lack for estate tax payments. And both receive a stepped-up basis at death — arguably the single most powerful feature of the U.S. estate tax system for holders of either asset.

Depreciation Interaction: Using Real Estate Losses to Offset Bitcoin Gains

If you're a real estate investor, you already live and breathe depreciation. Residential rental property depreciates over 27.5 years on a straight-line basis. Commercial property over 39 years. Land improvements, appliances, and certain building components can be accelerated through cost segregation studies — front-loading years of depreciation into the first few years of ownership.

What most real estate investors don't fully appreciate is how deliberately this depreciation can be sequenced against Bitcoin capital gains events.

Consider the scenario: you sell a portion of your Bitcoin position in a given tax year, generating $500,000 in long-term capital gains. In the same year, you complete a cost segregation study on a recently acquired multifamily property, accelerating $400,000 in depreciation deductions into year one. If you qualify as a Real Estate Professional (more on that below), those depreciation losses aren't trapped as passive activity losses — they can offset your Bitcoin capital gains dollar for dollar.

The sequencing matters. A cost segregation study takes 4–8 weeks. Bitcoin sales settle immediately. If you know you need to take Bitcoin gains in a particular year — whether for liquidity, rebalancing, or charitable giving — you can time the cost segregation study to land in the same tax year, creating a depreciation shield for what would otherwise be a taxable event.

This isn't tax avoidance. It's the tax code working as designed: real estate depreciation exists to incentivize property investment, and the deduction is available against other income categories for taxpayers who meet the qualification requirements. The fact that it can absorb Bitcoin gains is a feature of the system, not a loophole.

For estate planning purposes, this interaction matters because it changes the calculus on when and whether to sell Bitcoin during your lifetime. If you have sufficient depreciation to shield gains, a partial Bitcoin liquidation might make sense to fund irrevocable life insurance trusts, make annual exclusion gifts, or seed a grantor trust — all of which require liquidity that your real estate portfolio doesn't provide.

1031 Exchange and Bitcoin: Two Completely Different Exit Strategies

Before 2018, there was at least an argument — never tested in court, but floated by aggressive practitioners — that Bitcoin might qualify for like-kind exchange treatment under the broader pre-TCJA definition. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act eliminated that argument entirely. Section 1031 now applies exclusively to real property. Bitcoin is explicitly excluded.

This creates a fundamental divergence in how you plan exits for each asset class.

Real estate exit strategy: sell property, defer gain via 1031 exchange into replacement property, repeat indefinitely, hold final property to death for stepped-up basis. This is the classic "swap till you drop" approach that real estate investors have used for decades. The chain of 1031 exchanges can span an entire career, with the ultimate exit being death itself — at which point the cumulative deferred gain vanishes through the step-up in basis.

Bitcoin exit strategy: there is no 1031 equivalent. Every sale is a taxable event. The only "free" exit for Bitcoin is death (stepped-up basis) or charitable donation (fair market value deduction with no capital gains recognition). This means Bitcoin estate planning is fundamentally simpler in one sense — you don't have to manage a chain of deferred exchanges — but more constrained in another, because your only tax-free exit is to never sell.

The combined strategy for a family holding both assets: use 1031 exchanges to keep deferring real estate gains during life, while simultaneously holding Bitcoin to death. The two assets have different optimal holding strategies, and trying to apply real estate thinking to Bitcoin (or vice versa) leads to suboptimal outcomes.

One exception worth noting: if you need to sell Bitcoin during your lifetime, and you also hold real estate with a low adjusted basis, you might consider selling both in the same year. The real estate sale triggers a gain, but you 1031 the real estate and use the depreciation from the replacement property (via cost segregation) to offset the Bitcoin gain. This is a sophisticated multi-asset tax play that requires precise coordination between your real estate attorney and your tax advisor — but it's available and it works.

Passive Activity Loss Rules: Why Real Estate Professional Status Matters for Bitcoin Holders

Here's where the tax code creates a massive disparity between real estate investors who qualify as Real Estate Professionals (REPs) and those who don't.

Under the passive activity loss rules (IRC §469), rental real estate is generally treated as a passive activity. Depreciation from rental properties generates passive activity losses (PALs) that can only offset passive income — not portfolio income, not active business income, and not Bitcoin capital gains.

For non-REP investors, there's a limited $25,000 allowance for rental real estate losses against non-passive income, but it phases out completely at $150,000 of adjusted gross income. If you're holding enough real estate and Bitcoin to need this article, you're well past that phase-out.

But if you qualify as a Real Estate Professional — meaning you spend more than 750 hours per year in real property trades or businesses, and more time in real estate than in any other trade or business — your rental activities are no longer automatically passive. With proper election and material participation in each rental activity (or a grouped election), your depreciation losses become non-passive and can offset any category of income.

Including Bitcoin capital gains.

This is enormously powerful. A REP with a large rental portfolio generating $500,000+ in annual depreciation can sell Bitcoin, recognize capital gains, and use real estate depreciation to zero out or substantially reduce the tax bill. For a non-REP, those same depreciation losses sit suspended as PALs, unusable against Bitcoin income until the property is sold in a fully taxable disposition.

For estate planning, the REP status question affects whether you should sell Bitcoin during your lifetime at all. If one spouse qualifies as a REP (and the couple files jointly), the depreciation shield is available. If neither qualifies, the math often favors holding Bitcoin to death and never triggering the gain.

A common structure for real estate + Bitcoin families: one spouse actively manages the properties (qualifying as REP), while the other manages the Bitcoin portfolio and broader investment strategy. The REP spouse's status unlocks depreciation against Bitcoin gains, while the non-REP spouse focuses on the hold-to-death strategy for the core Bitcoin position.

Delaware Statutory Trust: 1031 Exchange Meets Estate Planning

The Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) sits at a unique intersection of real estate investing and estate planning that makes it particularly relevant for Bitcoin holders.

A DST is a legal entity that holds real property and issues beneficial interests to investors. Crucially, the IRS ruled in Revenue Ruling 2004-86 that a DST interest qualifies as "like-kind" real property for 1031 exchange purposes. This means you can sell an actively managed rental property, 1031 into a DST, and transition from active property management to a passive, fractional interest in institutional-grade real estate — while still deferring all capital gains.

For aging real estate investors who also hold Bitcoin, the DST solves a specific problem: what happens when you're too old or too uninterested to manage rental properties, but you don't want to trigger the massive built-in gain from decades of 1031 exchanges?

The answer is: 1031 into a DST. The DST is managed by a professional trustee. You receive distributions without active management responsibilities. And when you die, your heirs inherit the DST interest with a stepped-up basis — the cumulative gain from your entire 1031 chain vanishes.

From an estate planning perspective, DST interests are easier to divide among heirs than physical properties. If you have three children and eight rental properties, dividing the real estate equitably is a nightmare. But DST interests are fractional by nature — each heir can receive an equal share without forcing a sale or creating co-ownership disputes.

The Bitcoin remains in a separate structure — typically a revocable living trust that becomes irrevocable at death, or a purpose-built Bitcoin dynasty trust. The key principle: don't commingle Bitcoin and real estate in the same entity. They need different management, different custodians, and different distribution rules. The DST handles real estate. A separate trust handles Bitcoin. Both benefit from stepped-up basis at death.

Family Limited Partnership: One Structure for Both Assets

The one structure that can effectively hold both real estate and Bitcoin is the family limited partnership (FLP). And for families with both asset classes, the FLP is arguably the single most powerful estate planning tool available.

Here's why: the FLP applies valuation discounts to the partnership interest, not to the underlying assets individually. When you gift a limited partnership interest to your children, that interest is valued at a discount to the net asset value of the partnership's holdings — typically 20–40% — because the limited partner has no control over distributions, no ability to force liquidation, and limited marketability of their interest.

The discount applies equally whether the underlying assets are apartment buildings or Bitcoin. A 35% discount on a $10 million FLP holding $6 million in real estate and $4 million in Bitcoin means you're transferring $10 million of assets at a gift tax value of $6.5 million. That's $3.5 million of wealth transferred to the next generation without using any additional gift tax exemption.

Annual exclusion gifts of FLP units (currently $19,000 per donee per year) chip away at the older generation's ownership over time, gradually shifting wealth to children and grandchildren at discounted values. Over a 20-year period, a couple with three children can transfer hundreds of thousands of dollars in FLP interest using annual exclusions alone — with the discount applied to the underlying real estate and Bitcoin values.

The operational consideration with an FLP holding both assets: the partnership needs a competent general partner (typically the senior generation or an entity they control) who can manage both real estate operations and Bitcoin custody. This usually means the FLP's operating agreement specifies different management protocols for different asset classes — a property management company handles the real estate, while a qualified custodian or multisig arrangement secures the Bitcoin.

One important caution: the IRS has successfully challenged FLP discounts in cases where the partnership lacked a legitimate business purpose beyond tax reduction (see Estate of Strangi, Estate of Turner, and other Tax Court cases). The FLP must have genuine economic substance — pooling assets for investment management, providing creditor protection, keeping family wealth consolidated — beyond just obtaining valuation discounts. With both real estate and Bitcoin in the partnership, the business purpose argument is actually stronger: the partnership provides unified management of a diversified family investment portfolio spanning multiple asset classes.

Estate Tax Valuation: Real Estate vs. Bitcoin at Death

Both real estate and Bitcoin are included in the gross estate at fair market value on the date of death. Both receive a stepped-up basis. But the mechanics of valuation — and the strategic opportunities around valuation — differ significantly between the two assets.

Real estate valuation at death requires a qualified appraisal. Appraisals are inherently subjective — they depend on comparable sales, income capitalization rates, condition assessments, and market assumptions that reasonable appraisers can disagree on. This subjectivity creates a legitimate opportunity to support a lower valuation through careful appraiser selection and documentation of property condition issues, deferred maintenance, environmental concerns, or unfavorable lease terms. The IRS can challenge real estate appraisals, but they have to bring their own appraiser and argue in Tax Court — a process that often results in a negotiated middle ground.

Bitcoin valuation at death is, in theory, straightforward: it's the spot price on the date of death, pulled from major exchanges. But "spot price" isn't as simple as it sounds when you're dealing with a 24/7 global market across multiple exchanges with varying prices. The estate needs to document which exchange, which timestamp, and which price it's using. For large holdings, there may be an argument for a blockage discount — the idea that liquidating a very large position would move the market and the true realizable value is lower than the spot price. This argument has been accepted for large stock positions and could apply to significant Bitcoin holdings, though there's no direct precedent yet.

The alternate valuation date election (IRC §2032) allows the estate to value assets six months after death instead of on the date of death, but only if the election reduces both the gross estate value and the estate tax liability. For a portfolio holding both real estate and Bitcoin, this election gets interesting: if Bitcoin drops 30% in the six months after death but real estate values remain stable, the alternate valuation date might produce a lower overall estate value. Conversely, if Bitcoin rises and real estate falls, the same election might still help. The executor needs to model both scenarios across both asset classes before making the election.

For estate planning during life, the valuation dynamics suggest different timing strategies for gifting each asset. Gift real estate when values are depressed and you can support a conservative appraisal. Gift Bitcoin when spot prices are low — Bitcoin's volatility actually works in your favor here, since a gift during a significant drawdown freezes the value for gift tax purposes while transferring all future appreciation to the next generation.

QBI Deduction (§199A): What Qualifies and What Doesn't

The Section 199A qualified business income deduction allows a 20% deduction on qualified business income from pass-through entities. For real estate investors, this deduction can be significant — but its application to a combined real estate + Bitcoin portfolio is nuanced.

Rental real estate income generally qualifies for the QBI deduction if the rental activity rises to the level of a trade or business (which most active rental operations do) or if the taxpayer uses the IRS safe harbor (250 hours of rental services per year per property or group). The 20% deduction on net rental income — after depreciation — reduces the effective tax rate on real estate income substantially.

Bitcoin income does not qualify for QBI in most circumstances. Capital gains from selling Bitcoin are not QBI. Interest or yield from lending Bitcoin is investment income, not QBI. Even if you're actively trading Bitcoin through an entity, the income is likely from a "specified service trade or business" (investment management) that phases out of QBI eligibility above income thresholds.

The practical implication for estate planning: keep real estate income and Bitcoin income in separate entities to maximize QBI eligibility on the real estate side. If real estate is held in an FLP or LLC that also holds Bitcoin, a sale of Bitcoin could create investment income within the entity that complicates the QBI calculation for the real estate income. Clean entity separation — real estate in one structure, Bitcoin in another — preserves the QBI deduction for the real estate operations.

When planning transfers to the next generation, this distinction matters: the heir who inherits the real estate operations also inherits potential QBI deductions that reduce their ongoing tax burden. The heir who inherits Bitcoin gets a stepped-up basis and future appreciation, but no QBI benefit. Equitable distribution between heirs should account for the ongoing tax advantages embedded in the real estate portfolio that don't exist in the Bitcoin position.

Self-Directed IRA: Holding Both Assets in Tax-Advantaged Accounts

A self-directed IRA (SDIRA) can hold both real estate and Bitcoin — making it one of the few structures that accommodates both asset classes in a single tax-advantaged wrapper. But the complexity of doing so correctly cannot be overstated.

Real estate in an SDIRA: the IRA (not you personally) purchases the property. All income flows to the IRA. All expenses are paid from the IRA. You cannot personally perform maintenance, manage the property, or benefit from it in any way — these are prohibited transactions that can disqualify the entire IRA. Most SDIRA real estate investors use a "checkbook IRA LLC" structure: the IRA owns an LLC, and the LLC's checking account is used to buy property, collect rent, and pay expenses. This provides operational flexibility while maintaining the tax-advantaged status.

Bitcoin in an SDIRA: the IRA holds Bitcoin through a qualified custodian that supports cryptocurrency, or through a checkbook IRA LLC that purchases and custodies Bitcoin directly. The same prohibited transaction rules apply — you can't use the Bitcoin personally, can't lend it to yourself, and can't commingle it with your personal holdings.

The critical tax trap with both assets: UBIT and UDFI. If your SDIRA real estate has debt financing (a mortgage), the income attributable to the leveraged portion triggers Unrelated Debt-Financed Income (UDFI), which is subject to Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) even inside the IRA. Similarly, if your SDIRA Bitcoin generates income through margin lending or DeFi protocols that involve leverage, that income may trigger UBIT.

For estate planning, SDIRA assets pass to designated beneficiaries outside of probate. Under the SECURE Act and SECURE 2.0, most non-spouse beneficiaries must empty an inherited IRA within 10 years — meaning your heirs will pay ordinary income tax on all distributions, including both real estate sales and Bitcoin sales within the inherited IRA. This 10-year rule makes the SDIRA a less efficient wealth transfer vehicle than it was before 2020, when beneficiaries could stretch distributions over their lifetimes.

The strategic consideration: given the 10-year distribution requirement, it may be more tax-efficient to hold real estate and Bitcoin outside of IRAs for estate planning purposes, where both assets receive a stepped-up basis at death. The SDIRA works for accumulation during your lifetime, but the transfer benefits have been significantly reduced. Consider Roth conversions of SDIRA assets during lifetime to eliminate the tax burden on heirs entirely — though converting a large SDIRA holding real estate and Bitcoin requires careful valuation and tax planning.

The Combined Exit Strategy: Never Sell Either Asset

For a high-net-worth family holding both real estate and Bitcoin, the optimal estate plan converges on a single principle: the estate planning system is the exit strategy.

Not a sale. Not a liquidation event. Not a taxable disposition. The exit is death itself — or more precisely, the stepped-up basis that death provides under current law.

Here's the combined playbook:

Real Estate Track

During life: continue 1031 exchanging as properties mature, markets shift, or management burden increases. Each exchange defers gain and resets the investment timeline. As you age and want to exit active management, 1031 into DST interests — passive, fractional, institutionally managed, and still 1031-eligible for the exchange that gets you there.

At death: all real estate (including DST interests) receives a stepped-up basis. The cumulative gain from decades of 1031 exchanges — the entire chain of deferred gains — vanishes. Heirs inherit properties at current fair market value. They can sell immediately with minimal capital gains, or continue holding for income and further appreciation.

Bitcoin Track

During life: hold. Do not sell unless you have sufficient depreciation to shield the gain, or unless you're making a charitable gift (where you donate appreciated Bitcoin at FMV and avoid recognizing the gain entirely). If you need liquidity, borrow against Bitcoin rather than selling — the loan proceeds aren't taxable income, and the Bitcoin continues appreciating inside your estate.

At death: Bitcoin receives stepped-up basis at date-of-death spot price. Transfer to a dynasty trust that holds BTC across multiple generations. The trust structure prevents individual heirs from panic-selling during drawdowns and keeps the Bitcoin position consolidated. Multisig custody within the trust ensures no single trustee can unilaterally move funds.

The Cardinal Rule

Never sell either asset if you can avoid it. The estate planning system — stepped-up basis, 1031 exchange, and generational trust structures — is the most powerful "exit" available. Every taxable sale erodes the family's wealth by 20–40% (capital gains + state tax + net investment income tax). Death erodes it by 0% on the basis step-up, plus potentially 40% in estate tax — but the estate tax can be mitigated through the unified credit ($13.99 million per person in 2026, pending legislative changes), FLP discounts, charitable structures, and irrevocable life insurance trusts funded by real estate cash flow.

The family that sells neither asset, uses real estate cash flow to fund estate tax mitigation structures, and holds Bitcoin to death will transfer dramatically more wealth than the family that sells assets to pay estate taxes or diversify.

Case Study: The Morales Family

Carlos and Maria Morales, both 62, have built a real estate portfolio worth approximately $8 million — a mix of residential multifamily and a small retail center in a secondary market. They've completed three 1031 exchanges over 25 years. Their combined adjusted basis across all properties is roughly $1.8 million, meaning there's $6.2 million in built-in gain lurking in the portfolio.

Carlos also holds 30 BTC, acquired between 2018 and 2021 at an average cost basis of $18,000 per coin — roughly $540,000 total invested. At current prices, the Bitcoin position is worth approximately $2.5 million, with nearly $2 million in unrealized gain.

Combined net worth: approximately $10.5 million in real estate and Bitcoin, plus $1.2 million in retirement accounts and $300,000 in cash. Total estate: approximately $12 million.

With the 2026 estate tax exemption at $13.99 million per person ($27.98 million for a married couple), the Morales family is currently below the exemption threshold. But given appreciation rates for both assets, they could exceed the individual exemption within a decade — especially if Bitcoin continues its historical growth trajectory.

The Plan

Step 1: Establish an FLP. Carlos and Maria contribute the real estate portfolio and 20 BTC to a family limited partnership. They retain 2% general partnership interests each (4% total) and receive 96% limited partnership interests. The FLP's operating agreement specifies different management protocols for real estate (outsourced to a property management company) and Bitcoin (held in a multisig arrangement with two of three keys required).

Step 2: Begin annual gifts of FLP units. With three adult children, the Morales couple can gift $19,000 × 2 donors × 3 donees = $114,000 per year in FLP units using the annual exclusion alone. With a 30% valuation discount, each $19,000 gift actually transfers approximately $27,143 in underlying asset value. Over 15 years, this transfers approximately $2.4 million in assets without using any lifetime gift tax exemption.

Step 3: 1031 the retail center into a DST. The retail center ($2.2 million value, $400,000 basis) is the most management-intensive property. Carlos is tired of tenant issues. They sell the retail center and 1031 into a DST — specifically a multifamily DST managed by a national sponsor. The gain is deferred. Management burden drops to zero. The DST interest stays inside the FLP.

Step 4: Maria qualifies as Real Estate Professional. Maria spends 800+ hours per year on the rental operations (coordinating with the property manager, handling capital improvements, bookkeeping, lease review). She qualifies as a REP. They run a cost segregation study on their largest multifamily property, accelerating $350,000 in depreciation into the current year.

Step 5: Strategic Bitcoin gifting. Carlos gifts 2 BTC directly to their donor-advised fund at a Bitcoin drawdown (valued at $65,000 per coin). He gets a $130,000 charitable deduction at fair market value and avoids recognizing the capital gain. The remaining 8 BTC (outside the FLP) stays in a revocable trust designated to pour into a dynasty trust at death.

Step 6: Hold everything to death. The real estate portfolio inside the FLP receives stepped-up basis when each spouse dies. The DST interest receives stepped-up basis. The Bitcoin in the dynasty trust receives stepped-up basis. The children inherit FLP units they've been receiving for years — already at discounted values — and the remaining FLP interest passes through the estate at stepped-up basis minus any applicable FLP discount.

The Result

Total built-in capital gains eliminated at death: approximately $8.2 million ($6.2M real estate + $2M Bitcoin). At a combined federal and state capital gains rate of 28%, that's approximately $2.3 million in tax savings from the stepped-up basis alone.

Wealth transferred via FLP annual gifts over 15 years: approximately $2.4 million at discounted values, using zero lifetime exemption.

Estate tax exposure: with proper planning and the combined $27.98 million exemption (2026), the Morales estate falls entirely within the exemption. Zero estate tax. Zero capital gains on inherited assets. Full wealth transfer to the next generation.

This is the power of integrating real estate and Bitcoin estate planning into a unified strategy. Neither asset class alone achieves this result. It's the combination — depreciation shielding Bitcoin gains, FLP discounts applying to both assets, 1031 deferring real estate gains while Bitcoin rides to stepped-up basis — that maximizes the transfer.

Implementation Checklist

For real estate investors holding Bitcoin, here's the prioritized action list:

  1. Determine REP status. If one spouse can qualify, the depreciation-against-Bitcoin-gains strategy unlocks immediately. Document hours meticulously — the IRS audits REP status aggressively.
  2. Run cost segregation studies. On any property acquired in the last 3–5 years where a study hasn't been done. Accelerated depreciation is the fuel for the Bitcoin gains offset strategy.
  3. Evaluate FLP formation. If you hold $5M+ in combined real estate and Bitcoin, the valuation discounts from an FLP likely justify the formation and ongoing administration costs. Get a business valuation from a qualified appraiser before you start gifting units.
  4. Separate entities by asset class. Real estate in one LLC/FLP structure, Bitcoin in another (or in a trust). Don't commingle. This protects QBI eligibility, simplifies custody, and prevents operational headaches.
  5. Review 1031 exchange chain. Map out the cumulative deferred gain across all prior exchanges. This is the number that vanishes at death via stepped-up basis — it's often much larger than people realize.
  6. Establish Bitcoin custody protocol for trusts. Whether your Bitcoin ends up in a revocable trust, dynasty trust, or FLP, the custody arrangement must be defined now: multisig threshold, key holders, recovery procedures, and trustee access. Don't leave this to your executor to figure out.
  7. Model the alternate valuation date. Work with your estate planning attorney to pre-model scenarios where Bitcoin drops or real estate drops in the six months after death. The alternate valuation date election can save millions, but it requires analysis across both asset classes simultaneously.
  8. Consider DST for management transition. If you're within 10 years of wanting to exit active real estate management, start identifying DST options for your next 1031 exchange. The transition to passive ownership should happen while you're still healthy enough to manage the exchange process.
  9. Fund an ILIT with real estate cash flow. An irrevocable life insurance trust funded by rental income can provide estate tax liquidity without selling either real estate or Bitcoin. The premiums are paid from cash flow, and the death benefit is excluded from the gross estate.
  10. Coordinate your professional team. You need a real estate attorney, a crypto-literate estate planning attorney, a CPA who understands both depreciation and digital asset reporting, and ideally a financial advisor who can model the combined portfolio. No single professional covers all of this. The integration is on you — or your family office.

The Bottom Line

Real estate investors who hold Bitcoin are sitting on the most powerful estate planning opportunity in the current tax code — but only if they recognize that the two asset classes require parallel strategies with carefully coordinated intersection points.

The depreciation from real estate can shield Bitcoin gains. The 1031 exchange defers real estate gains while Bitcoin waits for its own exit through stepped-up basis. The FLP wraps both assets in discounted packaging for generational transfer. And the DST provides the off-ramp from active management without triggering the tax bomb.

None of this works if you treat real estate and Bitcoin as separate problems with separate advisors who never talk to each other. The alpha is in the integration. The wealth transfer is in the coordination. And the estate planning system itself — not any sale, not any exit, not any liquidity event — is the exit strategy.

Hold both. Shield gains with depreciation. Transfer at a discount. Die with the assets. Let your heirs inherit a stepped-up basis on everything.

That's the plan. Execute it.
