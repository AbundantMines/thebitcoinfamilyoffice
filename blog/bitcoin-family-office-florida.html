Bitcoin Family Office in Florida: Complete 2026 Jurisdiction Guide

By Hal Franklin · February 26, 2026 · ~20 min read

Florida has become the destination of choice for high-net-worth individuals relocating from high-tax states — and Bitcoin families are no exception. Since 2020, Florida has experienced an extraordinary concentration of Bitcoin wealth migration, driven by a combination of factors that compound powerfully: no state income tax, no state estate tax, unlimited homestead creditor protection, a growing Bitcoin professional services ecosystem anchored in Miami, and a political environment that has been consistently and vocally supportive of Bitcoin as a legitimate form of wealth.

This guide is for Bitcoin holders who live in Florida, are considering relocating there, or who want to understand how Florida compares to other premier Bitcoin jurisdictions like Wyoming and Texas. We cover Florida's tax structure, its trust and LLC laws, the mechanics of domicile change planning, estate planning considerations specific to Florida, and a complete step-by-step guide to establishing a Bitcoin family office in the Sunshine State.

Section 1: Florida's Tax Advantages for Bitcoin Holders

The fundamental appeal of Florida for Bitcoin families is straightforward: the state imposes no income tax, no capital gains tax, no estate tax, and no inheritance tax. These four zeros, applied to a Bitcoin estate, can translate into tens of millions of dollars in savings relative to high-tax states over a family's lifetime and across generations.

No State Income Tax

Florida's constitution prohibits the state from levying a personal income tax without a vote of the people. This constitutional protection makes Florida's zero-income-tax status durable in a way that pure legislative choices are not — changing it requires a supermajority and a statewide referendum. Florida has not had a personal income tax since 1855 and the constitutional prohibition has been in place since 1924.

The practical effect for Bitcoin holders is that all capital gains from Bitcoin sales, staking rewards (if relevant), and distributions from Florida-domiciled entities are subject only to federal income tax. For a Bitcoin holder with $5 million in long-term appreciation, moving from New York (up to 10.9% combined state and city rate) or Bitcoin family office in California (13.3%) to Florida eliminates up to $665,000 in state capital gains tax on a single sale — on that single transaction alone.

No State Estate or Inheritance Tax

Florida eliminated its state estate tax in 2005. Prior to that, Florida had a "pick-up tax" that effectively piggybacked on the federal estate tax. When the federal state death tax credit was phased out, Florida's estate tax disappeared with it. The Florida legislature has not enacted a replacement estate tax, and the political environment makes reimposition extremely unlikely.

Florida also has no state inheritance tax, meaning beneficiaries who inherit Bitcoin from a Florida decedent owe no Florida tax on the inheritance. Federal estate tax still applies at the estate level for large Bitcoin estates, but Florida adds no additional state-level layer. This is particularly significant for multi-generational Bitcoin wealth transfer: each generation's inheritance is subject only to federal estate tax exposure, not a double-layer of state-plus-federal burden.

Florida Homestead Exemption — Unlimited on Primary Residence

Florida's homestead protection is among the most powerful creditor protection mechanisms available to any US resident. The Florida Constitution protects a Florida resident's primary residence from forced sale by creditors — with no dollar cap. A Florida homestead worth $50 million is as protected from creditors as one worth $300,000. (Note: this unlimited homestead exemption is separate from and more powerful than the $25,000 property tax exemption available to Florida homeowners.)

While the homestead exemption applies to real property rather than Bitcoin directly, it has an important indirect effect: it protects a Bitcoin family's most significant non-Bitcoin asset (their home), allowing more of the family's liquid resources to be deployed in Bitcoin wealth preservation structures rather than kept as a "safe" illiquid asset. Florida homestead creditor protection is so powerful that many high-net-worth individuals from across the country have held Florida real estate specifically for its creditor protection function.

How These Tax Advantages Compound for Bitcoin Families

The real power of Florida's tax environment is not any single advantage but the combination. A Bitcoin family that moves to Florida, holds Bitcoin in a properly structured entity, and establishes proper estate planning enjoys:

  • Zero state income tax on Bitcoin gains during their lifetime
  • Zero state estate tax on their Bitcoin estate at death
  • Zero state inheritance tax for their beneficiaries
  • Unlimited homestead protection for their primary residence
  • Tenancy by the entirety protection for jointly held Bitcoin (for married couples)

Section 2: Florida Trust Law

Florida's trust law framework is modern and well-developed, codified in the Florida Trust Code (Chapter 736, Florida Statutes), which was enacted in 2007 and modeled substantially on the Uniform Trust Code. While Florida's trust law is not as aggressively innovated as Wyoming's or South Dakota's, it provides solid foundations for Bitcoin wealth preservation with flexibility that has been incrementally improved by legislative amendments since 2007.

Florida Trust Code (2007, Modeled on UTC)

The Florida Trust Code establishes a comprehensive framework for trust creation, administration, trustee duties, and beneficiary rights. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families include:

  • Flexible trustee investment duties under the Florida Prudent Investor Act, which allows trustees to hold unconventional assets (including Bitcoin) if the investment is prudent in the context of the overall portfolio and documented in an Investment Policy Statement
  • Broad trustee discretion provisions allowing the trust document to grant trustees expansive or narrow investment authority
  • Clear rules for trust modification, reformation, and termination, including non-judicial modifications with beneficiary consent
  • Detailed provisions on trustee liability and exculpation clauses that can limit trustee liability for Bitcoin-specific investment decisions

Florida Revocable Trust — The Estate Planning Workhorse

For most Florida Bitcoin families, the revocable living trust is the starting point for estate planning. A Florida revocable trust:

  • Allows Bitcoin to be held in a trust during the grantor's lifetime, with the grantor maintaining full control and revocation rights
  • Transfers Bitcoin to beneficiaries at death according to trust terms, entirely avoiding Florida probate
  • Can be amended at any time during the grantor's lifetime as circumstances change
  • Provides clear instructions for a successor trustee to manage Bitcoin custody upon the grantor's death or incapacity — critical for Bitcoin where key access must be carefully managed

A revocable trust does not provide asset protection during the grantor's lifetime (since the grantor retains full control, creditors can reach trust assets). For asset protection, Florida families typically use irrevocable structures — LLCs, FLPs, or irrevocable trusts — in combination with the revocable trust.

Dynasty Trusts in Florida — 360-Year Perpetuities Period

Florida allows dynasty trusts that can last up to 360 years under its "wait-and-see" rule against perpetuities statute. A properly structured Florida dynasty trust can hold Bitcoin across many generations without mandatory termination. The 360-year maximum is significantly longer than traditional rule-against-perpetuities jurisdictions (typically 90 years), but shorter than Wyoming and South Dakota's perpetual trusts.

For Bitcoin families that want a truly perpetual dynasty trust, Florida law creates a structural reason to consider Wyoming or South Dakota as the trust situs while maintaining Florida domicile. This is a common and entirely legitimate planning approach: a Florida family establishes a Wyoming dynasty trust (governed by Wyoming law, administered by a Wyoming trustee) that holds Bitcoin for unlimited generations, while the family members live in Florida and benefit from Florida's tax and homestead advantages.

Florida Qualified Spendthrift Trust

Florida law recognizes spendthrift trusts that protect beneficiaries' interests from their creditors. A Florida trust with a spendthrift clause prevents beneficiaries' creditors from reaching trust assets before distribution. This protection is meaningful but has more exceptions than Wyoming's spendthrift law — Florida courts have recognized exceptions for certain creditors including child support, alimony, and in some cases tort judgment creditors. For the most robust spendthrift protection, Wyoming or South Dakota trusts are generally superior to Florida trusts.

Directed Trusts in Florida

Florida has adopted directed trust provisions that allow the trust document to separate investment and distribution authority. An investment advisor or directed trustee can be designated to make investment decisions — including Bitcoin custody decisions — separately from the administrative trustee's other functions. For Bitcoin trusts, this allows a technically competent Bitcoin advisor to control custody strategy while a Florida corporate trustee handles administrative and distribution functions.

Florida's directed trust statute is less developed than Wyoming's equivalent — it does not provide the same depth of statutory protection for trustees following directed investment decisions — but it is sufficient for most family office applications when properly drafted.

Section 3: Florida LLC for Bitcoin Holdings

Florida LLC Bitcoin family office in Nevada Protection

Florida's charging order protection for LLCs is solid, though not as strong as Wyoming's. Florida's LLC Act provides that a judgment creditor of an LLC member's only remedy is to obtain a charging order — the creditor cannot foreclose on the membership interest or force dissolution of the LLC to satisfy the debt. This protects Bitcoin held in a Florida LLC from the personal creditors of LLC members. However, unlike Wyoming, Florida's charging order protection for single-member LLCs has been somewhat less clear in case law, making Wyoming LLCs the preferred choice for pure holding entities with a single member.

Florida Series LLC

Florida adopted Series LLC legislation that allows a single Florida LLC to have multiple series, each with separate assets, liabilities, and members. For a Bitcoin family office, a Florida Series LLC can hold different categories of Bitcoin (long-term, operational, philanthropic) in separate legally protected cells. Florida's Series LLC statute is less extensively tested in the courts than Texas's, so for the most critical Bitcoin holdings, a Wyoming Series LLC may provide more predictable legal outcomes.

Florida Family Limited Partnership (FLP) — Popular for Miami and Boca High-Net-Worth Families

The Florida FLP remains one of the most popular estate planning vehicles for high-net-worth Florida families, particularly in the Miami, Boca Raton, and Palm Beach markets where multi-generational wealth planning is deeply embedded in the professional services culture. A Florida FLP holding Bitcoin allows:

  • The founding generation to retain control as general partners while transferring limited partnership interests to children and grandchildren
  • Valuation discounts for limited partnership interests (typically 20–35% for lack of control and lack of marketability), reducing the taxable value of gifts
  • Centralized control over Bitcoin custody and investment decisions while distributing economic interests across the family
  • Charging order protection for limited partnership interests from the creditors of limited partners

FLPs must be structured and maintained with genuine non-tax business purpose and proper formality to survive IRS challenge. The business purpose requirement is typically met by documenting investment management, family governance, and asset protection purposes.

Operating Agreement Digital Asset Provisions

Whether using a Florida LLC, Series LLC, or FLP, the governing document should include Bitcoin-specific provisions:

  • Explicit authority to hold and manage digital assets as defined under Florida law and applicable federal law
  • Custody protocol requirements (multisignature standards, hardware wallet requirements, key management procedures)
  • Authorized signatory requirements for any Bitcoin transaction — ideally requiring multiple manager approvals for any transfer above a minimum threshold
  • Succession provisions designating who takes custody authority on the death or incapacity of key managers
  • Fork, airdrop, and protocol change handling procedures

Bitcoin-focused estate planning attorneys, tax advisors familiar with digital asset transactions, Bitcoin custodians with institutional capabilities, and family office managers with Bitcoin-specific experience are all available within Florida's major metropolitan areas. Section 5: Domicile Change Planning for Bitcoin Holders

For Bitcoin holders currently domiciled in California, New York, Illinois, or other high-tax states, a domicile change to Florida is one of the highest-return estate planning moves available. But it must be done correctly — states like California and New York are aggressively audit-oriented and will challenge domicile changes that are not properly executed and documented.

The 183-Day Rule for Florida Domicile

Spending more than 183 days per year in Florida is necessary — but not sufficient — to establish Florida domicile. The 183-day test is a threshold that many states use to assert tax jurisdiction, and passing it for Florida purposes means spending fewer than 183 days in your prior state of domicile. Keep a contemporaneous log of where you spend each night — hotel receipts, flight records, credit card transactions — that can demonstrate your Florida-majority residency if audited by a former state's tax authority.

Day count alone will not establish Florida domicile if your other connections remain in the prior state. The legal standard for domicile is your primary, permanent place of abode — the state you intend to return to when you leave. Establishing Florida as your domicile requires affirmative action to make Florida your primary home, not merely spending a few extra days there.

Abandoning New York or California Domicile — The Required Steps

A complete domicile change from a high-tax state to Florida requires the following concrete steps, executed before the tax year in which you want Florida's tax benefits to apply:

  1. Florida driver's license: Obtain a Florida driver's license and surrender your old state license. This is often cited as the single most important documentary step.
  2. Florida voter registration: Register to vote in Florida and cancel your prior state registration.
  3. Florida Declaration of Domicile: File a Declaration of Domicile with the Florida county clerk in your county of residence. This is a notarized statement asserting Florida as your domicile — it creates a public record and is strong evidence in a domicile dispute.
  4. Primary residence in Florida: Your Florida home should be your largest and most significant home. If you maintain a home in a prior state, it should be clearly secondary — smaller, used less frequently, and treated as a vacation or secondary residence in all dealings.
  5. Banking relationships: Move primary banking to Florida-based branches or institutions.
  6. Professional relationships: Move primary attorney, accountant, and financial advisor relationships to Florida-based professionals.
  7. Healthcare providers: Establish primary care physician, dentist, and other healthcare providers in Florida.
  8. Vehicle registration: Register vehicles in Florida.
  9. Business connections: If possible, establish or re-establish business headquarters or primary business relationships in Florida.

Audit-Proofing a Florida Domicile Change

New York and California are the two states most aggressive in auditing domicile changes. New York's "statutory residency" rule creates a separate basis for taxation: if you maintain a permanent place of abode in New York and spend more than 183 days there in a year, New York treats you as a New York resident for income tax purposes — regardless of your claimed domicile. This means that even after establishing Florida domicile, you must count New York days carefully if you maintain a New York home.

California takes a similarly aggressive stance on "safe harbor" requirements for domicile changes. California's Franchise Tax Board (FTB) has a comprehensive audit program for taxpayers who claim domicile changes to zero-tax states, focusing on the "closest contacts" test — which state has the strongest ties to the individual's daily life.

Audit-proofing a Florida domicile change requires:

  • Maintaining contemporaneous records of all travel and location (a day-count log with corroborating receipts)
  • Reducing or eliminating real property ownership in the prior state where possible
  • Shifting as many personal and professional activities as possible to Florida
  • Consulting a multi-state tax attorney before and during the transition year

State-Level Capital Gains Tax Avoidance — Timing Considerations

For Bitcoin holders planning a large realization event (selling significant Bitcoin, transferring Bitcoin to a trust, or other taxable transactions), the timing of the domicile change relative to the transaction matters enormously. A taxable Bitcoin sale completed before effective Florida domicile is established will be subject to your prior state's income tax. A taxable sale completed after effective Florida domicile is established will not. This means that for a large Bitcoin position, it may be worth deferring a planned sale by months — or even a year — to ensure the Florida domicile is unambiguously established before the taxable event.

Multi-state tax attorneys frequently advise on "look-back" periods — the length of time your prior state may assert that a gain was earned while you were still its resident. California, notably, taxes gains on some assets that were accrued during California residency regardless of when sold — making early planning and careful documentation particularly important for California expatriates.

Section 6: Florida-Specific Estate Planning for Bitcoin Holders

Florida's Elective Share and Bitcoin

One of the most important and often overlooked features of Florida estate law for Bitcoin families is the elective share. Under Florida law (Section 732.2135, Florida Statutes), a surviving spouse is entitled to elect against the decedent's estate and receive 30% of the "elective estate." The elective estate is broadly defined and includes not only probate assets but also revocable trust assets, POD/TOD designations, and certain other transfers — meaning that Bitcoin held in a revocable trust may be subject to the surviving spouse's elective share claim.

For Bitcoin families where both spouses are committed to a multi-generational trust structure, the best approach is to have both spouses participate in and consent to the dynasty trust structure from the outset — making mutual waivers of elective share rights (if enforceable under the specific circumstances) or co-trustee arrangements that give the surviving spouse meaningful participation in the trust management. Elective share planning is particularly complex when one spouse holds significantly more Bitcoin than the other, or when pre-marital Bitcoin wealth is being protected in a trust structure.

Homestead Interaction with Bitcoin Estate Planning

Florida homestead law is protective but also restrictive. A Florida homestead cannot be devised by will to anyone other than a spouse or lineal descendants if the decedent had a spouse or minor children at death. This restriction — a constitutional provision — means that a Bitcoin family cannot simply leave their Florida primary residence to a dynasty trust if they have a surviving spouse or minor children. The interplay between homestead protection, homestead descent restrictions, and trust planning requires careful attention from a Florida estate planning attorney.

Bitcoin, not being real property, does not have the same descent restrictions. A Bitcoin holding LLC or trust can be structured to distribute Bitcoin to whatever combination of beneficiaries the family chooses, without the homestead descent constraints that apply to real property. This is one area where Bitcoin's nature as personal property (not real property) actually simplifies estate planning relative to the family's real estate holdings.

Avoiding Florida Probate for Bitcoin

Florida probate is a public process, potentially slow (particularly for contested or complex estates), and creates unnecessary security risks for Bitcoin — having a personal representative manage Bitcoin custody during a probate proceeding is not an appropriate security arrangement for meaningful Bitcoin holdings. Avoiding probate for Bitcoin is therefore not just an efficiency goal but a security imperative.

Bitcoin can be held outside of Florida probate through:

  • Revocable living trust: Bitcoin held in a properly funded revocable trust passes to beneficiaries outside of probate according to trust terms, without court involvement
  • LLC succession: Bitcoin held in a Florida or Wyoming LLC with a proper succession provision in the operating agreement transfers management authority without a probate proceeding
  • TOD (transfer on death) designation: Where supported by the custodian, a Bitcoin wallet or account can have a designated beneficiary who receives the Bitcoin directly at death — though this is not widely supported by all custodians
  • Tenancy by the entirety: Bitcoin held jointly by a Florida married couple as tenants by the entirety passes to the surviving spouse automatically at death outside of probate — and also provides creditor protection during joint lifetimes

Digital Assets in Florida — RUFADAA (2016)

Florida adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) in 2016, giving fiduciaries — including trustees, personal representatives, and agents under durable power of attorney — the legal authority to access and manage digital assets owned by a deceased or incapacitated person. This is foundational infrastructure for Bitcoin estate administration in Florida.

Under Florida's RUFADAA, to give a fiduciary access to Bitcoin, the owner should:

  • Use an online tool provided by the custodian (if available) to designate a beneficiary or agent with access rights
  • If no online tool is available, include explicit authorization in the trust document or durable power of attorney granting the fiduciary access to digital assets
  • Never rely solely on a will for digital asset access — wills are probate documents and Bitcoin access through a probate proceeding is neither quick nor secure

Setting up a Bitcoin family office in Florida follows a clear sequence. Here is a practical framework, with realistic timelines and cost estimates.

2
Engage Florida Estate Planning Counsel
Select a Florida attorney with digital asset experience and knowledge of both Florida trust law and Wyoming trust law (for hybrid structures). Budget $400–$700/hour for senior estate planning attorneys in major Florida markets.
3
Draft a Florida Revocable Living Trust
For the operational layer — holding assets during your lifetime, providing successor trustee authority on incapacity, and distributing assets at death outside of probate. Cost: $3,000–$8,000 depending on complexity. Timeline: 2–4 weeks.

For long-term Bitcoin holdings — perpetual duration, stronger spendthrift protection, purpose-built digital asset infrastructure. Annual trustee fees: 0.25%–0.75% AUM.
5
Form a Wyoming or Florida LLC for Bitcoin Holdings
For the holding entity beneath the trust — Wyoming LLC for superior charging order protection ($100 formation + $50–$200/year registered agent), or Florida LLC for operational entities. Draft a Bitcoin-specific operating agreement. Cost: $1,500–$5,000 for operating agreement drafting.
6
Establish Multisig Bitcoin Custody
Implement a 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multisig custody arrangement with geographically distributed keys. Document the custody configuration in the entity's records. Consider a third-party institutional custodian for at least one key. Cost: $500–$2,000 for hardware; ongoing for institutional custodian.
7
Fund the Structure
Transfer Bitcoin to the LLC or trust on-chain, with documentation of the transfer for tax purposes. If the transfer has gift tax implications, file a gift tax return. Engage a tax advisor to manage the funding transaction and document cost basis correctly.
8
Ongoing Compliance and Review
Annual trust accounting and tax return (Form 1041 for irrevocable trusts; Schedule E for LLCs). Annual review of custody arrangements and operating agreement. Quarterly review of investment policy statement. Budget $5,000–$12,000/year for ongoing tax and legal compliance.

Conclusion: Florida as the Optimal Bitcoin Family Domicile

Florida is not the most technically sophisticated jurisdiction for Bitcoin trust and LLC structures — Wyoming holds that distinction by a meaningful margin. But Florida is where the right Bitcoin families should live. The combination of zero state income tax, zero estate tax, unlimited homestead protection, a world-class lifestyle, and a growing Bitcoin-native professional ecosystem makes Florida the dominant choice for Bitcoin family domicile.

The insight that separates sophisticated Bitcoin family planning from naive approaches is that domicile and structure are separate decisions. Live in Florida. Hold Bitcoin in Wyoming. Use the best jurisdiction for each purpose, and don't let geography constrain your structural decisions. A Florida-domiciled family with a Wyoming dynasty trust holding Bitcoin in a Wyoming LLC, with a properly drafted Florida revocable trust for operational assets and a complete Florida RUFADAA-compliant estate plan, has built the optimal multi-jurisdictional framework available to a US-based Bitcoin family today.

The families who build this infrastructure now — before a health crisis, market event, or estate settlement forces the issue — are the ones who will successfully transfer Bitcoin wealth to the next generation. Structure is not a luxury; it is the difference between wealth that endures and wealth that dissipates.
