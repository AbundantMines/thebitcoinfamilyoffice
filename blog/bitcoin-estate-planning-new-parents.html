Bitcoin Estate Planning for New Parents: What to Do After Having a Baby

Published March 16, 2026 · 22 min read

You just had a baby. Congratulations — genuinely. The sleep deprivation is real, the love is overwhelming, and somewhere between the 3 a.m. feeding and the fourth diaper change of the night, a thought creeps in that you've never had before:

What happens to everything if something happens to us?

If you hold Bitcoin, that question has layers most new parents never have to think about. A bank account has beneficiary designations. A brokerage account passes through your estate with a few forms. But Bitcoin — especially self-custodied Bitcoin — can vanish permanently if the people who hold the keys aren't around to access it.

This guide is for Bitcoin-holding new parents who know they need an estate plan but haven't done it yet. No judgment. Most people don't think about estate planning until they have a child. You're right on schedule. Let's get to work.

Why a Baby Changes Everything About Estate Planning

Before your child was born, estate planning was theoretically important. Now it's urgent. Here's why the math changes overnight:

You now have a dependent who cannot care for themselves. If both parents die without an estate plan, a court decides who raises your child. Not your family. Not your closest friends. A judge who has never met you, working from a stack of petitions filed by relatives who may or may not share your values.

For families with significant Bitcoin holdings, the stakes compound. Without proper planning:

  • Your Bitcoin could end up in a court-supervised conservatorship where a judge controls spending decisions
  • A court-appointed administrator with zero crypto knowledge could be forced to liquidate your BTC at the worst possible time
  • Your child could receive the full inheritance at age 18 — old enough to buy a Lamborghini, not necessarily old enough to manage generational wealth
  • If your Bitcoin is self-custodied, the seed phrases could be lost entirely, turning your life's savings into an unrecoverable string of words

None of this is hypothetical. These are the default outcomes when Bitcoin holders die without a plan and minor children are involved.

The good news: this is entirely solvable. A proper Bitcoin estate plan takes a few weeks to set up and costs far less than most new parents expect.

The Three Documents Every New Parent Needs — This Month

Estate planning attorneys will tell you there are dozens of documents you might eventually need. Ignore that for now. As a new parent with Bitcoin, you need three things immediately. Everything else can come later.

1. A Will with Guardian Designation

The will's most critical function for new parents isn't distributing assets — it's naming who raises your child. This is the only legal document where you can designate a guardian for your minor children. Without it, the court decides. More on guardian selection below — it deserves its own section because it's the most important decision you'll make in this process.

A common mistake: writing a will that leaves everything directly to your child. For Bitcoin holders, this is a particularly bad idea. We'll cover why in the trust section.

2. A Revocable Living Trust

This is the workhorse of your Bitcoin estate plan. A revocable living trust lets you:

  • Control when your child receives their inheritance — not just at 18 when the law says they're an adult, but at ages you choose (25, 30, 35 are common staggered distributions)
  • Name a trustee who manages the assets until your child is ready — and that trustee can be someone different from the guardian who raises them
  • Include Bitcoin-specific instructions — how to access wallets, when to sell (or not sell), how to handle private keys
  • Avoid probate — your trust assets transfer immediately without court involvement, which means no public record of your Bitcoin holdings

For Bitcoin holders specifically, the trust is more important than the will. The will handles guardianship. The trust handles everything else.

3. Durable Power of Attorney + Healthcare Proxy

These aren't about death — they're about incapacity. If you're in a car accident and survive but can't make decisions, who manages your Bitcoin? Who makes medical decisions for you? Without these documents, your spouse may need to petition a court for authority — even over jointly held assets. For self-custodied Bitcoin that only you can access, a power of attorney with crypto-specific language is essential.

Your healthcare proxy (also called a healthcare power of attorney or advance directive) ensures someone you trust makes medical decisions if you can't. Every new parent needs one. Period.

Guardian Designation: The Most Important Decision You'll Make

Ask any estate planning attorney what keeps new parents up at night, and they'll say the same thing: choosing a guardian. It's an emotionally loaded decision, and most couples agonize over it far longer than they should.

Here's the framework that actually works.

What You're Really Deciding

You're choosing who will raise your child to adulthood if both parents die. Not who babysits them. Not who loves them most. Who will make daily parenting decisions — school, discipline, values, lifestyle — for the next 18 years.

Factors That Actually Matter

Values alignment. Does this person share your fundamental approach to life? Not political opinions — deeper than that. How they think about work ethic, education, money, independence.

Practical capacity. Are they physically, emotionally, and financially capable of adding a child to their life? A 72-year-old grandparent who adores your child may not be the right choice for a newborn who needs 18 years of active parenting.

Willingness. Have you actually asked them? This seems obvious, but many parents name guardians in their will without ever having the conversation. Don't do that. It's a massive ask — make sure they're genuinely willing, not just obligated.

Location and stability. Where does this person live? Would your child need to change schools, leave friends, move across the country? Stability matters enormously for a child who just lost both parents.

Existing family dynamics. Do they already have children? How would your child fit into their household? Sometimes the person you'd pick as a friend isn't the person whose household best accommodates another child.

The Split-Role Strategy: Why Bitcoin Families Should Separate Guardian and Trustee

Here's where Bitcoin families have a unique consideration. The person who's best at raising children is often not the person who's best at managing a significant Bitcoin portfolio. And that's completely fine — because you can split the roles.

  • Guardian — raises your child day-to-day. Named in your will.
  • Trustee — manages the money (including Bitcoin) on your child's behalf. Named in your trust.

This separation creates a natural system of checks and balances. The guardian requests funds from the trust for the child's needs. The trustee evaluates those requests based on the trust's terms. Neither person has unchecked control.

For Bitcoin specifically, your trustee needs to understand — or be willing to learn — how cryptocurrency custody works. They don't need to be a crypto expert, but they need to be capable of working with the technical instructions you'll leave behind (more on that in the seed phrase section).

A common mistake to avoid: naming your parents as both guardian and trustee out of default loyalty. Your parents may be wonderful grandparents and terrible Bitcoin managers — or vice versa. Match the role to the person's actual strengths.

What Happens If You Don't Name a Guardian

If both parents die without a guardian designation, the probate court holds a hearing. Family members petition for custody. If multiple relatives want the child, it turns into a contested hearing — essentially a custody battle, but without either parent there to advocate for the outcome they would have wanted.

The court will decide based on "best interests of the child" — a standard that's well-intentioned but impossible to apply perfectly when the judge has never met your family. Name a guardian. Name an alternate. Do it this month.

Building a Bitcoin-Specific Trust for Your Children

A revocable living trust is the single most important document in a Bitcoin holder's estate plan. For new parents, it's non-negotiable. Here's why a simple will — leaving Bitcoin directly to your minor child — creates a cascade of problems.

Why "I Leave My Bitcoin to My Child" Doesn't Work

Minors can't legally own property. If you die and your will leaves Bitcoin to your 2-year-old, the court must appoint a conservator to manage those assets until your child turns 18. That conservator is court-supervised, meaning every significant financial decision — including whether to sell Bitcoin — requires court approval.

Think about what that means in practice. Bitcoin drops 40% and the conservator wants to hold? They may need court permission. Bitcoin spikes and the conservator wants to rebalance? Court permission. The conservator needs to move Bitcoin from one wallet to another for security reasons? You guessed it — court permission. For an asset as volatile and technically complex as Bitcoin, court-supervised management is a recipe for value destruction.

Your child gets everything at 18. Without a trust, the moment your child reaches the age of majority, they receive the full inheritance outright. No restrictions, no guidance, no guardrails. If you're holding 8 BTC today and Bitcoin appreciates significantly over the next 18 years, you could be handing a teenager millions of dollars with zero oversight.

Some 18-year-olds would handle that responsibly. Most wouldn't. A trust solves this completely.

How a Revocable Living Trust Works for Bitcoin

A revocable living trust is a legal entity you create during your lifetime. You transfer your assets — including Bitcoin — into the trust. While you're alive, you control everything. You're the trustee, and you can add, remove, or change anything at any time.

When you die, the trust becomes irrevocable and your successor trustee takes over. For your children, the trust document specifies:

  • Age-staggered distributions: Instead of dumping the full inheritance on an 18-year-old, you release it in stages. A common structure is one-third at 25, one-third at 30, and the remaining third at 35. This gives your child time to mature, make mistakes with smaller amounts, and develop financial responsibility before receiving the bulk of the inheritance.
  • Trustee discretion for needs: Before the distribution ages, the trustee can use trust funds for your child's health, education, maintenance, and support. Your kid still gets what they need — they just don't get unrestricted access to the capital.
  • Bitcoin-specific provisions: Your trust can include language about how Bitcoin should be custodied, when (if ever) it should be sold, and how the trustee should handle private keys. This is where a Bitcoin estate plan diverges significantly from a traditional one.
  • Spendthrift protection: A properly drafted trust protects the assets from your child's future creditors, divorcing spouses, and lawsuits. The Bitcoin stays in the trust, shielded from external claims.

Trustee Selection for Bitcoin Trusts

Choosing a trustee for a Bitcoin trust is different from choosing a trustee for a traditional investment portfolio. Your trustee needs to be:

  • Technically capable or willing to learn: They need to follow your instructions for accessing cold storage, managing hardware wallets, or interfacing with exchanges. They don't need to be a Bitcoin developer — but they can't be someone who refuses to touch technology.
  • Financially responsible: They're managing potentially significant wealth on behalf of your child. Poor financial judgment disqualifies someone regardless of their technical skills.
  • Trustworthy with access to private keys: This person will eventually have access to your seed phrases. That's an enormous amount of trust. Choose accordingly.
  • Available for the long term: If your child is a newborn, the trustee may need to serve for 25-35 years. Name successors in case your primary trustee can't continue.

Corporate Trustees: When to Consider One

If you don't have someone in your life who checks all the boxes — technical competence, financial responsibility, long-term availability, absolute trustworthiness — consider a corporate trustee. Trust companies and some Bitcoin-native custodians now offer trustee services specifically designed for digital asset trusts.

The downside: fees (typically 0.5-1.5% of assets annually) and impersonal management. The upside: professional administration, continuity that doesn't depend on one person's health, and increasingly sophisticated Bitcoin custody infrastructure.

A hybrid approach works well for many families: name a trusted individual as co-trustee alongside a corporate trustee. The individual provides personal judgment and family knowledge. The corporate trustee provides infrastructure and continuity.

The Seed Phrase Problem: How Your Trustee Actually Gets the Bitcoin

This is where Bitcoin estate planning gets genuinely unique. Traditional assets — stocks, bonds, real estate — can be located and accessed through institutional records. If you die with a Fidelity account, your executor contacts Fidelity, provides a death certificate, and eventually gains access. The assets don't vanish because the account holder died.

Self-custodied Bitcoin has no such safety net. If nobody knows your seed phrase, your Bitcoin is gone. Not frozen. Not pending. Gone. Permanently unrecoverable. For a new parent with significant Bitcoin holdings, solving the seed phrase problem isn't optional — it's the most urgent technical challenge in your entire estate plan.

Creating a Letter of Instructions

A Letter of Instructions is a separate document — not part of your will or trust — that provides detailed information about how to access your Bitcoin. It should include:

  • What you own: A complete inventory of your Bitcoin holdings, including hardware wallets, exchange accounts, and any other cryptocurrency
  • Where the keys are: Location of seed phrases, hardware wallets, PINs, and passphrases
  • How to access it: Step-by-step instructions for someone who may not be deeply familiar with Bitcoin. Assume your trustee knows the basics but has never recovered a wallet from a seed phrase.
  • Who to contact for help: Names and contact information for any advisors, attorneys, or technical consultants who can assist with the process
  • What NOT to do: Equally important — warn against common mistakes like entering seed phrases on websites, sharing recovery information over email, or rushing the process

Why the Letter of Instructions Stays OUT of Your Will

Your will becomes a public document when it enters probate. Anyone can read it. If your seed phrases or wallet locations are in your will, they're effectively published. The Letter of Instructions stays private — shared only with your trustee and stored securely.

For detailed guidance on seed phrase security and storage strategies that survive your death, see our guide on cold storage estate planning.

Keeping the Letter Current

A Letter of Instructions is only useful if it's accurate. Set a calendar reminder to review it every six months. Update it whenever you:

  • Move Bitcoin between wallets
  • Change your custody setup (new hardware wallet, new multisig arrangement)
  • Open or close exchange accounts
  • Change your passphrase or PIN

This is the single most common failure point in Bitcoin estate plans. People create the letter, put it in a safe, and never update it. Two years later, it's useless because they've completely reorganized their custody setup. Treat it like a living document.

Life Insurance: Funding the Trust When You Have Bitcoin but Not Cash

Here's a scenario that's more common than most people realize: a couple in their early 30s holds 5-10 BTC (potentially worth hundreds of thousands to over a million dollars) but doesn't have significant liquid savings or income. The Bitcoin is the wealth. But Bitcoin is volatile, and if both parents die during a bear market, the trust may not have enough value to fund a child's upbringing through age 18.

Term life insurance solves this problem cheaply and efficiently.

How Much Coverage New Parents Need

The standard rule of thumb is 10-20x your annual income in term life insurance. For Bitcoin-holding new parents, consider coverage that accounts for:

  • Child's living expenses to age 18: Housing, food, clothing, activities — whatever it costs the guardian to raise your child. Estimate $20,000-40,000 per year depending on location.
  • Education: K-12 private school if that's your preference, plus four years of college. Budget $200,000-500,000+ total if you want to fully fund this.
  • Trust funding: Enough to ensure the trust has adequate resources even if Bitcoin's price is depressed at the time of your death.
  • Guardian compensation: Raising someone else's child is an enormous commitment. Your trust should allow the trustee to compensate the guardian for additional housing costs, childcare, and other expenses. Factor this into your insurance amount.

For a couple in their early 30s, a 20-year term policy of $1-2 million costs roughly $50-100 per month. That's remarkably cheap insurance against the scenario where your child needs to be raised by someone else.

Policy Ownership: Personal vs. ILIT

If your total estate (including Bitcoin) might exceed the federal estate tax exemption — currently around $13.6 million per person, though this may change — consider owning your life insurance through an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT). When you personally own a policy and die, the death benefit is included in your taxable estate. When an ILIT owns the policy, it isn't.

For most new parents, personal ownership is fine. The estate tax exemption is high enough that you'd need a very large Bitcoin position for this to matter. But if you're holding 50+ BTC and Bitcoin appreciates significantly, the ILIT conversation becomes relevant. Talk to your estate planning attorney about where you fall.

UTMA Accounts vs. Trust: Why the Trust Almost Always Wins for Bitcoin

Some parents consider Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (UTMA) accounts as a simpler alternative to a trust. UTMA accounts let you transfer assets to a minor with a custodian managing them until the child reaches 18 or 21 (depending on your state).

For Bitcoin, UTMA accounts have significant drawbacks:

  • Mandatory distribution at 18 or 21: The child gets full, unrestricted access to everything in the account when they reach the age of majority. There's no option to extend custodial control. If Bitcoin has appreciated significantly, you're handing a teenager or young adult a potentially life-changing sum with zero guardrails.
  • No Bitcoin-specific provisions: A UTMA account doesn't accommodate instructions about how to manage private keys, when to sell, or how to handle custody transitions.
  • No spendthrift protection: Once the child receives the assets, they're fully exposed to creditors, lawsuits, and divorcing spouses.
  • Irrevocable once funded: Once you put Bitcoin into a UTMA account, it belongs to the child. You can't take it back, even if circumstances change.

A trust costs more to set up ($2,000-5,000 vs. essentially free for a UTMA), but the control, flexibility, and protection it provides make it the clear winner for any Bitcoin holding of meaningful size. If you're holding a fraction of a BTC, UTMA might be fine. If your Bitcoin represents a significant portion of your family's wealth, use a trust.

Timing: Do This Before the Baby Comes Home

If you're reading this before your child is born — ideal. Get your estate plan done before the due date. Here's why timing matters so much:

The first six months are the highest-risk window for any family. Not because something is likely to happen — the odds of both parents dying are very small. But the gap between "we should do this" and "we actually did this" is where the risk lives. New parents are exhausted, overwhelmed, and focused on keeping a tiny human alive. Estate planning falls to the bottom of the priority list, and months become years.

Data from estate planning surveys consistently shows that more than half of American adults don't have a will. Among those who do, most created it after a triggering event — usually the birth of a child. But the median delay between the trigger and the action is 6-12 months. That's 6-12 months of completely unprotected risk.

What It Costs

A basic estate plan with a revocable living trust, pour-over will, power of attorney, and healthcare proxy typically costs $2,000-5,000 from a qualified estate planning attorney. Bitcoin-specific provisions (trust language for digital assets, seed phrase succession planning) may add $500-1,500 to that base.

Online legal services offer cheaper alternatives ($500-1,500), but for Bitcoin holders with significant positions, the customization and expertise of a specialized attorney is worth the premium. Generic trust templates don't include Bitcoin custody provisions, and a trust that doesn't address how the trustee accesses your Bitcoin is a trust that fails at its most important job.

The One-Week Plan

You don't need to do everything at once. Here's what you can accomplish this week:

  1. Monday: Have the guardian conversation with your partner. Agree on your top choice and an alternate.
  2. Tuesday: Call the chosen guardians. Have the conversation. Confirm they're willing.
  3. Wednesday: Research estate planning attorneys in your state who have experience with digital assets. Ask for referrals from your Bitcoin network.
  4. Thursday: Schedule a consultation. Most attorneys offer a free or low-cost initial meeting.
  5. Friday: Start your Letter of Instructions. Write down what you own, where it's stored, and how to access it. Even a rough first draft is better than nothing.

The estate plan itself will take 2-4 weeks to draft and finalize with your attorney. But by the end of this week, you'll have the most critical decisions made and the process in motion.

Already Have a Will? What to Update After Having a Baby

If you created a will or estate plan before your child was born, it almost certainly needs updating. Here's what to look for:

Common Gaps in Pre-Baby Estate Plans

  • No guardian designation: If you didn't have children when you created your will, there's no guardian named. This is the most critical addition.
  • Spouse as sole beneficiary with no contingency: Many couples' wills say "everything to my spouse." That's fine if your spouse survives you. But what if you both die? Without contingent beneficiary provisions — ideally flowing into a trust — your assets go through intestate succession (state law decides who gets what).
  • No trust provisions for minors: Even if your will names your child as a contingent beneficiary, without a trust, they receive the inheritance outright at 18. Add a testamentary trust or create a standalone revocable living trust.
  • No Bitcoin-specific language: If your pre-baby estate plan was created before you held Bitcoin, it won't include provisions for digital asset custody, seed phrase succession, or trustee selection criteria based on crypto competency.
  • Outdated executor/trustee designations: Life changes. The person you named as executor five years ago may no longer be the best choice now that children and Bitcoin are in the picture.

Schedule a review with your attorney within 60 days of your child's birth. Most attorneys will update an existing plan for $500-1,500 — significantly less than starting from scratch.

If your existing estate plan doesn't include provisions for children at all, treat this as a new planning engagement rather than a simple update. The structural changes — adding a trust, naming guardians, creating the Letter of Instructions — are substantial enough to warrant a fresh approach.

Bitcoin ETF vs. Self-Custody: The New Parent Simplicity Tradeoff

This is going to be a controversial section for Bitcoin maximalists, but it needs to be said: for some new parents, moving Bitcoin from self-custody to a spot Bitcoin ETF or regulated exchange dramatically simplifies estate planning.

Why ETFs Simplify Succession

A Bitcoin ETF (like IBIT, FBTC, or similar products) is held in a standard brokerage account. When you die:

  • Your executor contacts the brokerage with a death certificate
  • The account transfers to your trust or beneficiary through standard financial infrastructure
  • No seed phrases, no hardware wallets, no technical knowledge required
  • The trustee managing your child's inheritance can handle it exactly like any other investment

There's no seed phrase problem. There's no risk of permanent loss due to technical failure. The succession process is identical to inheriting stocks or bonds.

The Tradeoff

You're giving up self-custody — the foundational principle for many Bitcoin holders. You're trusting a financial institution with your Bitcoin. You're paying management fees (typically 0.15-0.25% annually). And you're losing some of the privacy and censorship resistance that attracted many people to Bitcoin in the first place.

This isn't a values judgment. It's a practical decision. Some new parents look at their self-custody setup, consider the seed phrase succession challenge, and decide that the simplicity of an ETF is worth the tradeoffs — especially if their alternative is an estate plan that never quite gets the technical details right.

The Middle Ground

You don't have to go all-or-nothing. Some families keep a core self-custodied position (with proper estate planning around it) and move a portion to an ETF or exchange account that serves as the "easy access" inheritance vehicle. The self-custodied BTC flows through the trust with a detailed Letter of Instructions. The ETF shares flow through the trust via standard beneficiary designation.

For single parents especially, where there's no second parent to serve as backup key holder, the ETF simplification argument becomes even stronger.

Case Study: Sarah and Marcus — From No Plan to Protected in One Week

Sarah (31) and Marcus (31) just had their first child, a daughter named Ellie. They hold 8 BTC in a hardware wallet — currently their most valuable asset by a wide margin. They have a combined income of $180,000, a small emergency fund, and no estate plan whatsoever.

Here's exactly what they did.

Week 1: The Critical Decisions

Monday evening: After putting Ellie down, Sarah and Marcus had the guardian conversation. They considered Marcus's sister (35, two kids, stable household, lives nearby) and Sarah's college friend (33, no kids, financially successful but lives across the country). They chose Marcus's sister as primary guardian and Sarah's friend as alternate.

Tuesday: Marcus called his sister. She was honored and agreed. They discussed the basics — it would mean adding a child to her household, and the trust would cover all of Ellie's expenses so it wouldn't be a financial burden.

Wednesday: Sarah searched for estate planning attorneys in their state with digital asset experience. She found three through referrals from a Bitcoin meetup group and scheduled consultations for the following week.

Thursday: They discussed the split-role question. Marcus's sister was the obvious guardian choice, but she had minimal interest in Bitcoin or cryptocurrency. They decided to name Marcus's brother — a software engineer who holds his own Bitcoin — as trustee. Guardian raises Ellie. Trustee manages the Bitcoin and funds Ellie's needs through the trust.

Friday: Marcus wrote the first draft of their Letter of Instructions. He documented the hardware wallet model, its location, the seed phrase storage arrangement (split across two geographic locations), the passphrase, and step-by-step recovery instructions. He also listed their exchange accounts, a small amount of Bitcoin held at a custodian, and contact information for a Bitcoin-savvy financial advisor they'd consulted previously.

Week 2-3: The Legal Work

They met with an estate planning attorney who had digital asset experience. The attorney drafted:

  • Revocable Living Trust: Marcus and Sarah as co-trustees during their lifetimes. Marcus's brother as successor trustee. Trust provisions for Ellie: trustee discretion for health, education, and support until age 25, then one-third distribution at 25, one-third at 30, remainder at 35. Bitcoin-specific language allowing the trustee to maintain self-custody or transition to institutional custody at their discretion.
  • Pour-Over Will: Names Marcus's sister as guardian for Ellie, with Sarah's friend as alternate. Any assets not already in the trust pour over into it at death.
  • Powers of Attorney: Each spouse named the other as primary agent, with Marcus's brother as alternate. Specific language authorizing the agent to manage cryptocurrency holdings, access hardware wallets, and make custody decisions about digital assets.
  • Healthcare Proxies: Each spouse named the other, with a parent as alternate.

Week 3: Life Insurance

Their attorney recommended term life insurance to ensure the trust would be well-funded regardless of Bitcoin's price at the time of their death. They each purchased a 20-year, $1 million term policy. Combined monthly cost: about $110. The trust was named as beneficiary on both policies.

Week 4: Finalization

They signed all documents, transferred their Bitcoin holdings into the trust (a paper transfer — the Bitcoin didn't physically move, but the trust became the legal owner), and placed the Letter of Instructions in a fireproof safe at home with a copy in a bank safe deposit box.

Marcus set a six-month calendar reminder to review and update the Letter of Instructions.

Total Cost and Time

  • Attorney fees: $3,800 (revocable trust, pour-over will, POA, healthcare proxy, Bitcoin-specific provisions)
  • Life insurance: $110/month ($1,320/year)
  • Time invested: Roughly 8-10 hours total across four weeks, mostly in the attorney meetings and writing the Letter of Instructions

For less than $5,000 upfront and $110 a month, Sarah and Marcus went from completely unprotected to a comprehensive estate plan that ensures Ellie will be raised by people they chose, funded by a trust they designed, with Bitcoin access instructions that actually work.

Special Situations

If Your Child Has Special Needs

A standard revocable living trust with age-staggered distributions won't work for a child with special needs. You'll need a special needs trust (also called a supplemental needs trust) that provides for your child without disqualifying them from government benefits like SSI and Medicaid. This is highly specialized work — find an attorney with specific experience in both special needs planning and digital assets.

If You're a Single Parent

Everything in this guide applies, but with higher urgency. There's no second parent as backup. Guardian designation, trustee selection, and seed phrase succession planning are even more critical when you're the only parent. See our single parent estate planning guide for specific considerations.

If You're in a Blended Family

Children from prior relationships, stepchildren, and complex family dynamics require more nuanced trust structures. Multiple trusts (one per child or one per family unit) may be appropriate. Be explicit in your planning — ambiguity in blended family estate plans leads to litigation.

  • ☐ Decide whether to split guardian and trustee roles

  • ☐ Evaluate self-custody vs. ETF simplification for succession purposes

Get Started This Week

You don't need to have everything figured out to start. You need three decisions: who raises your child, who manages the money, and how someone accesses your Bitcoin if you're not here. Everything else — the legal documents, the trust structure, the insurance — flows from those three decisions.

Your child doesn't need a perfect estate plan. They need a plan that exists. Start there. Refine over time.
