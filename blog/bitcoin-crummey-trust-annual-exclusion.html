Bitcoin Crummey Trust: How Withdrawal Rights Unlock the Annual Gift Tax Exclusion for Irrevocable Trusts

By The Bitcoin Family Office  |  Updated March 2026  |  Estate Planning  •  Trust Structures

The annual gift tax exclusion — $19,000 per recipient in 2026 — only applies to gifts of present interests. A contribution to an irrevocable trust is ordinarily a future interest gift: the beneficiary doesn't get immediate access, so no annual exclusion applies. A Crummey trust solves this by giving beneficiaries a temporary right to withdraw each year's contribution — converting a future interest into a present interest. The withdrawal right makes the gift qualify for the annual exclusion, even though the beneficiary almost never actually exercises it.

For Bitcoin families, the Crummey structure is most commonly used to fund an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) — paying premiums gift-tax-free using annual exclusion gifts year after year. But Crummey withdrawal rights also appear in dynasty trusts, education trusts, and other irrevocable vehicles. And they carry a hidden trap: every gift subject to a Crummey withdrawal right is an "indirect skip" for generation-skipping transfer tax purposes — and can silently drain your lifetime GST exemption if you don't opt out on Form 709.

The Present Interest Requirement

IRC §2503(b) allows a donor to exclude up to $19,000 per recipient per year from gift tax — no Form 709 required, no lifetime exemption consumed. But there's a catch: the exclusion only applies to gifts of present interests — the right to immediate use, possession, or enjoyment of the gifted property.

A contribution to an irrevocable trust fails this test on its face. The beneficiaries cannot immediately access trust assets — the trustee controls them. Without the annual exclusion, every annual premium payment to an ILIT would be a taxable gift consuming the donor's lifetime exemption. A $200,000/year life insurance premium would exhaust the entire $15M exemption in 75 years.

The Crummey trust — named for the landmark 1968 Tax Court case Crummey v. Commissioner — established that a beneficiary's temporary right to withdraw a gift from a trust converts that gift into a present interest, even if the beneficiary almost certainly won't exercise the right. The IRS tried to challenge this in Crummey and lost. The technique has been IRS-approved through decades of subsequent guidance.

How the Crummey Mechanism Works

  1. Donor makes a contribution to the trust — typically cash to pay a life insurance premium, or in a Bitcoin-holding trust, a cash or Bitcoin contribution.
  2. Trustee notifies beneficiaries of their right to withdraw their pro-rata share of the contribution, within a stated window (typically 30–60 days).
  3. Beneficiaries can exercise the withdrawal right during the window. If they withdraw, they get the cash. If they don't (the intended outcome), the funds remain in the trust and the trustee uses them for the trust's purpose (e.g., paying an insurance premium).
  4. The contribution qualifies as a present interest gift because the right to withdraw existed, even if not exercised. Each beneficiary's withdrawal right generates a separate $19,000 annual exclusion for the donor.
  5. Each year, the process repeats — new contribution, new Crummey notice, new present-interest exclusions.

Scaling the annual exclusion: A donor contributing to a trust with 5 Crummey beneficiaries (spouse + 4 children) can exclude up to 5 × $19,000 = $95,000 per year from gift tax. A married couple with gift-splitting can exclude 10 × $19,000 = $190,000 per year. Used over 20 years, that's $3.8M transferred to the trust — entirely gift-tax-free.

Bitcoin and the Crummey Structure: Where It Applies

Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

The most common application. The ILIT holds a life insurance policy on the grantor's life. Annual contributions fund premium payments. Crummey withdrawal rights on each contribution allow the annual exclusion to cover the premium — no gift tax, no exemption consumed. At the grantor's death, the death benefit pays into the ILIT (not the estate), providing tax-free liquidity for estate settlement or for purchasing estate assets. For Bitcoin families, the ILIT is often the primary estate tax liquidity tool: the death benefit funds payment of estate tax on the Bitcoin without requiring any Bitcoin to be sold at death.

Dynasty Trust with Annual Exclusion Gifts

A dynasty trust can include Crummey withdrawal rights on annual contribution gifts. This allows the grantor to fund the trust with small, regular contributions gift-tax-free — building the trust's Bitcoin position over time — while preserving the lifetime exemption for larger transfers. The dynasty trust grows with each annual exclusion contribution, compounding tax-free inside the trust's GST-exempt structure.

Education / Incentive Trusts

Trusts structured to fund education, first-home purchases, or business ventures for children and grandchildren often use Crummey rights to accept annual exclusion gifts without consuming lifetime exemption.

The 5-and-5 Lapse Rule: The Key Risk for Large Crummey Powers

When a Crummey withdrawal right lapses (the beneficiary doesn't exercise it and the window closes), the lapse itself can be treated as a taxable gift from the beneficiary back to the trust — specifically, a gift of a future interest that may be subject to gift tax or, more commonly, to inclusion in the beneficiary's estate.

This sounds alarming, but Congress provided a safe harbor: under IRC §2514(e) and §2041(b)(2), the "5-and-5" rule. A lapse of a withdrawal power is not a taxable gift or estate inclusion event to the extent the lapsed amount does not exceed the greater of:

  • $5,000, or
  • 5% of the trust's assets at the time of lapse

For most ILIT annual exclusion gifts ($19,000 per beneficiary per year), the 5-and-5 rule covers the full lapse — no adverse gift or estate tax consequences to the beneficiary for not exercising the withdrawal right. In practice, for trusts holding modest assets (under $380,000 at the time of the annual lapse), the 5% rule covers the full $19,000 Crummey power without triggering the lapse problem.

For larger trusts or larger Crummey powers, the lapse exceeding the 5-and-5 threshold requires care. A "hanging power" is the drafting solution: instead of having the full Crummey power lapse each year, only the 5-and-5 safe harbor amount lapses each year. The remainder "hangs" — it remains a potential withdrawal right until a sufficient amount of trust assets exist to bring it within the 5% test.

Trust Value 5-and-5 Annual Lapse Safe Harbor Crummey Power of $19K — Problem? Solution if Over Threshold

⚠️ New ILITs in their early years are most at risk. When an ILIT is first funded and holds only a modest insurance policy cash value, the trust's total assets may be well under $380,000 — putting the Crummey power lapse over the 5-and-5 threshold. A hanging power provision in the trust document ensures the lapse problem doesn't arise while the trust is small. This must be drafted into the original trust document — it cannot be added retroactively without amending the trust (which is often not possible in an irrevocable trust).

The GST Exemption Trap: The Most Dangerous Crummey Risk for Bitcoin Families

This is where Bitcoin families with dynasty trusts need to pay particular attention. As covered in our GST exemption guide, the automatic allocation rules for the generation-skipping transfer tax consume GST exemption on "indirect skip" trusts — trusts that include skip persons (grandchildren) among potential beneficiaries.

An ILIT or dynasty trust with Crummey withdrawal rights granted to grandchildren — even token withdrawal rights for the sole purpose of generating additional annual exclusion capacity — is almost certainly an indirect skip trust. Every annual exclusion contribution to that trust automatically consumes GST exemption from the grantor's $15M lifetime allocation.

The problem: you may be using $19,000 annual exclusion gifts to fund an ILIT with nominal grandchild Crummey rights — and inadvertently burning $19,000 per grandchild per year of your GST exemption on $190/year insurance premium contributions per grandchild. Over 20 years with 4 grandchildren, that's $1,520,000 of GST exemption consumed on what were intended to be routine premium payments.

That's $1.5M of exemption that won't be available when you fund the dynasty trust with actual Bitcoin.

The Fix: Opt Out on Form 709

File Form 709 each year for contributions to ILITs and other indirect skip trusts where grandchildren hold Crummey rights. On Schedule D of Form 709, elect to opt out of automatic GST exemption allocation for those transfers. The contributions still qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion — but GST exemption is not automatically consumed.

This requires annual Form 709 filing even for gifts that would otherwise be below the reporting threshold. It is one of the most consistently overlooked compliance requirements in estate planning — and one of the most consequential.

The rule: If your ILIT has grandchildren as Crummey beneficiaries, file Form 709 every year and opt out of automatic GST exemption allocation. Reserve your $15M GST exemption for the dynasty trust Bitcoin transfer — where it will shelter tens of millions in future appreciation. Not for annual insurance premium contributions that cost you $190 per grandchild per year.

Crummey Notices: Requirement, Format, and Consequences of Missing Them

The Crummey withdrawal right must be a genuine right — not a formality. The IRS has challenged Crummey trusts where the notice requirement was ignored in practice, treating the contributions as future-interest gifts (no annual exclusion) after the fact.

Requirements for valid Crummey notices:

  • Written notice to each beneficiary upon each contribution, informing them of their right to withdraw their allocable share of the contribution
  • Reasonable time to exercise — typically 30–60 days is standard; the IRS has challenged notices with fewer than 30 days as inadequate
  • Minor beneficiaries need a guardian or parent to receive notice on their behalf — a child cannot receive a Crummey notice directly
  • Contemporaneous documentation — keep copies of all notices sent, with dates, and written confirmation of receipt where possible
  • No pre-commitment by beneficiaries not to exercise — if beneficiaries sign agreements in advance promising never to withdraw, the IRS will treat the right as illusory and deny the annual exclusion

Courts have consistently held that beneficiaries who consistently don't exercise withdrawal rights are not penalized — the mere existence of the right is sufficient. But the right must be real and the notice must be genuine. Missing notices for a year or two is recoverable; a systematic pattern of no notices can result in the IRS reclassifying all affected contributions as future-interest gifts.

Bitcoin-Specific Application: Annual Contributions to a Bitcoin Dynasty Trust

A dynasty trust structured to hold Bitcoin over generations can be funded through annual Crummey gifts in addition to larger one-time transfers at formation. The mechanics:

  1. The dynasty trust document includes Crummey withdrawal rights for all current beneficiaries (children, and potentially grandchildren — with careful GST opt-out planning).
  2. Each year, the grantor contributes up to $19,000 per beneficiary to the trust in cash. The trustee notifies beneficiaries of their 30-day withdrawal window.
  3. Beneficiaries don't withdraw (they've been educated on the plan). Funds remain in trust.
  4. The trustee uses the accumulated contributions to purchase Bitcoin at the trustee's direction (or the Investment Trust Director's direction under a directed trust structure).
  5. Form 709 is filed annually, with GST opt-out elections for any indirect skip trust contributions involving grandchild Crummey rights.

Over 20 years with 4 beneficiaries at $19,000/year: $1,520,000 contributed to the trust, entirely gift-tax-free, without consuming a dollar of lifetime exemption. At Bitcoin's historical growth rate, that $1.52M compounds substantially — all inside the dynasty trust, outside every beneficiary's taxable estate, and protected from creditors and divorce.

Key Takeaways

  • A Crummey trust gives irrevocable trust beneficiaries a temporary withdrawal right on each contribution, converting a future-interest gift into a present-interest gift that qualifies for the $19,000 annual gift tax exclusion.
  • The annual exclusion scales with the number of Crummey beneficiaries: $19,000 per recipient, per donor, per year. A married couple with 5 beneficiaries can contribute $190,000/year to a Crummey trust entirely gift-tax-free.
  • The 5-and-5 lapse rule protects beneficiaries from adverse gift/estate tax consequences when they don't exercise the withdrawal right — as long as the lapsed amount is within the greater of $5,000 or 5% of trust assets. Hanging power provisions handle lapses that exceed this threshold in small trusts.
  • The GST exemption trap: if grandchildren hold Crummey rights in an indirect skip trust, every annual contribution automatically consumes GST exemption. Opt out on Form 709 each year to preserve GST exemption for the dynasty trust Bitcoin transfer.
  • Crummey notices must be genuine written notices with a real 30–60 day withdrawal window. Missing notices — even informally — can cause the IRS to reclassify contributions as future-interest gifts, retroactively eliminating the annual exclusions.
  • For Bitcoin dynasty trusts, Crummey contributions provide a steady annual funding stream ($19K per beneficiary) that builds the trust's Bitcoin position over time without consuming lifetime exemption — a complement to the large one-time transfer at trust formation.
