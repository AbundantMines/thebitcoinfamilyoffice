Bitcoin Incapacity Planning: What Happens to Your Bitcoin If You Can't Act

Death gets all the attention in estate planning. But incapacity — the period when you are alive but unable to manage your own affairs — presents an equally serious problem for Bitcoin holders, and one that most estate plans address inadequately or not at all.

By Hal Turner The Bitcoin Family Office February 25, 2026

Estate planning conversations focus almost exclusively on what happens at death. But incapacity — a stroke, a serious accident, a degenerative illness, or even a protracted medical emergency — can disable a Bitcoin holder's ability to manage their estate for months or years while they are still alive. The legal and operational challenges of Bitcoin incapacity planning are distinct from death planning, and they require specific solutions.

The core problem: Bitcoin custody is controlled by the holder. When the holder cannot act, no institutional mechanism automatically transfers that control. Unlike a brokerage account or bank account, there is no "incapacity department" to call, no branch manager who can authenticate your identity by other means, and no regulatory framework that grants your family automatic access. If you become incapacitated and have not established the right legal and operational structures in advance, your Bitcoin may effectively be frozen — inaccessible to your family and potentially at risk.

The Legal Authority Gap

When you are fully competent, you authorize every Bitcoin transaction yourself. When you become incapacitated, someone else needs to step in. But "needing to step in" is not the same as "having the legal right to step in." Without specific legal authority, even your spouse — even your adult children — may lack the standing to manage your Bitcoin on your behalf. They cannot sign transactions. They cannot manage exchange accounts. They cannot restructure custody arrangements. They are legally unable to act, even if they are the right person to act.

This legal authority gap is closed by two documents that work together:

1. Durable Power of Attorney with Digital Asset Authority

A durable power of attorney (POA) grants another person — your agent — the legal authority to act on your behalf in financial matters. "Durable" means the authority continues even after you become incapacitated (a non-durable POA terminates at incapacity, which is precisely when you need it).

For Bitcoin, a durable POA must specifically address digital assets. General financial authority language — the boilerplate in many standard POA templates — may not be sufficient to grant your agent authority over digital assets under your state's RUFADAA statute. Your POA should explicitly include language authorizing your agent to: access and manage digital assets, including Bitcoin; operate hardware wallets and software wallets; manage exchange accounts and custody arrangements; and transfer Bitcoin on your behalf.

Critical warning: A standard POA template that doesn't mention digital assets may not grant your agent authority to manage your Bitcoin under RUFADAA. Ask your estate attorney specifically whether your POA includes language sufficient to grant digital asset authority in your state. Many people discover the gap only when they need the authority — at the worst possible time.

The agent you name in your POA is making decisions about your Bitcoin while you're alive. This person should be someone you trust completely, who understands Bitcoin at a basic level (or has access to a Bitcoin-competent advisor), and who is geographically accessible enough to act when needed.

2. Revocable Living Trust with a Successor Trustee

A revocable living trust can hold your Bitcoin and provide a seamless transition of management authority when you become incapacitated. The trust document names you as the initial trustee and names a successor trustee who takes over when you are unable to serve — without a court proceeding, without probate, and without a formal "activation" mechanism other than your incapacity (as defined in the trust document).

This is significantly cleaner than a POA for incapacity planning. A POA requires your agent to present the document and have third parties recognize it — exchange platforms, custodians, and other institutions may refuse to act on a POA even when it's valid. A successor trustee of a trust holding the Bitcoin has clear legal ownership rights and doesn't need external party recognition.

For Bitcoin held in a trust: when you become incapacitated, your successor trustee takes over management. They can access exchange accounts held in the trust's name, manage custodial arrangements, and in the case of self-custodied Bitcoin, use the Letter of Instruction (which should be updated to include incapacity scenarios, not just death scenarios) to access the custody arrangements.

The Operational Access Problem

Legal authority solves the "right to act" problem. Operational access solves the "ability to act" problem. These are separate.

Your agent under a POA or your successor trustee needs to be able to physically and technically access your Bitcoin. Even with perfect legal authority, if your agent doesn't know:

  • Where the hardware wallets are physically located
  • Which devices are hardware wallets and which are not
  • The PINs for those hardware wallets (if applicable)
  • Where the seed phrases are stored
  • How your multisig quorum works and where each key is held
  • What exchange accounts exist and how they're structured

...then the legal authority is functionally useless. They have the right to act but not the ability to act.

This is precisely what the Letter of Instruction addresses. But the Letter of Instruction must be written with incapacity in mind — not just death. The questions to answer for an incapacitated holder are different in important ways from the questions to answer for a deceased holder:

For incapacity: Your agent needs to access the Bitcoin without triggering irreversible transactions, preserve your estate while you're incapacitated, potentially make ongoing decisions about custody and management (not just a one-time transfer), and ideally be able to consult you when you are lucid enough to provide guidance.

Include in your Letter of Instruction: a specific section addressed to your agent under POA (separate from the executor section), describing what to do during incapacity versus what to do at death. These are not the same protocol.

Multisig and Incapacity

Multisig custody arrangements — where multiple keys are required to sign a transaction — present a specific incapacity planning consideration. In a 2-of-3 multisig arrangement, your incapacity removes you as a key holder. Your agent needs to be able to sign on your behalf, which means:

  • They need access to your key (either physical possession during incapacity, or guidance on where it is)
  • They need to understand the multisig signing process
  • The other key holders need to know that you are incapacitated and your agent has authority to sign

One solution: a directed trust structure that holds the Bitcoin in multisig, with the trust document specifying that on the incapacity of the primary key holder (you), the successor trustee has authority to replace your key with a key held by a designated professional co-trustee. This removes the operational dependency on your personal incapacity status from the multisig signing process.

Another solution: designating your agent under POA as a key holder (or as a backup key holder) in your multisig arrangement explicitly, with documentation that they have authority to sign on your behalf during incapacity.

The Incapacity Communication Protocol

One of the least-discussed aspects of incapacity planning is the communication protocol — how your family knows what to do when incapacity occurs, and in what sequence. Unlike death (which is a definitive event), incapacity often occurs on a spectrum: there may be a period of reduced capacity before full incapacity, there may be periods of lucidity during an otherwise incapacitated period, and recovery is possible in ways it is not at death.

Your estate plan should include a clear incapacity protocol:

  • Who determines incapacity? Most POAs and trusts define incapacity as certification by one or two physicians. Know what your document requires.
  • Who is notified first? Your spouse, your successor trustee, your estate attorney? In what order?
  • What actions are taken immediately? Secure hardware wallets, review custody documentation, contact the estate attorney.
  • What actions are deferred? No transfers until legal authority is confirmed. No changes to custody structure without consultation.
  • Who manages ongoing decisions? Your agent or successor trustee — with what advisory resources?

Document this protocol in your Letter of Instruction, specifically in the incapacity section. Walk your designated agent through it while you are well. The goal is that if incapacity occurs, the people around you know exactly what to do and in what order — without improvising under pressure.

What to Prepare Right Now

If incapacity planning for your Bitcoin holdings is incomplete, these are the priority actions:

  • Durable POA with digital asset authority. If you don't have one, or if yours doesn't specifically address digital assets, ask your estate attorney to review and update it. This is the most urgent gap for most holders.
  • Revocable trust with successor trustee provisions. Ensure your trust explicitly covers digital assets and gives your successor trustee clear authority and instruction.
  • Letter of Instruction with an incapacity section. Separate from the death section — describe what your agent should do if you are incapacitated but alive, including what to NOT do (no transfers without attorney confirmation).
  • Successor trustee / agent briefing. Your designated person needs to know they are designated, where the Letter of Instruction is, and the contact for your estate attorney and CPA.
  • For multisig: document the signing process for your agent. Without this, your agent has authority but no ability to use it.

The planning cost for incapacity preparation is minimal — it's mostly adding language to existing documents and documenting existing arrangements. The cost of failing to prepare is potentially irreversible: Bitcoin locked for months or years while you are incapacitated, your family unable to manage it, and the estate at risk if the market moves significantly during the incapacitated period.
