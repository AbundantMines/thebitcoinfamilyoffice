Bitcoin Tax-Loss Harvesting: The Complete 2026 Guide

Bitcoin has a tax advantage that stock investors don't: no wash sale rule. Under current IRS guidance, you can sell Bitcoin at a loss, immediately repurchase it, and claim the full capital loss on your tax return — something prohibited for stocks and ETFs. This guide covers every dimension of Bitcoin tax-loss harvesting: when to do it, how much to harvest, the critical stepped-up basis trade-off, crypto-to-crypto swap strategies, year-end timing, and how harvesting interacts with your estate plan.

Legislative Risk: Congress has repeatedly considered extending wash sale rules to cryptocurrency. The Digital Asset Anti-Money Laundering Act and other proposals have included wash sale provisions. Current law (as of March 2026) does not apply wash sale rules to Bitcoin or other digital assets — but this could change. Harvesting strategies that depend on the no-wash-sale advantage should be implemented with awareness that legislative change is possible. Consult a CPA.

What Is Tax-Loss Harvesting?

Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of selling an asset that has declined in value from your purchase price, realizing a capital loss that can offset capital gains or ordinary income on your tax return. For Bitcoin holders, the mechanics are straightforward:

  1. You purchased Bitcoin at some cost basis (the price you paid)
  2. Bitcoin's price has declined below your cost basis
  3. You sell the Bitcoin, realizing a capital loss equal to (sale price − cost basis)
  4. That loss offsets capital gains from other sales, reducing your tax bill
  5. If losses exceed gains, up to $3,000 in net capital losses can be deducted against ordinary income per year; excess losses carry forward indefinitely
  6. You immediately rebuy Bitcoin to maintain your position

For stock investors, step 6 is blocked by the wash sale rule (IRC §1091): if you sell a stock at a loss and repurchase the "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale, the loss is disallowed and added back to your basis. Bitcoin is currently classified as property, not a security — so the wash sale rule does not apply.

Worked Example: $500K Position, 40% Drawdown

You purchased 5 Bitcoin at $100,000 each ($500,000 total cost basis). Bitcoin drops to $60,000 (current value: $300,000). You have an unrealized loss of $200,000.

Harvest action: Sell all 5 BTC at $60,000, realize $200,000 capital loss. Immediately repurchase 5 BTC at $60,000 (new cost basis: $300,000).

Tax benefit: $200,000 loss offsets $200,000 in gains realized elsewhere. At a 37.1% combined rate (CA): $74,200 in tax savings. At 23.8% (WY): $47,600 in tax savings.

Trade-off: Your new cost basis is $300,000 (not $500,000). When Bitcoin recovers to $100,000 and you sell, you'll owe tax on $200,000 more gain than if you hadn't harvested. The harvesting benefit is tax deferral — moving the tax bill forward in time — unless you hold until death (stepped-up basis) or donate the appreciated Bitcoin to charity.

The Critical Trade-Off: Harvesting vs. Stepped-Up Basis

This is the most important concept in Bitcoin tax-loss harvesting — and the one most guides get wrong by omission.

When you harvest a loss and rebuy, you reset your cost basis lower. The subsequent gain when Bitcoin recovers will be larger. If you sell that recovered Bitcoin during your lifetime, you've deferred the tax but you'll eventually pay it. The benefit is the time value of money — a tax bill deferred 10 years is worth less in present-value terms than a tax bill paid today.

But here's the estate planning interaction that changes the calculus entirely:

IRC §1014 — the stepped-up basis rule: When a Bitcoin holder dies, heirs receive the Bitcoin with a cost basis equal to the fair market value on the date of death. All unrealized gains accumulated during the holder's lifetime are permanently eliminated. The heir owes zero capital gains tax on appreciation that occurred before the holder's death.

The Estate Planning Implication: If you plan to hold Bitcoin until death and pass it to heirs, every dollar of loss you harvest is a dollar of basis you permanently lose to stepped-up basis elimination. Here's why: you harvest a $200K loss (tax value: ~$47K–$74K now), reset basis to $300K. Bitcoin recovers. You die with Bitcoin worth $500K and a $300K basis. Your heir gets $500K stepped-up basis — the $200K additional gain you created by harvesting is eliminated. But if you hadn't harvested, you'd have died with a $500K basis (original), and the same step-up would have occurred on the full $500K. The harvesting benefit was real ($47K–$74K in tax savings) but you gave up nothing at death because the step-up eliminates ALL unrealized gains regardless.

Wait — that means harvesting is almost always the right move for Bitcoin held until death? Not quite. The interaction depends on whether you'll actually hold until death:

Key takeaway: For Bitcoin you plan to hold until death and pass to heirs, harvesting losses is almost always beneficial — you capture the immediate tax savings, and the step-up at death eliminates the lower cost basis you created. The one exception is Bitcoin held in a dynasty trust or other irrevocable structure that won't receive a step-up.

Specific Harvesting Strategies for Bitcoin Holders

1. Lot-Specific Harvesting: Sell Your Highest-Basis Lots First

If you've purchased Bitcoin at multiple prices over time, you have multiple "lots" — each with its own cost basis. When Bitcoin declines, some lots may be at a loss while others are still at a gain. By specifically identifying which lots you sell (using specific identification accounting, not FIFO), you can harvest only the lots at a loss while leaving profitable lots untouched.

Example: You bought 1 BTC at $15,000, 1 BTC at $50,000, and 1 BTC at $80,000. Bitcoin is at $40,000. Your $15K lot has a $25K gain. Your $50K lot has a $10K loss. Your $80K lot has a $40K loss. Sell the $50K and $80K lots (harvesting $50K in losses), immediately rebuy 2 BTC at $40,000. Your $15K lot remains untouched.

Requirement: You must specify which lots you're selling at the time of the transaction and have records to support it. Most Bitcoin exchanges support lot identification. If you don't specify, the IRS default method applies (generally FIFO). Get this in writing with your exchange and CPA.

2. Crypto-to-Crypto Swap Harvesting

A common strategy is to sell Bitcoin at a loss and immediately purchase a different cryptocurrency — for example, selling Bitcoin and buying Ethereum — with the intent to swap back to Bitcoin after some waiting period. This approach attempts to maintain crypto market exposure while harvesting the Bitcoin loss. Important considerations:

  • Because wash sale rules don't apply to crypto under current law, there's no required waiting period — you can sell BTC and immediately rebuy BTC. The crypto-to-crypto swap adds risk of price divergence and is not required for the wash sale benefit.
  • The swap itself is a taxable event — selling BTC at a loss and buying ETH realizes the BTC loss but also realizes any ETH gain/loss when you eventually sell the ETH.
  • For Bitcoin maximalists who don't want altcoin exposure: you can simply sell BTC and immediately rebuy BTC. No swap needed. No wash sale rule applies.

3. Year-End Harvesting vs. Continuous Harvesting

Two schools of thought:

  • Year-end harvesting: Review your positions in November/December and harvest any positions at a loss. Simple, requires one annual review. Risk: you may miss significant harvesting opportunities during mid-year corrections if you only look at year-end.
  • Threshold-based continuous harvesting: Set a threshold — e.g., harvest whenever a lot is down 15%+ from cost basis — and harvest throughout the year as opportunities arise. More complex to execute but captures intra-year corrections (which are often the largest).

Bitcoin's volatility makes continuous/threshold-based harvesting more valuable than year-end only. A 30% drawdown in March that recovers by December would be invisible to year-end-only harvesters.

4. Offset Strategies: What to Harvest Against

Harvested Bitcoin losses can offset:

  • Short-term capital gains from any asset — including business asset sales, stock options exercised and sold within a year, other cryptocurrency gains. Offset value: your ordinary income rate (32–37% federal + state)
  • Long-term capital gains from any asset — stock sales, real estate, other Bitcoin lots sold at a gain. Offset value: LTCG rate (20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + state)
  • Ordinary income (limited) — net capital losses can offset up to $3,000/year in ordinary income. Excess losses carry forward indefinitely at $3,000/year. For large Bitcoin positions, this is a slow but real benefit.

Priority: harvest losses to offset the highest-rate gains first (short-term > long-term > ordinary income carryforward).

5. Gifting vs. Harvesting: The Basis Decision

If you intend to gift appreciated Bitcoin, harvesting losses beforehand can be valuable — but timing matters:

  • If you gift loss positions: the recipient takes the lower of your cost basis or FMV at the date of the gift. Gifting a loss position doesn't allow you to claim the loss — you must realize it first (sell, harvest) before gifting.
  • If you harvest the loss first, then gift the (now lower-basis) Bitcoin: the recipient has a lower basis to work with. If they hold until death, the step-up eliminates this; if they sell during their lifetime, they face the lower-basis gain.
  • Best approach: harvest losses yourself (capturing the deduction at your higher rate), then gift the cash or the reacquired Bitcoin to lower-bracket family members who will sell during their lifetime at their lower rate — or hold in a dynasty trust for step-up at trust beneficiary's death.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Harvesting Bitcoin You Plan to Donate

Never harvest a Bitcoin loss and then donate the Bitcoin to charity. If you donate appreciated Bitcoin directly, you receive a charitable deduction equal to FMV and owe zero capital gains tax on the appreciation. If you first harvest the loss (realizing the loss for tax purposes) and then donate the Bitcoin at its new lower basis, you've claimed the loss deduction and the charitable deduction — but the IRS will scrutinize this as a step-transaction. More importantly, you've made the transaction more complex for no additional benefit. Donate appreciated Bitcoin directly; harvest losses on Bitcoin you'll sell or hold.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the AMT Interaction

High-income Bitcoin holders subject to the Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) should model harvesting decisions under both the regular tax and AMT regimes. Capital losses reduce regular taxable income but their AMT interaction is complex. If you're in AMT territory, a CPA should model both before executing harvesting transactions.

Mistake 3: Poor Record-Keeping on Lot Selection

Claiming specific identification of lots sold requires contemporaneous documentation — which lots you sold, at what price, on what date, with what cost basis. "Contemporaneous" means at the time of sale, not reconstructed later. Bitcoin exchanges vary in how they support this. Maintain a cost basis spreadsheet or use specialized crypto tax software (Koinly, CoinTracker, TaxBit) and confirm your lot selection with your exchange's transaction records before filing.

Mistake 4: Harvesting Inside a Tax-Advantaged Account

Bitcoin in a Roth IRA, Traditional IRA, or Bitcoin ETF in a retirement account cannot be used for tax-loss harvesting. Gains and losses inside retirement accounts are not recognized for tax purposes during the account's life. Tax-loss harvesting only applies to Bitcoin held in taxable accounts. If you're buying Bitcoin through ETFs in a 401(k), the harvesting rules don't apply.

Mistake 5: Forgetting State-Level Treatment

Most states follow federal capital loss treatment, but not all. California conforms to federal capital loss rules. New York conforms. But a handful of states have their own specific rules around capital loss carryforwards, deduction limitations, or treatment of crypto specifically. Confirm your state's treatment with a state-licensed CPA — especially if you've moved between states with different loss treatment rules.

Mistake 6: Wash Sale Rule Legislative Risk

Harvesting strategies implemented today may be invalidated retroactively if Congress passes wash sale rules that apply to cryptocurrency. While retroactive application would be unusual and legally contestable, the possibility is real. Consider the legislative environment when implementing large harvesting positions.

Harvesting in the Context of Your Bitcoin Family Office

Tax-loss harvesting is one component of a comprehensive Bitcoin family office tax strategy. The interactions with other planning tools:

  • Stepped-up basis: Harvesting losses you plan to hold until death is almost always beneficial — the step-up at death eliminates the lower basis you created. Harvest aggressively for Bitcoin in your estate plan.
  • GRATs: A GRAT funded during a Bitcoin price correction captures the subsequent appreciation outside your estate. Harvesting losses and then funding a GRAT is a powerful combination — the harvest offsets gains realized at the GRAT funding, and the GRAT captures the recovery appreciation tax-free.
  • Annual exclusion gifts: Gift harvested (lower-basis) Bitcoin to dynasty trust beneficiaries who have long time horizons — the lower basis matters less when held across generations.
  • Dynasty trust: Bitcoin held in an irrevocable dynasty trust may not receive a stepped-up basis at the grantor's death. Model the step-up vs. no-step-up trade-off before placing low-basis Bitcoin in an irrevocable trust — it may be better to keep high-basis Bitcoin in the trust and retain low-basis Bitcoin personally (where it will receive a step-up at death).
  • State domicile: The value of harvested losses scales directly with your state income tax rate. A California holder saves $37,100 per $100K harvested; a Wyoming holder saves $23,800. If you're planning a domicile change from a high-tax state, harvest losses before the move and realize gains after (at the lower state rate).

Year-End Harvesting Checklist

  • Pull complete Bitcoin cost basis report from your exchange and/or tax software — identify every lot, its cost basis, and current FMV
  • Identify all lots currently showing a loss — calculate the total harvestable loss by lot
  • Identify what you have to offset — short-term gains, long-term gains, or ordinary income carryforward need
  • Prioritize highest-rate offsets — short-term gains first, then long-term, then ordinary income
  • Decide lot strategy — specific identification to target only loss lots while preserving gain lots
  • Execute the sale — document lot selection contemporaneously with transaction
  • Immediately repurchase if you want to maintain position — no waiting period required under current law
  • If planning to donate any Bitcoin to charity — do NOT harvest those positions; donate appreciated Bitcoin directly
  • Model AMT impact if you're in AMT territory — confirm with CPA before executing
  • Confirm your state's treatment of capital loss carryforwards — especially if you've changed domicile this year
  • Update basis records immediately after harvest — don't reconstruct in April
  • Review carryforward losses from prior years — apply against this year's gains before harvesting new losses

Bitcoin Mining: The Proactive Tax Strategy That Works Every Year

Tax-loss harvesting is reactive — it requires Bitcoin to decline for the opportunity to exist. Bitcoin mining is proactive: bonus depreciation on mining equipment generates ordinary income deductions regardless of Bitcoin's price direction. For Bitcoin holders with significant income, mining creates deductions that pair naturally with harvesting losses to minimize the overall tax burden.
