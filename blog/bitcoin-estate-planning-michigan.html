Bitcoin Estate Planning in Michigan

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Michigan Est. 9 min read

Michigan imposes no state estate tax and no inheritance tax — a meaningful planning advantage for Bitcoin families that removes an entire layer of transfer tax complexity present in states like New Jersey, Massachusetts, and Oregon. Michigan Bitcoin holders face only the federal estate tax (applicable to estates over $13.99 million per individual in 2025), which means the vast majority of Michigan Bitcoin families can focus their planning energy on federal-level structures, custody architecture, and multi-generational succession rather than navigating a parallel state tax regime.

This guide covers Michigan's trust law framework, LLC structures for Bitcoin holding, digital asset succession under Michigan law, and the strategies that allow Michigan families to protect and transfer Bitcoin wealth across generations efficiently. Bitcoin estate planning Michigan is a discipline that rewards proactive structuring — even without a state estate tax, the absence of proper trust and custody documentation can result in delays, disputes, and technical access failures that defeat even the best-intentioned estate plan.

Michigan's No-Estate-Tax Advantage: What It Means for Bitcoin Planning

Michigan repealed its state estate tax effective for deaths occurring on or after January 1, 1993 — one of the earlier states to eliminate its separate state-level transfer tax. Michigan also does not impose an inheritance tax. This creates a clean planning environment where the only estate tax concern is the federal estate tax, which applies to estates above the federal exemption ($13.99 million per individual, $27.98 million per married couple with portability, in 2025).

For Michigan Bitcoin holders, this has two important practical implications. First, lifetime gifting and trust strategies can focus on optimizing federal estate tax treatment and stepped-up basis outcomes, without layering in a separate state tax calculation at every step. Second, the planning horizon is longer — there is no urgency to restructure Bitcoin holdings before a state-level threshold is crossed, which means families have more time and flexibility to implement comprehensive custody and succession architecture before making irrevocable structural decisions.

Michigan's zero estate and inheritance tax means Bitcoin families can focus exclusively on federal planning — and on getting custody succession right — without a parallel state tax clock running against them.

Michigan's Uniform Trust Code Framework

Michigan enacted the Michigan Trust Code (MTC), codified at MCL 700.7101 et seq., effective April 1, 2010. The MTC substantially follows the Uniform Trust Code and provides the statutory foundation for modern trust planning in Michigan. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families include:

  • Directed trusts: The MTC does not have a separate directed trust statute as comprehensive as Wyoming's DPTA, but Michigan trust instruments can create trust advisors, trust protectors, and distribution advisors with legally enforceable roles. Properly drafted, a Michigan directed trust can separate custody decisions from administrative functions — important for Bitcoin where custody requires specialized technical knowledge.
  • Trust protectors: Michigan trust law expressly recognizes trust protector roles (MCL 700.7103), allowing a named individual or entity to hold specified powers — including the power to change trustees, modify investment directions, or adapt custody arrangements — without those powers constituting trustee duties. For Bitcoin trusts, a technically sophisticated trust protector can manage custody evolution over time.
  • Decanting: Michigan's trust decanting authority under MCL 700.7820a allows trustees of irrevocable trusts to transfer assets to a new trust with updated terms, without court approval in many circumstances. This allows older Bitcoin trusts with outdated custody provisions to be modernized without court proceedings.
  • Dynasty trusts: Michigan allows perpetual trusts for personal property. Under MCL 554.1408, a trust of personal property — including Bitcoin — can last indefinitely, providing a multi-generational holding structure without forced termination.

Michigan LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Michigan LLCs (governed by the Michigan Limited Liability Company Act, MCL 450.4101 et seq.) provide effective Bitcoin holding structures with several advantages:

Valuation discounts: Bitcoin held in a properly structured Michigan family LLC can qualify for minority interest and lack-of-marketability valuation discounts when LLC interests are transferred to heirs or trusts. These discounts — typically 20–35% — allow more Bitcoin value to be transferred per dollar of federal gift tax annual exclusion or lifetime exemption.

Operating agreement governance: Michigan's LLC statute gives members broad latitude to customize operating agreement provisions, including specifying multi-signature custody requirements, key succession protocols, mandatory hold periods, and restrictions on voluntary transfers. The operating agreement becomes the legal governance document for Bitcoin custody decisions.

Charging order protection: Michigan LLC law provides charging order protection for single-member and multi-member LLCs (MCL 450.4507). Creditors of an LLC member cannot force dissolution or seize LLC assets — including Bitcoin — but are limited to a charging order against distributions. This asset protection feature is important for Bitcoin holders with significant positions.

Series LLC structures: Michigan enacted series LLC legislation allowing a single LLC to have multiple "series" with separate assets, liabilities, and members. For Bitcoin families with multiple wallets, custody arrangements, or investment purposes, a series LLC can segregate different Bitcoin positions within a single legal entity — simplifying administration while maintaining segregation.

Federal Estate Planning Strategies for Michigan Bitcoin Holders

With no state estate tax to navigate, Michigan Bitcoin families can focus their structural energy on federal-level strategies that optimize both estate tax treatment and stepped-up basis outcomes:

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs): A GRAT transfers Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust while the grantor retains annuity payments for a fixed term. If Bitcoin appreciates above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate during the term, the excess growth passes to heirs estate-tax-free. GRATs are particularly powerful for Bitcoin given its appreciation potential, and Michigan's lack of a state estate tax means the entire economic benefit of GRAT planning flows to the federal level.

Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs): An IDGT allows a grantor to sell Bitcoin to the trust in exchange for a promissory note, removing the Bitcoin's appreciation from the federal taxable estate while the grantor continues to pay income tax on the trust's earnings (which is itself an additional gift to the trust). IDGTs work particularly well with Bitcoin held in a Michigan LLC where the LLC interests are sold to the trust at a valuation-discounted price.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs): A SLAT allows a married Michigan Bitcoin holder to transfer Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust for the benefit of the other spouse and descendants, removing the assets from the federal taxable estate while the family retains indirect access through the spouse's trust distributions.

Digital Asset Succession in Michigan

Michigan enacted the Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (FADAA), codified at MCL 700.1001 et seq., effective June 27, 2016. This statute provides important protections for Bitcoin succession planning in Michigan:

Under the Michigan FADAA, a personal representative, trustee, or agent under a power of attorney has the authority to access, manage, and transfer digital assets — including Bitcoin — if the governing estate planning documents grant that authority. The statute establishes a priority hierarchy: first, any directions provided through a platform's online tool (e.g., a custodial exchange's succession designation); second, the estate planning documents; third, platform terms of service.

For Bitcoin held in self-custody (hardware wallets, multi-signature arrangements), the FADAA creates a legal right of access for authorized fiduciaries — but the technical access itself depends entirely on proper documentation of keys, seed phrases, and custody protocols. The statute grants authority but cannot unlock a wallet without the actual technical access credentials.

Every Michigan Bitcoin estate plan should include: explicit FADAA authority language in all governing documents, a technical access document maintained separately from the estate plan and updated regularly, and a tested succession protocol coordinated with the custody architecture. Wyoming Trust Situs for Michigan Families

Michigan families are not limited to Michigan trust law. A trust sited in Wyoming — with a Wyoming corporate trustee — accesses Wyoming's directed trust statute, perpetual dynasty trust provisions, and Bitcoin-specific legal infrastructure regardless of where the grantor and beneficiaries reside. For Michigan Bitcoin families whose estate plans include a family LLC holding significant Bitcoin, establishing a Wyoming-sited trust as the LLC's owner captures Wyoming's most advantageous features while the family maintains Michigan residency without state estate tax concern.

The income tax analysis for out-of-state trust siting requires careful review: Michigan imposes income tax on trust income distributed to Michigan residents. Accumulation trusts that retain income rather than distributing it may achieve tax deferral, but families should model the specific tax consequences with counsel. Practical Priorities for Michigan Bitcoin Families

  1. Establish a Michigan LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle — with an operating agreement that governs custody, succession, and transaction authorization. This creates the legal governance framework that the estate plan references.
  2. Fund an irrevocable trust with LLC interests — either through lifetime gifting at discounted valuations or through a note sale to an IDGT — removing Bitcoin appreciation from the federal taxable estate over time.
  3. Grant explicit FADAA digital asset authority in all governing documents — trust instruments, powers of attorney, and will provisions should each contain specific Bitcoin access authority under Michigan's Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act.
  4. Maintain a tested technical succession protocol — the legal authority created by the FADAA and estate plan documents is worthless without the actual technical access credentials. A documented, regularly tested key succession protocol is the operational foundation of any Bitcoin estate plan.
  5. Consider Wyoming trust situs for the primary family trust if the position is significant — capturing directed trust, perpetuity, and Bitcoin-specific advantages while Michigan's zero estate tax removes any state tax complexity from the situs decision.
