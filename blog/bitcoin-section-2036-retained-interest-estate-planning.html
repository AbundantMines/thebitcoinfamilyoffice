Bitcoin & §2036: The Retained Interest Trap That Pulls Bitcoin Back Into Your Estate

You transferred Bitcoin to a trust or LLC. You thought it was out of your estate. IRC §2036 says: not so fast. If you kept any right to enjoy, control, or direct that Bitcoin — even informally — the IRS can pull it all back. Here's when §2036 applies, when it doesn't, and how to structure Bitcoin transfers that survive scrutiny.

📅 March 13, 2026 ⏱ 16 min read 🏷 §2036 · GRAT · FLP · Retained Interest

Estate planning for Bitcoin often focuses on getting assets out of the taxable estate — into GRATs, FLPs, SLATs, dynasty trusts, and IDGTs. Less attention goes to the provision that can reverse all of that: IRC §2036, the retained interest rule.

§2036 is the IRS's most powerful tool for challenging estate planning transfers. It requires that a decedent's gross estate include any property transferred during life if the decedent retained certain rights over that property. For Bitcoin holders, this has direct implications for GRATs (if you die during the term), family limited partnerships (if you kept control), SLATs (if the reciprocal trust doctrine applies), and any Bitcoin trust where the grantor retained more than they should have.

Understanding §2036 is not optional for serious Bitcoin estate planning. It is the provision your estate planning attorney most needs to design around — and the one the IRS most reliably uses when a large estate is audited.

1. What §2036 Says

IRC §2036(a) requires inclusion in the gross estate of all property transferred by the decedent during life if the decedent retained, for any period that does not end before death:

  • §2036(a)(1): The right to the possession or enjoyment of the property, or the right to the income from it
  • §2036(a)(2): The right, either alone or in conjunction with any person, to designate the persons who shall possess or enjoy the property or its income

Both prongs are broad. §2036(a)(1) captures economic enjoyment retained by the transferor. §2036(a)(2) captures retained decision-making power over who gets the property. Either one, if retained at death, pulls the transferred property back into the gross estate at its full fair market value on the date of death.

⚠️ §2036 Applies to the Full Date-of-Death Value

When §2036 applies, the included amount is the fair market value at death — not the value at the time of transfer. If you transferred $1 million in Bitcoin to a trust and Bitcoin appreciated to $10 million by your death, §2036 inclusion brings $10 million into your estate. The appreciation that seemed to be outside the estate is recaptured entirely.

The Bona Fide Sale Exception

§2036 has one carve-out: the last sentence excludes transfers that constitute "a bona fide sale for an adequate and full consideration in money or money's worth." This exception is the primary defense in FLP and LLC planning. If the transfer to the entity is genuinely arm's-length — not just a family arrangement disguised as a sale — §2036 should not apply because the transferor received full consideration and retained no gratuitous benefit.

2. §2036 and Bitcoin GRATs

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is explicitly designed around §2036: the grantor transfers Bitcoin to the trust and retains an annuity for a fixed term. The retained annuity is a §2036(a)(1) interest — the right to income from the transferred property.

Congress sanctioned the GRAT structure by enacting §2702, which governs how retained interests in family trusts are valued for gift tax purposes. Under §2702, a retained annuity paid for a fixed term is valued using the §7520 interest rate. The result: the gift element equals the transferred value minus the present value of the retained annuity.

The GRAT Death Risk

The §2036 risk in a GRAT arises if the grantor dies during the annuity term. Under §2036(a)(1), the estate includes the amount of GRAT assets needed to fund the remaining annuity obligations — valued as of the date of death. In practice, this means:

  • If Bitcoin appreciated significantly since the GRAT was funded, the estate inclusion can be large — potentially up to the full trust value
  • The precise inclusion amount is calculated as the amount of trust assets needed (at the §7520 rate at death) to produce the annuity for the remaining term
  • If Bitcoin has grown substantially, this amount approaches the full trust value
  • The grantor's estate is in roughly the same position as if the GRAT had never been created — except the estate may also owe gift tax on the original GRAT gift, further worsening the outcome

💡 The GRAT Mortality Hedge

The GRAT is sometimes called a "mortality bet" — the planner bets the grantor will outlive the term. If they do, the appreciation above the §7520 hurdle passes gift-tax-free. If they don't, §2036 applies and the planning largely fails. For older grantors or those with health concerns, short GRAT terms (2–3 years) minimize the mortality window. Some planners use life insurance (in an ILIT) to hedge the GRAT death risk — if the grantor dies during the term, the life insurance proceeds replace the estate inclusion amount.

Rolling Short-Term GRATs

The most practical solution to GRAT §2036 risk is using a rolling series of short-term GRATs rather than one long-term GRAT. Instead of one 10-year GRAT, execute five consecutive 2-year GRATs, reinvesting annuity proceeds into the next GRAT. At any given time, the maximum §2036 exposure is limited to the two-year term in progress. If the grantor dies, only the current GRAT's remaining annuity obligation is included — not a decade of accumulated appreciation.

3. §2036 and Bitcoin Family Limited Partnerships

Family Limited Partnerships (FLPs) and LLCs used to hold Bitcoin have been a frequent target of IRS §2036 challenges. The structure is attractive for estate planning: transfer Bitcoin to an FLP at a discount (lack of marketability, minority interest), gift or sell LP interests to heirs at the discounted value, and retain a general partner role with management fees and pro-rata distributions.

The IRS challenges FLPs under §2036(a)(1) when the transferor retained the effective right to the economic benefit of the Bitcoin — even if the legal title was in the FLP. Courts have found §2036 inclusion when:

  • The senior family member paid personal expenses from FLP funds (commingling)
  • The FLP distributed to the senior member substantially all of its income as needed for living expenses
  • The FLP was created on the senior member's deathbed or during final illness (no time for genuine business purpose)
  • All assets contributed were liquid and investable — no genuine management activity required
  • FLP formalities were not observed (no capital accounts, no operating agreement compliance)
  • The general partner retained unilateral ability to dissolve the FLP and return assets

⚠️ Bitcoin-Specific FLP Risk

Bitcoin held in self-custody by the senior family member who also serves as general partner of the FLP creates exactly the §2036 fact pattern the IRS targets. If the Bitcoin private keys are in the grantor's hardware wallet, the grantor technically controls the Bitcoin regardless of what the FLP documents say. Institutional or third-party custody — where the custodian holds keys and the FLP is the account holder of record — is necessary for genuine transfer of control. Self-custody Bitcoin in an "entity" structure is legally and practically fragile under §2036 scrutiny.

The Bona Fide Sale Defense for Bitcoin FLPs

The FLP survives §2036 if the bona fide sale exception applies. Courts evaluate:

  • Business purpose: Was there a genuine non-tax reason for forming the entity? (Investment management, asset protection, professional management of a multi-party Bitcoin position, family governance)
  • Adequate consideration: Did the transferor receive FLP interests worth the contributed Bitcoin? (Generally yes if LP interests reflect proportionate value)
  • Arm's-length dealing: Was the transfer conducted as a market participant would, or was it purely a family gifting mechanism?
  • Subsequent conduct: Did the FLP operate as a genuine entity? (Meeting minutes, capital accounts, pro-rata distributions, arm's-length management fee)

4. §2036 and Bitcoin SLATs

A Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) transfers Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust for the benefit of the grantor's spouse. The grantor retains no direct interest — the §2036 concern is addressed by design, because the grantor cannot receive distributions.

However, §2036 risk in SLAT planning arises through two mechanisms:

The Reciprocal Trust Doctrine

If both spouses simultaneously create SLATs for each other — Spouse A creates a SLAT for Spouse B's benefit, and Spouse B creates a SLAT for Spouse A's benefit — the IRS (and courts, applying United States v. Grace, 1969) may "uncross" the trusts. The result: each grantor is treated as having created a trust for their own benefit. §2036(a)(1) applies — each grantor retained the right to income from their own trust's assets — and both trusts are pulled back into their respective estates.

To avoid the reciprocal trust doctrine:

  • Stagger SLAT creation by at least 6–12 months
  • Use different trust terms, distribution standards, and beneficiary classes
  • Use different trustees and investment advisors
  • Fund with different assets (Spouse A's SLAT with one tranche of Bitcoin; Spouse B's SLAT with a different tranche at a different cost basis)

Indirect Economic Benefit

If SLAT distributions to the grantor's spouse free up marital income that would otherwise be spent on joint living expenses, courts may find that the grantor is indirectly enjoying the trust's assets through community property or family support obligations. This is a lower-probability risk than the reciprocal trust doctrine, but it is more relevant in community property states.

5. §2036 and Retained Control in Bitcoin Trusts

§2036(a)(2) — the right to designate who receives the property — captures a different type of retained interest: retained control over distribution decisions. This provision applies when a trustee:

  • Retains the power to make discretionary distributions to themselves as beneficiary
  • Has the power to add or remove beneficiaries
  • Has the power to change the trust's dispositive provisions
  • As grantor-trustee, can distribute principal to themselves

For Bitcoin trusts where the grantor also serves as trustee with broad discretionary powers, §2036(a)(2) is a live risk. The standard solution: use an independent trustee for any trust that is intended to be outside the grantor's estate. Independent means someone who:

  • Is not related or subordinate to the grantor
  • Does not have a beneficial interest in the trust that is affected by their distribution decisions
  • Has genuine fiduciary discretion that is not subject to the grantor's direction

6. §2036(b): Retained Voting Rights

§2036(b) is a specific provision targeting retained voting rights in transferred corporate stock. If a decedent transferred shares in a controlled corporation but retained voting rights (directly or indirectly), §2036(b) includes the transferred shares in the gross estate.

For Bitcoin company founders who transfer Bitcoin mining company stock to trusts or family members while retaining voting rights via dual-class share structures, §2036(b) is a direct risk. Transfers of non-voting shares (economic interest only, no voting rights) avoid §2036(b) entirely. Any transfer of voting stock in a Bitcoin company held by a founding family requires careful structuring to avoid §2036(b) inclusion.

7. How the IRS Audits §2036 Issues

Estate tax audits of large estates frequently begin with an examination of inter vivos transfers — property transferred out of the estate during life. Examiners look for:

  • Transfers to trusts or entities where the decedent also appeared as a trustee or officer
  • Entities (FLPs, LLCs) that were funded shortly before death
  • Trust distributions or entity distributions to the decedent during their lifetime
  • Shared bank accounts between the decedent and any transferred entity
  • Self-custody Bitcoin that was nominally "transferred" to an entity but where the decedent held the keys
  • Evidence of beneficial enjoyment: the decedent lived in a transferred property, drove a transferred vehicle, or spent time managing transferred assets

Bitcoin's on-chain transparency creates a unique evidentiary challenge: the blockchain is a permanent record of which address held the Bitcoin and when it moved. If Bitcoin was supposedly transferred to an FLP but remained at the same address controlled by the decedent's hardware wallet, there is on-chain evidence of the retained possession. Genuine FLP transfers must be accompanied by actual on-chain movement of Bitcoin from the transferor's address to an entity-controlled address.

8. §2035: The Three-Year Rule Companion

§2035 works in tandem with §2036 in one specific context: transfers of life insurance. Under §2035(a), if the decedent transferred a life insurance policy to an ILIT within three years of death, the death benefit is pulled back into the gross estate — even if the ILIT was otherwise properly structured to keep the policy outside the estate.

For Bitcoin holders using an ILIT to hedge GRAT mortality risk (funding the ILIT with insurance to offset §2036 inclusion if the grantor dies during the GRAT term), the §2035 three-year clock is critical: the policy must be transferred to the ILIT more than three years before death to be excluded from the estate. This argues for establishing the ILIT early — before the GRAT is funded — to ensure the three-year clock runs.

9. Practical Checklist: Designing Bitcoin Transfers to Survive §2036

  1. GRATs: Use short (2–3 year) rolling terms. Consider ILIT-held life insurance as a mortality hedge. Ensure the trust document clearly terminates the grantor's annuity interest at death so the estate includes only the §2036 inclusion amount, not a contractual annuity obligation.
  2. FLPs and LLCs: Document genuine business purpose at formation. Fund with at least some operating assets (mining equipment, real estate) alongside passive Bitcoin. Conduct quarterly meetings, maintain capital accounts, make only pro-rata distributions, and charge market-rate management fees. Bitcoin must actually move on-chain from the contributor's address to the entity's address — on-chain transfers are required, not just a ledger entry.
  3. SLATs: Do not create mirror SLATs simultaneously. Stagger by at least 6–12 months, use different trust terms, trustees, and distribution standards. Document the non-tax purposes for each trust.
  4. Dynasty trusts: Use an independent trustee — not the grantor. If the grantor wants investment control, a directed trust structure (grantor as investment director, independent trustee for distributions) is available in most dynasty trust jurisdictions and doesn't create §2036(a)(2) issues.
  5. Bitcoin custody: All transfers to entities must be genuine on-chain transfers. The entity must control the Bitcoin — either through institutional custody with the entity as account holder, or through multi-signature wallet where no single key holder is the grantor alone.
  6. No personal expense payment from entities: Every entity that holds transferred Bitcoin must maintain strict separation from the grantor's personal finances. No personal expenses paid from the entity's account. No co-mingled bank accounts.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is IRC §2036 and how does it affect Bitcoin transfers?

§2036 requires a decedent's gross estate to include any property transferred during life if the decedent retained (a) the right to possession or enjoyment of the property or income, or (b) the right to designate who receives it. For Bitcoin, this means a transfer to a trust or LLC that appears to remove Bitcoin from the estate is reversed by §2036 if the transferor retained control — including the private keys, management authority, or practical access to the Bitcoin.

What happens to a GRAT if the grantor dies during the term?

§2036(a)(1) pulls a portion of trust assets back into the gross estate — specifically, the amount needed to fund the remaining annuity obligations at the §7520 rate at death. If Bitcoin appreciated dramatically during the GRAT term, this inclusion can approach the full trust value. Short rolling GRAT terms minimize the mortality window. Life insurance in an ILIT can hedge the estate inclusion risk.

How does §2036 apply to Bitcoin held in a family limited partnership?

The IRS successfully challenges FLPs under §2036(a)(1) when the senior family member retained practical economic control — commingling funds, paying personal expenses from the entity, holding Bitcoin keys personally, or making selective non-pro-rata distributions. The bona fide sale exception protects FLPs with genuine business purpose, adequate consideration, strict formalities, and actual transfer of control — including on-chain movement of Bitcoin to an entity-controlled address.

What is the bona fide sale exception to §2036?

Transfers made for adequate and full consideration in a genuine arm's-length transaction are excluded from §2036. For Bitcoin entities, this means the transfer must have genuine non-tax business purpose, the transferor must receive proportionate entity interests as consideration, entity formalities must be observed, and the entity must operate as an independent economic enterprise — not merely as a family gifting vehicle.

Does §2036 apply to a SLAT?

Generally no, if properly structured. The grantor retains no direct right to trust income or principal. The risk arises through the reciprocal trust doctrine (mirror SLATs created simultaneously are uncrossed by courts, treating each grantor as having retained their own trust's benefits) and indirect enjoyment through marital distributions. Staggered creation with different terms and trustees eliminates the reciprocal trust risk.

Can §2036 be avoided through proper planning?

Yes. Primary defenses: the bona fide sale exception for FLPs (genuine business purpose + adequate consideration); GRAT survival (entire §2036 risk disappears if grantor outlives the term); short rolling GRAT terms to minimize mortality exposure; independent trustees for dynasty trusts and SLATs; strict entity formalities including actual on-chain Bitcoin transfers to entity-controlled addresses; no commingling of entity and personal funds.
