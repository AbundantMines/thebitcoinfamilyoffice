Spreadsheets stopped making sense. Coinbase accounts started feeling inadequate. Conversations about what happens to the coins when they die became unavoidable.

What Is a Bitcoin Family Office?

A family office is a private wealth management firm that serves a single family (or a small number of families) rather than outside clients. It consolidates investment management, tax planning, estate planning, legal coordination, and family governance under one structure — with the family's interests as the sole mandate.

A Bitcoin family office is a family office specifically designed for families whose primary wealth is in Bitcoin. The distinction matters. Traditional family office infrastructure — custody models, legal templates, reporting systems, advisor networks — was built for liquid securities, private equity, and real estate. Bitcoin's properties (self-custody, bearer instrument status, irreversible settlement, unique tax treatment) require purpose-built infrastructure at every layer.

For a deeper overview of what Bitcoin family offices are and why they're structurally different from traditional wealth management, see our Bitcoin Family Office Governance guide. This article focuses on the practical question: how do you actually build one, step by step?

Step 1: Assess Whether You Need a Bitcoin Family Office

Step 01 of 10

Before designing infrastructure, verify that the problem actually exists at the scale you're imagining.

The Thresholds That Matter

There is no universal minimum for a family office — but there are practical thresholds driven by cost-benefit math:

  • $1M–$5M in Bitcoin: A well-designed virtual family office or multi-family office arrangement is typically sufficient. Self-custody with a structured estate plan and a Bitcoin-knowledgeable CPA covers the core risk surface. Full single-family office overhead isn't justified.
  • $5M–$20M in Bitcoin: The complexity begins to demand dedicated infrastructure. At this level, a single missed tax optimization or a custody failure represents hundreds of thousands of dollars in avoidable loss. A virtual family office with a dedicated administrator makes economic sense.
  • $20M+ in Bitcoin: A full single-family office is optimal. The annual operating cost of a professional family office (typically $500K–$1M+ for staffed infrastructure) represents a small fraction of assets, and the governance, legal, and tax complexity fully justifies it.

Signs You've Outgrown DIY Management

Portfolio size is necessary but not sufficient. The following operational signals indicate that informal management has become a liability:

  • You have more than one hardware wallet and no formal documentation of which keys are where
  • You've never written a letter of instruction for your heirs regarding key access
  • Your estate plan references "cryptocurrency" without specifying wallet addresses, seed backup locations, or multi-sig quorums
  • Your CPA learned about Bitcoin from you rather than from prior clients
  • You've never had an independent review of your custody architecture
  • You have more than three family members who would be affected by your Bitcoin at death, with no formal plan for how they'd access it
  • You've never documented your Investment Policy Statement in writing

If four or more of these apply, you've outgrown informal management regardless of portfolio size.

Step 2: Choose Your Structure

Step 02 of 10

The structural decision determines your cost base, governance flexibility, and legal architecture for decades. Get it right the first time.

Structure Comparison Table

Structure Best For Annual Cost Range Key Advantages Key Limitations
Single-Family Office (SFO) $20M+ Bitcoin portfolios; complex multi-generational families $500K–$2M+ Full control; completely customized; privacy; dedicated staff High fixed cost; requires significant management attention
Multi-Family Office (MFO) $5M–$20M; families who want institutional quality without full SFO cost $50K–$250K Shared infrastructure cost; established systems; professional staff Less customization; shared attention; potential conflicts of interest
Virtual Family Office (VFO) $1M–$10M; families who prefer coordinated specialists over centralized staff $30K–$120K Lowest cost; maximum flexibility; no operational overhead Coordination burden on principal; less integrated reporting

Legal Entity Options

Regardless of which family office model you choose, the legal entity structure sits underneath everything else:

  • LLC (most common): Flexible, tax-transparent, easy to update via operating agreement. Wyoming LLCs have specific digital asset provisions that make them the preferred jurisdiction for Bitcoin family offices. See our Wyoming Trust & LLC guide for details.
  • Trust Company: Provides fiduciary status and can serve as institutional trustee of family trusts. Higher regulatory overhead. Rarely justified below $50M.
  • Hybrid (LLC + Trust): The most common Bitcoin family office structure at the $10M–$50M level. An LLC serves as the operating entity; one or more trusts hold Bitcoin for estate planning and succession purposes. This pairing provides operational flexibility alongside estate planning optimization.

Step 3: Define Your Mandate

Step 03 of 10

Before any legal or technical infrastructure exists, you need a written statement of what the family office is for.

The mandate answers three questions: What are we trying to accomplish? What are the boundaries of acceptable risk? How do we make decisions? These answers become the foundation of the Investment Policy Statement (IPS).

Investment Policy Statement Essentials

A Bitcoin IPS differs from a traditional IPS in important ways. It must address not just allocation targets and risk tolerance, but the technical custody layer — because in Bitcoin, investment policy and custody policy are inseparable.

Core IPS components for a Bitcoin family office:

  • Investment objective: Long-term purchasing power preservation in Bitcoin-denominated terms; no return target expressed in fiat
  • Asset allocation policy: Bitcoin-only, or Bitcoin with a defined ceiling for other holdings (e.g., "minimum 80% Bitcoin at all times")
  • Risk tolerance: Explicit acknowledgment of 40–80% drawdown scenarios; liquidity requirements; no forced selling thresholds
  • Custody standards: Approved custody models, multi-sig quorum requirements, approved custodians, hardware wallet requirements
  • Transaction authority: Who can authorize transactions, at what size, with what approval requirements
  • Rebalancing policy: Trigger conditions for changes to custody arrangements; not return-chasing rebalancing
  • Review schedule: Annual IPS review; event-triggered review conditions
"The Investment Policy Statement is not a document you write because your advisors told you to. It is the constitution of your family's relationship with Bitcoin — the document that prevents future-you from doing something present-you would regret."

Bitcoin-Only vs. Multi-Asset Mandate

This decision shapes everything that follows. Bitcoin-only mandates are simpler to administer, easier to articulate to heirs, and avoid the portfolio complexity that dilutes Bitcoin's unique properties. Multi-asset mandates may be appropriate if the family has significant non-Bitcoin wealth that requires integrated management — but they substantially increase complexity in reporting, governance, and tax planning.

For most families whose primary wealth is Bitcoin, a Bitcoin-first or Bitcoin-only mandate is the right starting point. You can always expand the mandate later; narrowing it is harder.

Step 4: Solve Custody

Step 04 of 10

Custody is the most technically consequential decision in the entire setup process. There is no undo.

Traditional family offices outsource custody entirely to regulated intermediaries. Bitcoin family offices cannot do this safely — not because institutional custody is unavailable, but because the tradeoffs are different at the fundamental level. Bitcoin in institutional custody is Bitcoin whose security depends on the institution. Bitcoin in self-custody is Bitcoin whose security depends on the key management system you build. Neither is obviously superior; the right answer depends on portfolio size, technical capacity, and risk tolerance.

The Three Custody Models

Model Description Best For Key Risks
Self-Custody (Multi-Sig) Family controls all keys using a multi-signature quorum (e.g., 3-of-5). Keys held by family members and trusted advisors. Technically capable families; Bitcoin-only mandates; deep holders who prioritize sovereignty Key loss or compromise; succession complexity; technical errors; no institutional backstop
Institutional Custody Bitcoin held at regulated custodian (Fidelity Digital Assets, Anchorage, BitGo, etc.). Custodian holds keys on family's behalf. Fiduciary trust accounts; less technically confident families; regulatory compliance requirements Counterparty risk; no true self-sovereignty; custodian fees; potential freezes or bankruptcy
Hybrid (Tiered) Operational funds (5–10%) in accessible self-custody; core holdings (70–85%) in multi-sig cold storage; legacy tranche (10–20%) at institutional custodian. Most Bitcoin family offices above $5M; optimal balance of sovereignty and fiduciary compliance Complexity of maintaining multiple systems; higher administration overhead

For the vast majority of Bitcoin family offices, the hybrid tiered model is the right answer. It preserves self-sovereign custody for the core holdings while providing institutional backstop for the legacy tranche — important for trust fiduciary requirements and heir confidence.

The Director of Keys Role

Any family office using self-custody or hybrid custody needs a designated Director of Keys — the person responsible for the technical custody architecture. This is not necessarily a technical wizard; it is the person accountable for:

  • Maintaining an accurate inventory of all hardware devices, seed phrases, and multi-sig quorum participants
  • Conducting annual custody audits (verifying devices work, seeds are intact, quorum can be assembled)
  • Maintaining the Letter of Instructions for heirs
  • Coordinating custody changes through the approved governance process
  • Ensuring succession access — that the right people can access Bitcoin in the right circumstances

Step 5: Build the Governance Framework

Step 05 of 10

Governance is how the family makes decisions, resolves conflicts, and ensures the mandate survives personnel changes.

A Bitcoin family office without formal governance is just a group of people with a shared bank account and some hardware wallets. Governance creates the institutional memory, decision rights, and accountability structures that allow the family office to function across generations and personnel changes.

Core Governance Documents

The Family Constitution is the foundational document. It establishes the family's shared values and long-term mission, the governance structure and its legitimacy, and the process for amending governance rules. It is not a legal document — it is a statement of shared intent that gives legal documents their coherence.

Below the Family Constitution, the governance framework includes:

  • Investment Committee Charter: Composition, meeting frequency, decision thresholds, quorum requirements, and voting procedures for investment decisions
  • Custody Committee Charter: Oversight of custody architecture, authority to approve custody changes, annual audit requirements
  • Decision Rights Matrix: Explicit table of who can authorize what — routine transactions, large transactions, custody changes, IPS amendments, distribution decisions — and at what approval threshold
  • Conflict Resolution Process: How disputes are resolved without family destruction; escalation path from informal discussion to formal mediation

For comprehensive guidance on governance structure design, see our Bitcoin Family Office Governance framework.

Succession Planning Integration

Governance and succession are not separate concerns. The governance framework must include explicit succession protocols: who becomes the next Director of Keys, how Investment Committee membership transitions, and how the family constitution is amended as family composition changes. Build succession into governance from day one, not as an afterthought.

Step 6: Assemble Your Team

Step 06 of 10

The team you build determines the quality of everything else. Generic advisors with Bitcoin added as an afterthought are a liability.

The most common and costly mistake in Bitcoin family office setup is hiring traditional professionals and expecting them to learn Bitcoin on the job. The legal, tax, and technical nuances of Bitcoin are consequential enough that generalist advisors create material risk. You need specialists, or generalists with deep demonstrated Bitcoin experience.

Core Team Roles

1. Bitcoin Estate Attorney
Your estate attorney must have specific experience drafting Bitcoin trust documents, LLC operating agreements with digital asset authority clauses, and Letters of Instruction for key access. Ask directly: "Have you drafted a Bitcoin dynasty trust?" If they haven't, move on. The cost of bad legal drafting is enormous and often only discovered at death.

2. CPA with Cryptocurrency Specialization
Bitcoin tax is its own discipline. Your CPA must understand cost basis tracking across wallets, UTXO-level accounting, Form 1099-DA reporting (mandatory beginning in 2026), mining income treatment, like-kind exchange history, and estate step-up in basis optimization. A general CPA who "does crypto" is not the same as a CPA who has built Bitcoin tax infrastructure for family offices.

3. Multisig Technologist / Custody Architect
For any family using self-custody or hybrid custody, a technical specialist is essential. This person designs the multi-signature quorum structure, selects hardware, establishes backup and recovery procedures, and trains family members on operational procedures. They may be an independent consultant or a firm specializing in Bitcoin custody solutions.

4. Family Office Administrator
The operational backbone. Responsible for financial reporting, document management, meeting coordination, bill payment, and compliance calendar. For Bitcoin family offices, this person must understand digital asset reporting sufficiently to interface with the CPA and custody team.

5. Bitcoin-Knowledgeable Financial Advisor (Optional)
At larger portfolio sizes, a family CFO or financial advisor role may make sense for consolidated reporting, scenario modeling, and strategic planning. This role is optional for Bitcoin-only families with strong internal governance, but valuable when multi-asset complexity or beneficiary distributions become significant.

Where to Find Bitcoin-Competent Advisors

The Bitcoin-competent professional network is smaller than it appears. Referrals from other Bitcoin family offices are the highest-quality lead source. Bitcoin-focused legal and accounting networks, conferences (Bitcoin 2025, Pacific Bitcoin Forum), and organizations like the Digital Assets Council of Financial Professionals provide another tier. When interviewing, ask for specific past work product — not general familiarity with cryptocurrency.

Step 7: Set Up the Legal Entity

Step 07 of 10

The legal entity is the container. Wyoming is the right container for most Bitcoin family offices.

Jurisdiction Selection

Wyoming has established the most comprehensive statutory framework for digital assets in the United States. Key advantages for Bitcoin family offices:

  • Wyoming Special Purpose Depository Institutions (SPDIs) can hold digital assets as a regulated banking function
  • Wyoming's DAPT (Domestic Asset Protection Trust) statutes explicitly accommodate digital asset trust property
  • LLC operating agreements can explicitly authorize digital asset transactions and custody arrangements with more statutory clarity than other states
  • Wyoming's dynasty trust statutes allow perpetual trusts — Bitcoin held in trust for 1,000 years is legally permissible
  • No state income tax

Delaware is the second-best jurisdiction for the LLC itself, particularly if you anticipate raising outside capital or have complex equity arrangements. However, for the trust layer, Wyoming is generally superior for Bitcoin-specific purposes.

Nevada offers strong asset protection statutes and no state income tax. It is a viable alternative to Wyoming, particularly if the family has existing Nevada connections. However, Wyoming's digital asset statutory specificity currently provides a meaningful edge.

LLC Operating Agreement: Critical Clauses

A standard LLC operating agreement is insufficient for a Bitcoin family office. Your attorney must include:

  • Digital Asset Authority Clause: Explicit authorization for the LLC to hold, transfer, and manage Bitcoin and other digital assets
  • Custody Protocol Integration: By-reference incorporation of the family's custody policy, or explicit custody standards within the agreement
  • Decision Rights for Digital Asset Transactions: Specific authorization thresholds for Bitcoin transactions separate from general business decisions
  • Key Management Succession: Provisions for what happens to custody rights if a key holder is incapacitated or dies
  • Amendment Procedures for Custody Changes: Governance process for changing custody arrangements, including required votes and notice periods

Trust Layer

For most families with significant Bitcoin holdings, one or more trusts should sit alongside the LLC. Common structures include:

  • Bitcoin Dynasty Trust: Perpetual trust designed to hold Bitcoin across multiple generations. Removes assets from taxable estate while maintaining family control through carefully drafted trustee provisions. See our Bitcoin Dynasty Trust guide.
  • Revocable Living Trust: Simpler structure that avoids probate and provides basic succession continuity without irrevocable commitment. Appropriate for families not yet ready for a dynasty trust.
  • Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT): Removes Bitcoin from taxable estate while providing indirect access through spouse distributions.

For estate planning structures beyond the family office entity itself, see our comprehensive Bitcoin Estate Planning Guide.

Step 8: Establish Reporting & Compliance

Step 08 of 10

What you don't measure, you can't manage. Reporting is the operational heartbeat of a functioning family office.

Performance Reporting

Bitcoin family office reporting differs fundamentally from traditional portfolio reporting. The key metrics are:

  • Bitcoin balance (in satoshis and BTC): The primary measure. Fiat-denominated performance reports are secondary.
  • Cost basis by lot: Essential for tax planning. Maintains UTXO-level or wallet-level cost basis with acquisition date and amount.
  • Custody distribution: Percentage of holdings in each custody tier (hot, cold self-custody, institutional). Track against IPS targets.
  • Unrealized gain/loss: Relevant for tax-loss harvesting and estate planning timing decisions.
  • Estate tax exposure: Dollar value of Bitcoin that would be subject to estate tax at current price, relative to applicable exclusion amounts.

Tax Compliance: Form 1099-DA

Beginning in 2026, brokers are required to issue Form 1099-DA for digital asset transactions. This changes the compliance posture for Bitcoin family offices in important ways: the IRS now has third-party reporting for exchange-held Bitcoin, creating a need for precise reconciliation between family-reported figures and broker-issued 1099s.

Self-custodied Bitcoin transactions remain outside 1099-DA reporting, but the family office must maintain its own records sufficient to reconstruct basis and gains for any transfers to or from exchanges. Your CPA must have a clear system for this reconciliation.

Bitcoin mining income also affects compliance for any family office using Bitcoin mining as a tax strategy — depreciation on mining equipment, energy costs, and the income recognition timing are all significant variables that must be tracked at the entity level.

Annual Family Meeting

A functioning family office holds a formal annual meeting that covers:

  • IPS review and reaffirmation
  • Custody audit results
  • Portfolio performance review
  • Estate tax exposure assessment
  • Tax planning for the upcoming year
  • Governance updates (personnel changes, succession review)
  • Heir education (if applicable)

Document the minutes of this meeting. They are evidence of institutional governance that matters for legal and tax purposes.

Step 9: Plan for Succession

Step 09 of 10

The most valuable thing your family office can do is survive you. Succession is not an add-on — it is the point.

Trustee Selection

The trustee of any Bitcoin trust bears fiduciary responsibility for custody decisions that most traditional trustees have never faced. Selecting the right trustee — individual or corporate — is one of the highest-stakes decisions in the entire setup process. See our Bitcoin Trustee Selection Guide for a comprehensive framework.

Key trustee selection criteria for Bitcoin:

  • Demonstrated understanding of Bitcoin's custody properties and self-sovereign key management
  • Willingness to engage with a multisig custody arrangement rather than demanding custodian transfer
  • Experience with digital asset trust administration (or willingness to partner with a specialist)
  • Geographic separation from the principal for multi-sig key distribution
  • Clear succession plan for the trustee role itself

Heir Education

Bitcoin is one of the few asset classes where heirs who cannot technically operate it cannot benefit from it. An heir who receives a Bitcoin estate but cannot access the keys has received nothing. Heir education is therefore not optional for Bitcoin succession planning — it is a core fiduciary obligation.

A multi-stage heir education program includes:

  • Stage 1 (Understanding): What Bitcoin is, why the family holds it, what makes it different from bank accounts
  • Stage 2 (Technical literacy): How to verify a wallet balance, what a seed phrase is, how multi-sig works at a conceptual level
  • Stage 3 (Operational competence): How to access the Letter of Instructions, who to call, what steps to follow
  • Stage 4 (Governance participation): Active participation in Investment Committee or Custody Committee as appropriate

Letter of Instructions

The Letter of Instructions is the operational successor guide — the document that tells heirs, trustees, and advisors exactly what to do when the principal is incapacitated or dies. It is not a legal document (it does not override a will or trust), but it is operationally indispensable for Bitcoin succession.

A Bitcoin Letter of Instructions must include:

  • Inventory of all wallets, devices, and custodians
  • Multi-sig quorum participants and how to contact them
  • Step-by-step access procedures for each custody tier
  • Location of all seed phrase backups
  • Contact information for the Bitcoin estate attorney, CPA, and custody technologist
  • IPS summary and immediate decision restrictions

Key Transfer Protocols

At the operational level, succession requires a formal key transfer protocol — the sequence of steps by which custody authority transfers from the current Director of Keys to a successor. This protocol should be documented, tested, and reviewed annually. It should never be the first time anyone attempts it when a death occurs.

Step 10: Review and Evolve

Step 10 of 10

A Bitcoin family office that doesn't adapt will eventually fail. Annual review is the operating system.

No governance framework, IPS, or custody architecture survives contact with reality unchanged. Annual reviews are mandatory. Event-triggered reviews are often more important.

Annual IPS Review

The Investment Policy Statement review asks: does this document still reflect the family's mandate? The review covers allocation targets (still appropriate?), custody standards (still adequate given changing threat landscape?), transaction authority thresholds (still appropriate given changed portfolio value?), and approved custodians (still appropriate given changing institutional landscape?).

Triggers for Out-of-Cycle Review

  • Major price moves: A 3x increase in Bitcoin price changes estate tax exposure materially. Gifting strategy, trust funding decisions, and GRAT timing may all need to be revisited.
  • Legislative changes: Tax law changes (especially estate tax exemption changes), new digital asset regulations, or new state-level digital asset statutes may require entity restructuring.
  • Family structure changes: Marriage, divorce, birth, death, or significant changes in a beneficiary's circumstances may require trust amendments, IPS updates, or governance changes.
  • Security incidents: Any custody near-miss, key compromise, or loss of a key holder triggers an immediate custody review and potentially a full architecture redesign.
  • Team changes: Loss of a key team member (attorney, CPA, custody technologist) requires immediate replacement planning and transition protocols.

Common Mistakes When Setting Up a Bitcoin Family Office

These are the five mistakes that appear most frequently — and most expensively:

1. Building the legal entity before solving custody. Families often rush to form an LLC or trust and then discover that their custody architecture doesn't fit. The operating agreement references custody procedures that don't exist, or the trust document transfers Bitcoin to a corporate trustee that can't actually hold it. Solve custody architecture first; let it inform the legal structure.

2. Using traditional advisors for Bitcoin-specific problems. A general estate attorney who adds "cryptocurrency" to a standard trust template is not providing Bitcoin-competent legal counsel. The specific statutory requirements for digital asset authority clauses, key succession provisions, and custodian standards are not intuitive extensions of traditional law. Require demonstrated, verifiable Bitcoin experience from every advisor.

3. Creating single points of failure in custody. A single-signature cold wallet — even one controlled by the family — is a single point of failure. A family office with $10M in a hardware wallet controlled by one person has a catastrophic succession risk. Multi-signature custody is not optional at family office scale.

4. Skipping heir education. The most beautifully constructed family office governance framework fails if heirs cannot access or understand the assets. Heir education is not a soft concern — it is an operational prerequisite for Bitcoin succession. Document it, formalize it, and test it before you need it.

5. Treating the setup as a one-time event. A family office is not a project with a completion date; it is an operating institution. Annual reviews, governance meetings, custody audits, and IPS updates are not bureaucratic overhead — they are the mechanism by which the family office fulfills its mandate. Families that treat setup as a box to check typically have governance failures within three to five years.

The 10-Step Bitcoin Family Office Setup Checklist
  1. Complete a threshold assessment — confirm $5M+ holdings justify family office infrastructure
  2. Select your structure — single-family office, multi-family office, or virtual family office; LLC vs. hybrid
  3. Draft your Investment Policy Statement — mandate, risk tolerance, custody standards, transaction authority
  4. Design custody architecture — tiered model, multi-sig quorum, institutional tranche; appoint Director of Keys
  5. Build governance framework — Family Constitution, Investment Committee charter, Custody Committee charter, Decision Rights Matrix
  6. Assemble your team — Bitcoin estate attorney, crypto-specialized CPA, multisig technologist, family office administrator
  7. Form the legal entity — Wyoming LLC (or Delaware LLC); LLC operating agreement with digital asset authority clauses; trust layer if appropriate
  8. Establish reporting and compliance — reporting systems, custody audit calendar, annual family meeting protocol, 1099-DA compliance process
  9. Execute succession plan — trustee selection, heir education program, Letter of Instructions, key transfer protocols
  10. Schedule annual reviews — IPS review, custody audit, governance review, estate planning update; set event-triggered review triggers

Frequently Asked Questions

How much Bitcoin do you need to set up a family office?
A meaningful threshold is $5M or more in Bitcoin holdings, where the complexity of self-managed custody, tax planning, and estate planning begins to justify dedicated infrastructure. The optimal threshold for a full single-family office is typically $20M or more, where operational costs represent a small fraction of assets under management. Below $5M, a virtual family office or multi-family office structure usually provides better cost efficiency.
What is the best legal structure for a Bitcoin family office?
For most Bitcoin family offices, a Wyoming LLC combined with one or more Bitcoin-specific trusts (such as a Wyoming DAPT or dynasty trust) provides the strongest combination of flexibility, asset protection, and digital asset legal clarity. Wyoming has the most developed statutory framework for digital assets. Delaware is the second-best jurisdiction for LLCs but lacks Wyoming's digital asset statutory depth.
What custody model should a Bitcoin family office use?
Most Bitcoin family offices use a tiered custody model: operational funds (1–5% of holdings) in accessible self-custody for liquidity; core holdings (70–85%) in a multi-signature cold storage arrangement; and a legacy tranche (10–20%) at institutional custody for fiduciary compliance. The exact split depends on portfolio size, technical capacity, and risk tolerance. Avoid single points of failure in any tier.
Who should be on the team for a Bitcoin family office?
A Bitcoin family office requires: an estate attorney with specific Bitcoin trust and LLC experience; a CPA with cryptocurrency tax specialization and Form 1099-DA expertise; a multisig technologist who can design and maintain the key management system; and a family office administrator for reporting and operations. The critical mistake is hiring traditional advisors and expecting them to learn Bitcoin on the job — the technical and legal nuances are too consequential.
How long does it take to set up a Bitcoin family office?
A basic structure — LLC formation, initial IPS, custody architecture design, and core legal documents — can be established in 60 to 90 days with the right team. A comprehensive setup including dynasty trust formation, full governance documentation, heir education, and operational reporting systems typically takes 4 to 6 months. Do not rush the custody architecture — it is irreversible if done wrong.
