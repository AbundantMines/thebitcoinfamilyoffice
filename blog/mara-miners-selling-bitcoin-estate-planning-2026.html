When the Miners Sell: What MARA and Core Scientific's Bitcoin Exit Means for Your Estate Plan

MARA Holdings is now authorized to sell its entire $3.8 billion Bitcoin stash. Core Scientific already moved $175 million out the door on its way to becoming an AI company. This isn't noise — it's a structural inflection point with direct consequences for anyone using mining stocks as Bitcoin exposure in an estate plan.

March 4, 2026

Two things happened in recent months that, separately, are interesting. Together, they represent something your estate plan needs to grapple with directly.

First: MARA Holdings, the largest publicly traded Bitcoin miner in North America, reversed its HODL policy in its March 2026 10-K filing. The company is now authorized to sell its entire $3.8 billion Bitcoin stash at management's discretion. The position that was once framed to investors as a core part of MARA's value proposition — the perpetual accumulation of BTC on the corporate balance sheet — is now a liquid asset that can be deployed however the board sees fit.

Second: Core Scientific, once holding 9,618 BTC at its peak, has systematically liquidated its position to fund an AI infrastructure pivot. As of early 2026, the company holds approximately 630 BTC — a 93% reduction. The firm that once marketed itself as a Bitcoin-native infrastructure company is now deploying the proceeds into high-density computing for AI workloads. CoinDesk summarized the trend bluntly: "End of Bitcoin HODL: Public miners going all-in on AI."

Here's what this means in plain terms: the era of public Bitcoin miners functioning as de facto Bitcoin treasury companies is ending. The thesis that holding MARA or CORZ stock gave you meaningful Bitcoin exposure in your portfolio — or, more dangerously, in your estate plan — has just been invalidated in real time.

This is not a commentary on whether MARA or Core Scientific made the right capital allocation decision. That's their business. This is a commentary on what happens to families who structured their estate planning around mining stocks as Bitcoin proxies, and what the correct response looks like now.

The Thesis That Just Broke

Starting around 2020, a segment of institutional and high-net-worth investors adopted a specific playbook: use public Bitcoin mining stocks as a leveraged proxy for Bitcoin exposure in accounts and vehicles where holding actual BTC was operationally difficult. The logic was coherent at the time. Mining companies were accumulating Bitcoin faster than the market could price it, their operational leverage amplified Bitcoin's price moves, and they held substantial BTC reserves on the balance sheet.

For estate planning purposes, this was often presented as a "close enough" solution. Bitcoin in an IRA? Complicated. Bitcoin in a trust that your estate attorney doesn't know how to handle? Even more complicated. Mining stocks in a brokerage account that rolls cleanly into an existing estate structure? Much simpler. The IRS knows how to value public equity. Your estate attorney knows how to handle it. Your broker can move it. The custody problem evaporates.

The flaw in this thesis was always the same one: you don't own Bitcoin. You own a claim on a corporation's equity, and the corporation's Bitcoin exposure is entirely at management's discretion. When that management decides to sell the Bitcoin — as MARA is now authorized to do, and as Core Scientific has already done — your Bitcoin "exposure" exits the building without your permission.

The Core Problem

Bitcoin held by a public company can be sold, pledged, swapped, or redirected by the board at any time. Bitcoin held in your name in a properly structured trust cannot. These are not equivalent exposures. Treating them as equivalent is an estate planning error that the MARA reversal and Core Scientific pivot just made impossible to ignore.

It's worth being precise about what "authorized to sell" actually means in MARA's case. The 10-K language authorizes management to sell the entire Bitcoin position at its discretion. MARA has not announced it will sell all its Bitcoin immediately — but the contractual constraint against selling, which was the original thesis, is gone. The structural protection that made the stock function as a Bitcoin treasury vehicle has been removed. What remains is a company with a large Bitcoin position that it may sell in whole, in part, at any time, for any reason the board deems appropriate.

For a family that held MARA stock in a trust specifically to get Bitcoin exposure inside the trust — that trust no longer holds what it was intended to hold. Not immediately, but potentially. The underlying asset is no longer protected from corporate discretion. That's a fundamental change to the investment thesis, and it demands a fundamental review of the position.

Mining Stock vs. Direct Bitcoin in an Estate Plan: Not Even Close

Let's be precise about the structural differences, because they compound in significance when estate planning is involved:

Characteristic Direct BTC in a Trust Mining Stock (MARA, CORZ)
What you own Bitcoin itself, on-chain Equity in a company that may hold Bitcoin
Bitcoin exposure 100% direct; 1:1 with price Indirect; subject to management decisions
Bitcoin can be sold without your consent No — you control the keys Yes — board authorized at any time
Subject to company debt/dilution risk No Yes
Business pivot risk None — Bitcoin doesn't pivot High — see Core Scientific (AI pivot)
Self-custody possible Yes — private keys, multisig No
Estate valuation BTC price × coins held Share price on date of death (may not track BTC)
Annual gifting treatment Gift of BTC; simple valuation Gift of stock; subject to wash-sale rules
Step-up basis on death Yes — Bitcoin FMV at death Yes — stock FMV at death
Dynasty trust compatibility Excellent — perpetual BTC holding Poor — company may pivot or fail

Every one of these differences matters for estate planning purposes. But two deserve special emphasis given the MARA and Core Scientific news.

First: business pivot risk. A dynasty trust is designed to hold assets across generations — potentially 50, 100, or more years. Funding a dynasty trust with MARA stock creates an implicit bet not just on Bitcoin's price, but on MARA's continued existence as a Bitcoin-relevant company, its management team making Bitcoin-aligned capital allocation decisions, and the mining industry remaining structurally important to Bitcoin over multi-decade time horizons. Core Scientific — which was worth billions and held nearly 10,000 BTC at its peak — is now an AI infrastructure company with 630 BTC. That happened in under two years. A dynasty trust funded with CORZ at the peak now holds equity in a company that bears almost no resemblance to the investment thesis that motivated the transfer.

Second: management authorization to sell. When you hold Bitcoin in a trust, the trust document governs what can be done with those coins. You choose the trustee, the investment policy, the distribution rules, the successor trustees. You have meaningful control over the governance of the asset. When you hold mining stock in a trust, a board of directors that you didn't choose, following an investment policy you had no hand in, makes decisions about the underlying Bitcoin every quarter. The MARA reversal means that board now has explicit authorization to exit the entire Bitcoin position. Your trust's investment thesis is subject to their vote, not yours.

What Families Who Own Mining Stocks Should Do Now

This is not an article arguing that everyone should immediately sell their MARA and CORZ positions. The correct action depends entirely on your specific situation — your cost basis, your estate tax exposure, what role the mining stocks play in your overall portfolio, and what your current estate documents say. What this article argues is that every family holding meaningful mining stock positions should conduct a deliberate review using the following framework.

Step 1: Clarify the Original Thesis

Why did you buy the mining stock? There are several legitimate answers, and they lead to different conclusions:

  • You bought it as a leveraged Bitcoin proxy. This thesis has weakened materially. MARA retaining authorization to sell its BTC removes the structural protection that made it function as a treasury vehicle. CORZ has already pivoted. If the thesis was Bitcoin exposure, evaluate whether the current company still delivers it.
  • You bought it as an AI infrastructure play. Then the Core Scientific pivot may actually be a thesis confirmation, not a violation. Review accordingly.
  • You bought it as a mining operations bet. The operational mining business remains intact — hashrate, revenue, margins. Evaluate on those metrics rather than Bitcoin treasury holdings.
  • It was inherited or received as compensation. The original thesis may be irrelevant. The relevant questions are: what is your basis, what is your estate tax exposure, and does continuing to hold this position serve your estate planning goals?

Step 2: Evaluate Cost Basis and Tax Position

Mining stocks have been volatile. Depending on when you acquired your position, you may be sitting on significant gains, significant losses, or close to flat. The cost basis analysis matters for two reasons:

Tax-loss harvesting opportunity: If you're holding at a loss, realizing the loss before year-end creates a capital loss that offsets other gains — including Bitcoin gains, stock gains, or other investment income. Unlike Bitcoin (which has no wash-sale rule), mining stocks are subject to wash-sale rules. Selling MARA at a loss means you cannot repurchase MARA within 30 days and claim the loss. You could, however, purchase a different mining stock as a substitute without triggering the wash-sale rule, if you want to maintain some mining industry exposure while harvesting the loss.

GRAT reset opportunity: If you funded a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust with mining stock during the 2024 bull run, those trusts may now be significantly underwater relative to the funding price. As with Bitcoin GRATs, the response is the same: allow the underwater GRAT to run its course (assets simply return to your estate — no penalty, no loss), and fund a new GRAT at today's lower valuations. If you believe mining stocks will recover — or if you're holding the stock for its AI infrastructure business rather than its Bitcoin treasury — a reset GRAT at current prices captures the full recovery inside the trust for your heirs' benefit.

Step 3: Review Estate Documents for Mining Stock Holdings

If you have an existing irrevocable trust, dynasty trust, or SLAT that holds mining stocks, pull the trust document and investment policy statement and answer these questions:

  • Does the trust's investment policy statement describe the purpose of the mining stock holding? Does the thesis still hold?
  • Is there a trustee with authority to rebalance or exit the position if the investment thesis changes materially? A trustee's fiduciary duty requires acting in beneficiaries' best interests — a material change in the underlying company's strategy may be exactly the kind of triggering event that requires trustee action.
  • Is there a trust protector with power to modify investment provisions if market conditions change significantly?
  • Are the mining stocks inside an IRA? If so, the analysis changes — you can't do a tax-loss harvest inside an IRA, and the estate planning tools available are different. But the question of whether the investment still serves your retirement and estate goals is just as relevant.

Step 4: Evaluate the Swap — From Mining Stock to Direct BTC

For families who held mining stocks specifically as Bitcoin proxies inside estate vehicles, the cleanest long-term solution is to convert to direct Bitcoin ownership. This means:

  1. Selling the mining stock position (with tax planning to optimize the timing and method).
  2. Acquiring actual Bitcoin.
  3. Contributing the Bitcoin to a properly structured irrevocable trust, dynasty trust, or other estate vehicle — or transferring it into existing structures if appropriate.

The tax event on step 1 is the friction point. If you have significant gains in mining stocks, selling triggers a capital gains tax event. This is often worth it when: (1) the estate tax benefit of removing Bitcoin from your taxable estate exceeds the capital gains cost; (2) you're in a position to use Section 1031-adjacent strategies or installment sales to manage the gain; or (3) your mining stock position is at a loss, making the swap essentially free from a tax standpoint.

If you're inside an IRA or 401(k), the analysis is different — there's no capital gains event on the swap, and you can typically move freely between investment options depending on your custodian's available Bitcoin-related vehicles. Some self-directed IRA custodians allow direct Bitcoin holding. Moving from MARA stock to a Bitcoin position inside the IRA may not require any tax event at all.

The Gifting Window: Mining Stocks at Depressed Valuations

If you intend to continue holding mining stocks and haven't yet used your 2026 annual gift tax exclusion, depressed valuations create a specific opportunity worth naming explicitly.

The annual gift tax exclusion allows you to transfer $19,000 per recipient (indexed; confirm current figure) without gift tax or exemption consumption. For mining stocks that have declined from 2024-2025 highs, you can transfer more shares per dollar of exclusion than was possible at peak prices.

A concrete example: if MARA traded at $25 per share at its 2024 high and is now trading at $14 per share, you can gift approximately 80% more shares per $19,000 exclusion than you could at the peak. If MARA recovers — whether because of its AI pivot, its remaining Bitcoin position, or broader market conditions — that incremental share transfer compounds inside your heirs' hands, outside your taxable estate.

The caveat applies with full force here: gifted mining stock transfers your cost basis to the recipient. If your shares were acquired at $8, your heirs inherit an $8 basis and will owe capital gains on recovery. If you're prioritizing estate tax reduction over capital gains optimization, this typically makes sense for estates above the exemption threshold. If you're below the threshold and holding Bitcoin-adjacent assets primarily for appreciation, the step-up at death may be more valuable than the annual gifting.

This is exactly the kind of situation where estate planning is not a one-size-fits-all exercise. The right answer depends on your estate size, your state of residence, your cost basis, and your conviction about the investment going forward.

What This Signals About Bitcoin's Maturation — and Why It's Actually Bullish

Let's pull back from the estate planning mechanics for a moment and look at what this trend actually tells us about Bitcoin's structural position.

For much of Bitcoin's early institutional history, miners holding large BTC reserves served a signaling function. It communicated that the people closest to Bitcoin's production — the ones who understood the economics intimately — believed in its value enough to hold rather than sell. A miner that HODLed was a miner that had conviction. The accumulation was a vote of confidence.

MARA reversing its HODL policy is not a loss of confidence in Bitcoin. It's a recognition that Bitcoin has matured to the point where miners no longer need to function as treasury vehicles to signal institutional legitimacy. BlackRock, Fidelity, and Vanguard are now Bitcoin custodians. Sovereign wealth funds are exploring allocations. Corporate treasuries hold Bitcoin. The institutional acceptance that Bitcoin's early mining advocates were trying to demonstrate by HODLing is now demonstrated daily by a trillion-dollar market and global ETF flows.

When MARA sells Bitcoin, they sell it to someone — and that someone is typically an institution or a long-term holder with a longer time horizon than a mining company's quarterly earnings cycle. The capital doesn't leave Bitcoin. It rotates into stronger hands.

The First Principles View

Miners are not Bitcoin's foundation. Miners are Bitcoin's service layer. They secure the network and earn block rewards in exchange. Whether they hold or sell those rewards is a capital allocation decision — not a referendum on Bitcoin's value. A world where miners sell Bitcoin freely to institutions is a more mature Bitcoin market, not a weaker one.

For estate planning purposes, this maturation is relevant for a specific reason: it confirms that Bitcoin's value proposition no longer depends on proxy holders like mining companies to maintain conviction. The case for holding Bitcoin directly in estate structures — rather than through equity proxies — has never been stronger, precisely because the institutional infrastructure to hold, custody, and transfer direct Bitcoin has become genuinely robust.

What Core Scientific's AI Pivot Tells You About Operational Risk

Core Scientific's transformation from a Bitcoin mining company with 9,618 BTC to an AI infrastructure company with 630 BTC happened in roughly 24 months. This isn't a cautionary tale about Bitcoin — it's a cautionary tale about the operational risks of holding equity in capital-intensive infrastructure businesses.

AI data center demand has created extraordinary pricing power for anyone with large-scale compute infrastructure and reliable power supply. Mining facilities have both. The economics of re-pointing that infrastructure toward hyperscaler AI contracts, at the margins being offered by Microsoft, Google, and CoreWeave, are compelling enough that even Bitcoin-native management teams are choosing AI revenue over Bitcoin accumulation.

This is a rational business decision. It is also a complete thesis invalidation for anyone who held CORZ as a Bitcoin proxy. The company pivoted from one industry to another at speed, and the Bitcoin balance sheet went with it. No mining stock investor had a contractual right to object.

This is precisely why direct Bitcoin ownership in a properly structured trust is categorically different from equity ownership in a company that holds Bitcoin. The Bitcoin cannot pivot. The trust document cannot be overridden by a board resolution. The private keys are not subject to management discretion. What you put in stays in — and appreciates or depreciates based solely on Bitcoin's value, not on management's view of AI computing margins.

Mining as Operational Strategy vs. Mining Stock as Investment Vehicle

Before closing, it's worth drawing a distinction that gets lost in coverage like the CoinDesk piece on the "end of HODL." There are two very different relationships one can have with Bitcoin mining, and they carry entirely different risk profiles for high-net-worth families.

Mining stock as an investment vehicle means you are a passive equity investor in a corporation. You have no operational control. You cannot influence custody decisions. You cannot prevent a Bitcoin sale. You bear all the business risks of the company — execution, leverage, dilution, management quality, regulatory exposure — on top of Bitcoin price risk. The MARA and Core Scientific situations are the inevitable consequence of this structure: management makes decisions you cannot control, and those decisions can completely change the nature of your exposure.

Operational Bitcoin mining — participating directly in mining infrastructure through hosted mining arrangements, co-location agreements, or owned hardware — is a fundamentally different proposition. You earn Bitcoin directly. You take immediate custody. The Bitcoin lands in your wallet, under your control, immediately upon generation. There is no management layer, no board authorization required, no corporate pivot that redirects your earnings. You mine Bitcoin; you hold Bitcoin. The correlation to Bitcoin is perfect because the output is Bitcoin.

For HNWI families building multi-decade estate structures, operational mining serves two purposes simultaneously: it generates BTC at or below market cost (improving acquisition cost basis over time), and it creates tax deductions through equipment depreciation and operating expenses that can significantly reduce current-year income tax liability. Both benefits are entirely independent of what MARA or Core Scientific decides to do with their balance sheets this quarter.

The families getting this right are treating operational mining as a Bitcoin acquisition and tax optimization strategy — not as a passive investment. Mining stock is a passive investment. Operational mining is active participation in Bitcoin's network with direct economic benefit. These are not the same thing, and the distinction matters enormously for estate planning.

Estate Planning Action Items: What to Do This Month

Here's a prioritized checklist for families who need to respond to the MARA and Core Scientific developments:

1

Inventory All Mining Stock Positions Across All Accounts

Where to look: Taxable brokerage accounts, IRAs, 401(k)s, existing irrevocable trusts, dynasty trusts, SLATs, and any custodial accounts held for minor children or other beneficiaries.

Create a complete list: ticker, number of shares, cost basis per share, current market value, unrealized gain or loss, account type (taxable vs. tax-advantaged vs. trust). This is the foundation for every decision that follows.

2

Audit Trust Documents for Mining Stock Provisions

Specific questions to answer: Does the trust's investment policy statement reference the mining stock's Bitcoin treasury as part of the investment thesis? Does the trustee have authority to rebalance out of positions when the thesis changes? Is there a trust protector who can modify investment provisions?

If mining stocks are inside irrevocable trusts and those trusts lack clear authority to rebalance when the investment thesis changes, that's a trust administration problem worth addressing with your estate attorney now — before any action is needed.

3

Model the Swap: Mining Stock → Direct Bitcoin

For taxable accounts: Calculate the tax cost of selling mining stock positions (long-term vs. short-term capital gains rates, state tax) versus the estate tax benefit of moving to direct BTC held outside your estate. For estates significantly above the exemption threshold, the estate tax benefit often exceeds the capital gains cost, especially at current valuations.

For IRAs: Self-directed IRA custodians may allow a direct move from publicly traded mining stocks to Bitcoin, with no capital gains event. Model this with your custodian and IRA administrator. This is often the cleanest path for large IRA positions.

4

Use Depressed Mining Stock Prices for Annual Gifting

If you intend to continue holding mining stocks and have family members you'd like to gift shares to, depressed prices mean more shares transfer per dollar of annual exclusion. Coordinate the gifting to maximize shares transferred before any anticipated recovery. Document the gift properly — including the cost basis being transferred to the recipient.

5

Review Any GRAT Funded With Mining Stocks

If you funded GRATs with mining stocks during 2024 or early 2025 when valuations were higher, those GRATs may now be underwater. Engage your estate attorney to model whether a reset GRAT at current valuations is appropriate. The calculus is the same as with Bitcoin GRATs: the failed GRAT returns assets to your estate with no penalty; the reset captures future recovery for your heirs tax-free.

6

Establish Direct Bitcoin Holdings in Properly Structured Vehicles

For families who have been deferring direct Bitcoin estate planning because the operational complexity seemed too high, the MARA and Core Scientific situation makes the cost of deferral concrete. A dynasty trust holding direct Bitcoin in Wyoming multisig custody gives your heirs multi-generational Bitcoin exposure that cannot be pivoted, diluted, or redirected by anyone's board decision. The infrastructure to do this well is mature. The arguments for using equity proxies instead of direct holdings are weaker than they've ever been.

The Bottom Line: Proxies Don't Survive Management Decisions

Bitcoin's institutional story has entered a new chapter. The miners who once served as proxies for institutional Bitcoin conviction are now optimizing their capital elsewhere. Some, like Core Scientific, have already made the move. Others, like MARA, have removed the constraints that prevented it.

This is not a problem for Bitcoin. It's a problem for the estate planning thesis that treated mining stock as an adequate substitute for direct Bitcoin ownership.

First principles analysis of estate planning always comes back to the same question: what is the asset you actually want your heirs to inherit? If the answer is "Bitcoin," then hold Bitcoin. Not shares in a company that may hold Bitcoin, depending on the quarter. Not equity in a management team that makes capital allocation decisions you cannot control. Bitcoin. The bearer instrument. The asset that cannot be pivoted.

The MARA reversal and Core Scientific pivot have done families a service by making this distinction impossible to ignore. The families who respond by auditing their mining stock positions, modeling the swap to direct Bitcoin ownership where appropriate, and establishing properly structured irrevocable trust vehicles for direct BTC are the ones who will look back on March 2026 as a catalyst — not a setback.

Bitcoin doesn't need miners to hold it to justify its value. It never did. That understanding, arriving on cue just as the miners exit, is precisely what a maturing asset class looks like from the inside.
