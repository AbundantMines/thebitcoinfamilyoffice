Bitcoin Long-Term Capital Gains Rate 2026: Brackets, 0% Strategy & NIIT

Hold Bitcoin more than 12 months and your gains qualify for preferential rates — 0%, 15%, or 20% federal, depending on your total income. But the NIIT stacks 3.8% on top for high earners, and state taxes add more. Here's the complete rate picture, how gains stack on your ordinary income, and how to use the 0% bracket as a year-round harvesting opportunity.

The preferential long-term capital gains rate is the foundation of every Bitcoin tax strategy. It is the reason holding Bitcoin for 12 months and one day meaningfully changes your financial outcome — and why the timing of Bitcoin sales relative to the rest of your income picture matters so much.

Most investors know the rates exist. Fewer understand exactly how they're calculated, how capital gains "stack" on ordinary income, or how to engineer their income in a given year to qualify for a lower rate bracket. This guide covers the complete mechanics of long-term Bitcoin capital gains taxation in 2026.

Educational Disclaimer: Tax rates and brackets reflect 2026 projections based on current law with inflation adjustments. Consult a qualified tax advisor for analysis specific to your situation. This article is educational and does not constitute legal or tax advice.

The Three Long-Term Rates: 0%, 15%, 20%

Under IRC §1(h), long-term capital gains on property held more than 12 months are taxed at three preferential rates based on the taxpayer's taxable income (not just their ordinary income). The 2026 income thresholds (inflation-adjusted):

Filing Status 0% Rate 15% Rate 20% Rate
Single Up to $48,350 $48,351 – $533,400 Over $533,400
Married Filing Jointly Up to $96,700 $96,701 – $600,050 Over $600,050
Head of Household Up to $64,750 $64,751 – $566,700 Over $566,700
Married Filing Separately Up to $48,350 $48,351 – $300,000 Over $300,000

These thresholds are based on taxable income — your total income after deductions (standard deduction or itemized deductions). The standard deduction for MFJ in 2026 is approximately $30,000, meaning a married couple with $126,700 in gross income ($96,700 + $30,000 standard deduction) can realize up to $96,700 of capital gains at 0%.

How Bitcoin Gains Stack on Ordinary Income

This is the most misunderstood aspect of capital gains taxation — and getting it wrong leads to costly surprises. Capital gains do not replace ordinary income in the tax brackets. They stack on top.

Here's how it works:

  1. First, calculate your ordinary taxable income (wages, self-employment, interest, rental income, IRA distributions, etc.) after deductions
  2. Your ordinary income fills the ordinary income tax brackets from the bottom (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%)
  3. Your long-term capital gains are then placed on top of the ordinary income stack
  4. The LTCG rate that applies is determined by where the gains land in the income stack — not where your ordinary income lands

The stacking example: A married couple has $180,000 in ordinary taxable income (wages minus standard deduction) and realizes $500,000 in Bitcoin long-term capital gains. Their total taxable income is $680,000. The capital gains don't get the 15% rate just because their ordinary income is $180,000 — the gains stack on top to $680,000, which is above the $600,050 MFJ threshold for 20%. Most of the gain is taxed at 20% (plus NIIT), not 15%.

The Bracket Boundary Effect

When capital gains straddle a rate threshold, gains on each side are taxed at different rates. Using the example above: if total taxable income (ordinary + gains) is $680,000, and the 20% threshold is $600,050 MFJ, then:

  • Gains that bring income from $180,000 to $600,050 → taxed at 15% ($420,050 × 15% = $63,008)
  • Gains from $600,050 to $680,000 → taxed at 20% ($79,950 × 20% = $15,990)
  • Total LTCG federal tax: $79,000 (blended 15.8% effective LTCG rate)

This marginal calculation means the applicable rate isn't always simply "15%" or "20%" — it depends on exactly where your gains land in the income stack.

The NIIT: Why the Real Top Rate Is 23.8%

Bitcoin long-term capital gains are subject to the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax for taxpayers with MAGI above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (MFJ). This is separate from and in addition to the regular LTCG rate.

The result: for taxpayers above the NIIT threshold who are also in the 20% LTCG bracket, the effective marginal federal rate on Bitcoin long-term gains is 23.8%.

LTCG Bracket Base Rate + NIIT (if MAGI > threshold) Effective Federal Rate
0% bracket0%0% (below NIIT threshold)0%
15% bracket, below NIIT threshold15%0%15%
15% bracket, above NIIT threshold15%3.8%18.8%
20% bracket20%3.8%23.8%

Note the important NIIT threshold interaction: the NIIT threshold ($200K/$250K) does not align with the LTCG bracket boundaries. A taxpayer in the 15% LTCG bracket may still owe NIIT if their MAGI exceeds $250K MFJ. Their effective rate is 18.8% — not 15%, not 23.8%.

The 0% Bracket: Bitcoin's Biggest Underused Opportunity

The 0% long-term capital gains bracket is one of the most powerful and underutilized tax planning tools available to Bitcoin investors. In years where your total taxable income (ordinary + capital gains) stays below the 0% threshold, long-term Bitcoin gains are completely federal-tax-free.

Who can access the 0% bracket in 2026:

  • Single filers with taxable income (after deductions) below $48,350
  • Married couples with taxable income below $96,700

The 0% Bracket Harvesting Strategy

Even if your ordinary income is well above the 0% threshold, there are scenarios where you can engineer access:

  • Retirement years before RMDs: After leaving employment but before Social Security and Required Minimum Distributions begin (roughly ages 60–72), many retirees have reduced ordinary income. If your ordinary taxable income is below the 0% threshold, you can realize Bitcoin long-term gains up to that threshold at zero federal tax — effectively "harvesting" gains for free.
  • Low-income years: Sabbatical, business transition, early retirement, reduced work. Any year with lower-than-normal income is an opportunity to realize long-term Bitcoin gains at 0% up to the threshold.
  • Young investors before career peak: Bitcoin investors in their 20s and early 30s with modest income can systematically harvest long-term gains each year up to the 0% threshold — permanently eliminating embedded gains before income rises.
  • Part-year residents: A taxpayer who establishes Wyoming or Florida residency mid-year may have lower income in that year due to the transition — a harvesting window.

Bracket-filling calculation: Calculate your projected ordinary taxable income for the year (after standard or itemized deductions). Subtract from the 0% bracket ceiling. The remainder is how much Bitcoin gain you can realize at 0% federal. For a married couple with $60,000 in ordinary income (after $30K standard deduction = $60K taxable), the 0% window is $96,700 − $60,000 = $36,700 of Bitcoin gain at $0 federal tax. Repeat every year.

The 0% Harvest and Basis Reset

When you realize gains in the 0% bracket, you pay no federal tax — but you also reset your cost basis to the sale price. If you repurchase the same Bitcoin immediately after (no wash sale rule for Bitcoin), your new basis is the current price. You've permanently eliminated the embedded low-basis gain at zero cost.

This is one of the most elegant strategies available to long-term Bitcoin holders with significant unrealized gains and periodic low-income years. The gains don't disappear — but the tax liability does, permanently, on the amount harvested.

Roth Conversion vs. Capital Gain Realization: The Income Competition

One of the most important year-end planning conflicts for Bitcoin investors is the competition between Roth IRA conversions and Bitcoin capital gain realization. Both generate taxable income, and both can push you into higher brackets. The interaction requires careful coordination.

The mechanics: if you convert $100,000 from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA, that $100,000 is ordinary income — it fills the ordinary income brackets and pushes your capital gains higher in the income stack. If you also realize $500,000 in Bitcoin gains the same year, the Roth conversion may push a portion of your gains from the 15% LTCG bracket to the 20% bracket — costing an additional $50,000 in tax on the gains.

The calculus: Roth conversions are most tax-efficient in low-income years (filling the 22–24% ordinary income brackets). Bitcoin gain realization is most efficient in the same low-income years (staying in the 15% LTCG bracket or below). These two strategies compete for the same low-income-year bandwidth — you can't fully execute both simultaneously without pushing into higher brackets.

Priority framework:

  1. Identify your total available "bracket room" for the year (gap between ordinary income and 15%/20% LTCG threshold and 24%/32% ordinary income threshold)
  2. Allocate bracket room between Roth conversion and Bitcoin gain realization based on relative value
  3. Generally: if your Roth has high-appreciation Bitcoin (Roth conversion now, then hold), the conversion rate may be worth the ordinary income rate. If you have unrealized gains in taxable Bitcoin, realizing at 15% LTCG is almost always better than converting at 22–24% ordinary rates.

Year-End Income Management Strategy

Every October, run a year-end tax projection for your Bitcoin portfolio. The key questions:

  1. What is my projected ordinary taxable income for the year? Include W-2, self-employment, rental, interest, dividends, IRA distributions, and Social Security if applicable.
  2. How much capital gains have I already realized this year? Check your tax software for gains/losses already locked in from prior sales.
  3. Where does my total income (ordinary + realized gains) sit relative to the 15%/20% LTCG bracket boundary? If you're comfortably in the 15% bracket, there may be room to realize additional gains before crossing into 20%.
  4. Am I above the NIIT threshold? If your MAGI will exceed $200K/$250K regardless of Bitcoin sales, the NIIT applies to any additional NII. This doesn't change whether to sell — but it changes the true marginal rate calculation.
  5. Can I fill the 0% bracket? If total taxable income (ordinary + gains) is below $96,700 MFJ, realize gains aggressively to lock in zero federal tax and reset basis.
  6. Should I defer any planned sales to January? If you're near the 20% threshold and deferring a sale to January keeps your LTCG at 15% in the current year (and possibly 15% again next year if income is similar), deferral may be worth more than the time value of money.

8-Item Long-Term Rate Optimization Checklist

  1. Always confirm holding period before selling. Long-term treatment requires more than 12 months — 12 months and 1 day minimum. Check every lot's acquisition date before executing any sale. See the Short-Term Capital Gains Guide for full holding period mechanics.
  2. Run a year-end income projection in October. Calculate projected ordinary taxable income, already-realized capital gains, and identify your rate bracket position. This determines whether additional Bitcoin sales this year land at 0%, 15%, or 20%.
  3. Fill the 0% bracket in low-income years. Any year where your total taxable income (ordinary + gains) would remain below $96,700 MFJ, realize long-term Bitcoin gains up to that threshold. Pay zero federal tax and permanently reset basis.
  4. Identify the 15%/20% bracket boundary and stay below it when possible. Gains that cross the $600,050 MFJ threshold (2026) go from 15% to 20% — a 5 percentage point increase worth $50,000 per $1M of gain. Plan sales to stay below this line when possible, deferring the balance to the next year.
  5. Account for NIIT separately from the LTCG rate. The NIIT applies if MAGI exceeds $200K/$250K — independent of whether you're in the 15% or 20% LTCG bracket. Don't assume 15% rate = 15% total rate. For many investors in the 15% bracket, the true combined rate is 18.8% once NIIT is included.
  6. Coordinate Roth conversions and Bitcoin gain realization. Both compete for the same "bracket room" in low-income years. Allocate thoughtfully — don't inadvertently push gains from 15% to 20% with an aggressive Roth conversion in the same year.
  7. Consider domicile if state rates are significant. The difference between California (37.1% combined) and Wyoming (23.8%) is 13.3 percentage points on long-term gains. For $10M+ positions, establishing residency in a no-income-tax state before realizing gains is the highest-leverage single action available. See the 50-State Tax Comparison.
  8. Use HIFO or Specific ID to apply the highest-basis lots to sales that will be taxed at higher rates. If some of your Bitcoin gain this year will land at 15% and some at 20%, use specific identification to apply your highest-basis lots to the 20% portion — minimizing the gain taxed at the highest rate. The Bottom Line

Long-term capital gains rates are the foundational tax advantage that makes holding Bitcoin worth optimizing around. But the rates are not automatic or fixed — they depend on your total income stack, the NIIT threshold, your state, and your year-end planning discipline. A high-income investor in California faces 37.1% even on long-term gains. A lower-income investor in Wyoming can realize the same gains at 0%. That spread is not luck — it's the result of intentional planning around income levels, holding periods, domicile, and annual bracket management.
