Bitcoin Estate Planning in California

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

California is simultaneously home to one of the largest concentrations of Bitcoin wealth in the United States and one of the most challenging tax and legal environments for managing that wealth. The state's 13.3% top marginal income tax rate — which applies to capital gains without distinction — combined with aggressive residency enforcement, community property rules, and complex trust taxation creates a planning environment that demands precision. This is not a jurisdiction where generic estate planning frameworks suffice.

The Income Tax Burden: 13.3% on Every Realized Gain

California's top marginal income tax rate is 13.3% — the highest in the United States. Crucially, California does not distinguish between ordinary income and long-term capital gains. A Bitcoin position held for 30 years and realized by a California resident is taxed at the same rate as a day's wages. Federal long-term capital gains rates top out at 23.8% (including the 3.8% net investment income tax); add California's 13.3% and effective marginal rates on Bitcoin gains can approach 37%.

California also levies a 1% mental health services tax on income above $1 million (California Revenue and Taxation Code §17043), bringing the top effective rate to 13.3% — a rate that has no upper threshold. There is no California preferential capital gains rate, no exclusion for long-term holding, and no deferral mechanism comparable to federal Opportunity Zone structures (which California has not conformed to).

The practical implication: realizing large Bitcoin gains while a California resident is among the most expensive tax events available to U.S. investors. Planning structures that defer or avoid recognition — including installment sales, charitable remainder trusts, and GRATs — are particularly valuable in California for this reason.

No State Estate Tax — But Capital Gains at Death Are Different

California does not impose a separate state estate or inheritance tax. This is a meaningful advantage relative to states like Oregon and Washington that impose estate taxes starting at $1 million. However, California's absence of an estate tax does not mean death is a clean tax event.

Unlike many states, California does not conform to IRC §1014's step-up in basis for assets held in irrevocable trusts. This distinction matters: while a California resident who holds Bitcoin outright until death receives a federal step-up in basis, assets transferred to an irrevocable trust during lifetime lose the step-up for California income tax purposes if those assets are later sold by the trust. The interaction between federal estate planning structures and California's income tax basis rules requires careful modeling before implementing any irrevocable trust strategy.

California has no estate tax — but it has something almost as consequential: a 13.3% income tax rate with no capital gains preference, applied to every realized Bitcoin gain regardless of how long it was held.

California's Aggressive Trust Taxation

California is among the most aggressive states in asserting income tax jurisdiction over trusts. Under California Revenue and Taxation Code §17742 and related regulations, California taxes a trust's income if:

  • The trust has a California-resident trustee, or
  • The trust has a California-resident non-contingent beneficiary

This "California beneficiary rule" means that a Wyoming-sited trust — regardless of where it was created, where its trustee is located, or what law governs it — may still be subject to California income tax on undistributed income if any California-resident beneficiary has a non-contingent interest. California's Franchise Tax Board has been active in asserting this jurisdiction, and the California Supreme Court's 2019 decision in Swart Enterprises v. FTB (while addressing corporate income tax) reflects the state's posture on reach.

The practical implication: moving a trust's situs to Wyoming, South Dakota, or Nevada does not automatically eliminate California income tax if the trust's beneficiaries remain in California. A California-resident beneficiary holding a non-contingent beneficial interest pulls California tax jurisdiction regardless of where the trust is administered or what state's law governs it. Planning for California beneficiaries requires specific attention to beneficiary structure — contingent interests, discretionary trusts, and trustee discretion over distributions are all relevant tools for limiting California's jurisdictional reach.

Community Property: The Bitcoin Basis Planning Opportunity

California is a community property state (California Family Code §760). Bitcoin purchased with community funds during marriage is community property, belonging equally to both spouses regardless of whose name appears on the wallet or exchange account. This requires careful coordination between Bitcoin custody arrangements and marital property characterization.

The community property step-up (IRC §1014(b)(6)) is the significant planning opportunity: at the first spouse's death, both halves of the community estate receive a stepped-up basis. For a married couple with large, long-held Bitcoin holdings, this step-up can eliminate decades of unrealized capital gains — representing potentially enormous federal income tax savings. The surviving spouse holds the entire Bitcoin position at current fair market value as their cost basis.

This basis step-up opportunity is a reason some California Bitcoin families choose not to move all assets to irrevocable trusts before death, despite the estate planning advantages those structures offer. A GRAT or irrevocable trust that removes Bitcoin from the estate forfeits the step-up; holding Bitcoin outright preserves it. The trade-off must be modeled for each family's specific estate and income tax situation.

California LLCs and the Annual Franchise Tax

California LLCs (California Corporations Code §17701.01 et seq.) impose a minimum $800 annual franchise tax and an additional fee based on total income ($0 to $11,790 annually depending on revenue). For a pure Bitcoin holding entity with no revenue, the annual cost is modest — the $800 minimum tax plus any applicable gross receipts fee. However, California LLCs provide weaker charging order protection than Wyoming, Nevada, or Texas entities, and California courts have been more willing to pierce the LLC veil in certain circumstances.

Many California-resident Bitcoin families form their holding LLCs in Wyoming or Nevada and register them as foreign LLCs in California — paying the California franchise tax while benefiting from the stronger legal protections of the formation state. This approach has limitations (California can still assert jurisdiction over California-source income), but provides improved structural protection for assets held within the entity.

Strategies for Bitcoin Families Remaining in California

For families committed to California residency, the priority strategies are:

  1. Maximize tax-deferred structures: GRATs, Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs), and installment sales allow Bitcoin appreciation to transfer or be realized over time without immediate full taxation. California conforms to federal GRAT rules, making GRATs a viable — though complex — planning tool.
  2. Structure trust beneficiary interests carefully: Trusts with California-resident beneficiaries holding discretionary (rather than non-contingent) interests may have stronger arguments against California income tax jurisdiction over undistributed income.
  3. Model the step-up basis opportunity: For married couples with long-held, highly appreciated Bitcoin, the community property step-up at first death may be worth more than the estate tax savings from an irrevocable trust. Every situation requires individual analysis.
  4. Consider domicile change proactively: For families with mobility, establishing domicile in a no-income-tax state before realizing large gains is the single highest-impact tax planning decision available. The planning horizon for a genuine domicile change — with California's audit posture — is typically 18–24 months of careful documentation.

Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
