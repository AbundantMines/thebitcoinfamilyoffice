Bitcoin Short-Term Capital Gains Tax 2026: Rates, Rules & How to Avoid It

Sell Bitcoin before holding it 12 months and the preferential capital gains rate disappears entirely. Your gain becomes ordinary income — taxed at up to 37% federal instead of 20%, plus the 3.8% NIIT. On a $500,000 gain, that difference is $66,000 in additional federal tax. Here's how the holding period works and how to avoid the short-term trap.

The U.S. tax code applies two completely different rate structures to capital gains depending on one single variable: how long you held the asset. Hold Bitcoin for more than 12 months and sell at a profit — you pay the preferential long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). Sell before 12 months and the gain is "short-term" — taxed at your ordinary income rate, the same rate that applies to your salary, bonuses, and interest income.

For high-income Bitcoin investors, this distinction is worth tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars per transaction. Understanding exactly how the holding period is measured, where the clock starts for different types of Bitcoin acquisition, and when waiting the extra days or months is financially rational — and when it isn't — is foundational Bitcoin tax literacy.

How Short-Term vs. Long-Term Classification Works

Under IRC §1222, capital gains are classified based on the holding period of the asset sold:

  • Short-term: Asset held for 12 months or less. Gain taxed as ordinary income.
  • Long-term: Asset held for more than 12 months (12 months + 1 day minimum). Gain taxed at preferential rates (0%/15%/20%).

The holding period begins the day after the date of acquisition and ends on the date of sale (inclusive). So if you purchase Bitcoin on March 15, 2025, the long-term threshold date is March 16, 2026 — sell on or after March 16, 2026 for long-term treatment. Sell on March 15, 2026 and the gain is short-term.

The "more than" rule: IRC §1222 says "more than 1 year" — not "1 year or more." This means exactly 365 days (in a non-leap year) is still short-term. You need at least 366 days of holding. One extra day is all that stands between 37% and 23.8% on a large Bitcoin position.

Note that the NIIT (3.8%) applies to both short-term and long-term capital gains for taxpayers above the $200K/$250K MAGI threshold. The NIIT does not distinguish between holding periods — it applies to all net investment income regardless of whether the gain is short-term or long-term.

Holding Period Mechanics: When Does the Clock Start?

The holding period clock starts the day after acquisition. The rules for different acquisition methods:

Exchange Purchases

For Bitcoin purchased on an exchange, the acquisition date is the trade date — the date the order executes. The holding period clock begins the following day. If you buy Bitcoin on January 10, 2025, the clock starts January 11, 2025, and the 12-month threshold is January 11, 2026 (sell on or after that date for long-term treatment).

Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Each DCA purchase is a separate lot with its own acquisition date and holding period. If you buy $1,000 of Bitcoin every Monday for a year, you have 52 separate lots — each with a different holding period clock. When you sell, you choose which lots to sell (using specific identification) or default to FIFO (first in, first out). Specific ID lets you sell your oldest (longest-held) lots first to get long-term treatment, while retaining your newer lots.

Peer-to-Peer or OTC Purchases

Acquisition date is the date the transaction settles and you have control of the Bitcoin. Keep records of the exact date.

Gifts Received

When you receive Bitcoin as a gift, your holding period includes the donor's holding period (tacked-on holding period under IRC §1223). If the donor held Bitcoin for 8 months before gifting it to you, and you hold it for 5 more months before selling, your total holding period is 13 months — long-term treatment, even though you personally only held it 5 months.

Inherited Bitcoin

Inherited Bitcoin is automatically treated as long-term capital gain property, regardless of how long the decedent held it or how long you hold it before selling. The heir always gets long-term treatment — and typically pays zero capital gains tax due to the §1014 step-up in basis.

Mining Income and Airdrops: How the Clock Works

Bitcoin acquired through mining, staking, airdrops, and forks has a two-layer tax treatment that many investors misunderstand:

Mining Income

  1. Step 1 — Ordinary income at receipt: When you mine Bitcoin, the fair market value on the date received is ordinary income (Schedule C if a business, Schedule 1 Line 8 if hobby). You pay income tax on this amount at your ordinary rate in the year received. Your cost basis in the mined Bitcoin equals this ordinary income amount.
  2. Step 2 — Capital gain clock starts at date mined: The holding period for the mined Bitcoin begins on the date it was received. If you mined Bitcoin on June 1, 2025, and sell it on June 15, 2026 (more than 12 months later), the sale proceeds minus your basis (the value at mining) generates a long-term capital gain.
  3. Sell before 12 months: If you sell the mined Bitcoin before 12 months, you realize a short-term capital gain on any appreciation above the amount you already reported as ordinary income at mining.

Mining example: You mine 0.1 BTC when Bitcoin is at $80,000. You report $8,000 as ordinary income. Your basis in those 0.1 BTC is $8,000. If Bitcoin rises to $120,000 and you sell after 14 months: proceeds $12,000 minus basis $8,000 = $4,000 long-term capital gain. If you sell after 8 months: same math, but $4,000 is a short-term capital gain taxed as ordinary income.

Airdrops and Hard Forks

Airdropped tokens and hard fork coins are ordinary income at fair market value at the time of receipt (per Revenue Ruling 2019-24). The holding period clock for the new coins begins on the date of the airdrop or fork. Appreciation after that date is capital gain — long-term if held more than 12 months from the receipt date.

Short-Term Gains and the NIIT

The 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax applies to both short-term and long-term Bitcoin capital gains for taxpayers above the MAGI threshold ($200K single / $250K MFJ). There is no distinction in the NIIT between short-term and long-term gains — both are NII.

This means the true combined federal rate for a top-bracket investor is:

  • Short-term Bitcoin gain: 37% ordinary income + 3.8% NIIT = 40.8% combined federal
  • Long-term Bitcoin gain: 20% LTCG rate + 3.8% NIIT = 23.8% combined federal

State taxes stack on top of both. In California: 40.8% + 13.3% = 54.1% combined on short-term gains. In Wyoming: 40.8% + 0% = 40.8%. The already-severe short-term penalty is amplified by state taxes in high-rate jurisdictions.

When Selling Before 12 Months Still Makes Sense

Waiting for long-term treatment is almost always rational — but not always. There are scenarios where selling before 12 months is the right financial decision despite the higher tax rate:

Volatility Risk Exceeds Tax Savings

Bitcoin can decline 50–80% in a bear market. If you're sitting on a $500,000 gain in a Bitcoin position purchased 10 months ago, and you believe a significant correction is likely, the tax cost of selling early ($85,000 in additional federal tax) may be less than the risk of holding for 2 more months and watching $200,000+ in unrealized gains evaporate. The math: 2 more months of market risk vs. $85,000 in tax cost. This is a judgment call based on your conviction, position size, and ability to absorb downside.

Estate Planning Trigger Events

Sometimes a liquidity event — a business sale, divorce settlement, estate distribution — forces Bitcoin sales on a timeline that doesn't align with the 12-month mark. In these cases, the short-term tax cost may be unavoidable, and structuring the rest of the transaction around it is more productive than trying to defer the sale.

Tax-Loss Harvesting to Offset Short-Term Gains

If you have significant short-term capital losses available (from other positions or from prior Bitcoin harvests), those losses can offset short-term gains dollar-for-dollar — eliminating the short-term rate differential entirely. In that case, selling before 12 months costs nothing in incremental tax. See the Bitcoin Wash Sale Rule Guide for how to harvest Bitcoin losses without restriction.

Charitable Donation

Donating appreciated Bitcoin to charity eliminates capital gains entirely — regardless of holding period. Short-term or long-term, the donation extinguishes the gain and generates a deduction equal to fair market value (for publicly traded property donated to public charities). If you have Bitcoin with a large short-term gain and genuine charitable intent, donation before 12 months eliminates the tax completely.

Year-End Timing Strategy

The interaction between the holding period rule and the calendar year creates straightforward year-end planning opportunities:

Scenario: Bitcoin Purchased in Late Year

If you purchased Bitcoin in November or December of last year, your 12-month anniversary falls in November or December of this year. Selling in October to "lock in gains before year-end" would be a short-term sale. Waiting until January of next year (12 months + 1 day) converts the same gain to long-term — and pushes tax recognition to next year's return.

Double benefit of January timing: (1) Long-term rate instead of short-term, and (2) one additional year of tax deferral. On a $1M gain, this is worth $170,000 in rate savings plus the time value of deferring $238,000 in tax by 12 months.

Review All Lots in October

Every October, review your Bitcoin lots by acquisition date. Identify positions approaching their 12-month anniversary in November and December. Mark calendar alerts for those dates. Don't let a holiday, distraction, or market volatility event trigger a sale that converts a would-be long-term gain to short-term.

Use Specific Identification When Selling

If you've accumulated Bitcoin over time via DCA and need to sell a portion, use specific identification to select your oldest lots — the ones with long-term holding periods. This keeps your newest (potentially short-term) lots intact while realizing gains at preferential rates on the older positions.

Short-Term Loss Harvesting: The Highest-Value Harvest

When you do have short-term capital losses to harvest (Bitcoin acquired within the last 12 months that has declined in value), these losses are particularly valuable — because they preferentially offset short-term gains first, which are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%) rather than the preferential 20% long-term rate.

A $50,000 short-term loss that offsets a $50,000 short-term gain saves $20,400 in federal tax (40.8%). The same loss applied to a long-term gain saves only $11,900 (23.8%). Short-term losses are 71% more valuable than long-term losses when you have short-term gains to offset. Harvest short-term losers first, always.

And remember: Bitcoin has no wash sale rule. You can harvest a short-term Bitcoin loss and immediately repurchase at the same price. The loss is fully deductible; your economic position is unchanged. This is Bitcoin's structural tax harvesting advantage. Full mechanics in the Bitcoin Wash Sale Rule Guide.

Bitcoin Mining: Convert Mining Revenue to Long-Term Capital Gains

Bitcoin mining has a unique tax characteristic: mined Bitcoin is ordinary income at receipt, establishing your basis. From that date forward, any appreciation becomes capital gain — long-term if held over 12 months. Mining is the only way to accumulate Bitcoin where the entire appreciation above your basis converts to long-term capital gains automatically as you hold.

8-Item Short-Term Tax Avoidance Checklist

  1. Know every lot's acquisition date. Your crypto tax software should maintain a complete lot-by-lot history. Verify the acquisition date and holding period for every Bitcoin position you own. Flag any positions approaching the 12-month mark in the next 60 days.
  2. Set calendar alerts for your 12-month anniversaries. For each significant Bitcoin purchase, set a calendar reminder 30 days before the 12-month anniversary. Avoid any planned sales during that window without checking the holding period first.
  3. Use specific identification when selling. When you sell Bitcoin, explicitly designate which lots you're selling. Select your oldest lots first to ensure long-term treatment. Document the lot selection in writing at the time of sale — not retroactively.
  4. Consider a January delay for December gains. If you're approaching the 12-month mark on a large position in November or December, evaluate waiting until January. You defer both (a) the tax rate (short → long-term) and (b) the tax year (current → following year). A double deferral opportunity.
  5. Harvest short-term losses first. If you have unrealized losses in positions held less than 12 months, harvest those losses before long-term losses. Short-term losses are more valuable when you have short-term gains to offset. Use Bitcoin's no-wash-sale advantage to harvest and immediately repurchase.
  6. Offset unavoidable short-term gains with charitable donations. If you must realize a short-term gain (volatility risk, estate event, business liquidity), consider donating the appreciated Bitcoin directly to charity before the sale. Eliminates capital gains entirely regardless of holding period.
  7. Never sell DCA positions without checking which lots you're selling. DCA creates many lots at different prices and dates. Selling without specifying lots defaults to FIFO — which may inadvertently sell your oldest long-term lots (losing favorable treatment on your best positions) or your newest short-term lots (triggering ordinary income rates). Always specify.
  8. Model the full rate before selling. Before any Bitcoin sale, calculate: (1) Is this short-term or long-term? (2) What is my marginal ordinary income rate or LTCG rate? (3) Does the NIIT apply? (4) What is my state rate? The total tax rate matters — not just federal, not just capital gains. The difference between "I should sell now" and "I should wait 45 days" can be six figures.

The Bottom Line

Short-term capital gains treatment on Bitcoin is not an inevitable tax — it's a choice made by selling too soon. The 12-month holding period rule is the single simplest, most reliable tax optimization available to any Bitcoin investor: just wait. One extra day converts a 37% tax rate to a 23.8% rate on the same gain.

Combined with lot-specific identification, strategic year-end timing, and Bitcoin's no-wash-sale harvesting advantage, managing the short-term vs. long-term distinction is the foundation of Bitcoin tax planning. It costs nothing to implement — just discipline around timing.
