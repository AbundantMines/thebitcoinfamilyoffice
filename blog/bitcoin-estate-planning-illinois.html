Bitcoin Estate Planning in Illinois: The $4 Million Estate Tax and What Bitcoin Holders Must Know

Illinois imposes one of the lowest state estate tax exemptions in the country — and Bitcoin appreciation is closing the gap faster than most holders realize.

By Hal Turner
February 25, 2026
~11 min read

Most Bitcoin holders think about the federal estate tax exemption — currently $13.61 million per person. At today's prices, that feels comfortable for all but a handful of holders. But if you live in Illinois, the federal threshold is largely irrelevant. The state of Illinois imposes its own estate tax starting at just $4 million — one of the lowest exemptions of any state in the country. And at $95,000 per Bitcoin, it takes only 42 BTC to put a single person over that threshold before accounting for a house, brokerage accounts, retirement assets, or any other property.

This guide is written specifically for Illinois Bitcoin holders. We'll cover exactly how Illinois estate tax works, why it catches so many people off guard, and the concrete planning strategies — credit shelter trusts, Wyoming dynasty trusts, GRATs, and annual gifting — that can dramatically reduce or eliminate your exposure.

Understanding Illinois Estate Tax: The Basics

Illinois is one of only twelve states (plus the District of Columbia) that impose a separate state-level estate tax. Unlike most Americans who only need to plan around the federal $13.61 million exemption, Illinois residents face a parallel tax system with its own rules, its own rates, and its own much lower threshold.

The $4 Million Exemption

Illinois exempts the first $4 million of a person's taxable estate from state estate tax. Everything above that amount is subject to Illinois estate tax at graduated rates ranging from 0.8% to 16%. That 16% top rate applies to estates significantly above the threshold, and for large Bitcoin positions, the tax can be substantial.

The $4 million exemption has remained flat for years. It is not indexed for inflation. It is not adjusted for Bitcoin's price appreciation. Every year that Bitcoin rises and Illinois fails to adjust its threshold, more Illinois holders quietly cross into taxable territory.

Illinois Estate Tax is NOT Portable

At the federal level, married couples can effectively double the estate tax exemption through a concept called "portability." If one spouse dies without fully using their exemption, the surviving spouse can inherit the unused portion — creating a combined $27.22 million federal shelter for married couples who plan properly.

Illinois does not allow this. Illinois estate tax portability does not exist. Each spouse has a separate $4 million exemption, and if the first spouse to die does not use their full $4 million exemption through proper planning, it disappears. This makes proactive trust planning — not just a simple will — essential for married Illinois Bitcoin holders.

Illinois Has No Inheritance Tax

One important clarification: Illinois imposes an estate tax, not an inheritance tax. The distinction matters. The estate tax is paid by the estate of the deceased before assets are distributed to heirs. Beneficiaries who receive an inheritance from an Illinois estate pay no additional Illinois tax on what they receive. However, federal income tax rules (like capital gains on inherited assets) still apply based on their own rules.

For the early adopters and conviction holders who accumulated Bitcoin at lower prices, this is not a hypothetical future problem — it may already be a present one.

The combination of a flat exemption, no portability, and an asset class with significant appreciation potential creates a uniquely dangerous situation for Illinois Bitcoin holders who fail to plan.

Illinois-Specific Bitcoin Estate Planning Strategies

1. Credit Shelter Trust (Bypass Trust)

Because Illinois estate tax portability does not exist, the most fundamental planning tool for married Illinois Bitcoin holders is the credit shelter trust, sometimes called a bypass trust or A/B trust.

The structure works as follows: when the first spouse dies, assets up to the Illinois exemption ($4 million) are placed into an irrevocable credit shelter trust instead of passing directly to the surviving spouse. The surviving spouse can receive income and benefit from the trust, but the trust assets are not included in the surviving spouse's taxable estate when they die.

The result: a married couple can shelter up to $8 million from Illinois estate tax — $4 million for each spouse — using credit shelter trust planning. Without this structure, the surviving spouse ends up with all the assets in their own estate, capped at a single $4 million exemption.

For Bitcoin specifically, this means placing BTC directly into the credit shelter trust. Any future appreciation of the Bitcoin inside the trust does not increase the surviving spouse's taxable estate. If the Bitcoin doubles in value after the first spouse's death, the entire gain remains inside the trust — outside the second estate.

2. Wyoming Dynasty Trust

For larger Bitcoin positions, or for holders who want to protect multiple generations, a Wyoming dynasty trust offers capabilities that Illinois trusts cannot match.

Wyoming has no state income tax on trust income and permits perpetual trusts — trusts that can hold assets for multiple generations without mandatory termination. An Illinois resident can establish a Wyoming dynasty trust with a Wyoming trustee and transfer Bitcoin into it. The Bitcoin, and all future appreciation, is held outside of Illinois entirely.

Critically, Illinois has no state income tax on trust income if the trust is properly structured in another state like Wyoming. A Wyoming trust with a Wyoming trustee, administering assets in Wyoming, is not subject to Illinois income tax on its accumulated income. This makes Wyoming trusts doubly attractive: they remove assets from the Illinois estate tax base and eliminate Illinois income tax on trust earnings.

The transfer to a dynasty trust is a taxable gift (using federal lifetime exemption or paying gift tax), but future appreciation escapes both Illinois estate tax and the federal estate tax for the life of the trust — potentially indefinitely in Wyoming, which has no rule against perpetuities.

3. Annual Gifting Program

The IRS allows every individual to give up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2024 annual exclusion) without using any lifetime exemption or incurring gift tax. These gifts are also removed from the Illinois estate tax base immediately.

For a married couple, each spouse can give $18,000 to the same recipient — a practice called gift-splitting — effectively allowing $36,000 per recipient per year. If you have two adult children and four grandchildren, a married couple can remove $216,000 per year from the taxable estate with zero gift tax consequences.

Applied to Bitcoin, this can mean gifting fractional BTC annually. Given Bitcoin's appreciation history, removing Bitcoin from the estate early — even in small amounts — can eliminate substantially more future estate tax liability than the gift's current value suggests.

4. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

A GRAT is an estate freeze technique particularly well-suited to volatile, high-growth assets like Bitcoin. You transfer Bitcoin into the GRAT and receive fixed annuity payments back over a set term (typically two to five years). At the end of the term, whatever remains in the trust — all the appreciation above the IRS's assumed interest rate — passes to your heirs gift-tax free.

If Bitcoin significantly outperforms the IRS's assumed rate (currently low by historical standards), the entire excess appreciation passes to heirs without estate tax. The "risk" is that if you die during the GRAT term, the assets revert to the estate — but in that scenario, you're no worse off than if you hadn't done the GRAT at all. It is a no-lose strategy when executed correctly.

For Illinois purposes, the Bitcoin that passes to heirs at the end of the GRAT term is removed from your Illinois taxable estate, reducing your exposure to Illinois estate tax rates.

Finding a Bitcoin-Literate Illinois Estate Attorney

Bitcoin estate planning requires a very specific combination of expertise: Illinois estate tax law, federal estate and gift tax, and practical knowledge of how cryptocurrency is held, transferred, and valued. Most estate attorneys have the first two; very few have the third.

When evaluating an Illinois estate attorney for Bitcoin planning, ask these questions:

  • Have you drafted trust documents that specifically address cryptocurrency held in self-custody? A will or trust that doesn't account for private key access planning can make Bitcoin permanently inaccessible — regardless of who's named as beneficiary.
  • How do you value Bitcoin for Illinois estate tax purposes? The IRS uses the average of the high and low trading price on the date of death. Make sure your attorney understands this — and the documentation required to defend it.
  • Do you have experience with Wyoming or South Dakota dynasty trusts for Illinois clients? Out-of-state trust situs planning requires knowing both the originating state's rules and the destination state's advantages.
  • Have you worked with clients who hold Bitcoin on hardware wallets or in multisig arrangements? The mechanics of Bitcoin inheritance planning — including inheritance wallet software, letter of instruction, and key recovery planning — are distinct from any other asset class.
  • Are you familiar with the Illinois credit shelter trust strategies specific to the $4 million exemption? Many attorneys know credit shelter trusts conceptually but have not tailored them for the Illinois-specific portability gap.

If an attorney cannot answer these questions with specificity, they may be excellent at traditional estate planning but may not be the right fit for your Bitcoin situation. The stakes are too high to settle for generalist advice.

What Happens Without a Plan

The default outcome for an Illinois Bitcoin holder who dies without proper planning is sobering. Illinois estate tax becomes due nine months after death. The estate must file an Illinois Estate Tax Return, value the Bitcoin at the date of death, and pay the tax due — often before the estate has been fully distributed.

If heirs lack the liquidity to pay the estate tax (a common problem when the estate is concentrated in illiquid assets like Bitcoin), they may be forced to sell Bitcoin to cover the bill. Selling Bitcoin at a potentially suboptimal time, for an entirely avoidable tax, is precisely the outcome that planning is designed to prevent.

Additionally, without a credit shelter trust, a married couple in Illinois loses one entire $4 million exemption when the first spouse dies. For a couple with $10 million in Bitcoin, this miscalculation alone could cost $400,000 to $600,000 in unnecessary Illinois estate tax.

Action Steps for Illinois Bitcoin Holders

If you're an Illinois resident holding Bitcoin, here's where to start:

  1. Estimate your total estate value today. Add up Bitcoin at current market prices, real estate, retirement accounts, brokerage accounts, business interests, and life insurance death benefits. Compare to the $4 million threshold.
  2. Model future scenarios. Run the numbers at $150K, $250K, and $500K Bitcoin. Understand at what price your estate becomes materially exposed to Illinois estate tax.
  3. Determine whether your current documents address Illinois credit shelter trusts. Many older wills and trusts use federal exemption amounts as trigger points. These may not be calibrated for the $4 million Illinois threshold.
  4. Consult with a Bitcoin-literate Illinois estate attorney. Bring your current documents, your current Bitcoin holdings, and your projections to the meeting.
  5. Implement a gifting program immediately. Annual gifting is simple, costs nothing in tax, and removes Bitcoin from your estate each year.

Illinois is a Planning Jurisdiction — Not a Friendly One

Some states impose high estate tax exemptions or no estate tax at all. Illinois is firmly in the other camp. The $4 million threshold, the lack of portability, and the graduated rates up to 16% make Illinois one of the more demanding states for high-net-worth Bitcoin holders.

But demanding does not mean hopeless. Every strategy discussed in this guide — credit shelter trusts, Wyoming dynasty trusts, GRATs, annual gifting — is legal, established, and available to Illinois residents today. The difference between paying hundreds of thousands in Illinois estate tax and passing your Bitcoin stack largely intact to your heirs is almost entirely a function of whether you planned or not.

The time to plan is before the threshold is crossed — before the estate becomes illiquid — and before the IRS sets the valuation date at death. Bitcoin's appreciation trajectory makes waiting increasingly costly. If you've been meaning to get your estate planning in order, Illinois's $4 million threshold is one of the clearest signals that "eventually" should become "now."
