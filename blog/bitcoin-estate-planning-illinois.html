Bitcoin Estate Planning in Illinois: $4M State Estate Tax Threshold

Illinois levies its own estate tax with a $4 million exemption that has never been indexed for inflation — and it is not portable between spouses. Here is what every Illinois Bitcoin holder needs to know.

By Hal Turner | February 25, 2026 | ~12 min read

Illinois is one of a shrinking number of states that maintains its own estate tax entirely separate from the federal system. For Bitcoin holders in the Land of Lincoln, this creates a planning challenge that simply does not exist in states without a state-level death tax: the Illinois exemption is $4 million per person, has not moved in years, and — critically — is not portable between spouses.

With Bitcoin at current levels, a family that has accumulated even a modest long-term Bitcoin position can easily exceed this threshold. And with no inflation adjustment on the horizon from Springfield, the real value of that $4 million exemption shrinks every year. If you hold meaningful Bitcoin in Illinois and have not reviewed your estate plan, this article is for you.

Illinois Estate Tax: The Basics

Illinois imposes an estate tax under 35 ILCS 405/1 et seq. on the estates of individuals who die as Illinois residents, regardless of where their assets are located. The tax also applies to non-residents who owned Illinois real estate or tangible personal property situated in Illinois at death.

The current exemption is $4 million per person. Estates below this threshold owe no Illinois estate tax. Estates above it are taxed at graduated rates ranging from 0.8% to 16%. The top rate kicks in on the amount of the estate exceeding approximately $10 million. There is an unlimited marital deduction — meaning that assets passing outright to a surviving spouse are not taxed — but this only defers the tax; it does not reduce the couple's combined exposure. And because the exemption is not portable, the deceased spouse's $4 million exemption is lost if not used at death.

⚠ Critical Planning Point

Unlike the federal estate tax, Illinois does not allow a surviving spouse to "inherit" the deceased spouse's unused exemption. A married couple in Illinois has a combined exemption of just $4 million — not $8 million — unless a bypass trust (also called a credit shelter trust) is used.

Why Bitcoin Holders in Illinois Are Especially Exposed

The $4 million threshold sounds generous until you do the math. A Bitcoin holder who has been accumulating since 2019 or 2020 — even a relatively modest accumulator — may now hold an estate that brushes up against or exceeds this exemption without owning any real estate, business interests, or other traditional wealth assets. Bitcoin has a way of turning middle-class savers into high-net-worth individuals faster than they update their estate plans.

Consider: 42 Bitcoin at $95,000 per coin equals $3.99 million. A single-person household with that position and modest other assets is already at the doorstep of Illinois estate tax. A married couple in that position — if they have not structured their estate plan properly — faces a scenario where the first spouse to die wastes an entire $4 million exemption if assets simply pass to the survivor outright.

Chicago, in particular, has one of the most sophisticated Bitcoin-holding communities in the country. The CME Group — where Bitcoin futures first launched in December 2017, bringing institutional-grade price discovery to the asset class — is headquartered in Chicago. Many of the traders, quantitative analysts, and institutional finance professionals who surround CME and the broader Chicago financial ecosystem have been accumulating Bitcoin since its earliest days. These are not casual retail holders. They are sophisticated investors with significant positions who need sophisticated planning.

Non-Residents and Illinois Estate Tax: The Bitcoin Advantage

One nuance of Illinois estate tax law that is particularly relevant to Bitcoin holders is how the tax treats non-residents. Illinois estate tax applies to non-residents only with respect to real property and tangible personal property located in Illinois at death. It does not apply to intangible personal property owned by non-residents.

Bitcoin is intangible personal property. It is a cryptographic entry on a distributed ledger — it has no physical situs. A non-resident who owns Bitcoin held on an exchange, stored in a hardware wallet, or controlled via a multi-sig custody arrangement generally has no Illinois estate tax exposure on that Bitcoin, even if that same person owns an investment property in Chicago.

This has interesting planning implications. A non-resident who sold Illinois real estate before death, converting the asset from taxable-situs Illinois property to cash or Bitcoin (both intangible), would likely eliminate the Illinois estate tax nexus. This is an aggressive strategy that requires careful legal analysis, but it illustrates how Bitcoin's intangible nature interacts with Illinois's jurisdiction rules in ways that traditional wealth planning does not.

For Illinois residents, this distinction matters less — all assets of a resident are subject to Illinois estate tax regardless of situs — but it is worth understanding the rule if your clients are non-residents with Illinois real estate exposure.

The Bypass Trust: Non-Negotiable for Illinois Couples

Given that Illinois does not permit portability of the estate tax exemption, the bypass trust — also called a credit shelter trust or family trust — is the cornerstone of estate planning for any married Illinois resident with meaningful assets.

Here is how it works: when the first spouse dies, instead of leaving everything to the survivor outright (which would trigger portability if this were a federal-only issue, but Illinois has no portability), assets up to $4 million are directed into a bypass trust for the benefit of the surviving spouse and/or children. These assets are sheltered from Illinois estate tax at the first death and, critically, do not count as part of the surviving spouse's taxable estate at the second death.

For a Bitcoin-holding couple:

  • Without a bypass trust: First spouse dies, leaves everything to survivor. Survivor now has the full Bitcoin position plus $0 additional exemption from the deceased spouse. At second death, estate over $4M is taxed.
  • With a bypass trust: First spouse dies, funds bypass trust with up to $4M (including Bitcoin). Survivor enjoys trust benefits. At second death, the bypass trust assets pass to heirs free of Illinois estate tax, and the survivor's own estate gets a separate $4M exemption. Combined effective exemption: $8 million.

This is not merely a planning nicety — it is a difference of potentially hundreds of thousands of dollars in Illinois estate tax.

Illinois Trust Law

Illinois has adopted a comprehensive trust code based on the Uniform Trust Code (755 ILCS 5 et seq.), making it a sophisticated trust jurisdiction. Illinois courts have a substantial body of trust law precedent, and Illinois-sited trusts offer significant flexibility in trustee selection, distribution standards, and administrative provisions.

Illinois RUFADAA: Digital Asset Access After Death

Illinois has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), which governs how fiduciaries — executors, trustees, agents under a power of attorney — can access and manage digital assets, including Bitcoin, after the account holder's death or incapacity.

Under Illinois RUFADAA, a fiduciary's right to access digital assets depends on:

  • Express authorization in the governing estate planning document (will, trust, or power of attorney)
  • The terms of service of any custodian holding the assets (exchange, custody platform)
  • The type of digital asset (content communications vs. other digital assets)

For self-custodied Bitcoin — held in a hardware wallet or via multi-sig — RUFADAA is less directly relevant. What matters is whether your fiduciaries can find and access the keys. Every Illinois Bitcoin estate plan should include a secure, tested key access protocol. This is not optional. Bitcoin that cannot be accessed after death is Bitcoin that is lost forever, regardless of how thoughtfully your trust was drafted.

Dynasty Trusts: Illinois' 1,000-Year Rule

One of Illinois's most underappreciated estate planning features is its perpetuities period. Illinois permits trusts to last for up to 1,000 years — among the longest periods allowed by any state. This effectively makes Illinois a dynasty trust state, allowing multi-generational wealth preservation without the trust assets ever becoming subject to estate tax at each generation's death.

A properly structured Illinois dynasty trust funded with Bitcoin today could, in theory, shelter that Bitcoin and its appreciation from estate tax for ten centuries. While most families have more modest horizons, the underlying mechanic — using a trust to skip the estate tax at each generation — is a powerful planning tool for larger Bitcoin holders.

However, there is a caveat: Illinois does not have a Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) statute. If creditor protection is a primary objective — as it often is for entrepreneurs, physicians, and financial professionals — an Illinois trust will not provide the self-settled spendthrift protection that states like Wyoming offer. Illinois residents who want DAPT protection should use a Wyoming trust for that purpose.

Wyoming Trusts for Illinois Residents: Asset Protection Layer

Wyoming is broadly acknowledged as one of the best trust jurisdictions in the country for asset protection. Wyoming DAPTs allow the settlor to be a discretionary beneficiary of their own trust while still shielding assets from future creditors — something Illinois law does not permit.

For an Illinois Bitcoin holder, the strategy often looks like this:

  1. Establish a Wyoming DAPT to hold Bitcoin. The Wyoming trust provides creditor protection.
  2. The Wyoming trust may be structured to also function as an Illinois estate tax bypass trust at death, provided the trust terms and funding mechanics are coordinated.
  3. Layer a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) over the top if the estate is large enough to need further transfer tax reduction.

This layered approach allows an Illinois Bitcoin holder to get creditor protection (Wyoming), estate tax efficiency (bypass at death), and transfer of appreciation to heirs (GRAT), all working together.

The GRAT: Capturing Bitcoin Appreciation Outside Your Estate

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is a federal transfer tax strategy that can be layered on top of Illinois planning. The mechanics: you transfer Bitcoin into a GRAT, receive annuity payments back for a fixed term, and any appreciation above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate passes to your heirs estate-tax-free at the end of the term.

Bitcoin's volatility actually makes it well-suited for GRATs. If you fund a GRAT during a period of lower Bitcoin prices and Bitcoin appreciates significantly during the GRAT term, a large amount of wealth transfers to heirs with no gift or estate tax. The downside: if you die during the GRAT term, the trust assets are pulled back into your estate. Zeroed-out GRATs are a common strategy for this reason.

For Illinois residents, the GRAT reduces federal estate tax exposure and also reduces the Illinois taxable estate — both important given the two separate tax systems operating in parallel.

Planning Priorities for Illinois Bitcoin Holders

If you are an Illinois resident holding meaningful Bitcoin, here is the strategic priority order:

  1. Bypass Trust — Critical. If you are married, this is the single highest-priority action. Doubling your effective Illinois exemption from $4M to $8M is not optional if your estate may exceed $4M. Work with an estate planning attorney to ensure your will or revocable trust funds a bypass trust at the first death.
  2. Digital Asset Provisions. Update your estate planning documents to explicitly authorize fiduciaries to access, manage, and transfer Bitcoin. Create a secure, tested key access plan for self-custodied Bitcoin.
  3. Wyoming DAPT (if creditor risk warrants). Entrepreneurs, physicians, and executives in high-liability professions should consider a Wyoming self-settled trust as a creditor protection vehicle.
  4. GRAT for Larger Holdings. If your Bitcoin position is significantly above the $4M threshold, a GRAT is worth modeling. The strategy works best when funded during periods of lower valuation and when the trust term is likely to be survived.
  5. Annual Gifting. The annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2024; $19,000 in 2025) allows regular transfers out of your estate without gift tax. Systematic gifting of Bitcoin to heirs compounds nicely over time.
  6. Illinois Dynasty Trust. For larger holdings intended to benefit multiple generations, consider a 1,000-year Illinois dynasty trust. The assets inside grow without estate tax at each generation's death.
