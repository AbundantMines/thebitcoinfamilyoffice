How to Find a Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney: The 10-Question Vetting Guide

Published March 16, 2026 · 20 min read

Most estate attorneys have never handled a single Bitcoin estate plan. Here's how to find the ones who have — and how to identify pretenders in the first five minutes.

You've spent years accumulating Bitcoin. You've figured out self-custody, hardware wallets, maybe even multi-sig. You understand seed phrases, passphrases, and the difference between a hot wallet and a cold storage setup. You've done the hard part.

Now you need an estate plan. And you're about to discover that the legal profession is roughly a decade behind you.

The vast majority of estate planning attorneys in the United States have never written a plan that accounts for Bitcoin's unique custody mechanics. They know how to title a house in a trust. They know how to designate beneficiaries on a brokerage account. They can draft a GRAT or a dynasty trust in their sleep. But ask them how a successor trustee would access Bitcoin held on a Coldcard with a passphrase, and you'll get a blank stare — or worse, confident nonsense.

This guide exists because finding the right attorney for a Bitcoin estate plan is genuinely difficult, and hiring the wrong one is genuinely dangerous. Not dangerous in a "you'll overpay" sense. Dangerous in a "your heirs may permanently lose access to millions of dollars" sense.

Here's how to find an attorney who actually knows what they're doing — and how to identify the ones who don't before you write them a check.

Why Most Estate Attorneys Fail Bitcoin Clients

Traditional estate planning works because traditional assets have institutional custodians. Your brokerage has a beneficiary designation form. Your bank has a POD (payable on death) designation. Your house has a deed that transfers through the trust. In every case, there's a third party — a bank, a brokerage, a title company — that facilitates the transfer to heirs.

Bitcoin doesn't work that way. Not if you hold it properly.

If your Bitcoin is self-custodied on a hardware wallet, there is no institution to call. There's no customer service number your executor can dial. There's no form to fill out. There is a 12- or 24-word seed phrase, possibly a passphrase, possibly a multi-sig configuration involving multiple keys held by multiple parties — and if your estate plan doesn't address the operational mechanics of accessing those keys, the plan is legally valid and practically useless.

An attorney who drafts a trust that says "I leave my Bitcoin holdings to my children, to be distributed equally" has created a document that a probate court will honor. But honoring it doesn't help if nobody can actually move the Bitcoin. The trust doesn't unlock the hardware wallet. The court order doesn't regenerate a lost seed phrase.

This is the fundamental disconnect. Traditional estate planning is about legal authority to access assets. Bitcoin estate planning requires legal authority plus operational capability. An attorney who doesn't understand both sides of that equation is only solving half the problem.

The Three Types of Attorneys You'll Encounter

After years of working with Bitcoin-wealthy families, we've identified three distinct categories of estate planning attorneys when it comes to digital assets. Knowing which type you're sitting across from will save you thousands of dollars and months of wasted time.

Type A: The Uninitiated

This attorney knows nothing about Bitcoin beyond what they've seen on cable news. They'll write a generic trust, charge you $2,000–$4,000, and add a line that says something like "all digital assets, including cryptocurrency" to the asset schedule. They won't ask you how you hold your Bitcoin. They won't create a Letter of Instructions. They won't address seed phrase custody, hardware wallet access, or multi-sig key succession.

Type A attorneys are dangerous not because they're incompetent — many are excellent traditional estate planners — but because they don't know what they don't know. They'll produce a clean, professional-looking trust document that gives your family a false sense of security. Everything looks handled. Nothing actually is.

How to spot them: They don't ask you a single question about how you hold your Bitcoin. They treat it like a stock portfolio — something that just needs to be listed on an asset schedule.

Type B: The Aware But Inexperienced

This attorney has read a few CLE articles about digital assets. They know Bitcoin is different from a stock. They might even know what a seed phrase is in theory. They'll add a "digital asset provisions" section to their standard trust template, maybe include a reference to a Letter of Instructions, and charge you a modest premium for the extra complexity.

Type B attorneys are better than Type A, but they're still risky for significant Bitcoin holdings. Their digital asset provisions tend to be generic — copy-pasted from a template or a CLE handout. They haven't actually walked a client through the process of documenting seed phrase access in a way that's both secure and accessible to heirs. They haven't coordinated with a multi-sig provider. They haven't thought through the difference between Bitcoin held on an exchange, in a self-directed IRA, and in cold storage.

How to spot them: They use the right vocabulary but can't go deep. Ask them to walk you through how a successor trustee would access Bitcoin held in a 2-of-3 multi-sig setup, and the answer gets vague fast.

Type C: The Bitcoin-Competent Attorney

This is who you're looking for. Type C attorneys have handled multiple Bitcoin estate plans. They understand hardware wallet custody, seed phrase storage security, multi-sig key succession, SDIRA prohibited transactions, and the interplay between trust structure and custody architecture. They've coordinated with providers like Unchained or Casa. They've drafted Letters of Instructions that include specific hardware wallet models, PIN codes, passphrase locations, and step-by-step access procedures.

Type C attorneys ask questions before you do. They want to know your exact custody setup before they draft a single sentence. They charge a premium because the work takes longer and requires specialized knowledge. And they're worth every dollar.

How to spot them: They ask you questions you didn't expect. "Which hardware wallet do you use?" "Is there a passphrase in addition to the seed phrase?" "Who holds the other keys in your multi-sig?" "Is any of your Bitcoin in an SDIRA, and if so, who's the custodian?" If they're asking these questions in the first meeting, you've found a Type C.

The 10 Diagnostic Questions to Ask Before Hiring

These questions are designed to reveal, within five minutes, whether you're talking to a Type A, B, or C attorney. Ask all ten. The answers — and especially the non-answers — tell you everything you need to know.

1. How do you handle seed phrase documentation in the estate plan?

What you want to hear: A discussion of the tension between security and accessibility. The attorney should explain that seed phrases should never be included in the trust document itself (which becomes a public record in some jurisdictions or is accessible to multiple parties). They should reference a Letter of Instructions, secure storage options (safety deposit boxes, specialized services like Cryptosteel or similar metal backup solutions), and a protocol for how the successor trustee accesses the seed phrase without exposing it to unnecessary parties.

Red flag answer: "We'll list it in the asset schedule" or "What's a seed phrase?"

For more on the security considerations around seed phrase custody in estate plans, see our guide on Bitcoin cold storage and estate planning.

2. Have you worked with self-directed IRAs holding Bitcoin before?

What you want to hear: An understanding that SDIRAs holding Bitcoin create unique compliance requirements. The attorney should know about prohibited transactions, the role of the SDIRA custodian, the difference between checkbook control LLCs and custodian-directed accounts, and the fact that certain estate planning maneuvers (like an in-kind distribution from an IRA to a trust) have specific tax consequences with Bitcoin.

Red flag answer: "An IRA is an IRA — the Bitcoin part doesn't change the estate planning." It absolutely does.

3. Can you explain how a successor trustee would access hardware wallet Bitcoin?

What you want to hear: A step-by-step description. The successor trustee needs the hardware wallet (or the seed phrase to restore it), any PIN or passphrase, the software wallet or coordinator software used to interface with the device, and clear instructions on how to initiate a transaction. The attorney should recognize that this is fundamentally different from calling Fidelity and presenting a death certificate.

Red flag answer: "The trustee would contact the company" — there is no company for a hardware wallet. The whole point is self-custody.

4. What's your approach to multi-sig key succession?

What you want to hear: An understanding that multi-sig involves multiple keys, that succession requires ensuring the successor has access to the threshold number of keys, and that this often involves coordinating with a collaborative custody provider. The attorney should know the difference between a 2-of-3 setup where you hold two keys versus one where a provider like Unchained holds one.

Red flag answer: "What's multi-sig?" — and you're done. Walk out politely.

Our multi-sig estate planning guide covers the technical and legal considerations in detail.

5. Are you familiar with the current estate tax exemption and recent legislative changes?

What you want to hear: Any competent estate attorney should know the current federal estate tax exemption level and be tracking legislative developments that may affect it. For Bitcoin clients specifically, the attorney should understand how volatile asset appreciation affects exemption utilization strategies — a client who used $5 million of exemption on Bitcoin that's now worth $50 million has an entirely different planning profile than someone who gifted $5 million in bonds.

Red flag answer: Not dangerous by itself, but combined with weak answers on other questions, it signals a generalist who isn't keeping current.

6. How do you coordinate with the client's custodian or multi-sig provider?

What you want to hear: The attorney should recognize that the estate plan can't exist in isolation from the custody infrastructure. For clients using collaborative custody providers, the attorney needs to understand the provider's inheritance protocol and ensure the estate plan aligns with it. For exchange-held Bitcoin, the attorney needs to know the exchange's death-and-estate process. This is coordination work, and a good attorney does it proactively.

Red flag answer: "That's outside the scope of what we do." If the attorney won't coordinate with the custody provider, the plan will have gaps.

7. Have you created a Letter of Instructions for a Bitcoin client before?

What you want to hear: Yes, with specifics. A Letter of Instructions for a Bitcoin client should include: wallet types and locations, hardware wallet models, software wallet applications used, seed phrase storage locations (not the phrases themselves), passphrase locations, multi-sig configuration details, exchange account information, SDIRA custodian contact information, and step-by-step instructions for the successor trustee. This is a Bitcoin-specific document that most attorneys have never drafted.

Red flag answer: "A Letter of Instructions isn't legally binding, so we don't usually do those." Correct that it's not binding. Irrelevant. It's the most important operational document in a Bitcoin estate plan.

8. How do you handle Bitcoin held in a trust — does the trust document need to specify the custodian?

What you want to hear: An understanding that trust-held Bitcoin raises unique titling questions. With traditional assets, you retitle the brokerage account in the name of the trust. With self-custodied Bitcoin, there's nothing to retitle — the trust "holds" the Bitcoin by virtue of the trustee controlling the keys. The trust document should grant the trustee explicit authority to manage digital assets, including the power to choose and change custodians, execute transactions, and maintain custody infrastructure.

Red flag answer: "We'll just add the wallet address to the schedule." Wallet addresses change. This answer reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of how Bitcoin custody works.

9. What states are best for trust siting with Bitcoin, and why?

What you want to hear: A discussion of state trust laws and how they interact with digital asset holdings. States like Wyoming, South Dakota, Nevada, and Delaware offer advantages — Wyoming has the most developed digital asset legal framework, South Dakota has strong dynasty trust provisions with no state income tax, Nevada offers asset protection and no state income tax. The attorney should be able to articulate why a Bitcoin holder might want to site a trust in a state different from where they live.

Red flag answer: "You should just use your home state." Sometimes that's the right answer, but if the attorney can't explain when and why it isn't, they haven't thought about this for Bitcoin clients.

10. Have you worked with Unchained, Casa, or similar multi-sig providers?

What you want to hear: Direct experience. The attorney should know what these providers offer, how their inheritance protocols work, and how the estate plan needs to account for the provider relationship. Even better if they have a working relationship with the provider's legal or concierge team.

Red flag answer: "I'm not familiar with those companies." Not disqualifying on its own — there are excellent Bitcoin estate attorneys who work primarily with single-sig self-custody clients. But combined with weak answers on multi-sig questions, it suggests limited Bitcoin estate planning experience.

Credentials and Specializations That Matter

Not all estate planning attorneys are created equal, even before you add Bitcoin to the equation. Here are the credentials that signal genuine expertise:

Legal Credentials

  • LL.M. in Taxation or Estate Planning: A master's degree in tax law, typically earned after the J.D. This is the gold standard for estate planning specialization. An attorney with an LL.M. in taxation has spent an additional year studying the exact intersection of tax law and estate planning that matters most for Bitcoin holders.
  • Board Certification in Estate Planning: Several states offer board certification through their bar associations. In Texas, for example, board certification in estate planning and probate law requires passing an exam, demonstrating substantial experience, and peer review. It's a meaningful credential.
  • ACTEC Fellow: The American College of Trust and Estate Counsel is an invitation-only organization of the top estate planning attorneys in the country. ACTEC Fellows have been nominated by their peers and vetted for expertise. If your attorney is an ACTEC Fellow, they're in the top tier of the profession.

Financial Credentials

  • Certified Trust and Fiduciary Advisor (CTFA): This certification, offered by the American Bankers Association, focuses on trust administration and fiduciary responsibility. Useful for attorneys who also serve as trustees or advise corporate trustees.
  • Certified Financial Planner (CFP®): Some estate planning attorneys also hold CFP certification, which gives them a broader financial planning perspective. Helpful for coordinating the estate plan with the client's overall wealth management strategy.

Digital Asset Specialization

  • Membership in digital asset law organizations: The Digital Assets Council of Financial Professionals (DACFP), the Blockchain & Cryptocurrency Committee of various bar associations, and similar groups. Membership alone doesn't prove expertise, but it signals active engagement with the space.
  • Speaking at Bitcoin or digital asset conferences: Attorneys who present at Bitcoin conferences, crypto law symposiums, or digital asset CLE programs are investing time in the space. Check their speaking history.
  • Published articles on digital asset estate planning: Look for attorneys who have written substantively about Bitcoin estate planning — not just a blog post, but detailed analysis of the legal and operational challenges.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Some warning signs are immediate deal-breakers. If you encounter any of these, end the consultation and find another attorney.

"We'll Just Add a Bitcoin Clause"

This is the single most common — and most dangerous — response. An attorney who treats Bitcoin as a line item to add to a standard trust template does not understand the problem. Bitcoin estate planning requires structural changes to how the trust handles custody, access, and succession. It's not an addendum. It's a different kind of planning.

No Questions About Your Custody Setup

An attorney who doesn't ask how you hold your Bitcoin cannot create a plan that addresses how your heirs will access it. This is foundational. If they're not asking whether you use a hardware wallet, which one, whether you have a passphrase, whether you use multi-sig, whether any Bitcoin is in an SDIRA — they're planning blind.

Can't Explain What a Seed Phrase Is

You don't need your attorney to be a Bitcoin developer. But they need to understand the basic mechanics of how Bitcoin custody works. If they can't explain what a seed phrase is and why it matters for estate planning, they cannot draft a plan that protects your Bitcoin.

Doesn't Ask to See an Asset Inventory

Any competent estate planner — Bitcoin or otherwise — needs to understand your complete asset picture before drafting a plan. For Bitcoin clients, this includes not just the value but the custody structure of each holding. Exchange accounts, hardware wallets, multi-sig setups, SDIRAs, mining operations — all of it affects the plan design.

Same Price as a Standard Trust

If an attorney quotes you the exact same price for a Bitcoin estate plan as they would for a standard trust, one of two things is true: they're undercharging because they don't understand the complexity, or they're planning to produce a standard trust with a Bitcoin veneer. Either way, you're not getting what you need.

Resistance to Creating a Letter of Instructions

Some attorneys dismiss Letters of Instructions because they're not legally binding documents. This is technically correct and practically idiotic. For Bitcoin estate planning, the Letter of Instructions is the operational bridge between the legal document (the trust) and the physical reality (the keys). An attorney who won't create one is leaving the most important part of the plan undone.

No Awareness of Tax Implications Specific to Bitcoin

Bitcoin has specific tax treatment that affects estate planning — cost basis tracking, the potential for stepped-up basis at death, the treatment of mining income, SDIRA prohibited transaction rules. An attorney who treats Bitcoin identically to stocks for tax purposes will miss planning opportunities that could save your heirs significant money.

What to Expect in Terms of Cost

Estate planning fees vary widely by complexity, geography, and attorney experience. Here's a realistic breakdown for Bitcoin clients:

Basic Documents

Simple will + durable power of attorney + healthcare directive: $1,000–$2,000. This is the minimum — and for most Bitcoin holders, it's insufficient. A will goes through probate, which is public and slow. If your primary goal is protecting Bitcoin, you need a trust.

Revocable Living Trust

Standard revocable living trust package: $2,500–$5,000. This typically includes the trust document, pour-over will, durable power of attorney, healthcare directive, and initial funding guidance. For a Bitcoin client, add $500–$2,000 for the digital asset provisions, Letter of Instructions, and custody coordination work.

Complex Estate Plans

Advanced structures (GRAT, SLAT, dynasty trust, irrevocable life insurance trust, charitable remainder trust): $10,000–$50,000+. These are planning tools for high-net-worth clients — and many Bitcoin holders qualify, sometimes unexpectedly. A Bitcoin position purchased for $50,000 that's now worth $5 million needs advanced planning, not a basic revocable trust.

The Bitcoin Premium

Expect to pay $500–$2,000 more than you would for an identical plan without Bitcoin. This premium reflects the additional time required to understand your custody setup, draft Bitcoin-specific provisions, create the Letter of Instructions, and coordinate with your custody provider. It's not a markup — it's compensation for specialized work that most attorneys can't do.

Why the Premium Is Worth Paying

Consider the alternative. An attorney who charges standard rates and produces a standard trust leaves your heirs with a document that grants them legal authority but no operational capability to access the Bitcoin. If your heirs can't access 10 BTC because the seed phrase wasn't properly documented, that's a loss that dwarfs any attorney fee premium. The stepped-up cost basis alone — properly documented and preserved — can save your heirs hundreds of thousands or millions in capital gains taxes. Getting that wrong because you saved $1,500 on legal fees is not a trade any rational person should make.

Preparing for Your First Meeting

Walk into your first meeting prepared and you'll accomplish two things: you'll get a better plan, and you'll immediately signal to the attorney that you're a serious client who understands what's at stake.

What to Bring

  • Complete asset inventory: All assets, not just Bitcoin. The attorney needs the full picture to design the right structure. Include real estate, brokerage accounts, retirement accounts, business interests, and all Bitcoin holdings with approximate values.
  • Bitcoin custody breakdown: For each Bitcoin holding, document: how much, where it's held (exchange, hardware wallet, multi-sig, SDIRA), which hardware wallet model, whether there's a passphrase, who the multi-sig provider is, and who the SDIRA custodian is. You don't need to bring seed phrases — and don't. The attorney needs to understand your custody architecture, not your keys.
  • Current beneficiary designations: For any accounts with beneficiary designations (IRAs, 401(k)s, life insurance, exchange accounts), bring the current designation. These override the trust, so they need to be coordinated.
  • Existing estate documents: If you have a current will, trust, power of attorney, or healthcare directive, bring copies. The attorney needs to know what exists before recommending what to create.
  • Family information: Spouse, children, ages, any special needs, any prior marriages, any family members you want to exclude or provide for differently. This shapes the trust structure.
  • Goals and concerns: What are you trying to accomplish? Asset protection? Tax minimization? Generational wealth transfer? Privacy? Control from beyond the grave? Different goals lead to different structures.

What the Attorney Needs to Know About Your Custody Setup

Be specific. "I have Bitcoin" is useless. The attorney needs to know:

  • How many distinct custody arrangements you have (e.g., some on Coinbase, some on a Ledger, some in a 2-of-3 multi-sig with Unchained, some in a self-directed IRA with Alto)
  • For hardware wallets: which device, whether you use a passphrase, where the seed phrase backup is stored
  • For multi-sig: the configuration (2-of-3, 3-of-5), who holds each key, and what the provider's inheritance protocol looks like
  • For SDIRAs: who the custodian is, whether it's a checkbook control LLC structure, and how the Bitcoin within the IRA is custodied
  • For exchange accounts: which exchange, whether you've set up a beneficiary or trusted contact

This information lets the attorney design a plan that actually maps to your real-world setup. Without it, they're drafting in the dark.

Choosing the Right Trustee for Bitcoin

Your attorney selection and your trustee selection are deeply connected. The trustee is the person (or institution) who will actually manage the Bitcoin after you're gone. If you choose a trustee who can't operate a hardware wallet, the best estate plan in the world won't help.

This is a big enough topic that we've written a dedicated guide: How to Choose a Trustee for Bitcoin Holdings. But here's the short version for the attorney-selection context:

  • Your attorney should ask who your successor trustee will be and whether that person has the technical capability to manage Bitcoin
  • If your successor trustee is not technically capable, the attorney should recommend a co-trustee structure or a corporate trustee with digital asset capabilities
  • The Letter of Instructions should be written for the specific trustee — their level of technical knowledge determines how detailed the instructions need to be
  • If you're using multi-sig, the trustee selection affects key distribution — the attorney needs to understand this interplay

An attorney who doesn't raise the trustee question in the context of Bitcoin custody is missing a critical piece of the puzzle.

The Ongoing Relationship: Why This Isn't a One-Time Engagement

Estate plans are not set-and-forget documents. They need regular review and updates. For Bitcoin holders, the review triggers are more frequent and more consequential than for traditional clients.

Tax Law Changes

Estate and gift tax law is a moving target. Legislative changes can dramatically affect optimal planning strategies. The SECURE Act changed inherited IRA rules. SECURE 2.0 made additional modifications. Future legislation may alter estate tax exemption levels, capital gains treatment, or digital asset-specific rules. Your attorney should be tracking these developments and proactively recommending plan updates.

Bitcoin Price Milestones

Bitcoin's volatility means your estate's value can change dramatically in short periods. A plan designed when your Bitcoin was worth $500,000 may be structurally inadequate when it's worth $5 million. Price milestones should trigger planning reviews — not because the plan is broken, but because different wealth levels open up different planning strategies (GRATs, SLATs, dynasty trusts, charitable structures) that weren't worth the complexity at lower values.

Custody Changes

If you move Bitcoin from an exchange to self-custody, from single-sig to multi-sig, or from a hardware wallet to a new hardware wallet, the Letter of Instructions needs updating. If you change SDIRA custodians or multi-sig providers, the estate plan may need structural updates. Your attorney should know about these changes.

Family Changes

Marriage, divorce, new children, death of a beneficiary, a child reaching adulthood, a family member developing a substance abuse issue or creditor problems — all of these trigger estate plan reviews. Standard stuff, but important.

Annual Review Cadence

For Bitcoin holders with significant positions, we recommend annual reviews with your estate attorney. Not a full re-draft every year — a check-in to review the plan against current tax law, current holdings, current custody setup, and current family circumstances. Budget $500–$1,500 annually for this review. It's insurance against drift between your plan and your reality.

DIY vs. Attorney: When to Use Each

Online estate planning tools have gotten surprisingly good. Services like Trust & Will, Willing, and similar platforms can produce legally valid trust documents for a fraction of the cost of an attorney. For simple situations, they're fine.

But "simple" has a specific meaning here, and most Bitcoin holders don't qualify.

DIY Is Reasonable When:

  • Your total estate (including Bitcoin) is under $500,000
  • Your Bitcoin is held on a single exchange with a beneficiary designation
  • You have a straightforward family situation (spouse, kids, no prior marriages)
  • You're not using any advanced custody (no multi-sig, no SDIRA, no hardware wallets)
  • You need a basic revocable trust to avoid probate

You Need an Attorney When:

  • Your Bitcoin holdings exceed $500,000
  • You use self-custody (hardware wallets, multi-sig)
  • You hold Bitcoin in a self-directed IRA
  • Your total estate may be subject to estate tax
  • You have a complex family situation
  • You want to use advanced planning structures (GRAT, SLAT, dynasty trust, CRT)
  • You have Bitcoin mining operations with depreciation and deduction strategies
  • You want asset protection planning

The math here is straightforward. An attorney who actually understands Bitcoin estate planning will charge you $5,000–$20,000 depending on complexity. A plan that fails because the attorney didn't understand Bitcoin custody — or because you used a DIY template that doesn't address seed phrase succession — can cost your heirs the entire Bitcoin position. For a family holding 10+ BTC, that's a potential loss that's orders of magnitude larger than the attorney's fee.

The stepped-up cost basis at death is particularly worth getting right. When Bitcoin passes through a properly structured estate plan, heirs receive a stepped-up basis to the fair market value at the date of death. If you bought Bitcoin at $5,000 and it's worth $150,000 when you die, your heirs' basis is $150,000 — not $5,000. If they sell immediately, there's zero capital gains tax. Get this wrong through poor planning, and your heirs could face capital gains on $145,000 per coin. At a 23.8% long-term capital gains rate (including NIIT), that's $34,510 per coin in unnecessary taxes. Multiply by a meaningful position and the cost of bad planning is staggering.

Building Your Bitcoin Estate Planning Team

The estate attorney is the quarterback, but they're not the whole team. A comprehensive Bitcoin estate plan typically involves:

  • Estate planning attorney: Drafts the legal documents, designs the trust structure, coordinates the overall plan
  • CPA or tax advisor: Handles cost basis tracking, tax return preparation, and tax projection for planning strategies. Ideally one who understands Bitcoin-specific tax issues (mining income, staking, hard forks)
  • Financial advisor: If you use one, they should be involved in the estate planning conversation. Look for an advisor who understands Bitcoin as a long-term holding, not one who'll push you to sell into a diversified portfolio
  • Custody provider: If you use Unchained, Casa, or similar, their team should coordinate with your attorney on the inheritance protocol
  • Insurance advisor: Life insurance can provide liquidity for estate tax payments, equalizing distributions among heirs, or funding trusts. Your insurance advisor needs to know about the Bitcoin to properly size coverage

The best estate attorneys will help you assemble this team — or work seamlessly with professionals you've already chosen. Ask about this in your initial consultation. An attorney who works in isolation, without coordinating with your other advisors, is leaving value on the table.
