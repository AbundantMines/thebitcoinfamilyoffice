Why 99% of Hong Kong Family Offices Are Keeping Their Bitcoin — And What US Families Should Do Differently

March 10, 2026  ·  13 min read  ·  The Bitcoin Family Office

This morning, a study landed from the Hong Kong Institute for Monetary and Financial Research that most people will skim past. They shouldn't. The HKIMR surveyed family offices across one of the world's densest concentrations of private capital — and found that only 1% plan to reduce their crypto exposure. Ninety-nine percent are holding or increasing.

That number isn't a sentiment indicator. It's a structural signal. Hong Kong family offices are staffed by people who have survived multiple cycles, managed multigenerational wealth through political upheaval, and operate in a jurisdiction actively competing for global capital with favorable tax policy. When that cohort votes 99-to-1 to stay, you don't wave it off as retail optimism.

And Hong Kong isn't just holding — it's formalizing. The government is extending its single-family office tax incentive regime to digital assets in H1 2026, creating a legal and fiscal architecture that treats Bitcoin as a legitimate long-term asset class deserving the same structural treatment as private equity or real estate.

For US Bitcoin-wealthy families, the Hong Kong news creates an interesting mirror. The question isn't whether to copy Hong Kong — you can't, not without abandoning your citizenship. The question is: what does this global institutional consensus tell you about timing, conviction, and the urgency of putting proper US-legal structures in place?

Section 1: What Hong Kong Is Actually Building

Hong Kong's single-family office tax regime is not new — it was introduced to compete with Singapore's Variable Capital Company framework and attract ultra-high-net-worth families who might otherwise domicile their structures in Switzerland, the Cayman Islands, or the British Virgin Islands. The core incentive: qualifying investment income managed through a recognized single-family office structure is exempt from Hong Kong profits tax (currently 16.5% for corporations).

Until now, the regime covered a defined list of qualifying assets: equities, bonds, funds, real estate investment trusts. Digital assets were excluded. The H1 2026 extension changes that, adding digital assets — including Bitcoin — to the qualifying asset list.

The implications for global capital allocation are significant. Family offices managing assets for non-Hong-Kong-resident ultra-high-net-worth individuals can now legally structure their Bitcoin positions through Hong Kong entities and receive favorable tax treatment on gains and income. This isn't theoretical. It's a direct invitation for institutional capital that has been sitting on the sidelines to formalize positions within a recognized legal framework.

"When 99% of family offices in one of the world's most sophisticated financial centers stay allocated, it's no longer a fringe thesis. It's the institutional consensus."

What this means for US families: capital moves to favorable jurisdictions. Pools of private wealth that compete with US family offices for deal flow, custody solutions, and asset management infrastructure are now operating in a lower-friction regulatory and tax environment. The US wealth management industry will respond — but it will take time. Families who structure now, using the US-legal tools already available, capture the window before advisors and structures are fully commoditized.

Why Hong Kong's Signal Is Credible

Family offices in Hong Kong are not venture funds chasing upside. They are multigenerational wealth preservation vehicles — the same function a US family office serves. Their investment mandate is preservation first, growth second, liquidity third. The fact that 99% of them see no reason to reduce Bitcoin exposure is functionally equivalent to a conservative endowment maintaining its alternative allocation through a drawdown. It's not enthusiasm. It's conviction grounded in structural analysis.

Section 2: The US Tax Reality — And Why It's Not the Obstacle Most Think

The first question any US family asks when they see Hong Kong's tax regime: can we do that? The short answer is no — at least not by simply relocating. The United States taxes its citizens and lawful permanent residents on worldwide income regardless of where they live. There is no "move offshore" solution for US persons. The only exit from the US tax system is formal renunciation of citizenship, which triggers an exit tax under IRC §877A on unrealized gains above the exemption threshold ($877,000 for 2024, indexed annually). For families holding large Bitcoin positions, that exit tax often exceeds the tax savings — and most families are not willing to sever citizenship for a tax benefit.

What this creates is a persistent misconception: that US families are simply stuck, paying 20–23.8% federal capital gains rates on every Bitcoin sale while their Hong Kong counterparts pay nothing. That framing is incorrect. The US tax code contains an extensive set of legitimate structures that substantially reduce — and in some cases eliminate — capital gains tax on Bitcoin appreciation. They require planning, they require time, and they require qualified counsel. But they exist, they are legal, and they are underused.

Section 3: The US Structures That Actually Work

The following structures are not loopholes. They are provisions of US tax and trust law that Congress enacted deliberately — to encourage long-term capital formation, charitable giving, and multi-generational wealth transfer. Sophisticated family offices have used them for decades with traditional assets. Bitcoin families are now beginning to apply them systematically.

Wyoming and South Dakota Dynasty Trusts

Wyoming and South Dakota are the two most Bitcoin-friendly trust jurisdictions in the United States. Both states allow dynasty trusts — irrevocable trusts that can last for up to 1,000 years (effectively perpetual). Both have no state income tax on trust income. Both have strong asset protection statutes. Wyoming additionally has explicit digital asset legislation, recognizing Bitcoin and other digital assets as property and providing legal clarity on custody and transfer.

The mechanics are straightforward: a grantor funds a dynasty trust with Bitcoin using their lifetime gift and estate tax exemption (currently proposed at $15M per individual under the OBBBA). All future appreciation — including decades of potential Bitcoin price growth — compounds inside the trust without ever returning to the taxable estate. Multiple generations of beneficiaries access the trust corpus under terms the grantor sets. The trust pays no Wyoming or South Dakota state income tax. Federal capital gains tax applies at the trust level when Bitcoin is sold, but the estate tax exposure is permanently eliminated on the funded amount and all appreciation.

For a family holding 100 BTC at today's $71,000 price ($7.1M), funding a Wyoming dynasty trust with half their position uses only $3.55M of exemption — well within the proposed $15M — and permanently shields all future appreciation on that tranche from estate tax. If Bitcoin returns to $150,000, that tranche is worth $7.5M inside the trust, entirely estate-tax-free.

Delaware Directed Trusts

Delaware's trust law provides a different set of advantages, particularly for families who want institutional custodians and sophisticated governance. Delaware directed trusts allow the separation of investment direction from trustee administration — meaning a family can retain a Bitcoin-specialist investment adviser directing custody and trading decisions, while an institutional Delaware trustee handles administrative and compliance functions. Delaware has no state income tax on trust income for non-Delaware beneficiaries. Delaware trust law is also among the most litigated and settled in the country, providing maximum legal certainty.

For large Bitcoin positions where governance complexity exceeds what a Wyoming directed trust can accommodate, Delaware is often the structure of choice for US family offices.

Puerto Rico Act 60 — The Most Aggressive Domestic Option

Puerto Rico Act 60 (consolidating the former Act 22 for individual investors) offers the most dramatic tax reduction available to US persons without renouncing citizenship. Bona fide Puerto Rico residents who qualify under Act 60 pay 0% Puerto Rico tax on capital gains accrued after establishing residency — and because Puerto Rico is a US territory, residents are not subject to federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains.

The requirements are real and the IRS scrutinizes them heavily. "Bona fide residency" means 183+ days per year in Puerto Rico, a home on the island, and demonstrable social and economic connections. It is not a paper residency. Gains accrued before moving remain subject to US tax. But for a Bitcoin-wealthy family that has not yet taken gains — particularly one holding a position that has appreciated significantly — the math is compelling: relocate, establish genuine residency, wait out the holding period, and realize gains at a 0% rate on all post-move appreciation.

The practical reality is that most families are not willing to make Puerto Rico their primary residence. For those who are, it is the most powerful tool in the US tax planning arsenal.

Bitcoin Mining as a Tax Efficiency Strategy

One of the most underutilized tools in the Bitcoin family office playbook is direct Bitcoin mining. Mining operations generate Bitcoin at cost basis, convert ordinary income into depreciable assets, and — under bonus depreciation rules — can produce significant current-year deductions that offset capital gains from other Bitcoin sales. Opportunity Zone Funds

Opportunity Zones are designated census tracts where investment of capital gains triggers substantial tax benefits: deferral of the original gain, basis step-up after five to seven years, and — for positions held ten years or more — complete elimination of tax on appreciation within the Opportunity Zone fund. The structure was designed for real estate and business investment, and most OZ funds are real property focused. However, the legal framework permits OZ funds that invest in operating Bitcoin mining businesses, data center infrastructure, and other qualifying businesses located in opportunity zones.

For a family that has realized a large Bitcoin gain and is looking to redeploy capital without paying the tax immediately, a qualifying opportunity zone fund investment can defer the gain to 2026 (or later, under current rules), allow the deferred tax to be paid at potentially lower rates, and build a new position where all future appreciation is tax-free. The window for maximum benefit requires investment within 180 days of the qualifying gain event.

Charitable Structures — DAF, CRT, CLAT

Charitable planning is the third major pillar of Bitcoin tax efficiency. The core tools:

  • Donor-Advised Fund (DAF): Contribute appreciated Bitcoin directly to a DAF. The contribution is a charitable deduction at fair market value on the contribution date. No capital gains tax on the appreciation. The DAF sells the Bitcoin tax-free and invests the proceeds; the donor recommends grants over time. Simple, immediate, powerful — and increasingly available at custodians with direct Bitcoin contribution capability.
  • Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT): Transfer appreciated Bitcoin to a CRT. The trust sells Bitcoin tax-free, reinvests the proceeds, and pays an annuity or unitrust amount to the donor (and/or other non-charitable beneficiaries) for a term of years or life. At the end of the term, the remainder passes to charity. The donor receives a partial charitable deduction at funding. CRTs are particularly useful for families who need income from their Bitcoin position but want to avoid an immediate large capital gains event.
  • Charitable Lead Annuity Trust (CLAT): The inverse of a CRT — the trust pays an annuity to charity first, with the remainder passing to the donor's heirs estate-tax-free. CLATs are most powerful in low-interest-rate environments (the IRC 7520 rate determines the calculation) and are best suited for families with meaningful charitable intent and a desire to transfer wealth to the next generation at reduced gift and estate tax cost.

Section 4: What US Family Offices Are Actually Doing

The most sophisticated US Bitcoin family offices are not choosing one structure. They are building layered architectures that address different pools of capital, different family members, and different planning horizons simultaneously. The institutional playbook, distilled:

Multi-Jurisdiction Trust Architecture

A typical institutional structure separates Bitcoin holdings into tranches: a Wyoming or South Dakota dynasty trust holds the core multigenerational position, funded using exemption at compressed valuations. A Delaware directed trust holds the family's trading and liquidity position, with institutional-grade custodians and clear governance. Charitable structures (DAF or CRT) receive appreciated tranches on a rolling basis, generating deductions that offset income events elsewhere in the family's structure.

The key insight: each vehicle is optimized for its purpose. The dynasty trust is for permanence and estate-tax elimination. The directed trust is for governance and liquidity. The charitable vehicle is for deduction generation and values alignment. Together they create a system where the family rarely, if ever, faces a large unplanned taxable event.

Bifurcated Custody for International Family Members

US Bitcoin family offices with non-US family members — children who have taken foreign citizenship, international branches of multigenerational families — increasingly use bifurcated custody architectures. US-person family members hold Bitcoin through domestic structures with full IRS compliance. Non-US family members hold through offshore structures (BVI, Cayman, Singapore, now Hong Kong) that take advantage of local favorable tax treatment without creating US tax exposure.

This bifurcation is not tax evasion. It is proper jurisdictional structuring — the same approach that any global family office with members in multiple countries would employ. The planning challenge is ensuring that US persons do not have constructive ownership or control of the non-US structures in ways that trigger US tax filing obligations (PFIC, CFC, FBAR, FATCA).

Investment Policy Statement Discipline

Every institutional family office — traditional or Bitcoin-native — operates with a written Investment Policy Statement that defines allocation targets, rebalancing triggers, liquidity requirements, and risk tolerance. For Bitcoin families, the IPS is particularly important because it creates a governance layer that insulates the family from reactive decision-making during price volatility.

An IPS that says "maintain 60% of portfolio in Bitcoin, rebalance when Bitcoin exceeds 75% or falls below 45%, sell no more than X% per quarter" turns what would otherwise be an emotional decision into a mechanical one. Tax planning is integrated directly: rebalancing sales are coordinated with charitable contributions, loss harvesting in other asset classes, and annual gifting programs to maximize after-tax efficiency.

Long-Term Hold with Trust-Level Planning

The single most powerful tax strategy for Bitcoin is also the simplest: hold. A Bitcoin position held inside a properly structured trust never triggers capital gains until sale. The step-up in basis at death eliminates unrealized gains entirely for assets that pass through an estate (subject to estate tax, which is why trust planning matters). And for positions inside a dynasty trust, the trust itself is the perpetual holder — there is no death event, no step-up, no taxable transfer for generations.

The institutional insight: the goal is not to minimize capital gains rate. It is to minimize taxable gain events entirely, using structural planning to hold Bitcoin in the form with the most favorable tax treatment until the family actually needs liquidity. Section 5: The Planning Window — Why 2026 Is Different

Planning windows for wealth transfer are not permanent. They open when valuations are depressed, exemptions are elevated, and interest rates create favorable calculation environments. Right now, several factors align in a way that may not recur:

Bitcoin at Compressed Valuations

At approximately $71,000 at time of writing, Bitcoin is roughly 44% below its all-time high. From a trust-funding perspective, this is feature, not bug. Every Bitcoin transferred to a dynasty trust, GRAT, or other estate-planning vehicle at $71,000 removes $71,000 of taxable estate exposure. If Bitcoin returns to $150,000, the $79,000 of appreciation per coin passes estate-tax-free inside the trust — permanently. The tax savings on the appreciation is the same whether you fund the trust at $71K or $150K; but you use half as much of your exemption at $71K.

This is the structural argument for acting now that has nothing to do with price prediction. You do not need to believe Bitcoin will outperform. You need only believe it will be worth more in 20 years than it is today — a view that 99% of Hong Kong family offices apparently share.

The OBBBA Exemption Window

The One Big Beautiful Budget Act, as proposed, would increase the federal estate and gift tax exemption to approximately $15 million per individual (indexed for inflation). This is the highest the exemption has ever been in nominal terms. A married couple can together exempt $30 million from estate and gift tax — meaning they can fund trust structures with $30M of Bitcoin using zero gift tax, with all future appreciation sheltered from estate tax forever.

Exemptions are political. They require Congressional action to change. The current elevated exemption environment was not the law in 2017, will not necessarily be the law in 2030, and has been subject to sunset provisions that have required last-minute legislative extensions. Families who use their exemption now lock in the benefit permanently. Families who wait may face a lower exemption — meaning less Bitcoin can be transferred estate-tax-free.

Annual Gifting and Superfunding

The 2026 annual gift exclusion allows each donor to give up to $19,000 per recipient (inflation-adjusted) without using any lifetime exemption. A family with three adult children can give $57,000 per parent per year — $114,000 per couple — directly in Bitcoin, completely outside the estate. Over 20 years, that compounds to meaningful numbers even before factoring in Bitcoin appreciation.

Superfunding of 529 education plans — contributing five years of annual exclusion gifts in a single year — is another underused tool for families with grandchildren. Up to $95,000 per beneficiary ($190,000 from a married couple) can be moved out of the taxable estate in a single year.

What the Hong Kong Signal Really Means

There is a pattern in institutional capital formation that repeats across asset classes. First, a small number of early adopters build conviction and position. Then, a larger cohort of sophisticated institutions studies the data, builds the legal and custody infrastructure, and formalizes positions. Then, when a supermajority of that sophisticated cohort signals through their allocation decisions that the asset is permanent — that it's not going away, not a trade, not an experiment — the late majority follows.

The HKIMR data point this morning is the second phase becoming the third. Hong Kong family offices are not early adopters by temperament. They are multigenerational wealth preservation entities. Their 99% consensus is not a signal about short-term price. It is a signal about permanence.

For US Bitcoin-wealthy families, the lesson is not to envy Hong Kong's tax treatment. Frequently Asked Questions

Can a US citizen avoid Bitcoin capital gains taxes by moving to Hong Kong?

No. The United States taxes citizens and green card holders on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Moving to Hong Kong does not eliminate US tax obligations. The only way to sever US tax residency is to formally renounce US citizenship, which triggers an exit tax (IRC §877A) on unrealized gains above the exemption threshold. For most Bitcoin-wealthy families, renunciation is not practical or desirable. US-legal structures — Puerto Rico Act 60, dynasty trusts, charitable vehicles — are far more accessible paths to meaningful tax efficiency.

What is a Wyoming dynasty trust and why does it matter for Bitcoin families?

A Wyoming dynasty trust is an irrevocable trust that can last for up to 1,000 years (effectively perpetual), with no state income tax on trust income, no state estate tax, and robust creditor protection. Wyoming is also among the most Bitcoin-friendly states, with clear legislation recognizing digital assets. Funding a dynasty trust with Bitcoin — particularly at compressed valuations — allows appreciation to compound inside the trust, benefit multiple generations, and avoid repeated estate tax exposure. Paired with a qualified trustee in Wyoming, this structure is the closest US equivalent to the perpetual trust vehicles used by sophisticated family offices globally.

What is Puerto Rico Act 60 and how does it apply to Bitcoin gains?

Puerto Rico Act 60 (formerly Act 22) provides a 0% Puerto Rico tax rate on capital gains accrued after establishing bona fide residency in Puerto Rico. Because Puerto Rico is a US territory, residents are not subject to federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income. For Bitcoin holders who have not yet sold, relocating to Puerto Rico before a sale — and meeting the bona fide residency requirements (183+ days per year, home in Puerto Rico, social and economic connections) — can dramatically reduce capital gains tax on future appreciation. Gains accrued before moving remain subject to US tax. The IRS scrutinizes Puerto Rico residency claims heavily; proper tax and legal counsel is essential.

Why is 2026 a significant year for Bitcoin estate planning?

Several planning windows align in 2026. The One Big Beautiful Budget Act (OBBBA) as proposed would increase the federal estate and gift tax exemption to approximately $15 million per individual. Bitcoin is currently trading around $71,000 — approximately 44% below its all-time high — meaning trust structures can be funded at compressed valuations, with all future appreciation passing estate-tax-free. The annual gift exclusion, superfunding of 529s, and GRAT strategies all benefit from lower asset valuations at funding. Families who act in 2026 lock in today's compressed prices and amplified exemptions — a combination that may not recur.
