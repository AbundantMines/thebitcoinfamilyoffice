Why Bear Markets Are Estate Planning Golden Windows

Most people think of bear markets as a time to hunker down. Reduce exposure, sit on cash, wait for clarity. For estate planning purposes, the opposite is true. Bear markets are when the wealthiest families in America accelerate their wealth transfers — not because they're being reckless, but because the fundamental mechanics of estate planning reward action during downturns. The 2008 financial crisis, the 2020 COVID crash, and now the 2026 Bitcoin correction all share this trait: sophisticated families used depressed valuations to move billions in future appreciation out of their taxable estates while everyone else froze.

Estate planning is, at its core, a game of moving future appreciation out of your taxable estate before it happens. You transfer an asset today at its current fair market value. The IRS counts that transfer against your lifetime exemption (or annual exclusion) at today's price. Any appreciation that occurs after the transfer belongs to the recipient — your heirs, your trust, your family — completely outside your estate.

Now apply that principle to Bitcoin at $73,000 versus Bitcoin at $126,000:

  • Gift tax impact: Transferring 10 BTC at $73K uses $730,000 of your lifetime exemption. The same transfer at $126K would have used $1,260,000. You preserve $530,000 of exemption — enough to shelter significant additional assets.
  • Future appreciation captured: If Bitcoin returns to $150,000 per coin, those 10 BTC transferred at $73K represent $770,000 in appreciation that passes estate-tax-free. That's $308,000 in federal estate taxes your family never pays (at the 40% rate).
  • GRAT annuity requirements: The required annuity payment on a GRAT is based on the initial funding value. Lower funding value = lower annuity payments = more appreciation retained in the trust for heirs.
  • Trust funding efficiency: Under the current OBBBA exemption of approximately $15 million per person, you can transfer roughly 205 BTC at $73K versus only 119 BTC at $126K. That's 86 additional Bitcoin you can move out of your estate within the same exemption.

Every single one of these dynamics tilts in your favor when Bitcoin is depressed. The math is unambiguous: bear markets are when sophisticated families build generational wealth, not when they retreat to the sidelines.

GRAT Resets at Depressed Values: The Power Play

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is arguably the single most powerful tool for transferring appreciating assets to heirs with minimal or zero gift tax cost. And it is dramatically more effective when funded with depressed Bitcoin.

Here's the mechanics: You fund a GRAT with Bitcoin. The trust pays you an annuity stream over its term (typically 2 years for a "zeroed-out" GRAT). The annuity is calculated using the IRS Section 7520 rate so that the present value of the annuity payments roughly equals the value of assets you contributed — making the taxable gift near zero. Any appreciation above the 7520 hurdle rate passes to your heirs completely free of gift and estate tax.

Bitcoin's volatility makes GRATs extraordinarily powerful here. A 2-year GRAT funded at $73K per BTC only needs Bitcoin to outperform the 7520 rate (currently around 5.4%) to generate a tax-free wealth transfer. If Bitcoin recovers to even $100K over that period — let alone $150K or beyond — the excess appreciation flows to your heirs without touching your exemption.

Rolling GRATs: Compounding the Advantage

The real power move is rolling GRATs. As each 2-year GRAT matures, you roll the annuity proceeds into a new GRAT. Each successive GRAT captures the next leg of appreciation. If Bitcoin goes from $73K to $100K in the first GRAT, then $100K to $150K in the second, you've captured two separate waves of appreciation — all tax-free.

If a GRAT fails — meaning Bitcoin drops further and the annuity payments return all value to you — you've lost nothing except legal fees. The assets come back to you at their original value. You simply fund a new GRAT at the even lower price. This is why practitioners call GRATs a "heads I win, tails I break even" strategy. In a bear market, you're loading the coin.

Irrevocable Trust Funding at the Bottom

When you transfer Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust — whether a dynasty trust, a Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT), or an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) — the transfer uses your lifetime gift and estate tax exemption based on the fair market value at the time of the gift.

Under the OBBBA framework, the current exemption sits at approximately $15 million per individual ($30 million for a married couple). That exemption is measured in dollars, not in Bitcoin. Which means:

  • At $126,000/BTC, your $15M exemption covers approximately 119 BTC
  • At $73,000/BTC, your $15M exemption covers approximately 205 BTC

That's 86 additional Bitcoin you can move out of your taxable estate within the same exemption amount — simply because you're acting during the correction instead of at the peak.

Every dollar of appreciation that those 205 Bitcoin experience after the transfer is permanently outside your estate. If Bitcoin returns to $150K, those coins are worth $30.75 million — but only $15 million counted against your exemption. The other $15.75 million transferred tax-free.

If you had waited for Bitcoin to recover to $150K before funding the trust, you could only move 100 BTC within the same exemption. The additional 105 Bitcoin — and all their future appreciation — would remain in your estate, subject to the 40% federal estate tax.

Dynasty Trust: Multigenerational Tax-Free Growth

A dynasty trust funded with Bitcoin at $73K is particularly compelling. These trusts are designed to last for multiple generations (or perpetually, depending on state law). Bitcoin transferred now at depressed prices grows inside the trust for decades — potentially centuries — completely outside any family member's taxable estate.

Consider the math over three generations: 100 BTC transferred at $73K into a dynasty trust. If Bitcoin reaches $500K in 20 years and $2M in 50 years, those 100 BTC grow from $7.3M to $200M — all sheltered from estate tax at every generational transfer. Without the trust, your family would face a 40% estate tax at each generation, potentially reducing the inheritance by more than half over three generations.

SLAT Funding: Access Without Estate Inclusion

For families who want to move Bitcoin out of their estate but maintain some access, a SLAT funded during the bear market offers the best of both worlds. One spouse creates the trust for the benefit of the other, using their exemption to transfer Bitcoin at $73K. The beneficiary spouse can access the trust assets, effectively giving the family access to the Bitcoin while permanently removing it from both estates.

The critical detail: the SLAT uses the grantor's exemption at the transfer date value. Funding now at $73K preserves significantly more exemption than waiting for a recovery.

Annual Exclusion Gifts: Small Moves, Massive Upside

The annual gift tax exclusion for 2026 is $19,000 per recipient ($38,000 for a married couple using gift-splitting). These gifts don't count against your lifetime exemption at all. And in a bear market, they're quietly one of the most powerful tools available.

At $73,000 per Bitcoin, a $19,000 gift equals approximately 0.26 BTC. If Bitcoin recovers to $200,000, that 0.26 BTC is worth $52,000 — but only $19,000 was counted as a gift. The other $33,000 in appreciation? Free. No gift tax. No estate tax. No reporting beyond the initial gift.

Key execution detail: gift the actual Bitcoin, not cash. When you gift BTC in-kind, the recipient takes your cost basis. But the gift is valued — for gift tax purposes — at the current FMV. By gifting the depressed BTC itself, you lock in the low FMV for the gift while transferring an asset with massive upside potential. These annual exclusion gifts can be made every single year. A family with 6 recipients making $38,000 gifts each year for 5 years during a prolonged bear market can transfer $1.14 million of Bitcoin — with potentially multiple millions in tax-free appreciation — without using a single dollar of lifetime exemption.

Installment Sale to an IDGT: Locking In the Bottom

An installment sale to an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) is one of the most sophisticated estate planning techniques available — and the bear market makes it extraordinarily powerful.

Here's how it works: You sell Bitcoin to an IDGT at fair market value. The trust gives you a promissory note, paying you back over time with interest (at the Applicable Federal Rate, which is low). Because the trust is a "grantor trust" for income tax purposes, the sale is ignored for income tax — no capital gain is triggered. But for estate tax purposes, the Bitcoin is now owned by the trust.

The magic: all appreciation above the sale price belongs to the trust (and ultimately your heirs) forever. You've "frozen" the value of the Bitcoin in your estate at the sale price.

At $73K per Bitcoin, you're locking in the sale at the bottom. If you sell 50 BTC to the IDGT at $73K, the trust owes you $3.65 million (plus AFR interest). If Bitcoin recovers to $150K, those 50 BTC are worth $7.5 million inside the trust. The $3.85 million in appreciation belongs to your heirs — estate-tax-free.

Had you waited and executed the same sale at $150K, the trust would owe you $7.5 million, and your heirs would capture zero appreciation. The bear market is the entire mechanism that makes this work.

Seed the Trust First

An IDGT installment sale typically requires "seeding" the trust with a gift equal to roughly 10% of the sale price, giving the trust enough equity for the note to be respected as legitimate debt. At $73K BTC, this seed gift is smaller: $365,000 for a $3.65M sale versus $750,000 for a $7.5M sale at $150K. Less exemption used, more Bitcoin transferred, more appreciation captured.

The Roth IRA Conversion Window

If you hold Bitcoin in a traditional IRA or self-directed IRA, the current correction creates a Roth conversion opportunity that may not come around again.

When you convert a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA, you pay ordinary income tax on the fair market value of the assets at the time of conversion. After conversion, all future growth in the Roth is tax-free — forever. No required minimum distributions. No income tax on withdrawals. No estate tax complications for your heirs (Roth IRAs are still included in the estate for estate tax purposes, but the income-tax-free nature makes them far more valuable to beneficiaries).

The conversion math at $73K versus $126K is stark:

You save nearly $200,000 in conversion taxes by acting now instead of at the peak — and you're converting the exact same 10 Bitcoin. Every dollar of recovery above $73K grows tax-free inside the Roth forever.

The conversion tax is 42% cheaper per coin at $73K compared to $126K. For families with significant Bitcoin IRA holdings, this is one of the highest-ROI financial decisions available right now.

Partial Conversions for Tax Bracket Management

You don't have to convert everything at once. Many families execute partial conversions — converting enough Bitcoin each year to fill up their current tax bracket without pushing into a higher one. In a bear market, the same dollar amount of tax bracket space converts more Bitcoin. If you have room for $500,000 in additional ordinary income, that converts 6.85 BTC at $73K versus only 3.97 BTC at $126K.

Tax-Loss Harvesting: The Bear Market Bonus

If you purchased Bitcoin above $73,000 — whether at the $126K peak or anywhere in between — the current correction hands you a tax-loss harvesting opportunity with a feature unique to cryptocurrency: the wash sale rule does not apply to Bitcoin.

In traditional securities, selling an asset at a loss and repurchasing it within 30 days triggers the wash sale rule, disallowing the loss deduction. Bitcoin, as property (not a security) under current IRS guidance, is not subject to this restriction. You can sell BTC at a loss and immediately repurchase it — harvesting the tax loss while maintaining your exact same position.

The practical impact: if you bought 5 BTC at $110,000 in late 2025, those coins have an unrealized loss of $185,000. Sell them, book the loss, and repurchase immediately. You now hold the same 5 BTC with a new cost basis of $73,000 — and you have $185,000 in capital losses to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio or carry forward ($3,000 per year against ordinary income, unlimited against future capital gains).

For families with significant taxable investment portfolios, these harvested losses are worth real money. A $185,000 capital loss offsets $185,000 in capital gains that would otherwise be taxed at 20% (plus 3.8% NIIT) — a tax savings of $44,030.

Combine Tax-Loss Harvesting With Estate Planning

Here's an advanced move: harvest your losses on personally held Bitcoin, then use separate Bitcoin (from other wallets, at different cost basis, or newly purchased) to fund your estate planning vehicles. You capture the tax benefit of the loss while simultaneously executing your GRAT, trust funding, or gifting strategy with maximally depressed Bitcoin.

Charitable Planning During Corrections

Charitable planning with Bitcoin during a bear market offers a set of advantages that are often overlooked. The key principle: when you contribute appreciated Bitcoin to a charity or charitable vehicle, you receive a deduction based on the fair market value at the time of contribution, and neither you nor the charity pays capital gains tax on the appreciation.

Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Contributions

Contributing depressed Bitcoin to a DAF may seem counterintuitive — why not wait until it recovers to get a bigger deduction? There are scenarios where the bear market timing actually works better:

  • If you have Bitcoin with a cost basis above the current price (a loss position), donating it directly is suboptimal — you're better off selling, harvesting the loss, and donating cash. This is a bear market nuance many advisors miss.
  • If you have Bitcoin with a very low cost basis (say, $5,000), contributing it now still generates a $73K deduction per coin while avoiding a $68K taxable gain. The deduction is lower than at $126K, but so is the opportunity cost of contributing.

Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

A CRT funded with depressed Bitcoin is a different calculus — and potentially very powerful. You contribute Bitcoin to the CRT, receive a partial charitable deduction, and the CRT pays you (or your designated beneficiaries) an income stream for life or a term of years. The remainder goes to charity.

Inside the CRT, the Bitcoin can be sold with no immediate capital gains tax. This means the CRT can sell the BTC, diversify into other investments, and pay you income from the full pre-tax amount. If you contributed 10 BTC at $73K and Bitcoin recovers to $150K inside the CRT before the trustee sells, the CRT captures the full $1.5M — not the $730K at funding — and uses that entire amount to generate your income stream.

The bear market entry point means: (1) you receive a charitable deduction now, (2) the Bitcoin potentially doubles or triples inside the CRT before being sold, and (3) your income stream is based on the recovered value, not the depressed contribution value.

What NOT to Do in a Bear Market

Bear markets create opportunity, but they also create emotional pressure that leads to costly mistakes. Avoid these:

Don't Sell Bitcoin to Pay Estate Planning Fees

Attorney fees, trust drafting costs, appraisal fees — pay them in cash. Selling BTC to cover $20,000 in legal fees means selling 0.27 BTC at the bottom. If that Bitcoin recovers to $200K, you've effectively paid $54,000 for a $20,000 service. Fund professional fees from cash reserves, always.

Don't Crystallize Capital Gains to Fund a Trust

Transfer Bitcoin in-kind to your trust or GRAT. Selling Bitcoin, paying capital gains tax, and then funding a trust with cash is a triple penalty: you pay tax on the sale, you transfer a non-appreciating asset (cash), and you lose the upside of the BTC itself growing inside the trust. In-kind transfers avoid the gain entirely (for grantor trusts) and preserve Bitcoin's appreciation potential inside the vehicle.

Don't Design an Estate Plan Around Current Prices

Your estate plan should function at any Bitcoin price — $30K, $73K, $200K, or $1M. The current correction is an opportunity to execute within an existing plan, not to create a plan that only works if Bitcoin is cheap. If your plan breaks at higher prices, it's a bad plan.

Don't Make Panic-Driven Decisions

Estate planning is a long game. The urgency here is real — the window of depressed prices is genuinely valuable — but the decisions themselves should be deliberate and well-structured. Don't rush into a GRAT without proper legal counsel. Don't fund an irrevocable trust without understanding you can't take the Bitcoin back. The urgency is in timing the execution, not in cutting corners on the structure.

Don't Ignore Basis Documentation

Every transfer during a bear market should be accompanied by meticulous documentation: the date, the fair market value at the time, the number of coins, the wallet addresses, the cost basis of the transferred coins. Bear market transactions will be scrutinized by the IRS years from now when the appreciation becomes visible. Document everything.

What we know is this: Bitcoin has never failed to make a new all-time high after a significant correction. It has always recovered. The question is not whether the window will close, but when. Families who combine depressed Bitcoin prices with elevated exemption amounts are taking advantage of a rare double window. If exemptions are cut and Bitcoin recovers simultaneously, the opportunity disappears from both directions at once.
