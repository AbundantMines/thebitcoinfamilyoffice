Bitcoin IRA Estate Planning: What Happens to Your Bitcoin IRA When You Die

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  11 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin IRA Estate Planning Est. 11 min read

Bitcoin inside a self-directed IRA is not the same as Bitcoin you hold in self-custody. The difference seems technical — it is, in fact, profound — and nowhere does it matter more than at death. The IRA wrapper that sheltered your Bitcoin from taxes during your lifetime becomes a complex inheritance problem for whoever you leave behind. Understanding how Bitcoin IRA estate planning works, and where the traps lie, can mean the difference between a tax-efficient transfer of generational wealth and a distribution event that hands a significant portion of your Bitcoin's gains directly to the IRS.

Your Bitcoin IRA Is Not Self-Custody

The term "Bitcoin IRA" is widely used but frequently misunderstood. When you open a self-directed IRA (SDIRA) through a custodian like Kingdom Trust, BitcoinIRA, Alto, or similar platforms, you are not holding Bitcoin in the conventional sense. The IRA custodian holds the Bitcoin on your behalf — they are the legal owner of the account assets. You control the investment decisions, but you do not control the private keys.

This distinction matters in three important ways. First, in the event of a custodian failure, your recourse is as a creditor — not as a sovereign holder of a cryptographic asset. Second, the Bitcoin cannot be transacted, moved, or secured by you in the way that self-custodied Bitcoin can be passed through a hardware wallet or a multisig arrangement. Third, and most critically for estate planning purposes, the Bitcoin passes at death through the IRA beneficiary framework — governed by federal law, IRS rules, and the custodian's own procedures — rather than through any private key transfer mechanism.

Self-custodied Bitcoin passes differently. It is property. It can be held in a trust, transferred via an instruction letter to a trusted heir, or moved through a multisig scheme where heirs receive key shares. It benefits from a stepped-up cost basis at death. Your IRA Bitcoin does none of these things.

IRA Bitcoin vs. Self-Custodied Bitcoin at Death

  • IRA Bitcoin: Governed by IRS inherited IRA rules, SECURE Act 2.0, and custodian procedures
  • IRA Bitcoin: No step-up in basis — all distributions taxed as ordinary income (traditional IRA)
  • IRA Bitcoin: Custodian controls the private keys; private key transfer is not available to heirs
  • IRA Bitcoin: Subject to Required Minimum Distributions for both owner and most heirs
  • Self-Custody Bitcoin: Passes as property; eligible for step-up in cost basis at death
  • Self-Custody Bitcoin: Can be held in trust, multisig, or transferred via key instructions
  • Self-Custody Bitcoin: Capital gains accumulated during lifetime are extinguished at death

RMDs, the SECURE Act 2.0, and the 10-Year Rule for Inherited Bitcoin IRAs

During your lifetime, a traditional IRA — including a Bitcoin SDIRA — requires you to begin taking Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) at age 73 under current law. SECURE Act 2.0 increased this threshold from 72 to 73 beginning in 2023, with a further increase to 75 scheduled for those born in 1960 or later. Each year, you must withdraw a minimum amount calculated by dividing your IRA balance by an IRS life expectancy factor. For a Bitcoin IRA, this creates a unique problem: if Bitcoin's price is down in December, you are still required to liquidate and distribute a calculated amount, potentially selling at the worst possible time.

The situation becomes more constrained for your heirs. Before the original SECURE Act of 2019, non-spouse beneficiaries could take distributions from an inherited IRA over their own life expectancy — the so-called "stretch IRA." A 40-year-old heir could spread taxable distributions over several decades, allowing the account to continue compounding and dramatically reducing the annual tax burden. That strategy is gone for most heirs.

Under SECURE Act 2.0, non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit an IRA from someone who had already reached their RMD age must now empty the account within 10 years — and take annual RMDs during those 10 years based on their own life expectancy. If the original owner had not yet reached RMD age, the 10-year rule still applies, but no annual distributions are required within that window. Either way, the result is the same: your heirs must fully liquidate the inherited Bitcoin IRA within a decade of your death. Every dollar distributed from a traditional inherited IRA is taxed as ordinary income in the year it is received.

The SECURE Act 2.0 compression risk: A non-spouse heir who inherits a $2 million Bitcoin IRA must fully distribute it within 10 years. If Bitcoin continues to appreciate during that period, the heir could find themselves forced to distribute — and pay ordinary income tax on — a significantly larger balance than existed at the time of inheritance. There is no flexibility to time distributions around market cycles once RMD obligations are triggered.

Surviving spouses are treated differently and more favorably. A spouse who inherits an IRA can roll it over into their own IRA, defer distributions to their own RMD age, and in some cases elect to treat the inherited IRA as their own. This is the only category of beneficiary for whom the old "stretch" logic still applies in meaningful form.

How to Designate Beneficiaries for a Bitcoin SDIRA

One of the most overlooked aspects of Bitcoin IRA estate planning is the beneficiary designation itself. Unlike a will or trust, your IRA passes by beneficiary designation — outside of probate and outside the reach of your estate plan documents, unless you specifically direct it that way. The designation on file with your IRA custodian controls, regardless of what your will says.

Primary and Contingent Beneficiaries

You should name both a primary beneficiary (who receives the account if they survive you) and one or more contingent beneficiaries (who receive the account if the primary does not survive you). Many IRA holders have outdated designations — naming an ex-spouse, a deceased parent, or no beneficiary at all. Failing to name a living beneficiary typically means the IRA passes to your estate, triggering probate and the worst possible distribution schedule.

Trusts as IRA Beneficiaries

Naming a trust as the IRA beneficiary is possible, but the mechanics are complex and the rules are strict. The IRS distinguishes between two complete guide to Bitcoin trust types for this purpose.

A conduit trust requires that any RMD or distribution received by the trust be immediately passed through to the trust beneficiaries. This "look-through" structure allows the IRS to treat the trust's individual beneficiaries as the designated beneficiaries for distribution timing purposes. An accumulation trust allows the trustee to retain distributions inside the trust rather than passing them through immediately. This offers greater asset protection and control — particularly useful if a beneficiary has creditor issues or lacks financial discipline — but it does not qualify for look-through treatment unless specific requirements are met, and the distribution timing rules that apply are based on the oldest trust beneficiary's life expectancy, which may compress the payout window significantly.

Never name your estate as IRA beneficiary. When no named individual beneficiary survives you, your IRA defaults to your estate. The account loses all stretch provisions and must be fully distributed within five years (if you died before your RMD start date) or within the remaining years on your life expectancy table. Probate also applies, creating delay, cost, and public exposure. This is one of the most costly and easily avoided mistakes in IRA estate planning.

Estate Tax Implications: The Double Taxation Problem

For high-net-worth Bitcoin IRA holders, there is a structural problem that no amount of careful beneficiary designation can fully resolve: traditional IRAs are among the most tax-inefficient assets to pass to non-spouse heirs.

Your IRA is included in your taxable estate at fair market value for federal estate tax purposes. If your estate exceeds the applicable exemption — currently approximately $13.99 million per individual under 2026 rules, though subject to legislative change — the IRA balance is subject to estate tax at the top marginal rate of 40%.

Then, on top of the estate tax, every dollar distributed from the traditional IRA to your heirs is subject to ordinary income tax. Unlike other inherited assets, IRAs carry no step-up in basis. A traditional IRA that was worth $1 million when you died — and contributed 40% to estate taxes — still generates 37% ordinary income tax on each dollar your heir withdraws. The effective combined rate in a worst-case scenario can exceed 60% of the original balance, leaving your heir with less than 40 cents on the dollar.

The traditional IRA, under the right conditions of estate size and beneficiary income, can deliver the worst after-tax outcome of any asset class you could pass to your heirs — including cash.

Strategic Responses to the Double Taxation Problem

Several planning strategies address this structural disadvantage. The most important:

  • Spend down IRA assets first during retirement. Use your traditional IRA distributions for living expenses, leaving non-IRA assets — including self-custodied Bitcoin — to pass to heirs with step-up treatment.
  • Hold long-term Bitcoin positions in self-custody, not an IRA. Self-custodied Bitcoin benefits from the step-up in basis at death; all capital gains accumulated during your lifetime are extinguished for your heirs.
  • Consider Roth conversions during lower-income years. Converting traditional IRA Bitcoin to a Roth IRA triggers income tax now, but eliminates all future taxation on distributions — including eliminating the ordinary income tax burden for heirs.
  • Use IRA assets for charitable giving. A Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) allows IRA holders over 70½ to direct up to $105,000 per year (indexed) from a traditional IRA directly to a qualified charity, satisfying RMD obligations without the funds entering your taxable income. Charities receive IRA assets tax-free. This effectively removes the most tax-burdened assets from your estate in a tax-efficient manner.

Roth IRA vs. Traditional IRA for Bitcoin: The Conversion Case

The Roth IRA is structurally superior for Bitcoin estate planning in almost every respect. The core differences are significant.

A Roth IRA has no Required Minimum Distributions during the account owner's lifetime. You are never forced to sell Bitcoin because of your age. A traditional IRA forces liquidations beginning at 73 regardless of market conditions or your desire to hold. For an asset with Bitcoin's volatility, this distinction is not trivial.

For heirs, the difference is even more pronounced. Qualified distributions from an inherited Roth IRA are income-tax-free. The 10-year rule still applies to non-spouse beneficiaries under SECURE Act 2.0, but when the account must be fully distributed, every dollar received is tax-free. By contrast, every dollar from an inherited traditional IRA is taxed as ordinary income — at rates that may be significantly higher than long-term capital gains rates the heir might pay on other inherited assets.

Inside a traditional IRA, Bitcoin's appreciation is ultimately subject to ordinary income tax rates — not the 15% or 20% long-term capital gains rate that would apply to self-custodied Bitcoin held for more than one year. This is perhaps the most underappreciated cost of the traditional IRA wrapper for a high-appreciation asset. A Bitcoin position that appreciated 10x in a traditional IRA does not benefit from preferential capital gains treatment; every dollar of gain is ordinary income when distributed.

The Roth Conversion Strategy

A Roth conversion converts some or all of your traditional IRA balance to a Roth IRA. You pay ordinary income tax on the converted amount in the year of conversion, but all future growth and all qualified distributions — for you and your heirs — are permanently tax-free. For Bitcoin holders who believe in continued long-term appreciation, paying tax on today's value to eliminate tax on a much larger future value is a straightforward mathematical exercise.

The optimal conversion strategy spreads conversions across multiple years to avoid pushing income into the highest marginal brackets. Years of lower ordinary income — early retirement before Social Security begins, years when Bitcoin has declined in price relative to prior years — can be particularly efficient conversion windows. Work with a CPA who understands both Bitcoin's valuation and the interaction between Roth conversions, Medicare IRMAA thresholds, and estate tax exposure.

Roth IRA vs. Traditional IRA for Bitcoin Heirs

  • Roth: No RMDs during owner's lifetime — never forced to sell Bitcoin
  • Roth: Qualified distributions to heirs are income-tax-free
  • Roth: 10-year distribution rule applies, but distributions create no tax event
  • Roth: Bitcoin appreciation escapes all ordinary income taxation
  • Traditional: RMDs begin at age 73, forcing liquidation regardless of price
  • Traditional: Every heir distribution taxed as ordinary income
  • Traditional: Bitcoin gains taxed at 37% (top rate) rather than 20% capital gains rate
  • Traditional: Combined estate + income tax can exceed 60% of balance

The Self-Custody Bitcoin Advantage in Estate Planning

The step-up in basis is one of the most powerful tools in American estate law, and it is completely unavailable to IRA assets. When you hold Bitcoin directly — in self-custody, in a revocable trust, in a family LLC — and you die, your heirs inherit that Bitcoin with a cost basis equal to its fair market value on your date of death. All the capital gains accumulated during your lifetime are permanently erased.

A Bitcoin position purchased for $10,000 that is worth $1 million at your death carries zero capital gains exposure for your heir — regardless of whether they sell immediately or hold for years. The only tax trigger is if the asset appreciates further after your death. The IRS effectively forgives decades of unrealized gain in a single step-up event.

IRA Bitcoin receives no such treatment. Your Bitcoin inside a traditional IRA was purchased with pre-tax dollars and has grown tax-deferred. Every dollar of that growth — and every dollar of the original principal — is ordinary income when distributed. There is no step-up, no capital gains rate, no forgiveness of appreciation. The IRA wrapper deferred taxation; it did not eliminate it.

For Bitcoin positions with very high appreciation, the long-term after-tax outcome of self-custody plus a dynasty trust often materially exceeds the outcome of an IRA, even accounting for the tax benefits received during the accumulation phase. The IRA provides a deduction or tax-free contribution upfront; self-custody provides step-up treatment at death. For an asset that may appreciate 10x, 50x, or 100x over a lifetime, the back-end benefit of the step-up frequently outweighs the front-end benefit of the IRA deduction.

Practical Checklist for Bitcoin IRA Holders

If you hold Bitcoin inside a self-directed IRA — or are considering opening one — the following steps reflect the minimum planning required to ensure your IRA does not become an unintended tax trap for your heirs.

Bitcoin IRA Estate Planning Checklist

  • Confirm your current beneficiary designations with your IRA custodian — primary and contingent — and update them after any major life event (marriage, divorce, death of a named beneficiary, birth of a child)
  • Never leave the beneficiary designation blank or name your estate as beneficiary — if you have no named beneficiary, fix this immediately
  • Understand your RMD start age (73 under current law for those born 1951–1959; 75 for those born 1960 or later) and model how Bitcoin price volatility interacts with your annual distribution obligation
  • Evaluate Roth conversion feasibility with a CPA familiar with Bitcoin — identify low-income years where conversions can be executed at lower marginal rates
  • If naming a trust as IRA beneficiary, confirm with an estate attorney whether a conduit trust or accumulation trust is appropriate for your specific beneficiary circumstances
  • Inform your estate attorney that your Bitcoin IRA is a separate asset class from any self-custodied Bitcoin you hold — they pass through different legal channels and require different planning
  • For large traditional IRA positions in a taxable estate, model the combined estate tax and income tax exposure and compare it against the after-tax outcome of Roth conversion or strategic spend-down during your lifetime
  • Review whether any portion of your Bitcoin holdings should be moved to self-custody to capture step-up treatment rather than being held indefinitely in an IRA wrapper
  • Revisit your IRA beneficiary designations at least annually — custodian records control, not your will
