Bitcoin Estate Planning in Kentucky

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Kentucky Est. 10 min read

Kentucky is one of only six states in the country that still imposes an inheritance tax — a transfer tax assessed based on the relationship between the decedent and the beneficiary, not the overall size of the estate. For Bitcoin families domiciled in Kentucky, the inheritance tax is a meaningful planning constraint that must be addressed in every estate plan. Transfers of Bitcoin to siblings, nieces, nephews, friends, or more distant relatives face Kentucky inheritance tax rates from 4% to 16%, with limited exemptions. Without deliberate planning, Kentucky can claim a significant portion of Bitcoin transferred to beneficiaries outside the immediate family circle.

This guide covers Kentucky's inheritance tax in full detail, the trust and LLC structures available to Kentucky Bitcoin families, the state's Uniform Trust Code provisions, and the practical strategies for protecting Bitcoin wealth from Kentucky's transfer tax while ensuring successful multi-generational succession. Bitcoin estate planning Kentucky requires advisors who understand both the inheritance tax's beneficiary-class mechanics and the technical realities of Bitcoin custody.

Kentucky's Inheritance Tax: How It Works for Bitcoin Families

Kentucky's inheritance tax is governed by KRS §§ 140.010 et seq. Unlike an estate tax — which is assessed on the total estate regardless of beneficiary — the inheritance tax is assessed on each individual beneficiary's share, with rates and exemptions depending on the beneficiary's relationship to the decedent. Kentucky uses a class system:

  • Class A beneficiaries (surviving spouse, parent, child, grandchild, brother, sister, and half-siblings): Exempt — no Kentucky inheritance tax. Note that Kentucky's Class A is somewhat broader than other states' equivalent categories, including siblings.
  • Class B beneficiaries (niece, nephew, daughter-in-law, son-in-law, aunt, uncle, great-grandchild): Taxed at 4–16% on amounts above a $1,000 exemption. The rate structure is progressive: 4% on the first $10,000 above the exemption, rising to 16% on amounts above $200,000.
  • Class C beneficiaries (all other persons — friends, business partners, more distant relatives): Taxed at 6–16% on amounts above a $500 exemption. The rate structure is 6% on the first $10,000 above the exemption, rising to 16% on amounts above $200,000.
Kentucky's inheritance tax has no minimum estate size threshold. A Bitcoin transfer of any amount to a niece, nephew, close friend, or business associate triggers Kentucky inheritance tax — making beneficiary mapping the first critical step in every Kentucky Bitcoin estate plan.

Why Kentucky's Inheritance Tax Is Particularly Consequential for Bitcoin Families

Bitcoin families often have complex beneficiary landscapes that extend well beyond the immediate family. Consider these scenarios that commonly arise:

Long-term partners who are not spouses: Unmarried partners who are not legally recognized under Kentucky's marriage laws are Class C beneficiaries. A Bitcoin holder in a long-term relationship who has not married faces a 6–16% inheritance tax on any Bitcoin transferred to that partner at death — on every dollar above $500. This is a powerful incentive to marry, establish proper trust structures, or make lifetime transfers to remove the inheritance tax exposure.

Nieces and nephews: Bitcoin holders who want to benefit siblings' children face Class B inheritance tax rates of 4–16% on amounts above $1,000. For families with significant Bitcoin positions and niece/nephew beneficiaries, the inheritance tax exposure can be substantial — a $500,000 Bitcoin transfer to a niece could generate $65,000 or more in Kentucky inheritance tax.

Business partners and key employees: Cryptocurrency entrepreneurs and Bitcoin-native business owners who want to transfer Bitcoin to business partners or key employees at death face Class C rates — 6–16% from the first dollar above $500.

Charitable remainder planning: Transfers to qualified charities are exempt from Kentucky's inheritance tax, creating opportunities to integrate charitable giving into the estate plan while eliminating the inheritance tax on that portion of the estate.

Trust Structures for Kentucky Bitcoin Families

Kentucky enacted the Kentucky Uniform Trust Code (KUTC), codified at KRS §§ 386B.1-010 et seq., effective July 15, 2014. The KUTC provides modern statutory authority for trust formation that supports the structures Kentucky Bitcoin families need:

  • Irrevocable lifetime trusts to avoid inheritance tax: The most direct way to avoid Kentucky's inheritance tax is to transfer Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust during the grantor's lifetime. Assets properly transferred to an irrevocable trust — with the grantor retaining no "string" that would cause estate inclusion — are not subject to the inheritance tax at the grantor's death because the grantor does not own them at death. Lifetime transfers to irrevocable trusts for Class B and Class C beneficiaries are the primary inheritance tax avoidance strategy available.
  • Spousal trusts: Bitcoin transferred to a trust for a surviving spouse (a Class A beneficiary) is inheritance-tax-exempt. Spousal trusts — whether outright marital trusts or credit shelter/bypass trusts — allow the full Bitcoin position to pass to the surviving spouse without Kentucky inheritance tax while preserving planning flexibility for the surviving spouse's estate.
  • Dynasty trusts: The KUTC supports multigenerational trust planning in Kentucky. While Kentucky's rule against perpetuities limits trust duration for real property, personal property trusts — including Bitcoin trusts — can be structured for very long periods, enabling multi-generational accumulation and distribution without triggering an inheritance tax event at each generation (provided the trust structure is properly designed).
  • Trust protectors: The KUTC expressly recognizes trust protector roles, allowing named individuals to hold specified powers — adapting custody arrangements, replacing trustees, modifying investment direction — without full trustee fiduciary duties.

Kentucky LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Kentucky LLCs (governed by the Kentucky Limited Liability Company Act, KRS §§ 275.001 et seq.) provide effective Bitcoin holding structures with inheritance tax planning advantages:

Lifetime gift strategy: LLC membership interests transferred during lifetime remove those interests from the inheritance tax calculation at death. A Kentucky Bitcoin holder who systematically gifts LLC interests to Class B or Class C beneficiaries during life — using the federal annual exclusion of $18,000 per recipient per year — removes that value from the inheritance tax base without triggering current tax. The LLC structure allows discounted gifting: a $100,000 LLC interest with a 25% discount has a fair market value of $75,000 for gift tax purposes, allowing more value to be transferred per exclusion dollar.

Operating agreement governance: The LLC operating agreement governs Bitcoin custody architecture, key-person succession, transaction authorization, and membership transfer restrictions — creating the legal governance framework for Bitcoin within the LLC structure.

Charging order protection: Kentucky LLC law provides charging order protection — creditors of a member cannot seize Bitcoin held by the LLC but are limited to a charging order against future distributions.

Wyoming Trust Situs for Kentucky Families

Kentucky families can access Wyoming's directed trust statute, perpetual dynasty trust provisions, and Bitcoin-specific legal infrastructure by establishing a Wyoming-sited trust — with a Wyoming corporate trustee — while maintaining Kentucky residency. For Kentucky inheritance tax purposes, the critical variable is whether Bitcoin is includible in the decedent's taxable estate at death. Bitcoin properly transferred to a Wyoming-sited irrevocable trust during the grantor's lifetime — with no retained interest causing estate inclusion — is not subject to Kentucky inheritance tax at the grantor's death, regardless of the trust's situs. Digital Asset Succession in Kentucky

Kentucky enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), codified at KRS §§ 395.417 et seq. Under Kentucky's RUFADAA, authorized fiduciaries have legal authority to access and manage digital assets — including Bitcoin — if the governing estate planning documents explicitly grant that authority. The three-tier priority structure applies: platform online tool first; governing documents second; platform terms of service third.

Every Kentucky Bitcoin estate plan should include explicit RUFADAA digital asset access authority in all governing instruments — trust agreements, durable powers of attorney, and wills — accompanied by a separate technical access document (not containing private keys, but containing the information needed to locate and access custody systems) maintained and updated separately. The legal authority created by RUFADAA is meaningless without the actual technical access credentials.

Practical Priorities for Kentucky Bitcoin Families

  1. Map all intended Bitcoin beneficiaries by class — identify every intended recipient and classify them as Class A (exempt), Class B (4–16%), or Class C (6–16%). This beneficiary map drives every downstream planning decision.
  2. Transfer Bitcoin to irrevocable trust structures during lifetime for any Class B or Class C beneficiaries — lifetime transfers remove Bitcoin from the inheritance tax calculation at death. The earlier this happens, the greater the compound benefit of removing Bitcoin's appreciation from the taxable base.
  3. Establish a Kentucky LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle — with annual exclusion gifting of discounted LLC interests to trusts for Class B and C beneficiaries, systematically reducing the inheritance tax exposure over time.
  4. Structure spousal trust for Class A transfer first — if the surviving spouse is the primary intended recipient, ensure the Bitcoin passes to a properly structured marital trust before any inheritance-tax-exposed beneficiaries receive assets.
  5. Incorporate RUFADAA digital asset authority in all governing instruments — Kentucky's RUFADAA creates the legal framework, but every document must explicitly invoke it.
