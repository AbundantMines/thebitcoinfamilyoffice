Bitcoin in an LLC: Estate Planning, Charging Order Protection, and Succession

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  11 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin in an LLC: Estate Planning & Charging Order Protection Est. 11 min read

The LLC has become the default holding structure for serious Bitcoin families — and for good reason. Properly structured, an LLC provides charging order protection against creditors, a layer of privacy between you and your Bitcoin holdings in public records, and a governance framework that can specify exactly who controls custody when circumstances change. But an LLC is often misunderstood as a cure for problems it cannot fix. The most important of these: an LLC does not solve the seed phrase problem. It provides a legal wrapper; it does not store or protect private keys.

This guide covers the full landscape of bitcoin LLC estate planning: why holders use LLCs, how custody actually works inside an LLC, which state to choose, how to combine an LLC with a Domestic Asset Protection Trust for maximum protection, how to use LLC interests for estate planning and valuation discounts, and critically — when an LLC is not the right tool at all.

Why People Hold Bitcoin in an LLC

Charging Order Protection

The core asset protection feature of an LLC is the charging order. When a creditor obtains a judgment against you personally — from a lawsuit, a business liability, or personal debt — they typically cannot seize LLC assets to satisfy that judgment. In most states, a creditor's only remedy against an LLC member's interest is a charging order: a court order directing that any future distributions from the LLC be paid to the creditor rather than to you.

This is materially different from direct ownership of Bitcoin. If you own Bitcoin personally and a creditor obtains a judgment against you, they can pursue your assets directly — including, in principle, your Bitcoin. If your Bitcoin is owned by an LLC and you are a member, the creditor is relegated to waiting for distributions — distributions you control as the manager. You can simply not make distributions. In many structures, the creditor's charging order position is so inconvenient that it creates negotiating leverage to settle at a fraction of the judgment.

The strength of this protection varies significantly by state, as discussed below. In Bitcoin family office in Wyoming, charging order protection for single-member LLCs is statutory and robust. In other states, courts have occasionally allowed creditors to pierce through to LLC assets, particularly in single-member structures where the charging order remedy is effectively meaningless if the member is the sole beneficiary of any distributions.

Privacy

When you own Bitcoin through an LLC, the LLC — not you — appears in any transaction or record that references the entity. Exchange accounts can be opened in the LLC's name. On-chain activity is associated with the LLC. This does not provide cryptographic anonymity, but it provides a meaningful layer of separation between your personal identity and your Bitcoin holdings in public-facing records. For high-net-worth individuals who prefer not to publicly advertise their Bitcoin wealth, this structural privacy has real value.

Wyoming allows anonymous membership — the state does not require member names to appear in public filings. Nevada offers similar provisions. Delaware does not require member disclosure in its certificate of formation, though registered agent records may create a paper trail depending on the structure.

Succession Planning and Management Structure

An LLC's operating agreement is a private contract that can specify exactly what happens to the LLC — and to the Bitcoin it holds — at every contingency. Who is the successor manager if you are incapacitated? Who votes on Bitcoin custody decisions? What multi-signature threshold is required to authorize a transfer? What happens to the LLC on your death? None of these questions need to be resolved by a probate court if the operating agreement answers them clearly.

A multi-member LLC creates a governance structure for Bitcoin decisions. Two or more managers can be required to authorize any transaction, mimicking at the legal level what multi-signature wallet structures accomplish at the technical level. A successor manager provision ensures that someone you have designated — and who has been briefed on custody protocols — steps into the management role automatically when you cannot serve.

The LLC is a legal wrapper, not a custody solution. It changes who owns the Bitcoin in law. It does not change who can sign for the Bitcoin in practice.

How Bitcoin Custody Works Inside an LLC

Understanding what an LLC does and does not do for Bitcoin custody is essential before committing to this structure. The LLC is the legal owner of the Bitcoin. On exchange accounts opened in the LLC's name, the entity name appears on the account. On-chain, the private keys controlling the wallet could be held by any person or device — the blockchain does not know or care about LLC formation documents.

In practice, a hardware wallet is in the physical possession of a person — the member, the manager, or a designated custodian. That person controls the private keys. The LLC operating agreement can specify who has custody authority, what threshold of signatures is required for transactions, and who succeeds to custody authority if the primary custodian is unavailable — but none of these provisions actually hold the seed phrase. The seed phrase exists somewhere in the physical world: engraved on metal, written on paper, memorized, or stored in a vault.

The LLC does not solve the seed phrase problem. Your operating agreement can name a successor custodian with crystal clarity. If that successor cannot find, access, or use the seed phrase, the Bitcoin is gone regardless of what the operating agreement says. The LLC must be paired with a Letter of Instruction that documents the custody architecture in detail — hardware wallet locations, seed phrase storage method, access procedures, and emergency contacts — maintained separately from the legal documents and updated regularly.

The LLC operating agreement should address Bitcoin custody explicitly:

  • Custody authority: Which member or manager holds the hardware wallet(s) and has signing authority for Bitcoin transactions.
  • Multi-signature requirements: If you use a multi-sig wallet, the agreement should specify the signing threshold and name each keyholder by role.
  • Successor custodian: Who assumes custody authority at the death or incapacity of the primary custodian, and how that person is designated in advance.
  • Transfer restrictions: What votes or approvals are required before the LLC transfers Bitcoin — useful for family LLC structures where multiple members need to consent to large movements.
  • Valuation for distributions: How Bitcoin will be valued when the LLC makes in-kind or cash distributions to members.

Wyoming vs. Delaware vs. Nevada: State Selection for Bitcoin LLCs

Wyoming

Statutory single-member charging order protection. No state income tax. Most favorable digital asset laws in the U.S. Series LLC available. Strongest option for Bitcoin-specific structures.

Delaware

Excellent for multi-investor and multi-class structures. Single-member charging order protection is weak — courts may allow piercing. No state income tax if no Delaware operations.

Nevada

Strong charging order protection, including single-member. No state income tax. Anonymous membership possible. Slightly less favorable digital asset statutory framework than Wyoming.

Home State

If you're in CA or NY and form a Wyoming LLC, you may still need to register it in your home state — exposing it to home state LLC law. California's $800 Bitcoin family office minimum requirements franchise tax applies regardless of where the LLC was formed.

The recommendation for most Bitcoin families: a Wyoming LLC. Wyoming has enacted the most comprehensive statutory framework for digital assets of any U.S. state. Its single-member charging order protection is explicit in the statute — it does not depend on judicial interpretation. It has no state income tax. Its series LLC structure allows a single master LLC to contain multiple sub-series, each with its own assets, liabilities, and membership — useful for families managing multiple Bitcoin cold storage wallets, each isolated from the others at the legal level.

A critical caveat: if you are a California resident and you form a Wyoming LLC, California treats you as operating an LLC in California. The California Franchise Tax Board will assert the $800 minimum franchise tax. California courts may apply California's more creditor-friendly LLC law to determine whether the charging order protection holds. Wyoming formation does not insulate a California resident from California law. Nevada residents face similar, though less aggressive, treatment. If you live in a state with aggressive LLC taxation or unfavorable charging order law, consult an attorney about how your home state will treat an out-of-state LLC formation before proceeding.

The LLC + DAPT Combination: The Gold Standard

For families holding significant Bitcoin wealth, the most protective structure combines a Wyoming LLC with a Wyoming Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT). The architecture: the DAPT owns the LLC. The LLC holds the Bitcoin. You are a beneficiary of the DAPT.

This structure provides protection at two distinct levels:

  • At the LLC level: Creditors of you personally are limited to a charging order against your interest in the LLC — which is now owned by the DAPT, creating an additional layer of separation between the creditor and the Bitcoin.
  • At the DAPT level: Wyoming's DAPT statute provides additional creditor protection for trust assets. After a two-year seasoning period from the date of the transfer, assets held in the DAPT are generally protected from future creditors — even though you retain a beneficial interest in the trust.

The LLC provides the management structure and operational governance for Bitcoin custody. The DAPT provides the asset protection wrapper at the trust level. Together, they create a layered defense that requires a creditor to defeat both the LLC's charging order protection and the DAPT's statutory protections — a significantly higher bar than either structure provides alone.

The Wyoming DAPT is governed by Wyoming Statutes §§ 4-10-510 through 4-10-523. Wyoming is consistently rated among the top DAPT jurisdictions in the U.S. alongside Nevada, South Dakota, and Alaska for the robustness of its statutory creditor protection framework and the clarity of its seasoning period rules.

Estate Planning with LLC Interests

Transferring LLC Interests

LLC membership interests are property that can be transferred by gift, by bequest in a will, or by holding the interest inside a revocable trust. The most common approach for Bitcoin family offices is to hold the LLC inside a revocable trust — so that at death, the trust trustee succeeds automatically to ownership of the LLC without probate. The revocable trust is the container; the LLC is the operating vehicle.

Annual gifting of LLC interests is a powerful technique for gradually transferring Bitcoin value to the next generation. Each year, you can gift an interest in the LLC equal to the annual gift tax exclusion amount ($18,000 per donee in 2024, with inflation adjustments) without using any of your lifetime estate and gift tax exemption. If your LLC is worth $1 million and you gift 10% per year to your child, you have transferred $100,000 of Bitcoin exposure annually — potentially passing significant value over time while staying within the annual exclusion.

Valuation Discounts

One of the most significant estate planning advantages of holding Bitcoin in a family LLC is the potential to apply valuation discounts when transferring LLC interests for gift or estate tax purposes. Two types of discounts apply:

  • Minority discount: A minority interest in an LLC — an interest that does not control management or compel distributions — is worth less than a pro-rata share of the underlying assets, because the minority holder cannot unilaterally access the assets. Discounts of 15–30% are common for minority LLC interests.
  • Lack of marketability discount: LLC interests subject to transfer restrictions (as most operating agreements impose) are illiquid. An illiquid asset is worth less than the same asset held in liquid form. Lack of marketability discounts of 15–25% are also common.

Combined, minority and lack-of-marketability discounts applied to LLC interests holding Bitcoin can result in effective valuation discounts of 20–40% compared to the underlying Bitcoin's market value. This means that a 10% LLC interest in a $1 million Bitcoin-holding LLC might be valued at $60,000–$80,000 for gift tax purposes rather than $100,000 — potentially saving significant estate tax at the margin. These discounts must be supported by a qualified independent appraisal and are subject to IRS scrutiny. They are not automatic — they require proper documentation, a defensible structure, and professional valuation support.

A family LLC holding Bitcoin that is properly structured and appraised may allow you to transfer significantly more Bitcoin value per year — and per lifetime — than direct ownership permits.

The Single-Member LLC Problem

A single-member LLC with no operating agreement and no succession plan is one of the most common — and most dangerous — Bitcoin holding structures we encounter. At your death, what happens to a single-member LLC with no operating agreement? The answer is: it depends on state law, and in many states, the answer involves probate and court supervision.

Some states automatically dissolve a single-member LLC at the death of the sole member unless the operating agreement specifies otherwise. Others treat the LLC interest as an asset of the member's estate, subject to probate. Either outcome can mean your family's access to the Bitcoin is delayed, supervised by a court, and potentially contested — all while Bitcoin custody protocols go undocumented and the hardware wallet sits in your desk drawer.

The operating agreement for a single-member Bitcoin LLC must explicitly address:

  • Successor manager: Who becomes manager of the LLC immediately upon your death or incapacity — named by role, with an alternate named as well.
  • Custody handoff: How the successor manager accesses the Bitcoin custody materials — hardware wallet location, seed phrase access protocol, and the connection to the Letter of Instruction that provides technical details.
  • Continuity: That the LLC shall continue and not dissolve on the death or incapacity of any member or manager, and that successor members are designated.
  • Transfer mechanism: Whether the LLC interest passes by the revocable trust (recommended), by will, or by direct assignment.

When NOT to Use an LLC for Bitcoin

The LLC is a powerful tool, but it is not appropriate for every Bitcoin holder. Consider carefully before committing to the structure and its ongoing costs.

Small Holdings

If you hold less than 1 BTC, the cost and complexity of maintaining a properly structured and administered LLC likely outweighs the benefits. Wyoming LLC formation costs around $100 in state fees, but registered agent fees, operating agreement drafting, annual report filings, and potential accounting complexity add up. For smaller positions, a revocable trust with a clear Letter of Instruction provides succession planning at far lower cost.

California Residents

California's treatment of out-of-state LLCs is aggressive. The $800 minimum franchise tax applies to any LLC doing business in California — and California's definition of "doing business" is broad. California courts have also taken an unfavorable view of single-member LLC charging order protection. A California resident holding Bitcoin in a Wyoming LLC may be paying California franchise tax and receiving weaker asset protection than Wyoming's statutes would suggest. Get California-specific legal advice before proceeding.

No Business Creditor Risk

The charging order protection of an LLC is most valuable to people with significant personal liability exposure — business owners, professionals in high-liability fields, and individuals with meaningful lawsuit risk. If you are a salaried employee with no business creditor exposure and low personal liability risk, the core asset protection rationale for the LLC structure is weaker. Your estate planning priorities may be better served by a revocable trust, a Letter of Instruction, and — if your estate approaches the federal threshold — an irrevocable trust structure that does not require the ongoing administration of an LLC.

Putting It Together: The Bitcoin LLC Structure Checklist

  1. Form in Wyoming unless your specific circumstances (multi-investor structure, home state complications) favor another jurisdiction — confirmed with an attorney familiar with both your home state and Wyoming LLC law.
  2. Draft a comprehensive operating agreement that addresses Bitcoin custody authority, multi-signature requirements, successor manager designation, and continuity on death or incapacity. A generic online operating agreement is not sufficient for this purpose.
  3. Hold the LLC inside a revocable trust so that LLC ownership transfers to your successor trustee at death without probate.
  4. Pair with a Letter of Instruction that documents the complete Bitcoin custody architecture — hardware wallet locations, seed phrase storage, access procedures — so the successor manager named in the operating agreement can actually access the Bitcoin.
  5. Consider a Wyoming DAPT if your holdings are significant and you face meaningful personal liability exposure — the LLC + DAPT combination provides the strongest available asset protection structure under current U.S. law.
  6. Implement fractional gifting of LLC interests annually if estate tax is a concern — using the annual exclusion and potentially valuation discounts to transfer Bitcoin value to the next generation efficiently.
  7. Get a qualified appraisal to support any valuation discounts you take — minority and lack-of-marketability discounts require professional valuation documentation to withstand IRS scrutiny.
