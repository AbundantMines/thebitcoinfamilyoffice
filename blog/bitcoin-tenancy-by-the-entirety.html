Bitcoin Tenancy by the Entirety: The Creditor Protection That Joint Tenancy Doesn't Have

Tenancy by the entirety is joint ownership's better-protected cousin — available only to married couples, only in certain states, and offering one thing joint tenancy with right of survivorship cannot: a creditor judgment against one spouse cannot reach the Bitcoin. For any married Bitcoin holder with business liability or litigation exposure in a TBE state, this distinction is worth understanding before you decide how to hold your Bitcoin.

What Tenancy by the Entirety Is — and What Makes It Different

Tenancy by the entirety (TBE) is a form of co-ownership recognized only for married couples, treating the spouses as a single legal unit — the "entirety" — rather than as two separate co-owners. Like joint tenancy with right of survivorship (JTWROS), TBE provides automatic succession: when one spouse dies, the surviving spouse becomes the sole owner instantly, without probate. But TBE adds a protection JTWROS does not have.

Under JTWROS, each joint tenant holds a severable interest. A creditor of one joint tenant can reach that tenant's share — in extreme cases forcing a partition sale of the entire asset to satisfy the judgment. For a JTWROS Bitcoin holding, a judgment creditor of one spouse could potentially compel liquidation of that spouse's share.

Under TBE, neither spouse holds a severable interest. The couple owns the property as a single legal entity. A creditor of one spouse only cannot reach TBE property at all — there is no individual share to attach. The creditor would need a judgment against both spouses jointly to have any claim on TBE Bitcoin.

The Core Distinction

JTWROS: one-spouse judgment creditor can potentially reach and force partition of that spouse's share. TBE: one-spouse judgment creditor cannot reach the property at all — no individual share exists to attach. For a married Bitcoin holder where one spouse has significant liability exposure (business owner, physician, contractor, guarantor), TBE provides a meaningful protection layer that JTWROS simply cannot.

For Bitcoin holders in Florida specifically: Florida has among the strongest TBE protections in the country. Florida courts have consistently held that personal property — including financial accounts and investment assets — can be held as tenancy by the entirety by married couples, shielding it entirely from one spouse's individual judgment creditors. Florida also allows TBE trusts (discussed below), creating the gold standard combination: TBE creditor protection inside a revocable trust with full estate planning flexibility.

Estate Tax Treatment: TBE Is the Same as JTWROS for Married Couples

TBE does not change the estate tax outcome compared to JTWROS for married couples. Under §2040(b), exactly 50% of TBE Bitcoin is included in the first deceased spouse's gross estate — the same automatic 50/50 rule that applies to spousal JTWROS. The marital deduction under §2056 shelters the transfer from immediate estate tax. The surviving spouse owns 100% of the Bitcoin with no estate tax at the first death.

TBE also does not change the basis step-up analysis. Only the 50% included in the deceased spouse's estate receives a stepped-up basis under §1014. The surviving spouse's 50% retains the original cost basis — the same partial step-up limitation that applies to spousal JTWROS in common-law states.

For the double basis step-up at first death — both spouses' halves stepped up — that benefit is unique to community property under §1014(b)(6) and is not available through TBE. Couples in community property states (Arizona, California, Nevada, Texas, Washington, etc.) do not have access to TBE but already receive the superior basis step-up through community property characterization.

The Limits of TBE Protection: When It Fails

TBE's creditor protection, while powerful, has specific limits that Bitcoin holders must understand:

Joint Debts Defeat TBE

TBE protects against one-spouse creditors only. If both spouses are jointly liable — co-signers on a loan, joint guarantors on a business debt, jointly named in a lawsuit — the joint creditor can reach TBE property. Business owners who personally guarantee company debt as a couple lose TBE protection for that debt entirely.

Divorce Terminates TBE

TBE automatically converts to tenancy in common at divorce — each former spouse holds an equal undivided fractional interest with no survivorship and no creditor protection. Post-divorce, a creditor of one former spouse can reach their TIC share. If divorce is anticipated, TBE planning should be reviewed promptly.

Federal Tax Liens Reach TBE

IRS federal tax liens can attach to TBE property in many circumstances, particularly for one spouse's individual tax liability. State judgment creditor protection does not operate against federal government claims. Bitcoin holders with significant tax liabilities or disputes should not rely on TBE as protection against the IRS.

Fraudulent Transfer

Converting Bitcoin from individually-owned to TBE after a creditor claim has arisen, or in anticipation of litigation, is a fraudulent transfer subject to reversal under the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act. TBE protection requires establishment well before any creditor claim — not as a reactive move once a threat materializes.

TBE Is Not a Last-Minute Move

Converting Bitcoin to TBE after a lawsuit is filed or a creditor claim arises is a fraudulent transfer. TBE planning works only when established proactively, before any creditor claim, as part of an overall asset protection strategy. Timing matters.

The Private Key Problem: TBE Still Doesn't Solve It

Like JTWROS, TBE solves the legal title succession question — the surviving spouse becomes sole owner at the first death — but does not address the technical problem of Bitcoin access. If Bitcoin is held in self-custody and only one spouse controls the private keys and seed phrases, the surviving spouse is the legal owner of Bitcoin they cannot retrieve.

For TBE to function correctly as a Bitcoin holding structure, both spouses must either: (a) independently have access to the wallet/seed phrase, or (b) a documented Letter of Instructions specifying exactly how the surviving spouse accesses the Bitcoin must exist and be kept current. Without this, TBE's survivorship benefit is legally perfected but practically useless — title transfers instantly; access does not.

The TBE Trust: Best of Both Worlds

In Florida, Maryland, and certain other states, married couples can hold property in a tenancy-by-the-entirety trust — a revocable living trust that is structured to preserve TBE creditor protection while providing all the estate planning benefits of a trust.

A TBE trust accomplishes what neither outright TBE nor a standard revocable trust can do alone:

  • Creditor protection — the trust holds Bitcoin as TBE, shielding it from one-spouse judgment creditors
  • Probate avoidance — same as any revocable trust or JTWROS
  • Incapacity management — the successor trustee manages Bitcoin if either spouse becomes incapacitated, with documented key access procedures in the Letter of Instructions
  • Credit shelter planning — the trust can include a credit shelter provision at the first death, funding a bypass trust with the deceased's estate tax exemption to shelter Bitcoin from the second estate
  • Private key succession — the trustee is documented with Bitcoin access instructions; no uncertainty about key access at death
  • Multi-generational planning — trust provisions can direct Bitcoin to dynasty trust structures, impose age restrictions, or coordinate with larger wealth transfer planning

Florida's statute (Fla. Stat. §§689.115, 736.1501) explicitly authorizes joint revocable trusts that preserve TBE characteristics, making Florida the most established jurisdiction for this structure. Maryland and other TBE states with similar statutes provide similar functionality. For Bitcoin holders in Florida with both liability exposure and estate planning needs, the TBE trust is the default recommendation over either outright TBE or a standard revocable trust.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is tenancy by the entirety and how does it differ from joint tenancy for Bitcoin?

Tenancy by the entirety (TBE) is a married-couple-only form of co-ownership that treats the spouses as a single legal unit. Like JTWROS, it provides automatic survivorship without probate. The key difference: a judgment creditor of one spouse cannot reach TBE property — there is no severable individual share to attach. Under JTWROS, a one-spouse creditor can potentially force partition and take the debtor's share. TBE's creditor protection is available only in states that recognize it for personal property (roughly 25 states for real estate, fewer for intangibles like Bitcoin), and only during a valid marriage.

Which states allow tenancy by the entirety for Bitcoin or personal property?

States with established TBE for personal property (including financial accounts and intangibles) include Florida, Maryland, Tennessee, Indiana, Wyoming, Missouri, Delaware, Hawaii, and Michigan, among others. Several states limit TBE to real property. Community property states (CA, TX, AZ, NV, WA, etc.) do not use TBE. The application of TBE to Bitcoin specifically — as intangible personal property — has not been litigated in most states. Florida has the clearest framework. Verify current state law with a licensed attorney before relying on TBE for Bitcoin.

Does tenancy by the entirety Bitcoin avoid estate tax?

No. TBE follows the same §2040(b) rule as spousal JTWROS: 50% is included in the first spouse's gross estate, with the other 50% passing to the surviving spouse under the unlimited marital deduction. No estate tax at first death; the surviving spouse owns 100% outright. TBE is a creditor protection tool, not an estate tax reduction strategy. For estate tax minimization, credit shelter trusts, dynasty trusts, or other irrevocable structures are required in addition to TBE.

Can a creditor of one spouse reach Bitcoin held as tenancy by the entirety?

Not in states that recognize TBE for personal property — a judgment against only one spouse cannot attach to or force partition of TBE Bitcoin. The creditor needs a judgment against both spouses. However: joint creditors (both spouses are liable) can reach TBE property; federal tax liens can reach TBE under certain rules; TBE converts to TIC at divorce, exposing the indebted former spouse's share; and fraudulent transfer laws can unwind TBE established after a claim arises. TBE is strong single-spouse judgment creditor protection — not immunity from all creditors.

What is a TBE trust and when should a married couple use one?

A TBE trust (tenancy-by-the-entirety trust) is a revocable living trust structured to preserve TBE creditor protection while adding trust planning benefits: incapacity management, documented Bitcoin key access for the successor trustee, credit shelter trust funding at first death, and multi-generational planning provisions. Florida and Maryland have explicit statutes authorizing TBE trusts. For Bitcoin-holding married couples in Florida with liability exposure, the TBE trust is typically the optimal structure — it combines creditor protection with full estate planning flexibility in a single document.
