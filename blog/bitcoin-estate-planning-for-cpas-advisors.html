Section 2: What CPAs Need to Know About Bitcoin

Bitcoin Is Property, Not Currency

The IRS settled this in 2014 with Notice 2014-21: Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies are property for U.S. federal tax purposes. This classification has significant consequences. Every taxable disposition of Bitcoin ‚Äî every sale, exchange, or use ‚Äî creates a capital gains or loss event. There is no currency exception. Buying a cup of coffee with Bitcoin is a taxable event; the customer realizes gain or loss equal to the difference between their cost basis and the Bitcoin's value at the time of the purchase.

This property classification also means that Bitcoin is not subject to the foreign currency rules under IRC Section 988, and that long-term capital gain rates apply to Bitcoin held more than one year before disposition.

FIFO vs. LIFO vs. HIFO: Lot Selection Matters Enormously

For a client who has accumulated Bitcoin at multiple price points over several years, the choice of lot selection method determines how much gain or loss is recognized on each sale. The IRS allows taxpayers to specifically identify which lots they are selling (Rev. Proc. 2024-28 confirmed that adequate identification requires documentation of the specific units disposed of at the time of the sale).

üí° HIFO = Highest-In, First-Out

HIFO sells the lots with the highest cost basis first, minimizing realized gains on each sale. For a client with a mix of old (low basis) and recent (high basis) Bitcoin, selling high-basis lots first can dramatically reduce ‚Äî or eliminate ‚Äî taxable gain on near-term sales. This is not a tax shelter; it's specific identification of which units are being sold, and it is fully permitted by the IRS when documented correctly.

The mechanics of HIFO require accurate lot tracking software. The client must be able to produce, for each sale: the acquisition date, the acquisition price (cost basis), and documentation that they specifically identified those units for sale at the time of the transaction. You cannot retroactively apply HIFO ‚Äî the identification must occur contemporaneously with or before the sale.

Software options for lot tracking: Koinly, CoinTracker, TaxBit, and TokenTax all support HIFO lot selection and integrate with major exchanges. Clients with self-custody Bitcoin will need to manually import transaction data or use wallet-connected syncing.

Cost Basis for Gifted vs. Inherited Bitcoin

The rules differ sharply between gifted and inherited Bitcoin:

  • Gifted Bitcoin: The recipient takes the donor's original cost basis (carryover basis). If the fair market value at the time of the gift is lower than the donor's basis, the recipient's basis for loss purposes is the lower FMV. This creates a basis "trap" ‚Äî if Bitcoin has declined in value, a gift may eliminate the donor's loss deduction without giving the recipient a usable loss basis.
  • Inherited Bitcoin: The heir receives a step-up in basis to FMV at the date of death (or alternate valuation date under IRC Section 2032). All pre-death appreciation is entirely excluded from income tax. For clients with large, long-held Bitcoin positions, this step-up is often worth more ‚Äî in tax savings ‚Äî than any other estate planning tool available.
üí° The Step-Up Calculus: Gifting vs. Holding

A client with 10 BTC purchased at $5,000 each ($50,000 basis) now worth $1,000,000 faces a potential $950,000 capital gain on sale. If they gift that Bitcoin, the recipient inherits a $50,000 basis. If they hold it until death, the heir gets a $1,000,000 basis ‚Äî zero capital gains tax on all prior appreciation. The decision to gift Bitcoin early (e.g., for annual exclusion gifts) must account for this tradeoff. For large positions, holding to death often dominates gifting from a pure tax perspective, absent other planning goals.

Wash Sale Rule: Does NOT Apply to Bitcoin

The wash sale rule under IRC Section 1091 disallows a loss on a security sale if the taxpayer purchases "substantially identical" securities within 30 days before or after the sale. Bitcoin is not a security; it is property. Therefore the wash sale rule does not apply.

The practical implication is significant: a client can sell Bitcoin at a loss to harvest that loss for tax purposes and immediately repurchase the same amount of Bitcoin ‚Äî the same day, even the same hour ‚Äî without any loss disallowance. This is a meaningful planning opportunity, especially after price corrections. Congress has periodically proposed extending wash sale rules to digital assets; monitor legislation, but as of 2026 this exemption remains intact.

Mining Income: Ordinary Income at Receipt

Bitcoin received as mining income is taxable as ordinary income at the fair market value at the time of receipt (IRS Notice 2014-21; Rev. Rul. 2023-14 further clarified timing for proof-of-stake, but the mining income rule is clear). The miner's cost basis in the received Bitcoin is that same FMV ‚Äî which becomes the basis for subsequent capital gain or loss calculations when the Bitcoin is sold.

Deductions available to miners include: electricity costs, hardware depreciation (Section 168 bonus depreciation), facility costs, and other operating expenses. For clients who mine Bitcoin, the interaction between ordinary income, deductions, and eventual capital gain on mined Bitcoin requires careful planning. Mining operations structured as businesses can deduct substantial capital expenditures that offset mining income and potentially other ordinary income.

Fork and Airdrop Income

Bitcoin hard forks that result in the receipt of new cryptocurrency (e.g., Bitcoin Cash in 2017) create ordinary income at the FMV at the time of receipt ‚Äî confirmed by Rev. Rul. 2019-24. The taxpayer's basis in the forked coins is that same FMV. If the client did not exercise dominion and control over the forked coins (e.g., they held on an exchange that didn't credit the fork, or in a wallet that doesn't support the forked coin), there is an argument no income was received until actual control was established.

Pure Bitcoin (no forks) simplifies this analysis considerably. Many Bitcoin-only holders regard forks as noise and never interact with forked coins.

Like-Kind Exchange: NOT Available for Bitcoin

Post-2017 tax reform (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act), Section 1031 like-kind exchanges are limited to real property. Bitcoin-to-Bitcoin or Bitcoin-to-other-cryptocurrency exchanges are taxable dispositions. This kills a planning argument that was sometimes made pre-2018. No current legal basis exists for Section 1031 treatment of Bitcoin dispositions.

Bitcoin Loss Harvesting

As noted above, the absence of wash sale restrictions makes Bitcoin loss harvesting unusually powerful. The strategy:

  1. Identify Bitcoin lots with unrealized losses (current value below cost basis)
  2. Sell those specific lots to realize the capital loss
  3. Immediately repurchase equivalent Bitcoin (basis now reset to current market price)
  4. The realized loss offsets capital gains elsewhere in the portfolio, or creates up to $3,000/year in ordinary income offset (excess carries forward)

This strategy is most powerful during periods of significant price decline. For clients with other appreciated assets (stocks, real estate) where you'd like to generate an offsetting loss, Bitcoin's wash-sale exemption makes it a uniquely useful harvesting vehicle.

Estate Tax: FMV at Death, Step-Up Applies

Bitcoin is included in the decedent's gross estate at fair market value on the date of death (or alternate valuation date). The FMV is determined by reference to quoted prices on active markets ‚Äî typically an average of exchange prices as of the valuation date. For clients with significant Bitcoin holdings, the estate tax exposure is direct and grows with Bitcoin's price.

The current federal estate tax exemption (as of 2026, post-TCJA extension resolution ‚Äî verify current exemption level) shelters substantial estates from estate tax. But for Bitcoin holders whose stacks may grow significantly, planning today avoids a massive tax bill later. Strategies: irrevocable trusts, GRATs, charitable structures, and dynasty trusts in favorable jurisdictions.

Form 8949 and Schedule D

Each Bitcoin disposition must be reported on Form 8949. Each lot sold is a separate line: description, date acquired, date sold, proceeds, cost basis, adjustment if any, and gain or loss. For active traders with hundreds or thousands of transactions, Form 8949 can be a substantial exercise. Lot tracking software produces Form 8949-ready exports. The resulting net capital gain or loss flows to Schedule D.

IRS Form 1040 now includes a direct question about digital asset transactions on the front page. Clients who answer "no" when they've had Bitcoin transactions have made a potentially false statement on their return. Make sure clients understand and accurately answer this question.

FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and FATCA (Form 8938)

FBAR: U.S. persons must file FinCEN Form 114 if they have a financial interest in or signature authority over a foreign financial account exceeding $10,000 in aggregate at any point during the year. Bitcoin held on a foreign cryptocurrency exchange generally qualifies as a foreign financial account. Bitcoin held in pure self-custody (hardware wallet, no exchange) does not ‚Äî there is no foreign account, only a cryptographic key that exists on a global network. FinCEN has indicated foreign-exchange-held virtual currency is reportable; the regulations have not yet been finalized but err conservative.

FATCA / Form 8938: Higher thresholds apply ($50,000 for single filers residing in the U.S.; higher for married and foreign residents). Bitcoin held on a foreign exchange may trigger Form 8938 disclosure. Failure to file Form 8938 carries substantial penalties. If your client has used any foreign exchange ‚Äî Binance International, Bitfinex, Kraken (if applicable), or others ‚Äî assess their FATCA exposure.

Section 3: What Financial Advisors Need to Know

The RIA Custody Rule and Bitcoin

The SEC's investment adviser custody rule (Rule 206(4)-2 under the Advisers Act, and its recent amendments) requires that client assets be held with a "qualified custodian." A traditional brokerage, bank, or registered trust company qualifies. A hardware wallet does not.

For RIAs, the implications are:

  • Never take possession of a client's private keys, seed phrase, or hardware wallet. Doing so creates custody under the rule and triggers compliance obligations ‚Äî including surprise examinations ‚Äî that most RIAs are not set up to handle.
  • You cannot manage a Bitcoin position held in client self-custody (where you have discretion to sign transactions) without triggering custody. You can give advice about a self-custody position without violating the rule.
  • If your client wants you to actively manage their Bitcoin position (execute buys and sells on their behalf), it must be held at a qualified custodian. Current qualified custodians for institutional Bitcoin include Fidelity Digital Assets, Coinbase Prime, BitGo, and others.
  • Directed trust structures allow a Bitcoin specialist to serve as investment trustee with fiduciary responsibility for the custody and management of Bitcoin within the trust ‚Äî potentially allowing an RIA to focus on financial planning while the investment trustee handles execution and custody.

Fiduciary Duty with Existing Bitcoin Positions

If your client holds a significant Bitcoin position that you did not recommend, you nonetheless have a fiduciary obligation to know about it, consider it, and advise on it. "I don't do crypto" is not a fiduciary discharge of duty.

The practical obligation:

  • Include Bitcoin in the client's net worth statement and overall financial plan
  • Assess concentration risk: what percentage of net worth does Bitcoin represent?
  • Model the estate tax exposure at current and projected Bitcoin values
  • Document your advice ‚Äî whether you recommend holding, reducing, or hedging
  • Ensure the client has made an informed decision with full understanding of the risks
  • If you lack the expertise to advise on Bitcoin specifically, refer to a specialist and document the referral

Documenting Bitcoin in Client Financial Plans

Bitcoin must appear in:

  • Net worth statements: Current FMV, cost basis (if known), unrealized gain/loss
  • Asset allocation analysis: Bitcoin as a separate, distinct asset class ‚Äî not lumped with "alternatives"
  • Estate tax projections: Current and projected (at various price scenarios) estate tax exposure from Bitcoin alone
  • Concentration risk analysis: What happens to the client's financial plan if Bitcoin drops 80%? (It has done this four times.)
  • Cash flow planning: If the client plans to sell Bitcoin to fund retirement or large purchases, what are the tax implications?
  • Beneficiary planning: Is Bitcoin addressed in the estate plan? If not, what is the succession risk?

Portfolio Concentration Analysis

A client with 50%+ of net worth in Bitcoin is in a concentrated position with characteristics unlike any other concentrated position in traditional finance. Bitcoin has demonstrated volatility of 50‚Äì80% drawdowns historically, with recovery periods of 2‚Äì4 years. Traditional concentrated position strategies (collar, exchange fund, gradual diversification) are all available in theory but have different mechanics and tax costs for Bitcoin.

Key modeling questions for Bitcoin-concentrated clients:

  • At what Bitcoin price does the client's estate tax exposure cross the current exemption threshold?
  • What is the tax cost of selling 10%, 20%, or 50% of the position today to diversify?
  • At what price does the estate tax on the Bitcoin position exceed the capital gains tax on a current sale? (The crossover point often justifies selling.)
  • What is the after-tax diversification target ‚Äî how much in other assets do they need to sleep at night?

Insurance: Homeowner's Policies Don't Cover Bitcoin

Standard homeowner's insurance policies typically cover theft of cash up to $200‚Äì$1,000 and may exclude digital assets entirely. A client with $1M+ in self-custody Bitcoin should have a separate Bitcoin-specific insurance policy. Options include:

  • Specialty insurers: Lloyd's syndicates, Coincover, and similar providers offer coverage for Bitcoin in self-custody and institutional custody
  • Multi-sig custodian insurance: Some multi-sig custody providers offer insurance as part of their product
  • Crime coverage: Commercial crime policies may cover Bitcoin theft for business clients

The coverage terms matter: "custodial loss," "cold storage loss," and "physical theft" are different covered perils, and policies vary. Refer clients to an insurance broker who specializes in digital asset coverage.

Rebalancing Triggers: Tax Cost vs. Diversification Benefit

For a client with 80% of net worth in Bitcoin at a very low basis, the tax cost of rebalancing can swamp the diversification benefit. A simplified example: a client with $5M in Bitcoin (basis: $250,000) who wants to sell 20% to diversify will realize approximately $950,000 in long-term capital gain, triggering ~$200,000 in federal tax. That $200,000 is the cost of diversification. Whether that cost is worth paying depends on the client's risk tolerance, estate planning situation, and projected future Bitcoin price.

Alternative diversification approaches that don't require selling:

  • Bitcoin-collateralized loans: Borrow against Bitcoin without selling; invest loan proceeds in other assets. Interest cost is the "price" of diversification.
  • Charitable structures: Donate appreciated Bitcoin to a charitable remainder trust or donor-advised fund; take income tax deduction; receive income stream or re-invested diversified portfolio.
  • Dynasty trust: Transfer Bitcoin into a multi-generational trust, removing future appreciation from estate while maintaining family wealth.
  • Gradual harvesting: Use the annual exclusion and lifetime exemption to transfer Bitcoin to heirs over time at reduced or zero transfer tax cost.

Section 4: Coordinating with Estate Attorneys on Bitcoin Plans

What the Attorney Needs from You

As the CPA or financial advisor, you are typically the most informed party about your client's financial position. The estate attorney needs:

  • Current Bitcoin holdings: Total BTC, current FMV, current unrealized gain/loss
  • Cost basis summary: Aggregate basis, and ideally a lot-by-lot breakdown for planning purposes
  • Custody setup: Is Bitcoin in self-custody? Exchange? Multi-sig? The attorney needs to know this to draft appropriate custody language.
  • Existing estate plan documents: What's already in place? Any beneficiary designations, existing trusts, prior wills?
  • Beneficiary situation: Who are the intended heirs? Are any of them minors, or with special needs, or in difficult financial situations that would argue for trust control rather than outright distribution?
  • State of domicile and any real property locations: For multi-jurisdiction analysis
  • Client's sophistication: Is the client Bitcoin-native with strong self-custody preferences, or does this feel like a compliance exercise to them?

What You Need from the Attorney

After the estate plan is established, you need:

  • Trust terms and distribution language: For tax planning purposes, you need to understand when and how Bitcoin can be distributed ‚Äî whether in-kind or as cash after liquidation, whether at trustee discretion or fixed schedule
  • Trustee tax reporting obligations: Who files the trust tax return? What is the trust's taxpayer identification number?
  • Grantor trust status: Is this a grantor trust (taxable to the grantor) or non-grantor trust (separate taxpayer)? This determines where income from the trust is reported.
  • Distribution language for Bitcoin: Can Bitcoin be distributed in-kind to beneficiaries, or must it be liquidated first? In-kind distribution avoids triggering gain at the trust level.
  • Cost basis tracking obligations: What records must the trust maintain, and who is responsible for maintaining them?

Directed Trust: Tax Reporting Mechanics

In a directed trust, the investment trustee holds and manages the Bitcoin. The distribution trustee manages beneficiary distributions. For tax purposes, the trust is still a single taxable entity (or a grantor trust, depending on structure), and all trust income is reported on a single trust tax return (Form 1041) or attributed to the grantor.

If the investment trustee sells Bitcoin within the trust, the resulting gain is trust income ‚Äî reportable on Form 1041 or attributed to the grantor. Cost basis records must follow the investment trustee's lot tracking, which you should verify is being maintained consistently with the client's prior lot identification methodology.

Grantor vs. Non-Grantor Trust Tax Treatment

This is a critical planning variable that affects who pays the tax on Bitcoin gains within the trust:

  • Grantor Trust: The grantor retains certain powers (e.g., right to substitute assets) that cause the trust to be taxed as if the grantor owned the assets directly. All trust income, including Bitcoin gains, is reported on the grantor's personal return. The grantor paying this tax is economically equivalent to a tax-free gift to the trust beneficiaries ‚Äî the trust benefits without being depleted by taxes. GRATs and IDGTs (Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts) use this structure intentionally.
  • Non-Grantor Trust: The trust is a separate taxpayer. Trust taxable income above approximately $14,000-$15,000 is taxed at the top rate (37% for ordinary income, 20% for long-term capital gains plus potential 3.8% NIIT). This compressed rate structure makes non-grantor trusts tax-inefficient for assets that generate current income ‚Äî but Bitcoin that is simply held (no sales, no distributions) in a non-grantor trust generates no current trust taxable income.

Section 6: Building a Bitcoin Practice

How to Market as a Bitcoin-Specialist Advisor

Bitcoin clients are a self-selecting, high-conviction, high-value segment. They are generally younger than traditional wealth management clients, often accumulate wealth faster, and are highly referral-active within their community. A single good Bitcoin client experience can generate multiple referrals through Bitcoin network effects.

Positioning as a Bitcoin specialist advisor:

  • Write publicly about Bitcoin tax and planning topics. Bitcoin clients search for advisors who understand the asset class; content is your discovery mechanism. Guest posts on Bitcoin-focused publications, LinkedIn articles, and a focused practice area page on your website all help.
  • Speak at local CFA/CPA society events on Bitcoin taxation. You're almost certainly ahead of 80% of your peers on this topic ‚Äî share it.
  • Build relationships with Bitcoin estate attorneys and CPA firms that specialize in the space. The referral network is small and reciprocal.
  • Be explicit in your marketing about your Bitcoin experience: "I work with clients who hold significant Bitcoin positions" is better than "I'm familiar with digital assets."

CPE Credits for Digital Asset Education

The formal education landscape for Bitcoin-specific professional knowledge is growing:

  • AICPA: Offers CPE courses on digital asset taxation, including a Digital Assets Audit Considerations course. Check their learning marketplace for current offerings.
  • Digital Assets Council of Financial Professionals (DACFP): Offers the Certificate in Blockchain and Digital Assets, which carries CPE credit for CFPs and CPAs. Strong curriculum on custody, custody regulation, and tax basics.
  • State CPA societies: Many now include digital asset sessions in their annual tax conferences. California, New York, and Texas bar and CPA associations all have active digital asset committees.
  • CFP Board: Has developed curriculum additions covering digital asset basics; look for CE credits in approved courses.
  • Bitcoin-native education: UNCHAINED, Braiins, and other Bitcoin-focused providers offer non-CPE-credited education that builds deep domain knowledge. Not CPE, but essential for genuine expertise.

Liability Considerations: Engagement Letter Language

Your engagement letter should explicitly address the scope of your Bitcoin-related services and any limitations. Consider including:

  • Scope definition: "This engagement includes advice on tax treatment of Bitcoin transactions and coordination with the client's estate planning attorney on Bitcoin-related planning matters."
  • Custody disclaimer: "This firm does not provide custody services for digital assets and does not take possession of private keys, seed phrases, or hardware wallets."
  • Historical records: "Accurate tax reporting for prior-year Bitcoin transactions requires complete historical transaction records. Client is responsible for providing complete records; firm is not responsible for errors resulting from incomplete or unavailable records."
  • Regulation risk: "Tax treatment of digital assets is subject to evolving IRS guidance and legislation. This advice reflects current law and IRS guidance as of [date]."
  • Valuation methodology: Specify how you'll determine FMV for each type of transaction ‚Äî exchange quoted price, average of bids, etc.

Referring to the Bitcoin Estate Planning Network

When your client needs estate planning services you can't provide, refer them to specialists within the Bitcoin estate planning network ‚Äî not to generic estate attorneys. The Unchained Capital attorney referral network, ACTEC members with digital asset experience, and attorneys who present at Bitcoin conferences are generally better choices than any general estate attorney, however distinguished.

Section 7: Case Examples

CPA Scenario
Client with 15 BTC Wants to Gift Some ‚Äî HIFO Analysis

Situation: Your client, Marta, has accumulated 15 BTC across four purchases. She wants to gift 3 BTC to her adult daughter this year. Current Bitcoin price: $100,000.

Lot A: 5 BTC at $8,000 eachBasis: $40,000
Lot B: 4 BTC at $25,000 eachBasis: $100,000
Lot C: 3 BTC at $50,000 eachBasis: $150,000
Lot D: 3 BTC at $90,000 eachBasis: $270,000
Total: 15 BTCTotal Basis: $560,000

HIFO-optimal gifting: To gift 3 BTC with maximum carryover basis (minimizing the daughter's future gain), select Lot D (3 BTC at $90,000 basis each). The daughter receives 3 BTC with a $270,000 carryover basis ‚Äî vs. $24,000 if Marta gifted Lot A shares instead.

Annual exclusion check: 3 BTC √ó $100,000 = $300,000 FMV. Annual exclusion ($18,000 in 2026) covers $18,000. The remaining $282,000 is a taxable gift against Marta's lifetime exemption. If Marta is married and does gift-splitting, up to $36,000 is covered by annual exclusion.

Step-up consideration: Marta's Lot A Bitcoin (basis $40,000, now $100,000) would receive a full step-up at death ‚Äî meaning gifting Lot A costs the daughter $60,000 per BTC in future gains that would have been eliminated entirely at Marta's death. The right question is whether Marta should be gifting Lot A at all, rather than holding it.

CPA recommendation: Gift Lot D (highest basis, minimizes daughter's future gain). Consider holding Lot A to death for step-up. Advise Marta on the lifetime exemption utilization and prepare Form 709 for the taxable gift.

Financial Advisor Scenario
Client with $8M Bitcoin (95% of Net Worth) Wants to "Diversify" ‚Äî The Tax-Adjusted Math

Situation: Marcus has 80 BTC purchased at an average of $2,000 each ($160,000 total basis). Current value: $8,000,000. He's asking you to help him "diversify into real assets." He earns $450,000/year in W-2 income.

Current Bitcoin value$8,000,000
Cost basis$160,000
Unrealized LTCG$7,840,000
Federal LTCG tax (20%)$1,568,000
NIIT (3.8%)$297,920
California state tax (13.3% on LTCG)$1,042,720
Total tax on full liquidation~$2,908,640

Marcus wants to diversify $4M of his position. The proportional tax cost: approximately $1.45M in taxes to receive $2.55M in after-tax diversified capital. The effective price of diversification is 18% of his current wealth.

Tax-adjusted alternatives:

1. Bitcoin-collateralized loan: Marcus borrows $2M against his Bitcoin at a 50% LTV ratio, pledging 40 BTC as collateral. He invests the $2M in real estate or other assets. Annual interest cost at 8%: $160,000. He maintains his Bitcoin position, has $2M invested elsewhere, and has deferred all tax. If Bitcoin appreciates, his net position improves; if it declines to the collateral threshold, he faces a margin call.

2. Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT): Marcus contributes $2M in appreciated Bitcoin to a CRUT. The trust sells the Bitcoin tax-free and reinvests in diversified assets. Marcus receives an income stream for life and a partial charitable deduction. At his death, the remainder goes to his chosen charity. He gets $2M invested diversified, avoids immediate capital gains tax, and gets a current-year deduction.

3. Nevada/Wyoming Dynasty Trust: Transfer $2M in Bitcoin to an irrevocable dynasty trust now while retaining an income interest. Removes future appreciation from estate, uses lifetime exemption, and allows structured distribution to descendants for generations ‚Äî without ever triggering gain until the trust sells.

Advisor recommendation: Present all three alternatives with after-tax analysis before executing a simple sale. The "diversify" instruction, if executed naively, costs Marcus $1.45M in taxes that could be avoided or deferred.

Joint Scenario ‚Äî CPA + Financial Advisor
Helping a Client Set Up a Wyoming Dynasty Trust ‚Äî Coordinator Role

Situation: Elena has $3M in Bitcoin and is working with a Wyoming estate attorney to establish a dynasty trust. She has asked both her CPA (you) and her financial advisor to be involved. Here's how the roles divide:

The estate attorney is responsible for: trust drafting, trustee designations, directed trust bifurcation, custody language, Letter of Instruction structure, Wyoming law compliance, trust funding mechanics.

The financial advisor is responsible for: overall portfolio impact analysis, estate tax exposure modeling, coordinating the Bitcoin-collateralized loan that Elena wants to use alongside the trust, insurance review, and integrating the trust into Elena's overall financial plan.

The CPA is responsible for: preparing Form 709 (gift tax return) for the funding of the irrevocable trust, advising on grantor vs. non-grantor trust selection for income tax purposes, ensuring lot tracking for the contributed Bitcoin is properly established at the trust level, advising on the trust's annual tax filing obligations, and coordinating cost basis documentation with the investment trustee.

Your specific handoffs:

  • Send the attorney Elena's complete lot-tracking spreadsheet ‚Äî the trust needs to continue her existing basis methodology
  • Confirm with the attorney whether the trust is a grantor trust ‚Äî this determines whether trust income appears on Elena's personal return or a separate Form 1041
  • Advise the financial advisor on the estate tax savings from the trust ‚Äî useful for justifying the planning cost
  • Prepare for an ongoing relationship: dynasty trusts have multi-decade tax reporting obligations, and the trust's CPA relationship should be established from day one

The joint scenario is the most common high-value situation ‚Äî and the most common source of errors. Gaps in communication between advisors produce planning documents that don't work together. A coordinating role (often filled by the financial advisor or by a family office like BFO) keeps the team aligned.

Section 8: Frequently Asked Questions for Professionals

Can I use HIFO lot selection for Bitcoin even if my client hasn't tracked their lots from the beginning?
Yes, but with significant work. Lot tracking software like Koinly, CoinTracker, or TaxBit can reconstruct transaction history from on-chain data and exchange records. The client needs to provide all wallet addresses and exchange account histories. Gaps in records may require conservative assumptions (unknown basis = $0, which overstates gain). Going forward, establish a lot tracking system and document cost basis methodology in the engagement letter. Do not retroactively apply HIFO to prior years without proper documentation ‚Äî this creates audit risk.
Does the wash sale rule apply to Bitcoin?
No. As of current IRS guidance, the wash sale rule (IRC Section 1091) applies to securities and does not apply to Bitcoin or other cryptocurrencies, which are classified as property. This means Bitcoin can be sold to realize a loss and repurchased immediately ‚Äî including the same day ‚Äî without disallowing the loss. This is a significant tax planning opportunity that is unavailable for stocks. Congress has periodically proposed extending wash sale rules to digital assets; monitor legislation, but as of 2026 this exemption remains intact.
What is an RIA's obligation when a client holds Bitcoin they didn't recommend?
An RIA has a fiduciary duty to know about and consider all of a client's assets, including Bitcoin they didn't recommend and don't custody. The advisor can't simply ignore a concentrated position because it's outside their normal asset universe. The obligation is to: (a) include Bitcoin in the client's overall financial plan, (b) assess the concentration risk and estate tax exposure, (c) document whether they recommended any action, and (d) ensure the client has made an informed decision. If you lack Bitcoin expertise, refer to a specialist ‚Äî and document that referral.
Can an RIA custody Bitcoin directly for clients?
Generally no. The SEC's Investment Advisers Act custody rule requires that client assets be held with a qualified custodian. Self-custody Bitcoin (hardware wallet under RIA control) would likely constitute custody under the rule and trigger significant compliance obligations including surprise examinations. Most RIAs either direct clients to a qualified Bitcoin custodian or require clients to self-custody independently. Do not take physical possession of a client's seed phrase or hardware wallet under any circumstances.
How is Bitcoin taxed when inherited?
Bitcoin receives a step-up in cost basis at death to the fair market value on the date of death (or alternate valuation date). This means heirs who inherit Bitcoin and immediately sell it pay zero capital gains tax on appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime. For clients with large, long-held Bitcoin positions, estate planning around the step-up ‚Äî including holding rather than gifting ‚Äî can produce massive tax savings. A client with 50 BTC purchased at $1,000 each who holds to death passes on $50 million in Bitcoin (at hypothetical $1M/BTC) with zero income tax on the $49,950,000 of appreciation.
Does FBAR apply to Bitcoin held on a foreign exchange?
Generally yes if the foreign exchange qualifies as a "financial account" under FinCEN rules. The IRS has indicated that virtual currency accounts on foreign exchanges are reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) if the aggregate value exceeds $10,000. Bitcoin held in pure self-custody (hardware wallet, no exchange) does not trigger FBAR obligations since there is no foreign financial account. FATCA (Form 8938) thresholds are higher and vary by filing status. Both require careful assessment for clients who use or have used international exchanges.
How do I get CPE credit for Bitcoin and digital asset education?
The AICPA offers formal CPE courses on digital asset taxation. The Digital Assets Council of Financial Professionals (DACFP) offers the Certificate in Blockchain and Digital Assets, which carries CPE credit for CFPs and CPAs. Several state CPA societies offer digital asset tax sessions at annual conferences. Bitcoin Magazine Pro and other Bitcoin-native publications don't carry CPE credit but are valuable for building domain expertise that no CPE course currently covers at adequate depth.
In a grantor trust holding Bitcoin, who pays the income tax?
In a grantor trust, the grantor (the person who established the trust) pays income tax on trust income, including any capital gains from Bitcoin sales within the trust. This is actually a feature, not a bug, for estate planning: the grantor paying income tax on trust gains effectively makes tax-free gifts to the trust beneficiaries. In a non-grantor trust, the trust itself is a separate taxpayer and pays tax at compressed trust income tax rates, which reach the top rate at a very low income threshold ($14,450 in 2025 for ordinary income).
