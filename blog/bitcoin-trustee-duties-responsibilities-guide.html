Trustee Duties and Responsibilities When Administering a Bitcoin Trust

Published March 16, 2026 · 22 min read

You were just named trustee of a trust holding Bitcoin. Here is exactly what that means, what you owe the beneficiaries, and how to avoid the mistakes that create personal liability.

Getting a letter — or a phone call from an attorney — telling you that you have been named trustee of a Bitcoin trust is one of those life moments that combines honor with low-grade panic. Someone trusted you enough to put you in charge of real wealth for the people they care about most. That trust comes with a legal framework you need to understand before you touch a single satoshi.

This guide is written for the person who just received that news. Maybe you have some familiarity with Bitcoin. Maybe you have administered a trust before. But probably not both at the same time — and certainly not in combination with the unique custody, tax, and volatility issues that make Bitcoin trusts unlike anything else in estate planning.

We are going to cover every major duty, walk through the real risks, and give you a concrete action plan for your first year. If you are also in the process of building your own Bitcoin estate plan, much of this will inform how you choose and prepare your own trustee.

What a Trustee Actually Is (and Is Not)

A trustee is a fiduciary. That single word carries more legal weight than any other term in trust law. It means you are legally required to act in the best interests of the trust beneficiaries — not your own interests, not the interests of the person who created the trust (the settlor), and not the interests of any one beneficiary at the expense of another.

You are not an owner of the Bitcoin. You hold legal title to it for the benefit of others. You cannot spend it, invest it for your own gain, or make decisions based on what would be convenient for you. Every action you take must be defensible as something a reasonable, prudent person in your position would do.

You are also not a passive holder. A trustee who simply sits on assets and does nothing can be just as liable as one who makes reckless decisions. The law expects active, informed management — and that is doubly true when the trust holds a concentrated position in a volatile asset like Bitcoin.

The Prudent Investor Rule and Bitcoin Concentration

The Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA) governs how trustees must manage trust assets in most states. The core principle: you must invest and manage the trust portfolio as a prudent investor would, considering the purposes, terms, distribution requirements, and other circumstances of the trust.

Under the UPIA, the default rule is that trustees should diversify investments. A trust holding 100% of its value in a single asset — whether that is Apple stock, commercial real estate, or Bitcoin — creates a concentrated position that a prudent investor would normally reduce.

Here is where Bitcoin trusts get interesting. The UPIA also says the duty to diversify can be modified or eliminated by the terms of the trust. If the settlor created a trust specifically to hold Bitcoin — and the trust document says "the trustee shall retain Bitcoin and is not required to diversify" — that language typically overrides the default diversification duty.

Your first job is to read the trust document carefully and identify whether the settlor included retention language. Look for phrases like:

  • "The trustee is authorized to retain Bitcoin without regard to diversification"
  • "The settlor's intent is that Bitcoin be held as a long-term store of value"
  • "The duty to diversify under the UPIA is waived with respect to Bitcoin holdings"

If such language exists, you generally have the legal cover to maintain the Bitcoin position. If it does not exist, you face a harder question: can you justify holding a concentrated Bitcoin position under the prudent investor standard? The answer depends on the trust's overall circumstances — its size, the beneficiaries' other resources, the distribution timeline, and current market conditions.

When in doubt, get a legal opinion. A written memo from a trust attorney confirming that retention is consistent with the trust terms and the UPIA is one of the cheapest forms of liability protection available to you. This is especially important for dynasty trusts where the holding period spans generations.

Duty of Loyalty and Impartiality

The duty of loyalty is straightforward: do not engage in self-dealing. Do not buy trust Bitcoin for yourself. Do not lend trust Bitcoin to your own business. Do not use trust funds to pay your personal expenses. Violations of the duty of loyalty are treated harshly by courts — they can result in personal liability, removal as trustee, and even criminal charges in extreme cases.

The duty of impartiality is more nuanced and comes up constantly in Bitcoin trusts. If the trust has both income beneficiaries (people entitled to distributions during their lifetime) and remainder beneficiaries (people who receive the trust assets after the income beneficiaries die), you must balance their interests.

Bitcoin does not generate income in the traditional sense. There are no dividends, no interest payments, no rental income. This creates a structural tension: income beneficiaries may feel shortchanged because there is nothing to distribute, while remainder beneficiaries benefit from Bitcoin's long-term appreciation.

Several approaches can address this tension:

  • Unitrust conversion: Many states allow trusts to convert to a unitrust format where a fixed percentage (typically 3-5%) of trust value is distributed annually, regardless of whether the asset produces income.
  • Power to adjust: Some trust documents give the trustee power to reclassify capital gains as income for distribution purposes.
  • Specific distribution provisions: The trust may specify distribution amounts or formulas that bypass the income vs. principal distinction entirely.

Review the trust document for the distribution standard. If the trust is silent on how to handle a non-income-producing asset, consult with a trust attorney about your state's unitrust statute or power-to-adjust provisions.

Distribution Standards — Understanding HEMS

Many trusts use an ascertainable standard for distributions, most commonly Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support (HEMS). This language is not decorative — it serves a critical tax function by limiting the beneficiary's power over trust assets, which keeps the trust outside the beneficiary's taxable estate.

As trustee, you must evaluate every distribution request against the HEMS standard. A beneficiary asking for funds to pay medical bills or college tuition clearly falls within HEMS. A beneficiary asking for $500,000 to buy a vacation home requires more analysis — does the beneficiary's accustomed standard of living include vacation properties?

With Bitcoin trusts, distribution requests raise additional considerations:

  • Liquidation impact: Selling Bitcoin to fund a distribution triggers capital gains tax. The cost basis of the Bitcoin sold, the current price, and the trust's tax bracket all affect how much the beneficiary actually receives versus how much goes to taxes.
  • Timing: If Bitcoin is trading at $74,000 today but the distribution is not urgent, you have discretion to time the sale thoughtfully — though you should never try to "time the market" in a speculative sense.
  • In-kind distributions: Depending on the trust terms and state law, you may be able to distribute Bitcoin directly to the beneficiary rather than selling it first. This can shift the tax burden to the beneficiary and preserve the trust's Bitcoin position.
  • Documentation: Record your reasoning for every distribution decision. If you approve a distribution, document why it meets the HEMS standard. If you deny one, document why it does not. This contemporaneous record is your best defense if a beneficiary later challenges your decisions.

Under the current 2026 federal estate and gift tax framework, the lifetime exemption sits at approximately $15 million per person, with an annual gift exclusion of $19,000. These numbers matter when evaluating whether distributions from the trust interact with the beneficiary's own estate planning.

Recordkeeping and Accounting Obligations

Trustees have a duty to maintain accurate records and provide accountings to beneficiaries. With Bitcoin, this obligation has layers that traditional trustees never had to think about.

Cost Basis Tracking

You need to know — and be able to prove — the cost basis of every Bitcoin unit in the trust. If the settlor transferred Bitcoin into the trust during their lifetime, the trust takes the settlor's original cost basis (carryover basis). If the Bitcoin passed into the trust at death, the trust receives a stepped-up basis equal to the fair market value on the date of death (or the alternate valuation date if the estate elected it).

This distinction matters enormously. A trust holding 108 Bitcoin at today's price of roughly $74,000 per coin has a total value near $8 million. If the Bitcoin entered the trust at death with a stepped-up basis, the trust's cost basis is approximately $8 million and a sale today triggers minimal gain. If the settlor transferred Bitcoin with a $5,000 original cost basis per coin, the trust is sitting on nearly $7.5 million in unrealized capital gains.

Maintain a detailed spreadsheet or use crypto tax software (tools like CoinTracker, Koinly, or TaxBit) that tracks:

  • Date each Bitcoin unit was acquired by or transferred into the trust
  • Cost basis per unit (carryover or stepped-up)
  • Any sales, exchanges, or distributions and the resulting gain or loss
  • Wallet addresses and transaction IDs for on-chain verification

Annual Trust Accounting

Most states require trustees to provide annual accountings to current beneficiaries. A trust accounting typically includes opening balances, all receipts and income, all disbursements and expenses, gains and losses on asset dispositions, and closing balances.

For a Bitcoin trust, the accounting should also include the number of Bitcoin held, the fair market value at the accounting date, any staking or lending income (if applicable), and custody arrangements. Transparency builds trust with beneficiaries and reduces the likelihood of disputes.

Custody Standards — Protecting the Bitcoin

Custody is where Bitcoin trusts diverge most dramatically from traditional trusts. When a trust holds publicly traded securities, the trustee opens a brokerage account and the custodian (Schwab, Fidelity, etc.) handles safekeeping. The risk of total loss is essentially zero.

Bitcoin is different. If the private keys are lost or stolen, the Bitcoin is gone — permanently, irreversibly, with no SIPC insurance backstop. This makes custody architecture the single most consequential decision you will make as trustee.

Your Custody Options

Qualified custodian. Institutions like Coinbase Custody, Fidelity Digital Assets, BitGo Trust, and Anchorage Digital hold assets under a qualified custodian framework with insurance, SOC 2 audits, and regulatory oversight. This is the most conservative option and the easiest to defend under the prudent investor standard. Fees typically range from 0.10% to 0.50% of assets annually.

Multisignature self-custody. A multisig arrangement requires multiple private keys (for example, 2-of-3 or 3-of-5) to authorize any transaction. The trustee holds one key, a co-trustee or trust protector holds another, and a third may be held by a collaborative custody provider like Unchained or Casa. This approach eliminates single points of failure while keeping the trust in control of its own Bitcoin.

Single-key self-custody. The trustee holds one private key (typically on a hardware wallet) and is solely responsible for its security. This is the riskiest option from a fiduciary standpoint. If you lose the key, the Bitcoin is gone. If someone steals the key, the Bitcoin is gone. And you are personally liable for the loss.

Liability for Loss

If trust Bitcoin is lost due to a custody failure — a hack, a lost key, a phishing attack — the trustee can be held personally liable for the full value of the lost Bitcoin. Courts will ask: did the trustee take reasonable steps to secure the asset? Using a qualified custodian or a well-implemented multisig arrangement demonstrates reasonable care. Keeping $8 million in Bitcoin on a single hardware wallet in your desk drawer does not.

Document your custody decisions. Write a memo explaining which custody solution you selected, why you selected it, what alternatives you considered, and what security measures are in place. Update this memo annually or whenever custody arrangements change.

Co-Trustee and Trust Protector Coordination

Many Bitcoin trusts name co-trustees or appoint a trust protector. Understanding these roles prevents confusion and conflict.

Co-trustees share fiduciary responsibility. In most states, co-trustees must act unanimously unless the trust document says otherwise. This means both (or all) co-trustees must agree on investment decisions, distributions, and custody arrangements. Co-trusteeship works well when one trustee has financial expertise and another has Bitcoin technical expertise, but it can create deadlocks if the co-trustees disagree.

A trust protector is a role with specific powers defined in the trust document — often including the power to amend administrative provisions, change trust situs (the state whose law governs the trust), remove and replace trustees, and modify distribution standards. The trust protector is not a trustee and does not have fiduciary duties in all states, though this varies.

If the trust has a trust protector, establish a communication protocol early. The trust protector should know who you are, how to reach you, and what custody arrangements are in place. If the trust protector has the power to remove you as trustee, maintaining a professional and transparent relationship is in everyone's interest — including yours.

When to Hire Professional Advisors

Being named trustee does not mean you have to do everything yourself. In fact, the prudent investor rule explicitly contemplates that trustees will delegate functions that require specialized expertise. The key: you must select advisors carefully, define the scope of their engagement, and monitor their performance.

For a Bitcoin trust, you will likely need some combination of:

  • Trust and estate attorney: To interpret the trust document, advise on distribution decisions, handle any judicial proceedings, and ensure compliance with state trust law. Hire one immediately — before you make any substantive decisions.
  • CPA or tax advisor with crypto experience: To prepare the trust's annual income tax return (Form 1041), track cost basis, and advise on the tax implications of distributions and sales. Not every CPA understands crypto taxation. Ask specifically about their experience with digital asset reporting.
  • Bitcoin custody specialist: To implement and maintain the trust's custody architecture. This might be a collaborative custody provider (Unchained, Casa) or an internal security consultant who can set up and audit multisig arrangements.
  • Investment advisor (if applicable): If the trust holds assets other than Bitcoin, or if you need advice on whether and how to diversify, a registered investment advisor with fiduciary obligations can provide documented recommendations.

The cost of professional advisors is a legitimate trust expense and can be paid from trust assets. Do not let the cost deter you — the cost of making an uninformed decision that results in personal liability is always higher.

The Duty to Diversify vs. the Settlor's Bitcoin-Only Intent

This is the tension that keeps Bitcoin trust attorneys employed. The UPIA says diversify. The settlor says hold Bitcoin. Which controls?

The trust document controls, but only to the extent it actually addresses the question. Clear retention language that specifically waives the duty to diversify with respect to Bitcoin gives you strong protection. Vague language like "the trustee may invest in any asset the trustee deems appropriate" is less protective — it gives you discretion but does not explicitly authorize concentration.

Even with clear retention language, you are not off the hook entirely. You still have a duty to monitor the investment and consider whether changed circumstances warrant revisiting the settlor's original intent. If Bitcoin's fundamental thesis changed dramatically — if a catastrophic protocol vulnerability were discovered, for example — blind adherence to a "hold forever" instruction could itself be a breach of fiduciary duty.

The practical approach: respect the settlor's intent as expressed in the trust document, document your ongoing analysis of whether retention remains prudent, and consult with advisors if circumstances change materially. Do not sell Bitcoin simply because the price dropped. Do not hold Bitcoin simply because the price is rising. Make decisions based on the trust's specific needs and the settlor's expressed wishes.

Reporting to Beneficiaries

Beneficiaries have a right to information about the trust. The scope of that right varies by state, but at minimum, current beneficiaries are generally entitled to:

  • A copy of the trust document (or at least the portions relevant to their interest)
  • Annual accountings showing trust assets, income, expenses, and distributions
  • Notice of trustee changes
  • Reasonably prompt responses to reasonable requests for information

For Bitcoin trusts, consider providing beneficiaries with quarterly or semi-annual statements that include the number of Bitcoin held, current market value, any transactions during the period, and trust expenses. You do not need to share private keys, wallet addresses, or custody details — in fact, sharing that information could create security risks.

Set expectations early. When you first accept the trusteeship, send a letter to all current beneficiaries introducing yourself, explaining how you plan to administer the trust, what information you will provide and how often, and how beneficiaries can contact you with questions or distribution requests. This proactive communication prevents the adversarial dynamic that develops when beneficiaries feel kept in the dark.

Tax Filing — Form 1041 and the K-1

A trust is a separate tax entity. It files its own income tax return — Form 1041 — annually, and issues Schedule K-1s to beneficiaries who received distributions during the tax year.

For a Bitcoin trust, the tax return must reflect:

  • Capital gains and losses from any Bitcoin sold during the year. Short-term gains (Bitcoin held less than one year) are taxed at ordinary income rates. Long-term gains (held more than one year) receive preferential capital gains rates.
  • Trust income from any other sources (interest on cash held pending distribution, for example).
  • Deductions for trustee fees, professional advisor fees, and other trust administration expenses.
  • Distributions to beneficiaries, which carry out distributable net income (DNI) and are reported on the beneficiaries' individual returns via the K-1.

Trusts reach the highest marginal tax bracket ($14,451 in income for 2026) far faster than individuals. This compressed bracket makes tax planning essential. Distributing income to beneficiaries in lower tax brackets can reduce overall tax burden — but distributions must be consistent with the trust terms and the HEMS standard.

File the Form 1041 by April 15 of the year following the tax year, or request an automatic extension to September 30. The trust will need its own Employer Identification Number (EIN), which you can obtain online from the IRS in minutes.

Handling Volatile Markets Without Panic-Selling

Bitcoin's price volatility is its most psychologically challenging feature for trustees. A 30-40% drawdown — which has occurred multiple times in Bitcoin's history — can feel catastrophic when you are personally responsible for the assets.

Here is how to handle it:

Anchor to the trust document. If the settlor intended for Bitcoin to be held long-term and the trust document reflects that intent, a price decline does not change your mandate. The settlor knew Bitcoin was volatile when they created the trust.

Do not make reactive decisions. Selling Bitcoin after a sharp decline locks in losses and likely violates the settlor's intent. Unless you have a specific, documented reason related to trust administration (an upcoming distribution obligation, for example), hold.

Communicate with beneficiaries. Volatility makes beneficiaries nervous, and nervous beneficiaries call lawyers. Proactive communication during drawdowns — a brief note explaining that the trust's long-term strategy remains unchanged and that you are monitoring the situation — prevents panic from escalating into litigation.

Document your analysis. Write a memo to the trust file explaining market conditions, your assessment, and your decision to hold (or sell, if that is what you decide). Contemporaneous documentation is the trustee's best friend.

Maintain a cash reserve. The trust should hold enough cash or liquid assets to cover anticipated distributions and expenses for 12-24 months. This prevents you from being forced to sell Bitcoin at distressed prices to meet obligations.

What Happens If the Trustee Dies or Resigns

Trustees are mortal and sometimes need to step away. The trust document should address succession — naming one or more successor trustees and specifying the process for transition.

Resignation

Most trust documents allow the trustee to resign by providing written notice to the beneficiaries and any co-trustee or trust protector. Some require court approval. Check the trust document for the specific resignation procedure.

Before resigning, ensure a successor trustee is in place and prepared to take over. A gap in trusteeship creates a dangerous period where no one has legal authority to manage or protect the Bitcoin. In Bitcoin terms, this is like leaving a multisig wallet with no one holding the required keys.

Death of the Trustee

If you are the sole trustee and you die, the successor trustee named in the document steps in. If no successor is named, a court will appoint one — a process that can take weeks or months. During that gap, the Bitcoin sits unmanaged.

This is why Bitcoin trusts need robust succession planning and why custody arrangements must account for trustee incapacity or death. If you are the trustee of a Bitcoin trust, make sure that:

  • At least one other person (successor trustee, co-trustee, or trust protector) knows that the trust exists and holds Bitcoin
  • Custody arrangements do not depend solely on your personal access — multisig setups with a key held by a collaborative custody provider solve this
  • A letter of instruction exists explaining how the successor trustee can access the Bitcoin, including custody provider contacts, account identifiers, and the general architecture (without including private keys in the letter itself)

Personal Liability for Trustee Errors

This is the section that should keep you honest. Trustees who breach their fiduciary duties can be held personally liable for losses caused by the breach. "Personally liable" means your assets — your home, your savings, your retirement accounts — can be used to compensate the trust for your mistakes.

Common breaches that create personal liability in Bitcoin trusts include:

  • Custody failures: Losing private keys, falling for phishing scams, using inadequate security measures
  • Self-dealing: Using trust Bitcoin for personal purposes, even temporarily
  • Failure to diversify: If the trust document does not waive diversification and you maintain a concentrated Bitcoin position without documented justification
  • Improper distributions: Distributing trust assets in a way that violates the trust terms or the HEMS standard
  • Failure to account: Not providing required accountings to beneficiaries
  • Tax failures: Not filing Form 1041, not paying estimated taxes, or not tracking cost basis accurately
  • Inaction: Failing to take any action when action was required — for example, not securing the Bitcoin after the settlor's death

Some trust documents include exculpation clauses that protect the trustee from liability for good-faith errors. These clauses generally cannot protect against willful misconduct, gross negligence, or bad faith — but they can provide protection for honest mistakes made with reasonable care.

Trustee liability insurance (errors and omissions coverage) is available and worth investigating for high-value trusts. The trust can typically pay the premium as an administration expense.

Case Study — James Whitfield's Year 1 as Trustee of an $8M Bitcoin Dynasty Trust

James Whitfield is 52 years old. His older brother, Robert, died unexpectedly in January 2026. Robert was an early Bitcoin adopter who accumulated approximately 108 Bitcoin over the past decade. Robert's estate plan included a dynasty trust funded with all of his Bitcoin — now worth approximately $8 million at Bitcoin's current price near $74,000.

The trust names James as sole trustee. The beneficiaries are Robert's two adult children, ages 24 and 28, and their future descendants. The trust uses a HEMS distribution standard and includes language authorizing the trustee to retain Bitcoin "without regard to the duty to diversify under the Uniform Prudent Investor Act."

James has managed his own finances responsibly, but he has never served as trustee, has limited Bitcoin knowledge, and has no experience with crypto custody. Here is what James's first year should look like.

Month 1 — Orientation and Legal Framework

  1. Read the trust document cover to cover. James reads it twice. He highlights the retention language, the HEMS standard, the trust protector provisions (Robert named a trusted attorney as trust protector), and the successor trustee provisions.
  2. Hire a trust and estate attorney. James engages a firm experienced in digital asset trusts. The attorney provides a memo confirming that the trust's retention language waives the duty to diversify and that James's primary obligations are prudent custody, proper accounting, and HEMS-compliant distributions.
  3. Obtain an EIN for the trust. James applies online at IRS.gov and receives the EIN immediately.
  4. Confirm the cost basis. Because the Bitcoin passed to the trust at Robert's death, it receives a stepped-up basis. The attorney coordinates with the estate's executor to establish the date-of-death value. At $74,000 per coin for 108 Bitcoin, the stepped-up basis is approximately $7.99 million.

Month 2 — Custody Architecture

  1. Assess current custody. Robert held the Bitcoin in a 2-of-3 multisig arrangement through Unchained, with one key on his personal hardware wallet, one held by Unchained, and one in a safety deposit box. James's attorney contacts Unchained to begin the key transition process.
  2. Establish trust custody. James decides to maintain the multisig approach — he takes possession of Robert's hardware wallet key (after legal verification), Unchained retains their key, and the trust protector takes custody of the backup key. James documents the entire arrangement in a custody memo to the trust file.
  3. Set up trust bank account. James opens a checking account in the trust's name to hold cash for distributions, expenses, and tax payments.

Month 3 — Professional Team and Beneficiary Communication

  1. Hire a crypto-experienced CPA. James engages a CPA firm that specializes in digital asset taxation. They set up cost basis tracking software and begin preparing for the trust's first tax return.
  2. Contact beneficiaries. James sends a formal letter to Robert's two children introducing himself as trustee, explaining the trust's basic structure (without sharing sensitive custody details), describing what information they will receive and when, and providing his contact information for distribution requests or questions.
  3. Contact the trust protector. James meets with the trust protector to establish a communication cadence and discuss the trust's strategy and custody arrangements.

Months 4-6 — Operational Rhythm

  1. Evaluate cash needs. James sells a small amount of Bitcoin (2 coins, approximately $148,000) to establish a cash reserve for trustee fees, professional advisor fees, estimated tax payments, and potential distributions. Because of the stepped-up basis, the capital gain is minimal.
  2. Process first distribution request. Robert's 24-year-old daughter requests $35,000 for graduate school tuition. James evaluates it against the HEMS standard — education is explicitly covered — documents his analysis, approves the distribution, and makes the payment from the trust checking account.
  3. File estimated tax payments. The CPA calculates estimated taxes owed based on the small Bitcoin sale and any income earned on the cash reserve. James files Form 1041-ES on the quarterly schedule.

Months 7-12 — Monitoring and Reporting

  1. Quarterly statements. James sends beneficiaries a simple statement each quarter showing Bitcoin held, current value, distributions made, and expenses paid.
  2. Annual accounting. At year-end, James prepares (with the CPA's help) a formal trust accounting covering all activity for the year.
  3. First Form 1041 filing. The CPA prepares and files the trust's income tax return and issues K-1s to beneficiaries who received distributions.
  4. Annual review. James meets with his attorney and CPA to review the trust's status, discuss any changes in tax law or market conditions, and plan for the coming year.
  5. Custody audit. James verifies that all keys are secure, the multisig arrangement is functioning properly, and succession procedures remain current.

James's total cost for Year 1 professional services: approximately $15,000-$25,000 in attorney, CPA, and custody provider fees — all paid from trust assets as legitimate administration expenses. Relative to an $8 million trust, that is a rounding error. Relative to the personal liability James would face for making uninformed decisions, it is the best money the trust will ever spend.

Year 1 Action Checklist for Any New Bitcoin Trustee

Whether your trust is worth $800,000 or $80 million, this checklist applies:

  1. Read the trust document completely — at least twice
  2. Hire a trust and estate attorney with digital asset experience
  3. Obtain an EIN for the trust
  4. Determine the cost basis of all Bitcoin (carryover vs. stepped-up)
  5. Evaluate and implement appropriate custody (qualified custodian or multisig)
  6. Document custody decisions in a formal memo
  7. Open a trust bank account for cash management
  8. Hire a CPA experienced in crypto taxation
  9. Set up cost basis and transaction tracking software
  10. Send introductory letter to all current beneficiaries
  11. Contact co-trustees and/or trust protector
  12. Establish a cash reserve for expenses and distributions
  13. File quarterly estimated tax payments (Form 1041-ES)
  14. Prepare and send quarterly statements to beneficiaries
  15. File Form 1041 annual trust income tax return
  16. Conduct annual custody security audit
  17. Prepare formal annual trust accounting
  18. Meet with attorney and CPA for annual review
  19. Review and update successor trustee procedures
  20. Write a letter of instruction for your successor

The Bottom Line

Being named trustee of a Bitcoin trust is a serious responsibility, but it is not an impossible one. The core duties — prudent investment, loyalty, impartiality, accounting, and reporting — are the same as any trust. What changes is the execution: custody requires specialized security, accounting requires crypto-specific tracking, and the duty to diversify requires careful analysis of the settlor's intent.

Build your professional team early. Document everything. Communicate proactively with beneficiaries. And never forget that the Bitcoin in the trust is not yours — you are its guardian, holding it for the people the settlor loved most.
