Bitcoin UTMA: How to Give Bitcoin to Your Children Without a Trust

If you want to start transferring Bitcoin to your kids now — without hiring an attorney or establishing a trust — a UTMA custodial account is the simplest tool available. Here's exactly how it works, what the kiddie tax will cost you, when the child gets full control, and the four situations where a trust does the job better.

Educational Content Only: This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal, tax, or financial advice. UTMA rules vary by state. Consult a qualified estate planning attorney and tax advisor before transferring significant assets to a UTMA account.

What Is a UTMA Account?

The Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (UTMA) is a law adopted in nearly every state that allows adults to make gifts to minor children through a custodial account. The adult custodian manages the account on the child's behalf until the child reaches the age of majority — at which point the child gains full, unrestricted control.

UTMA accounts are the simplest vehicle for transferring assets to children:

  • No attorney required — you can open one at most exchanges in minutes
  • No separate trust document, no EIN, no annual tax filing (unless the account generates reportable income)
  • Any US person can serve as custodian — typically a parent, grandparent, or trusted adult
  • The gift is irrevocable — once Bitcoin is in the UTMA, it belongs to the child

Bitcoin UTMAs are increasingly practical as major exchanges support custodial account structures. The mechanics are simple: open a custodial account for your child at an exchange that supports UTMA accounts, transfer Bitcoin into it, and you're the custodian until they reach majority.

Age of Majority: When Your Child Gets the Keys

This is the critical variable — and the one most parents don't think about until it's too late to change it. UTMA age of majority by state:

Age of Majority States
18 Alabama, Alaska, Arkansas, Georgia, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, Maine, Michigan, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, New Jersey (21 also available), North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah, Virginia, West Virginia, Wyoming
21 Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Iowa, Louisiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Missouri, New Hampshire, New Mexico, New York, North Dakota, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Texas, Vermont, Washington, Wisconsin
Up to 25 California (custodian may specify 18, 21, or 25 at time of gift), Delaware (18 or 21), Nevada (18 or 21), South Carolina (18 or 21)

The age-of-majority transfer is automatic and unconditional. A 21-year-old who receives $500,000 in Bitcoin from a UTMA account has complete, unrestricted access — they can sell it, give it away, or lose it in a day. Unlike a trust, there is no trustee who can delay the transfer, impose conditions, or exercise discretion.

This is why the trust is often better for larger amounts: the trust can hold Bitcoin until age 35 or 40 with incentive conditions, while the UTMA hands over full control at 18-25 with no strings attached.

The Gift Tax Rules

Transferring Bitcoin to a UTMA account is a completed gift for gift tax purposes. The gift qualifies as a "present interest" gift — the child has an immediate beneficial interest, even though the custodian manages it — which means it qualifies for the annual gift tax exclusion.

  • Annual exclusion (2026): $19,000 per donor per recipient. A married couple can give $38,000 per child per year (gift splitting) with no gift tax return required.
  • Above the exclusion: Transfers above $19,000 count against your lifetime gift and estate tax exemption. A Form 709 gift tax return must be filed, but no gift tax is due until lifetime gifts exceed the exemption (~$13.99M in 2026, potentially $15M under OBBBA).
  • Basis carries over: The child takes your original cost basis in the gifted Bitcoin. When they sell at majority, they pay capital gains tax on appreciation from your original purchase price — not from the date of the UTMA gift.

Annual Bitcoin UTMA Gifting Strategy

Married couple with 3 children. Annual gift: $38,000 per child (split gift) = $114,000/year in Bitcoin removed from the taxable estate, tax-free, via UTMA accounts. Over 10 years: $1,140,000 removed from estate — and all appreciation on those gifts compounds outside the parents' estate.

At $71,000/BTC: roughly 0.54 BTC per child per year. Over 10 years: ~5.4 BTC per child in UTMA accounts, accumulated at various cost bases.

The Kiddie Tax: Don't Ignore It

The "kiddie tax" (IRC §1(g)) is the IRS rule that prevents parents from shifting investment income to children at lower tax rates. For Bitcoin UTMA accounts:

  • First $1,350: Tax-free (2026 standard deduction for dependent)
  • Next $1,350: Taxed at child's rate (0% if child has no other income)
  • Above $2,700: Taxed at the parent's marginal rate — up to 37% plus 3.8% NIIT = 40.8%

The kiddie tax applies until the child turns 19 (or 24 if a full-time student). After that, all UTMA income and gains are taxed at the child's own rate.

Practical implication: If you gift Bitcoin with a low cost basis into a UTMA account and the child (or custodian) sells it before the child turns 19 or 24, the capital gains are taxed at your rate — defeating the income-shifting purpose. The smarter approach: hold the Bitcoin in the UTMA without selling until after the child is 19 (or 24 if in college), then let the child sell at their own (presumably lower) tax rate.

How to Open a Bitcoin UTMA Account

The process varies by exchange:

  • Coinbase: Does not currently support custodial UTMA accounts for crypto. An alternative is to open a joint account with the minor using a third-party custodial service.
  • Greenlight + others: Some fintech platforms offer custodial investment accounts for minors that include crypto. Check whether Bitcoin is offered specifically — many offer only ETFs or fractional stocks.
  • Self-directed UTMA brokerage: Some UTMA-capable brokerages (Fidelity Youth Account, Schwab's custodial accounts) allow ETF exposure to Bitcoin (e.g., IBIT) but not direct Bitcoin. If direct Bitcoin is the goal, specialized crypto custodians or a trust may be more practical currently.
  • State-chartered approach: In states with clear digital asset UTMA recognition, an attorney can structure a custodial arrangement for direct Bitcoin holdings. The custodian holds the private keys as a fiduciary for the minor's account.

For families wanting simple, direct Bitcoin (not ETF) in a UTMA, the infrastructure is still maturing. Practically, the most reliable current approach for direct Bitcoin transfers to children is either: (1) a formal trust (more setup, more flexibility), or (2) a custodial arrangement documented with a simple assignment agreement, with a parent holding keys as fiduciary for the child.

The Rule of Thumb

Use a UTMA when the gift is small enough that you're comfortable handing it over unconditionally at 18-21, and when the simplicity of not hiring an attorney is valuable. Use a trust when the amount is significant enough to justify the setup cost, when you want conditions or extended control beyond the UTMA's age limit, or when you're concerned about creditor protection or behavioral risk at majority.

For most Bitcoin-wealthy families, the UTMA is useful for systematic annual gifting ($10K–$19K per child per year) — accumulating small positions that the child will receive at majority. The primary family wealth (multi-BTC positions) stays in the estate plan's trust structure where it has proper conditions and protection.

What Happens to a UTMA If the Child Dies Before Majority?

If the minor dies before reaching the age of majority, the UTMA assets pass through the child's estate — not back to the donor. In most states, a minor's estate passes under their parents' intestate succession laws. This means the gifted Bitcoin could pass to the other parent, to siblings, or through a complex legal process depending on state law.

This is another reason a trust is often preferable for larger amounts: a trust can specify exactly what happens to the assets if a beneficiary predeceases the age of distribution — typically redirecting to other children or back into the family estate plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Bitcoin UTMA account?

A Bitcoin UTMA (Uniform Transfers to Minors Act) account is a custodial account holding Bitcoin for a minor child, managed by an adult custodian until the child reaches the age of majority (18–25 depending on state). No attorney or trust document is required. Any gift to a UTMA is irrevocable — it belongs to the child once transferred. At majority, the child gains full, unconditional control of the Bitcoin.

When does a child get control of a Bitcoin UTMA?

Age of majority varies: most states transfer control at 18 or 21. California allows up to 25. The transfer is automatic and unconditional — there is no mechanism to delay it once the child reaches the designated age. For large amounts, this lack of control beyond majority is the primary limitation — a trust can extend control to any age with conditions the UTMA cannot impose.

What is the kiddie tax on Bitcoin UTMA gains?

The kiddie tax (IRC §1(g)) taxes a minor's unearned income above $2,700 (2026) at the parent's marginal rate — up to 40.8% including NIIT. This applies until the child is 19 (or 24 if a full-time student). Capital gains from selling Bitcoin inside a UTMA before that age are largely taxed at your rate, not the child's. Strategy: hold Bitcoin in the UTMA without selling until after the child passes the kiddie tax age threshold.

Is a gift to a Bitcoin UTMA subject to gift tax?

Yes — it's a completed gift subject to the annual exclusion ($19,000 per donor per recipient in 2026; $38,000 for married couples gift-splitting). Transfers within the annual exclusion require no gift tax return. Larger transfers count against your lifetime exemption and require Form 709. The child takes your carryover cost basis — capital gains on appreciation from your original purchase price are the child's tax liability when they eventually sell.

When is a trust better than a UTMA for Bitcoin?

A trust is better when: the amount exceeds ~$100K (justifies attorney cost), the child is young (unconditional access at 18 is risky), you want behavioral conditions (income matching, education requirements), creditor protection is a concern, or you have multiple children sharing the same pool of Bitcoin. UTMAs work well for systematic annual gifting ($10K–$19K/year per child) and for older teenagers approaching majority who have demonstrated financial maturity.
