Bitcoin Step-Up in Basis at Death: What Heirs Need to Know

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  7 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Step-Up in Basis at Death Est. 7 min read

For long-term Bitcoin holders, the step-up in basis at death is one of the most consequential tax provisions in the entire tax code — and one of the least understood. If you bought 1 BTC for $10,000 and it's worth $95,000 when you die, your heir inherits that Bitcoin with a cost basis of $95,000, not $10,000. Sell it at $95,000: zero capital gains tax owed. The $85,000 in embedded appreciation disappears for tax purposes. Permanently.

This is not a loophole or a technicality. It is how IRC §1014 has operated for decades. And for Bitcoin families holding coins acquired at fractions of today's value, the step-up is potentially worth millions of dollars in tax elimination per generation.

How the Step-Up in Basis Works

Under Internal Revenue Code §1014, when a taxpayer inherits property from a decedent, the heir's cost basis in that property is adjusted to the fair market value (FMV) of the property on the date of the decedent's death. This is called the "step-up in basis" — though it can technically also be a step-down if the asset declined in value.

The practical effect for Bitcoin: the IRS treats your inherited BTC as if you purchased it at whatever Bitcoin was worth the day your benefactor died. All appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime simply vanishes from the tax ledger. The heir takes ownership, the tax clock resets, and any future gains are calculated only from the inherited basis forward.

If the heir then sells immediately at the date-of-death price, the gain is zero. If they hold and sell later at a higher price, they only owe capital gains on appreciation above the stepped-up basis. And because the holding period resets to "long-term" automatically for inherited assets (regardless of how long the heir held them), any future gain is taxed at the preferential long-term capital gains rate — currently 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income.

A Bitcoin holder who bought at $10,000 and holds until death eliminates $85,000 in taxable gains per coin — permanently. No trust structure or Bitcoin tax strategy hub produces this result more cleanly.

Why Bitcoin's Appreciation Makes This Especially Powerful

The step-up in basis applies to all capital assets — stocks, real estate, bonds. But Bitcoin's appreciation trajectory over the past decade makes the magnitude of the step-up in the Bitcoin context extraordinary compared to almost any other asset class.

Consider a holder who accumulated 5 BTC in 2017 at an average cost basis of $8,000 per coin ($40,000 total). At $95,000 per coin, their position is now worth $475,000 — with $435,000 in embedded capital gains. If sold today, federal capital gains tax on that appreciation could exceed $65,000 for a taxpayer in the 15% bracket, or over $87,000 at 20%, plus the 3.8% net investment income tax for high-income taxpayers.

At death, all of that evaporates. The heirs inherit a $475,000 position with a $475,000 basis. The entire $435,000 gain is forgiven. This is the step-up at work — and at Bitcoin's pace of appreciation, the numbers compound dramatically across longer holding periods and larger positions.

Step-Up vs. Carryover Basis: The Gifting Trap

One of the most important distinctions in Bitcoin estate planning is the difference between inheritance and gifting. When you gift Bitcoin to someone during your lifetime, they receive your original cost basis — a carryover basis. The appreciated gains do not disappear. They transfer.

If you paid $10,000 for 1 BTC and gift it to your child while you're alive, your child's basis is $10,000. If they later sell at $95,000, they owe capital gains tax on the full $85,000 gain — the same as if you had sold it yourself (though at their tax rate, not yours).

This is often called the "gifting trap" for appreciated assets: gifting transfers the tax liability along with the asset. Inheritance, by contrast, resets the basis entirely. For heavily appreciated Bitcoin, this creates a clear planning principle: hold appreciated Bitcoin until death rather than gifting it outright during your lifetime.

There are exceptions to this general rule. Annual gift exclusion gifts ($18,000 per recipient in 2024) can still make sense for tax-efficient wealth transfer when the recipient is in a lower tax bracket or the basis is relatively close to FMV. And certain trust structures — like grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs) — can transfer appreciation without triggering gift tax while preserving some planning flexibility. But the core principle holds: for heavily appreciated Bitcoin, the step-up is almost always more valuable than outright gifting.

Strategies That Maximize the Step-Up Benefit

Sophisticated Bitcoin estate planning incorporates the step-up deliberately. Key strategies include:

  • Hold, don't gift, your highest-basis-gap Bitcoin. The coins with the largest gap between your original cost and current FMV are the best candidates for step-up treatment at death rather than lifetime gifting.
  • Use revocable living trusts, not irrevocable trusts, for step-up preservation. Assets held in a revocable trust at death generally receive a full step-up in basis. Assets transferred to an irrevocable trust during your lifetime generally do not — they retain your original basis inside the trust. This is one reason Bitcoin families should be thoughtful before locking Bitcoin into irrevocable structures during their lifetime.
  • Coordinate with the federal estate tax exemption. The federal estate tax exemption is $13.99 million per person in 2025 ($27.98M for married couples with portability). For most Bitcoin families, this means no estate tax is owed — and all the inherited Bitcoin receives a full step-up. Families near or above the exemption need more nuanced planning to balance estate tax exposure against the step-up benefit.
  • Consider community property treatment in applicable states. In community property states (California, Bitcoin family office in Texas, Arizona, and others), both spouses' halves of community property receive a step-up at the death of either spouse — meaning 100% of community property Bitcoin gets a new basis when the first spouse dies. This is more favorable than the treatment in common law states, where only the decedent's half steps up.

Documentation Requirements

The step-up in basis requires proper documentation to substantiate the date-of-death FMV. For Bitcoin, this means:

  • Date-of-death valuation records. The executor or administrator of the estate should document Bitcoin's fair market value on the date of death using a reputable exchange price (Coinbase, Kraken, or similar). Screenshot and preserve timestamped price records. The IRS accepts the average of the high and low trading prices on the date of death as FMV.
  • Wallet and custody records. The estate needs to identify and document all Bitcoin wallets and holdings. This requires that the decedent maintained clear documentation of their custody arrangements — wallet addresses, holdings, and access instructions — separate from (but accessible to) the executor.
  • Estate tax return or Form 8971. When an estate files a federal estate tax return (Form 706), the estate is required to report the FMV of assets to the IRS and issue Form 8971 to beneficiaries, establishing their inherited basis. If the estate is below the filing Bitcoin family office minimum requirements and no Form 706 is filed, heirs should still maintain documentation of the date-of-death FMV for their own records.
  • Ongoing basis tracking. Heirs should update their own tax records to reflect the inherited basis. Any future sales of inherited Bitcoin should be reported with the stepped-up basis, not the decedent's original cost.
