How to Value Bitcoin for Estate Tax: IRS Rules, Date-of-Death Pricing, and Volatility Challenges

When a Bitcoin holder dies, the IRS requires their estate to report the fair market value of the Bitcoin on the date of death. For publicly traded stocks, this is straightforward — there is a well-established closing price on a regulated exchange with a single authoritative source. For Bitcoin, the picture is more complicated: prices vary across dozens of exchanges, trading occurs continuously across time zones, and Bitcoin's volatility means that the price can move significantly within a single day.

The valuation question is not merely administrative. For large estates, a difference of even 5% in the reported value of Bitcoin holdings can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in estate tax liability. Getting this right — and documenting it properly — is essential. This guide covers the IRS framework for valuing Bitcoin in an estate, the practical methodology for establishing a defensible date-of-death value, and the strategies available to manage valuation-related risk.

The IRS Framework: Bitcoin as Property

The IRS classifies Bitcoin as property, not currency. This classification, established in Notice 2014-21, has significant estate tax implications. Under the tax code, all property included in a decedent's gross estate must be valued at its fair market value on the date of death — defined as the price at which the property would change hands between a willing buyer and a willing seller, neither being under any compulsion to buy or sell and both having reasonable knowledge of the relevant facts.

This is the same "hypothetical willing buyer / willing seller" standard used to value stocks, real estate, and private business interests. The standard does not require that the property actually be sold on the date of death — it requires a determination of what a willing buyer would have paid on that date.

The IRS has not issued specific guidance on which exchange price to use for Bitcoin valuation or whether to use opening price, closing price, or a midpoint. This creates both uncertainty and opportunity — the estate has some flexibility in choosing a methodology, but must be prepared to defend it in an audit.

The Alternate Valuation Date

Estates have an option: instead of valuing Bitcoin on the date of death, the estate can elect to use the alternate valuation date — six months after the date of death — if:

  1. The election reduces the gross estate value, AND
  2. The election reduces the estate tax liability

Both conditions must be met. The election is all-or-nothing: it applies to all assets in the estate, not just Bitcoin. If Bitcoin has declined significantly in the six months following death but other estate assets have appreciated, the net effect on total estate value determines whether the election makes sense.

For Bitcoin holders who die during or near a market peak, the alternate valuation election can substantially reduce estate tax liability. For those who die during or following a significant price decline, it is irrelevant (the estate tax is already based on a lower value).

Important: property distributed, sold, or exchanged before the alternate valuation date is valued at the price on the distribution/sale/exchange date, not six months after death. This creates an incentive not to distribute or sell Bitcoin during the six-month window if the alternate valuation election may apply.

Practical Methodology: Establishing Date-of-Death Value

In the absence of IRS-specific guidance for Bitcoin, the most defensible approach follows the framework used for other actively traded assets:

Option 1: Average of High and Low Price on Date of Death

For stocks listed on major exchanges, the IRS uses the mean of the high and low prices on the valuation date. This approach is well-established, easily documented, and likely to be accepted by the IRS for Bitcoin. Compute the mean of the intraday high and low for the date of death using a major exchange's price data (Coinbase, Kraken, or Gemini are most commonly cited).

Document: the exchange used, the date, the high price, the low price, the mean, and a screenshot or archived price data showing the source.

Option 2: Closing Price on Date of Death

Use the closing price at midnight UTC on the date of death from a major exchange. This is less conservative than the high/low average if Bitcoin closed near its high for the day, but may be appropriate in certain circumstances. Document as above.

Option 3: Volume-Weighted Average Price (VWAP)

A VWAP — the price weighted by trading volume across exchanges over the 24-hour period of the date of death — is arguably the most defensible "fair market value" for an asset that trades continuously. This is more complex to compute but more defensible in a dispute because it reflects the actual weighted price at which Bitcoin traded hands on the valuation date. Some cryptocurrency data providers (CoinGecko, CoinMarketCap, Brave New Coin) offer historical VWAP data.

What About Large Holdings?

For very large Bitcoin estates, an argument exists for a blockage discount — the idea that selling a very large quantity of Bitcoin would depress the market price, so the estate's Bitcoin is worth less than spot price times quantity. Courts have applied blockage discounts to large stock holdings and art collections. Whether the IRS and courts will apply them to Bitcoin is an open question, but the argument is available for estate attorneys to make on behalf of large estates.

A professional appraisal from a qualified appraiser who can document the liquidity analysis strengthens this argument. For estates holding thousands of Bitcoin, the potential tax savings from a defensible blockage discount analysis likely justify the appraisal cost.

Bitcoin on Exchanges vs. Self-Custody

The valuation methodology is the same regardless of where Bitcoin is held — but the documentation differs:

Custody TypeDocumentation Required
Exchange accountAccount statement showing balance on date of death; exchange's historical price data for that date
Self-custody (hardware wallet / multisig)On-chain address balance from block explorer as of the block nearest date of death; price data for that block's timestamp or the date as above
Institutional custodianCustodian's account statement; their internal valuation methodology (document this)
Bitcoin in an LLC or trustThe LLC's or trust's Bitcoin balance, valued as above; the estate reports the value of the LLC/trust interest, which may include valuation discounts

For self-custodied Bitcoin, the key documentation is the on-chain address and the block height (or timestamp) that establishes the balance as of the date of death. This is public and verifiable — which is an advantage in an audit.

Bitcoin Price Volatility: The Unique Challenge

Bitcoin can move 10-20% in a single day during volatile markets. This creates a situation unique in estate tax administration: the value of the estate's largest asset may look very different depending on which hour of the date of death is used as the reference point.

Several practical implications:

  • Time of death matters. For very large estates, the time of death determines which 24-hour period is the "date of death" for price purposes. This is already critical for stock valuations; for Bitcoin it is even more so.
  • Document the price immediately. As soon as possible after a Bitcoin holder's death, document the current price from multiple exchanges with timestamps. This creates a contemporaneous record that is much harder for the IRS to challenge than a reconstruction months later.
  • Consider the alternate valuation date scenario proactively. Even before the decedent's death (in estate planning), consider whether a significant portion of the estate should be in structures that reduce the volatility of the date-of-death valuation issue.

The Stepped-Up Basis: The Most Important Valuation Consequence

The valuation of Bitcoin at date of death does more than determine estate tax. It establishes the stepped-up cost basis for the heirs. Under current law, heirs inherit Bitcoin with a basis equal to its fair market value on the date of death — regardless of what the original owner paid.

If you purchased Bitcoin at $10,000 and it's worth $200,000 at your death, your heirs inherit it with a $200,000 basis. They owe no capital gains tax on the prior $190,000 appreciation. This is one of the most significant wealth transfer tools in the tax code — and it is specific to assets that pass through the estate at death (rather than being gifted during life).

The stepped-up basis is why, for families with large embedded gains in Bitcoin, holding until death and paying estate tax (if the estate is taxable) may sometimes produce a better overall outcome than gifting during life to avoid estate tax. The tax analysis requires modeling both scenarios. Work with a qualified CPA and estate attorney who understand digital asset taxation — not all practitioners do. The full context is covered in Bitcoin tax optimization for high-net-worth investors.

Estate Tax Return Filing Requirements

If the gross estate exceeds the estate tax filing threshold (currently $13.99 million), the executor must file Form 706 (United States Estate Tax Return) within nine months of the date of death (with a possible six-month extension). Bitcoin holdings must be disclosed and valued on Form 706.

Required documentation attached to Form 706:

  • A schedule listing all Bitcoin held, with on-chain addresses (for self-custody) or account numbers (for exchange/custodian accounts)
  • The methodology used to establish fair market value on the date of death
  • Source documentation for the price used (exchange data, screenshots, data provider records)
  • If a blockage discount was applied: a qualified appraisal supporting the discount
  • If the alternate valuation date was elected: a statement and recalculation at the alternate date values

Inaccurate or under-documented Bitcoin valuations on Form 706 can trigger penalties — a 20% accuracy-related penalty applies to substantial understatements of value. For estates holding significant Bitcoin, a professional appraisal is worth considering even when not technically required.

Planning Implications

Understanding Bitcoin valuation for estate tax purposes has several planning implications:

  • GRATs funded when Bitcoin is lower transfer more appreciation tax-free — and the initial value is the GRAT's baseline for annuity calculation purposes
  • Annual gifts use current fair market value — document the price at the time of each gift with the same rigor as an estate valuation
  • Dynasty trust contributions require the same valuation methodology — the gift tax return (Form 709) must document the value of Bitcoin contributed
  • Charitable contributions of Bitcoin require a qualified appraisal for deductions above $5,000 (for non-cash charitable contributions) — document the value carefully
