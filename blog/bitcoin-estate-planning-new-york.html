Bitcoin Estate Planning in New York

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

New York presents a formidable tax environment for Bitcoin families — a top state income tax rate of 10.9%, an estate tax with a uniquely punishing "cliff" effect, and complex rules governing trust taxation that have been actively litigated. Yet New York remains a major hub for Bitcoin wealth, institutional activity, and financial infrastructure. For families who are in New York by choice or necessity, understanding the specific planning challenges — and the tools that address them — is not optional.

New York Income Tax: 10.9% on Bitcoin Gains

New York imposes a top marginal income tax rate of 10.9% on individuals with taxable income above $25 million (for 2023 and beyond under the Budget Act of 2021). For most high-income taxpayers, the more relevant bracket is 9.65% on income between $1,077,550 and $25 million. New York City residents face an additional city income tax of up to 3.876%. A NYC-resident Bitcoin holder at the top bracket faces a combined federal, state, and city effective capital gains rate that can exceed 54% when including the net investment income tax — among the highest in the developed world.

New York treats capital gains as ordinary income for state purposes, applying the same rates to long-term capital gains on Bitcoin as to wages. There is no state capital gains preference, no deferral election, and no holding period that reduces state tax. Every realized gain is fully taxable at the ordinary income rate applicable to that taxpayer's bracket.

The New York Estate Tax Cliff Effect

New York's state estate tax (New York Tax Law §§952–960) has a structure that is genuinely punishing and unlike any other state: the so-called "cliff effect." New York's estate tax exemption is $7,160,000 for 2024 (indexed for inflation). If an estate exceeds that exemption by more than 5%, the exemption is entirely phased out — meaning the entire estate is taxed from the first dollar, not just the amount above the exemption.

For a Bitcoin family whose estate is valued at $7.6 million — just 6% above the exemption — none of the $7.16 million exemption applies. The tax is calculated on the full $7.6 million at rates reaching 16%. An estate of $7.15 million owes zero New York estate tax; an estate of $7.6 million may owe over $1 million. The math creates a dangerous valuation zone around the exemption threshold where even modest Bitcoin price appreciation can push an estate from zero to six-figure New York estate tax liability.

New York's estate tax cliff is unlike anything else in U.S. estate planning: cross the exemption threshold by 5%, and your entire estate becomes taxable — not just the excess. For Bitcoin families near that threshold, the exposure can shift dramatically with Bitcoin's price.

This cliff effect creates specific planning imperatives:

  • Gifting strategies: Annual exclusion gifts ($18,000 per recipient in 2024) and lifetime taxable gifts made more than three years before death remove assets from the New York gross estate. New York does not have a gift tax (it is decoupled from the federal gift tax), but "deathbed gifts" made within three years of death are clawed back into the New York gross estate under New York Tax Law §954.
  • Irrevocable trust funding: Properly structured irrevocable trusts — including GRATs, SLATs, and dynasty trusts — can remove Bitcoin from the New York gross estate if funded more than three years before death. Timing matters significantly.
  • Life insurance: An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) can provide liquidity to pay New York estate tax without requiring a forced Bitcoin sale — a critical planning tool for illiquid Bitcoin estates near the cliff threshold.

New York Trust Rules: The Incomplete Gift Non-Grantor Trust

New York has specific — and in some cases unique — rules governing resident trusts. Under New York Tax Law §605(b)(3), a trust is taxed as a New York resident trust if it was created by a New York resident and has at least one New York-resident trustee, or if the trust received assets from a New York-decedent's estate. However, New York has also recognized the "Incomplete Gift Non-Grantor Trust" (ING trust) as a planning vehicle.

A properly structured New York ING trust — sometimes called a "NING" — can allow a New York resident to transfer assets to an out-of-state trust (typically Nevada or South Dakota) while retaining certain powers that prevent the transfer from being treated as a completed gift for gift tax purposes, while simultaneously removing the assets from the New York grantor trust rules and the New York taxable estate. The result: the trust accumulates income in a no-income-tax jurisdiction (Nevada or South Dakota), and those accumulated gains are not subject to New York income tax while retained in the trust. When distributed to New York resident beneficiaries, New York income tax applies — but the accumulation phase is tax-free at the state level.

The ING trust strategy is highly technical, requires a New York-experienced trust attorney, and has been reviewed (but not invalidated) by the New York Department of Taxation and Finance. It is not a simple structure — but for large Bitcoin holdings approaching or exceeding the estate tax threshold, it may be one of the most significant planning tools available.

New York's BitLicense and Regulatory Environment

New York's BitLicense framework (23 NYCRR Part 200), administered by the New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS), imposes significant regulatory requirements on businesses engaged in Bitcoin transmission or exchange in New York. While this primarily affects businesses rather than individual holders, Bitcoin families using New York-based custodians or financial intermediaries should ensure those entities are properly licensed. The BitLicense framework creates a regulated counterparty environment that is actually advantageous for institutional Bitcoin custody — BitLicensed custodians provide regulatory accountability that may satisfy trustee prudent investor requirements.

Domicile Changes From New York

New York, like California, aggressively pursues former residents who change domicile. The New York "statutory residency" rule (New York Tax Law §605(b)(1)) provides that a person is a New York resident for tax purposes if they maintain a permanent place of abode in New York and spend more than 183 days in the state, even if they are domiciled elsewhere. Selling a New York apartment or reducing time in the state below 183 days are both necessary but neither alone is sufficient without establishing a genuine domicile in another state and maintaining careful day-count records.

For Bitcoin families considering domicile changes from New York to Florida, Texas, or Wyoming, the planning horizon is typically 12–18 months and requires meticulous documentation. Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
