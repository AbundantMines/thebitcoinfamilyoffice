Bitcoin Charitable Remainder Trust: Give Appreciated Bitcoin, Keep Income

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  8 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Charitable Remainder Trust Est. 8 min read

For Bitcoin holders who are philanthropically inclined, sitting on a large position with a very low cost basis, and looking for income — the charitable remainder trust (CRT) is one of the most powerful tax structures available. Done correctly, a CRT lets you transfer highly appreciated Bitcoin to the trust, avoid paying immediate capital gains tax on the full appreciation, receive an income stream for the rest of your life (or a term of years), take a partial charitable deduction today, and direct the remaining assets to a charity of your choice at death.

It is not appropriate for everyone. If your goal is to pass Bitcoin wealth to your heirs, a CRT is the wrong structure — heirs receive nothing from a CRT remainder. But for the right holder, in the right situation, a Bitcoin CRT is an elegant convergence of tax efficiency and legacy-building.

How a Charitable Remainder Trust Works Mechanically

A charitable remainder trust is an irrevocable trust established under IRC §664. Here is the sequence of events:

  1. You transfer Bitcoin to the CRT. The transfer is a completed gift to the trust — you no longer own the Bitcoin. The CRT is now the legal owner.
  2. The CRT sells the Bitcoin. Because the CRT is a tax-exempt entity under IRC §501(c), it pays no capital gains tax on the sale. The full pre-tax proceeds remain in the trust, available for reinvestment into a diversified income-producing portfolio.
  3. The CRT pays you an income stream. For the duration of the trust term (your lifetime, or the joint lifetimes of you and a spouse, or a fixed term up to 20 years), the CRT distributes a specified percentage of the trust assets to you each year. This income is taxable to you — characterized as ordinary income, capital gains, or return of principal depending on the trust's composition — but you have the benefit of the full pre-tax proceeds working for you rather than a post-tax amount.
  4. At the end of the trust term, the remainder passes to charity. The charity you designated — a donor-advised fund, a private foundation, or a qualified public charity — receives whatever assets remain in the trust.
The CRT is the trust that converts an undiversified, illiquid, highly appreciated Bitcoin position into a lifetime income stream — without triggering immediate capital gains on the full appreciation.

The Tax Advantages

The CRT's tax benefits are substantial and multi-layered:

  • No immediate capital gains recognition. When you transfer Bitcoin to a CRT and the CRT sells it, no capital gains tax is triggered at the point of sale. The tax on the capital gain is instead spread over your income distributions from the CRT over time — significantly more favorable than a lump-sum recognition event.
  • Charitable income tax deduction. You receive an immediate charitable deduction equal to the present value of the charity's remainder interest — calculated using IRS actuarial tables based on your age, the payout rate, and the IRS §7520 rate in effect at the time. For a large Bitcoin position, this deduction can be substantial, though it is subject to the 30% of AGI limitation for appreciated property contributions (with a 5-year carryforward).
  • Estate tax reduction. Assets contributed to a CRT are removed from your taxable estate. For families near or above the federal estate tax exemption, the CRT reduces estate tax exposure while simultaneously providing lifetime income.
  • Full pre-tax proceeds available for reinvestment. If you sold Bitcoin directly outside a CRT, you would have only the post-tax proceeds to reinvest. Inside the CRT, the full pre-tax amount is reinvested — producing higher income over the trust's life even after accounting for the income taxes due on distributions.

CRAT vs. CRUT: The Critical Distinction

There are two primary types of charitable remainder trusts, and the distinction matters significantly for Bitcoin holders:

CRAT — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust

  • Fixed dollar amount distributed each year
  • Amount is set at funding — never changes
  • Cannot add assets after formation
  • Simpler to administer
  • No upside if trust grows — income is capped at original dollar figure

CRUT — Charitable Remainder Unitrust

  • Fixed percentage of trust value distributed each year (min 5%)
  • Amount fluctuates with portfolio performance
  • Additional contributions can be made after formation
  • More complex to administer
  • Participates in portfolio growth — income rises if trust appreciates

For Bitcoin holders, the CRUT is generally preferred. The fixed-percentage payout means income tracks the portfolio — if the reinvested proceeds grow, income grows. The ability to make additional contributions is also valuable for Bitcoin holders who may want to fund the CRT with additional tranches of appreciated Bitcoin over time. A special variant — the Net Income with Makeup CRUT (NIMCRUT) — allows the trust to accumulate underpayments in years where net income is low and make them up in later years, providing additional flexibility.

IRS Requirements and Payout Rules

To qualify as a CRT under IRC §664, the trust must meet specific IRS requirements:

  • Minimum payout rate of 5%. The annual payout to the income beneficiary must be at least 5% of the initial value (CRAT) or 5% of the annual value (CRUT).
  • Maximum payout rate of 50%. Payout cannot exceed 50%, which would too aggressively deplete the remainder for charity.
  • Remainder value test. The present value of the charitable remainder must be at least 10% of the initial contribution value at the time of funding. This test uses IRS actuarial tables — at low payout rates and with younger donors, the 10% test is easily satisfied; at high payout rates or with older donors, it can be constraining.
  • Trust term. The trust can run for the lifetime of one or more named individuals, or a fixed term not exceeding 20 years.
  • Qualified charitable beneficiary. The remainder must pass to a qualifying charity under IRC §170(c). This includes donor-advised funds (DAFs), private foundations, and public charities — but not individuals.

Who a Bitcoin CRT Is Appropriate For

A Bitcoin charitable remainder trust is well-suited for holders who:

  • Have a large, highly appreciated Bitcoin position with a very low cost basis
  • Are philanthropically motivated — they genuinely want the remainder to benefit charity, not heirs
  • Want or need an income stream during their lifetime that is greater than what they could generate by selling Bitcoin and reinvesting after taxes
  • Have a federal estate tax exposure and want to reduce the taxable estate while maintaining income
  • Are comfortable with the irrevocable nature of the transfer — once Bitcoin is in the CRT, it cannot be retrieved

Who a Bitcoin CRT Is NOT Appropriate For

Just as important is understanding when a CRT is the wrong tool:

  • Holders who want to pass Bitcoin wealth to heirs. Heirs receive nothing from a CRT remainder — it all goes to charity. If family wealth transfer is the primary goal, a dynasty trust, revocable trust, or other structure is appropriate.
  • Holders who may need liquidity beyond the annual payout. Once in the CRT, Bitcoin cannot be accessed in a lump sum. The trust is irrevocable, and distributions are limited to the specified payout stream.
  • Holders without genuine charitable intent. The IRS scrutinizes CRTs carefully. Structures where the "charity" is purely nominal and the real intent is tax avoidance without philanthropic purpose face audit risk.
  • Younger holders with long time horizons who want to maximize heir wealth. For a 35-year-old Bitcoin holder, a dynasty trust structure that compounds for generations is likely superior to a CRT that terminates the line at death.
