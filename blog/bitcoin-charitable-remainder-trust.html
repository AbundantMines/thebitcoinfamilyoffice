Bitcoin Charitable Remainder Trust: Give BTC, Get Income, Avoid Capital Gains

You've held Bitcoin for years. It's appreciated significantly. Selling creates a massive tax event. A Charitable Remainder Trust lets you transfer the asset, eliminate the immediate capital gains hit, receive an income stream for life, and ultimately benefit a cause you believe in.

By Hal Franklin · The Bitcoin Family Office
March 10, 2026
18 min read

Most Bitcoin holders understand the asset has appreciated. Many have positions with a cost basis near zero — coins purchased at $1,000, $3,000, or $10,000 now worth multiples of what was paid. The problem is what happens when you actually want to access that capital. A direct sale triggers long-term capital gains tax at 20% federal, plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax for high earners, plus state taxes. On a $2 million Bitcoin position with a $0 cost basis, you're looking at a tax bill of $470,000 to $600,000 or more — depending on your jurisdiction — paid in the year of sale, before you can reinvest a dollar.

The Charitable Remainder Trust solves this problem structurally. It isn't a workaround or a gray area. It's a federally recognized trust form that has existed in the tax code since 1969. The mechanics are straightforward: you contribute the Bitcoin to the trust, the trust sells the Bitcoin tax-free, reinvests the proceeds, and pays you an income stream for life or a fixed term. At the end of the trust's life, the remaining assets go to a charity of your choosing. In exchange for that charitable gift, you receive a partial charitable deduction today, eliminate the immediate capital gains tax, and convert an illiquid, non-yielding asset into a lifetime income stream.

For serious Bitcoin holders with large positions and philanthropic inclinations — or simply those who want to diversify without triggering a catastrophic tax event — the CRT is one of the most powerful tools available. This is the complete breakdown.

What a Charitable Remainder Trust Is — and Why Bitcoin Holders Should Care

A Charitable Remainder Trust is an irrevocable split-interest trust. "Split-interest" means the trust has two types of beneficiaries: an income beneficiary (typically you, the grantor, and/or your spouse) who receives payments during the trust term, and a remainder beneficiary (a qualified charity) who receives whatever is left when the trust terminates.

The trust is structured under IRC Sections 664 and 170. It must meet specific IRS requirements to qualify — including the 10% remainder rule (discussed below) — and must be administered by a qualified trustee. When those requirements are met, the trust is treated as a tax-exempt entity. That tax-exempt status is what makes the capital gains bypass possible: when the CRT sells appreciated property, the sale occurs inside a tax-exempt entity, and no capital gains tax is triggered at the time of sale.

Bitcoin holders care about this for a specific reason: Bitcoin is a highly appreciated, zero-yield asset. Most holders in year six or beyond have positions worth many multiples of their original investment, producing no income. The CRT converts that appreciation into income without first surrendering the bulk of it to taxes. For someone who wants to rebalance, access liquidity, generate retirement income, or simply diversify out of a concentrated position — while maintaining philanthropic intent — there is no more tax-efficient mechanism.

The Core Trade

You give Bitcoin to a trust. The trust sells it tax-free. You get income for life. The charity gets the remainder. The IRS gives you a partial deduction today. The trade is straightforward: you exchange a future estate (the remaining Bitcoin) for current income and a tax benefit, while directing the terminal value to a cause rather than to the estate tax.

The Capital Gains Problem: How a CRT Bypasses the Tax Wall

When you sell appreciated Bitcoin directly, the IRS treats the gain as long-term capital gain (assuming you've held more than a year). The federal long-term capital gains rate for high earners is 20%. The Net Investment Income Tax adds another 3.8% on top. Add your state's capital gains rate — 13.3% in California, 9.9% in Oregon, 8.82% in New York — and the all-in tax rate on Bitcoin appreciation can exceed 37% in high-tax states.

On a $2 million Bitcoin position with a $100 basis, you would owe capital gains on $1,999,900 of gain. In Oregon:

  • Federal long-term CGT (20%): $399,980
  • Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%): $75,996
  • Oregon state tax (9.9%): $197,990
  • Total tax: ~$673,966

You're left with $1,326,034 to reinvest. The remaining $673,966 is gone — paid to governments before you've deployed a dollar into a diversified portfolio.

The CRT changes the math entirely. You contribute the $2 million BTC position to the trust. The trust sells the Bitcoin. No capital gains tax is triggered on the sale — the trust is tax-exempt. The full $2,000,000 is reinvested in whatever the trustee determines appropriate (diversified equities, fixed income, a balanced portfolio). That entire $2,000,000 works for you going forward, generating income at the agreed payout rate. The tax you would have paid instead compounds inside the trust for the duration of your life and the lives of your beneficiaries.

The capital gains tax is not eliminated permanently. As income is distributed from the trust, it carries a character — the IRS tracks the trust's income in a four-tier ordering (ordinary income, capital gains, other income, corpus). The capital gains embedded in the Bitcoin sale will eventually be distributed to you as income and taxed accordingly. But the gain is spread over the trust term rather than recognized all at once. And the amount that was never paid to the IRS continues to compound inside the trust during that period, generating additional returns.

Two Types: CRUT vs. CRAT — Which Is Better for Bitcoin

There are two primary structures for a Charitable Remainder Trust. The choice matters, especially for a volatile underlying asset like Bitcoin.

CRUT: Charitable Remainder Unitrust

The CRUT pays a fixed percentage of the trust's assets, revalued annually. If the trust is worth $2 million and the payout rate is 6%, you receive $120,000 in year one. If the trust grows to $2.4 million by year two, you receive $144,000. If it drops to $1.8 million, you receive $108,000.

The payout percentage must be at least 5% and can be set as high as 50% (though higher rates make it harder to meet the IRS 10% remainder requirement, discussed below). The annual revaluation means your income is tied to the trust's current value — you participate in the upside and absorb the downside.

CRAT: Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust

The CRAT pays a fixed dollar amount annually, regardless of trust performance. If the trust is worth $2 million and the annuity is set at $120,000 per year, you receive exactly $120,000 whether the trust grows to $3 million or drops to $1 million. Additional contributions are not permitted once the CRAT is established (unlike the CRUT, which can accept additional contributions).

The fixed payment sounds attractive, but for a volatile asset like Bitcoin-derived trust assets, it creates a structural risk: if the trust's investments underperform the payout rate, the trust depletes faster than anticipated, potentially reducing the charitable remainder below the 10% threshold — which is not a retroactive disqualification but can affect planning.

Which Is Better for Bitcoin?

For a CRT funded with Bitcoin, the CRUT is almost always the better structure. The reasons:

  • Flexibility on additional contributions: If you acquire more Bitcoin later, you can contribute it to an existing CRUT — creating a single trust structure rather than multiple trusts.
  • Natural adjustment to volatility: If the reinvested portfolio fluctuates, your payout adjusts proportionally. A CRAT's fixed payment can deplete a declining portfolio faster than intended.
  • NI-CRUT option: The Net Income CRUT variation (discussed below) is available only to CRUTs, not CRATs. The NI-CRUT is particularly valuable for Bitcoin holders who want to defer income during high-growth years.

The CRAT has its place for donors who prioritize payment certainty over flexibility — particularly those with fixed income needs who will reinvest CRT proceeds into predictable fixed-income instruments. But for the typical BFO reader managing a large, appreciated Bitcoin position with a long time horizon, the CRUT or NI-CRUT is the right default.

Worked Example: $2M BTC with $0 Cost Basis

Let's run the numbers concretely. This is the scenario that clarifies the CRT's value better than any abstract description.

$

The Setup

Grantor: Age 60, married, high-income professional. Oregon resident.

Bitcoin position: 20 BTC. Acquired at various prices, average cost basis of $5,000/BTC ($100,000 total). Current price: $100,000/BTC. Total value: $2,000,000. Unrealized gain: $1,900,000.

Without a CRT (direct sale): Capital gains tax of approximately $640,000 (at combined federal + state rate of ~33.7%). Remaining capital: ~$1,360,000 to reinvest.

With a 6% CRUT: Transfer all 20 BTC to the CRUT. Trust sells Bitcoin tax-free. Full $2,000,000 reinvested. Year 1 payout: $120,000. Charitable deduction in year of contribution: approximately $400,000–$500,000 (depends on IRS 7520 rate and grantor's age — calculate with your CPA). Remainder to charity at end of term.

The numbers that matter: instead of starting with $1,360,000 in a diversified portfolio after the tax hit, you start with $2,000,000 working for you — $640,000 more, all of it compounding. At a 5% annual return on the reinvested portfolio, that $640,000 difference compounds to approximately $1.04 million over 20 years. That's not the charitable deduction or the income stream — that's purely the value of deferring the capital gains tax.

Meanwhile, the grantor receives $120,000 per year from the trust (at 6% of $2M in year one, adjusted annually), gets an immediate partial charitable deduction that reduces current-year taxable income, and knows that the eventual remainder will fund a cause aligned with their values. The estate tax benefit is additional: the $2 million in Bitcoin is no longer in the taxable estate.

The Income Stream: How Long, How Much, IRS Requirements

The CRT's income stream can be structured in several ways. Understanding the options — and the IRS constraints — is essential to designing a structure that works.

Trust Term Options

  • Life of one or two lives: Payments continue for as long as the named individual(s) live. If you name yourself and your spouse, payments continue until the death of the survivor. This is the most common structure for retirement planning applications.
  • Fixed term (not to exceed 20 years): Payments continue for a specified number of years regardless of whether the beneficiary is alive at the end. This option is used when the grantor wants the income to flow to heirs (who could be income beneficiaries) for a fixed period.
  • Shorter of life or term: Less common, but possible — payments continue for the shorter of the grantor's life or a fixed term.

Payout Rate Constraints

The CRUT payout rate must be between 5% and 50%. But the payout rate interacts directly with the IRS 10% remainder rule: the present value of the charitable remainder interest must be at least 10% of the initial fair market value of the assets contributed.

The 10% calculation depends on three variables: the payout rate, the trust term (or the grantor's life expectancy), and the IRS Section 7520 rate (the assumed rate of return for present value calculations, set monthly). In a higher interest rate environment, longer terms and higher payout rates become more feasible while still satisfying the 10% floor. In a low-rate environment, the math tightens.

For a 60-year-old grantor at a representative Section 7520 rate, a 6% CRUT typically satisfies the 10% remainder rule comfortably over a life term. At 8%, the math may still work but requires careful modeling. At 10% or above, you may need to shorten the term or accept a lower payout to maintain the charitable remainder. Your CPA or estate attorney should model this precisely before finalizing the trust.

Charitable Deduction

You receive a charitable deduction in the year you contribute assets to the CRT, equal to the present value of the remainder interest (the amount the charity is expected to receive). If that present value is $450,000, you can deduct $450,000 against your ordinary income — subject to the 30% AGI limit for contributions of appreciated property to CRTs. Excess deductions carry forward for up to five years.

This deduction is not the primary driver of the CRT decision — the capital gains bypass and income stream are more powerful — but it provides a meaningful current-year tax reduction that further enhances the net economic value of the structure.

Choosing the Charitable Beneficiary

The charitable remainder beneficiary can be any organization that qualifies under IRC Section 501(c)(3). The decision matters less for the tax mechanics — which work the same regardless of which qualified charity you name — and more for your values and long-term family planning goals.

Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)

A Donor-Advised Fund is a charitable account held at a sponsoring organization (Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Bitcoin-specific DAFs like Unchained or Unchained Capital's charitable program). When the CRT terminates, the remainder flows into your DAF. You then direct grants from the DAF to any qualifying charity of your choosing — over time, in any amount, to any number of organizations.

This is often the most flexible structure for donors who haven't fully decided which causes to fund at the outset. You lock in the tax benefit now, and make the philanthropic allocation decisions later. The DAF also provides an additional layer of privacy — the public record shows the DAF as the CRT remainder beneficiary, not the ultimate charities.

Family Foundation

For families with large positions (generally $5 million or more in anticipated charitable giving), a private family foundation provides the most control: you set the investment policy, grant-making strategy, and governance structure. Family members can serve as board members, directors, and staff. The foundation becomes an ongoing family institution — a vehicle for multigenerational philanthropic engagement.

The trade-off: private foundations have more administrative complexity and cost (annual Form 990-PF filing, mandatory 5% annual distribution requirement, self-dealing rules), and contributions to a private foundation receive a less favorable charitable deduction than contributions to a public charity or DAF (limited to 30% of AGI for appreciated property, and the deduction is limited to basis — not fair market value — for appreciated property contributed to a private foundation if it's not publicly traded stock). For this reason, many families use a DAF as the CRT remainder beneficiary and then make grants from the DAF to their private foundation as needed, capturing better deduction treatment at the contribution stage.

Direct Charity

Naming a specific qualified charity directly as the remainder beneficiary is the simplest structure. No intermediary, no ongoing administration beyond the CRT itself. The limitation: once named, the charitable beneficiary is locked in (changing it later is possible but requires trust amendment procedures, which may require court approval depending on the trust instrument). If your charitable priorities shift over the trust's lifetime, a DAF provides more flexibility.

The NI-CRUT: Deferring Income During Bitcoin's Growth Years

The Net Income Charitable Remainder Unitrust (NI-CRUT) is a CRUT variation worth understanding specifically in the Bitcoin context. It addresses a challenge that standard CRUTs don't handle elegantly: what if you want to contribute Bitcoin to the trust but don't want to begin taking distributions immediately?

The standard CRUT begins paying out the payout percentage immediately — in year one, the trustee revalues the trust and distributes the percentage. If the trust has been funded with BTC and the trustee reinvests in a portfolio producing 5% annual income, the trust's cash flow may be limited even while the trust's total return is high. The standard CRUT handles this by distributing whatever cash is available plus principal if necessary.

The NI-CRUT solves this differently: it pays the lesser of the stated payout percentage or the trust's actual net income. If the trust earns less than the payout percentage in a given year (because assets are in growth-oriented investments generating little current income), the shortfall is not paid — it's tracked as a "makeup" amount. In later years, when trust income exceeds the payout percentage, the trustee can make up those deferred distributions.

For Bitcoin holders, the NI-CRUT structure allows you to contribute Bitcoin to the trust, have the trust sell the BTC and reinvest in a portfolio weighted toward growth (which produces limited current income), defer the bulk of distributions during high-growth years, and then shift the portfolio to income-generating assets when you're ready to begin drawing down — triggering both current income and the makeup distributions from prior years.

The FLIP-CRUT adds one more layer: the trust begins as an NI-CRUT and automatically "flips" to a standard CRUT when a triggering event occurs (typically marriage, birth of a child, or reaching a specified date). Before the flip, the NI-CRUT rules apply and distributions are limited to actual income. After the flip, the full payout percentage applies. This is particularly useful for younger donors who want to contribute Bitcoin now, let the trust grow tax-free during their high-earning years, and convert to a standard income-generating CRUT at retirement.

Interaction with Estate Tax: Removing BTC from Your Taxable Estate

A CRT contribution is an irrevocable transfer. The Bitcoin you contribute no longer belongs to you — it belongs to the trust. At your death, the CRT assets are not part of your taxable estate. The estate tax exposure associated with those Bitcoin holdings disappears entirely from your estate calculation.

This is a powerful interaction, especially for families whose Bitcoin positions have appreciated into estate tax territory. If a $2 million BTC position was inside your taxable estate, it would eventually be subject to federal estate tax at 40% on the excess above the exemption. Remove it into a CRT, and that $2 million no longer sits in your estate — it generates income for you during your lifetime and then passes directly to charity. The estate tax savings on $2 million (at 40%) is $800,000 — though this calculation depends on your total estate relative to the exemption threshold and your state's estate tax structure.

One important nuance: the income interest retained by the grantor in a CRT has a present value that is included in the gross estate if the grantor dies during the trust term. This is because the retained income interest is technically an "interest in property" under estate tax rules. However, the charitable deduction for the charitable remainder interest offsets this, typically resulting in minimal net estate tax impact. The details depend on the trust term, interest rates, and the grantor's actuarial life expectancy at death — model this with your estate attorney.

For families with very large estates, the CRT is often used in combination with other tools: Dynasty Trusts to shelter the bulk of the Bitcoin position across generations, GRATs to transfer appreciation tax-free, and CRTs for the portion of the position the family is genuinely prepared to allocate to charitable purposes. Each tool addresses a different slice of the estate planning problem. See our overview of Bitcoin wealth transfer strategies for how they stack.

Setup Requirements: What You Actually Need to Execute

A CRT is not a DIY structure. The complexity of the IRS requirements — the 10% remainder test, the income character ordering rules, the annual valuation requirement for CRUTs, Form 5227 filing — means you need qualified professionals at every step. Here's what the setup actually involves:

Qualified Trustee

The CRT must have a qualified trustee. This can be an individual (you can serve as trustee of your own CRUT in most cases, subject to certain restrictions) or an institutional trustee — a bank trust department, trust company, or similar entity. For Bitcoin CRTs, the trustee selection has an additional dimension: the trustee must be capable of receiving, holding, and selling the Bitcoin contribution. Not every bank trust department is equipped to handle crypto custody. Some families use an individual trustee (a family member or attorney) for the custody and sale process, then transition to an institutional trustee for ongoing administration. Others use an institutional trustee with crypto capability from the outset — Unchained, Kingdom Trust, and similar firms have built CRT-compatible custody offerings.

Trust Document

The trust agreement must be drafted by a qualified estate attorney. The IRS publishes sample charitable remainder trust forms (Revenue Procedure 2005-52 and related guidance), which many attorneys use as a starting point. The agreement must specify: the payout rate (for CRUT) or annuity amount (for CRAT), the trust term, the income beneficiaries, the remainder beneficiary, the trustee powers, and the investment policy. For NI-CRUT and FLIP-CRUT structures, the triggering event language and income definition require precise drafting.

Bitcoin Valuation

When you contribute Bitcoin to a CRT, the trust must establish the fair market value of the contribution. For Bitcoin, this is relatively straightforward — Bitcoin has a publicly quoted spot price, and the IRS accepts exchange-reported prices as FMV. The trustee should document the price at the exact date and time of transfer, using a recognized exchange's spot price (Coinbase Pro, Kraken, or similar). This value determines your charitable deduction, the trust's initial asset base, and the CRUT's first-year payout calculation.

IRS Filing Requirements

CRTs are required to file Form 5227 (Split-Interest Trust Information Return) annually. This is a public document. The form reports the trust's income, distributions, and charitable remainder value. The trustee is responsible for filing. If you're serving as your own trustee, you'll need a CPA familiar with CRT administration to prepare this filing.

Ongoing Administration

The CRT requires annual asset valuation (for CRUTs), quarterly or annual income distribution calculations, tracking of the four-tier income character ordering (ordinary income → capital gains → other income → corpus return), and annual Form 5227 preparation. For most grantors, hiring a professional trustee or CPA to handle administration is cost-effective relative to the complexity of self-administration. Budget approximately $2,000–$5,000 per year for CRT administration, depending on the structure and asset complexity.

A Note on First Principles

The CRT is appealing precisely because it aligns incentives in a way that most tax planning vehicles don't. The IRS created this structure to incentivize charitable giving — the tax benefits (capital gains bypass, charitable deduction, estate exclusion) exist because society has decided that encouraging charitable intent is worth subsidizing through the tax code. You are not exploiting a loophole. You are using a tool that Congress intentionally designed to reward donors who commit assets to charitable purposes.

That said, the CRT is not a pure tax strategy. The remainder genuinely goes to charity — a significant portion of your wealth passes to a charitable beneficiary rather than to your heirs. If keeping maximum value within the family is the primary objective, a CRT is the wrong tool. The right tool for that objective is a Dynasty Trust combined with a GRAT. See the Bitcoin wealth transfer strategies guide for the full comparison.

Use a CRT when the philanthropic intent is genuine, the capital gains problem is real, and the income need is present. When all three conditions are true, the CRT outperforms every alternative on a net-after-tax basis. When one or more conditions are absent, a different tool will serve better.
