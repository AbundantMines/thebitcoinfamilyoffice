Bitcoin Estate Planning for Expats and Digital Nomads: The US Tax Trap

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  12 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning for Expats Est. 12 min read

There is a seductive logic to living abroad with Bitcoin. The coins travel with you. They require no bank account, no local currency, no wire transfer. You can hold five million dollars of purchasing power in a hardware wallet in your jacket pocket as you cross a border — and no customs agent can detect it. Many Bitcoin holders who have adopted the digital nomad lifestyle believe they have, at least in part, stepped outside the traditional financial system and its attendant burdens.

The IRS has a different view. The United States is one of only two countries in the world — the other being Eritrea — that taxes its citizens on their worldwide income and assets regardless of where they live. US citizenship creates a permanent tax nexus that geographic relocation does not sever. A US citizen who moves to Portugal, Thailand, El Salvador, or any other jurisdiction remains fully subject to US income tax, gift tax, and estate tax on their global wealth — including every satoshi of Bitcoin they hold anywhere on earth.

For the bitcoin estate planning expat, this creates a thicket of compliance obligations, structural traps, and planning requirements that are genuinely more complex than those facing US-resident Bitcoin families. This guide addresses the most important of them.

The Foundational Rule: Worldwide Taxation of US Citizens

The US federal estate tax applies to the worldwide assets of every US citizen, regardless of domicile. If you are a US citizen living in Lisbon, Chiang Mai, or Buenos Aires, your Bitcoin holdings — wherever they are custodied, whatever exchange or wallet you use, in whatever legal structure you hold them — are fully included in your US taxable estate at death.

The physical location of your hardware wallet is irrelevant. The jurisdiction of the exchange on which your Bitcoin is held is irrelevant. Whether your Bitcoin is titled in a foreign trust, a foreign LLC, or held directly on a Ledger device in a safe in your apartment in Medellín — it is US taxable estate. The IRS does not grant territorial exceptions based on where you choose to live.

This is the foundational tax trap for the bitcoin digital nomad: the sense that physical distance from the US creates distance from US tax obligations. It does not. The only way to exit US worldwide estate tax exposure is to relinquish US citizenship — and as we will discuss, that process itself carries a potentially enormous tax cost for Bitcoin-wealthy individuals.

A hardware wallet in a safe in Medellín is still US taxable estate. The IRS does not grant territorial exceptions based on where you live. Geography changes nothing.

FBAR and FATCA: Reporting Bitcoin Held Abroad

Beyond estate planning, US expats holding Bitcoin on foreign exchanges or in foreign trusts face ongoing annual reporting requirements that carry severe penalties for noncompliance.

FBAR — FinCEN Form 114

The Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR) requires US persons to report foreign financial accounts with an aggregate value exceeding $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. The Treasury's current position is that Bitcoin held in a foreign financial account (such as a foreign exchange) is subject to FBAR reporting. If you hold Bitcoin on a foreign exchange platform and the value of that account exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year, you likely have an FBAR filing obligation.

The FBAR penalties are not merely civil annoyances. Willful failure to file carries a penalty of the greater of $100,000 or 50% of the balance of the unreported account per violation. For a Bitcoin position worth several hundred thousand dollars held on a foreign exchange, a willful FBAR violation could result in a penalty exceeding the total account value — effectively a confiscation.

FATCA — Form 8938

The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA) requires US persons to report specified foreign financial assets on Form 8938, filed with the federal income tax return. The reporting thresholds are higher than FBAR: for expats, the threshold is $200,000 on the last day of the year or $300,000 at any point during the year (for married filing jointly, $400,000/$600,000).

The IRS has signaled that cryptocurrency held through foreign financial institutions is reportable under FATCA. Bitcoin held in a foreign trust may also trigger Form 3520 (reporting of foreign trusts with US owners) and Form 3520-A (annual information return), creating a multi-form compliance stack for expats using foreign Bitcoin Trust Type Selector tools.

Practical Note: FBAR and FATCA penalties are cumulative with tax liabilities — not substitutes for them. The IRS can assess both the tax owed on an unreported foreign account and the FBAR/FATCA penalties simultaneously. Self-custody Bitcoin held in a personal hardware wallet (not through a foreign financial institution) may not trigger FBAR/FATCA under current guidance, but the IRS has not issued definitive rules, and the landscape is evolving.

Renouncing Citizenship: The Exit Tax and Bitcoin

For US citizens who have concluded that the permanent worldwide tax nexus of US citizenship is incompatible with their long-term wealth planning, renunciation may eventually be considered. The US permits citizens to relinquish citizenship — but it imposes a significant financial cost on those who have accumulated meaningful wealth: the exit tax under IRC § 877A.

Covered Expatriates

The exit tax applies to "covered expatriates" — US citizens who expatriate and meet any of three tests at the time of expatriation:

  1. Net worth test: Net worth of $2 million or more on the expatriation date.
  2. Tax liability test: Average annual net US income tax liability exceeding $195,000 (2024, indexed for inflation) for the five tax years before expatriation.
  3. Compliance test: Failure to certify compliance with all US tax obligations for the five years preceding expatriation.

For a Bitcoin holder with a position worth $2 million or more, the net worth test is likely met. A US citizen with $3 million in Bitcoin who decides to renounce citizenship is almost certainly a covered expatriate.

The Deemed Sale: A Massive Capital Gains Event

Covered expatriates are treated as having sold all of their worldwide assets at fair market value on the day before their expatriation date — a deemed sale. This deemed sale creates immediate, recognized capital gain on every asset, including Bitcoin, at current fair market value regardless of whether the Bitcoin has actually been sold.

The first $866,000 of gain (2024, indexed for inflation) is excluded. Everything above that threshold is taxable as capital gain at applicable US rates in the year of expatriation. For a Bitcoin holder who purchased at an average cost basis of $10,000 per coin and holds 50 coins now worth $4.75 million, the unrealized gain is approximately $4.25 million. After the exclusion, roughly $3.38 million is taxable as capital gain at the time of expatriation — a tax bill potentially exceeding $800,000, triggered without a single bitcoin being sold.

Expatriation while holding a large, low-basis Bitcoin position requires extraordinarily careful planning — including considering whether to realize gains over multiple years before expatriation, whether to use charitable giving strategies to reduce basis exposure, and what the long-term net benefit of renunciation actually is after accounting for the exit tax cost.

QDOT: The Non-Citizen Spouse Problem

The unlimited marital deduction — which allows unlimited transfers between spouses free of estate and gift tax — does not apply when the surviving spouse is not a US citizen. This is one of the most consequential and frequently overlooked issues in bitcoin estate planning expat situations, particularly for American digital nomads who have married foreign nationals.

If a US citizen dies with a Bitcoin position worth $10 million and leaves it directly to a non-US citizen spouse, the portion above the basic federal exclusion (approximately $13.99 million in 2025) would be subject to estate tax — with no marital deduction available to defer it. If the estate is below the basic exclusion, there is no immediate tax, but the surviving non-citizen spouse's estate will not benefit from a portability election to use the deceased spouse's unused exclusion amount (DSUE) in the same way a US citizen spouse would.

The QDOT Solution

A Qualified Domestic Trust (QDOT) is the statutory mechanism for extending the marital deduction to non-citizen spouses. Bitcoin (or an LLC holding Bitcoin) transferred to a properly structured QDOT qualifies for the marital deduction at death — deferring estate tax until the non-citizen spouse takes distributions from the QDOT or until the non-citizen spouse dies.

QDOT requirements are specific: at least one trustee must be a US citizen or a domestic corporation with the authority to withhold estate tax on distributions of principal; the QDOT must comply with the requirements of IRC § 2056A; and it must be established and funded — or a QDOT election made — by the estate tax return deadline. For Bitcoin families with non-citizen spouses, designing the estate plan around QDOT-compliant structures in advance is far preferable to forcing the executor to create one under deadline pressure after death.

Annual Gifting to Non-Citizen Spouses

The annual gift tax exclusion for transfers to non-citizen spouses is significantly higher than the standard annual exclusion: $185,000 in 2024, indexed for inflation (compared to $18,000 for US citizen spouses). This elevated annual exclusion creates an efficient lifetime gifting channel for expat couples. A US citizen with a large Bitcoin position can gift Bitcoin worth up to $185,000 per year to a non-citizen spouse gift-tax-free, gradually reducing the US citizen's taxable estate over time.

Estate Tax Treaty Considerations and Bitcoin Situs

The United States has estate tax treaties with a limited number of countries — including Australia, Austria, Canada, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Japan, the Netherlands, Norway, South Africa, Sweden, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom. These treaties generally allocate estate taxing rights between the US and the treaty country, and may affect how Bitcoin is taxed for expats domiciled in treaty countries.

Bitcoin Situs: Where Is the Asset?

Estate tax treaties turn on the concept of situs — where an asset is legally located for tax purposes. For tangible property like real estate or physical gold, situs is determined by the asset's physical location. For intangible property like Bitcoin, the rules are less clear. Under US domestic law, the situs of intangible property of a nonresident alien is generally determined by the country of domicile of the owner — suggesting that Bitcoin held by a US domiciliary is US-situs property regardless of where the wallet is held.

Under estate tax treaties, the situs determination may follow different rules. Some treaties provide that intangible property is taxable only by the country of domicile; others allocate specific categories differently. The interaction between treaty situs rules and Bitcoin's novel nature as an intangible, borderless asset is not fully settled. For expats domiciled in treaty countries, the treaty analysis should be a specific component of the estate planning review — there may be significant deductions, credits, or outright exemptions available that are not available without treaty analysis. Planning Strategies for Expat Bitcoin Families

Wyoming or South Dakota Trust Siting

US expats who want to maintain US trust infrastructure — and benefit from the privacy, perpetual duration, and asset protection laws of the most Bitcoin-favorable domestic trust jurisdictions — can establish trusts with situs in Wyoming or South Dakota regardless of where they personally reside. A Wyoming or South Dakota irrevocable trust created by a US citizen living abroad provides the benefits of perpetual dynasty trust treatment, no state income tax on trust assets, modern directed trust and digital asset succession statutes, and favorable creditor protection — all without requiring the grantor to reside in those states.

Wyoming in particular has enacted explicit statutory authority for fiduciaries to hold and manage digital assets (Wyoming Stat. § 2-3-201 et seq.), making it the preferred domestic situs for Bitcoin family trusts regardless of where the family lives.

Letter of Instruction for Cross-Border Situations

The Letter of Instruction — the plain-language technical companion to the formal estate plan that provides wallet locations, seed phrase protocols, access credentials, and custody architecture details — is even more critical in cross-border situations. When an executor is located in a different country from the Bitcoin, when the trustee and the primary beneficiaries are in different time zones, and when the succession process may involve navigating both US probate and a foreign legal system simultaneously, the clarity of the Letter of Instruction is the difference between a smooth succession and a years-long impasse.

For expats, the Letter of Instruction should explicitly address: which jurisdiction's legal process governs which assets; who has technical signing authority and through what multi-signature protocol; what the succession process is if the primary key holder is incapacitated while abroad; and how the executor, trustee, and beneficiaries are to coordinate across jurisdictions.

Roth Conversion While Abroad

US expats using the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) — which allows exclusion of up to $126,500 (2024) of foreign earned income from US taxable income — often find themselves in unusually low US taxable income years. This creates a powerful opportunity for Roth IRA conversions. Traditional IRA and 401(k) assets are fully subject to US income tax and estate tax (IRAs are included in the taxable estate and the required Bitcoin family office minimum requirements distributions after death create an income tax liability for beneficiaries). Converting traditional IRA funds to Roth during a FEIE-reduced income year locks in a low conversion tax rate, reduces the future estate tax burden on the IRA (since Roth distributions are income-tax-free to beneficiaries), and eliminates required minimum distributions that would otherwise compound the estate.

Annual Gifting Program

Beyond the elevated annual exclusion for non-citizen spouses, US expat Bitcoin families should implement systematic annual gifting programs consistent with their overall estate plan. The $18,000 annual exclusion (2024) applies per recipient — a couple with three adult children can gift $108,000 of Bitcoin annually free of gift tax, regardless of which country the children reside in. Over a decade, that is $1.08 million of Bitcoin removed from the taxable estate. Combined with appreciation on the gifted Bitcoin, the estate tax savings compound significantly.

Self-Custody Abroad: The IRS Doesn't Care Where the Device Is

Many expat Bitcoin holders assume — incorrectly — that holding their Bitcoin in self-custody on a hardware wallet located outside the United States places the asset outside the reach of the US estate tax. This assumption is false.

US estate tax follows the citizenship and domicile of the decedent, not the physical location of the asset (for most asset classes, and particularly for intangible property like Bitcoin). A hardware wallet held in a safety deposit box in Zurich, in a home safe in Tokyo, or on your person anywhere in the world is still 100% includable in a US citizen's taxable estate. The IRS doesn't care where the device is. It cares where the owner held citizenship and domicile at death.

The practical planning implication is important: expats should not be lulled into believing that geographic relocation of their self-custody infrastructure has any estate tax effect. The only estate tax effect of relocating Bitcoin custody is if the relocation is part of a broader change in domicile accompanied by the full legal machinery of relinquishing US citizenship — and even then, only after navigating the exit tax.

Finding a Cross-Border Bitcoin Estate Attorney

Bitcoin estate planning for expats sits at the intersection of three specialized fields: US estate and trust law, international tax law, and digital asset custody. Attorneys who are genuinely competent across all three are rare. When evaluating cross-border Bitcoin estate counsel, look for:

  • Dual-jurisdiction background: Experience practicing in, or at minimum advising on the law of, both the US jurisdiction where the estate plan is anchored and the country of the client's current domicile. This is especially important for QDOT planning, which requires coordination between US estate counsel and local advisors regarding the spouse's nationality and the foreign trust law implications.
  • QDOT experience: Not all US estate attorneys have drafted QDOTs. A QDOT for a Bitcoin-heavy estate is a specialized document. Confirm that the attorney has specific QDOT experience, not just general estate planning credentials.
  • Expat tax background: The attorney should be conversant with FBAR/FATCA compliance, the exit tax mechanics of IRC § 877A, and the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion — or work closely with a CPA who specializes in expat taxation. The estate plan cannot be designed in isolation from the income and reporting obligations it intersects with.
  • Digital asset fluency: The attorney should understand how Bitcoin is held in self-custody versus exchange custody, the technical architecture of multi-signature setups, and how to draft fiduciary access provisions that are technically operable — not just legally authorized.
  • Treaty analysis capability: If you are domiciled in a treaty country, the attorney should be able to conduct or commission a treaty analysis, not simply default to domestic US estate planning rules without considering whether the treaty changes the analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are US citizens abroad subject to US estate tax on Bitcoin?

Yes — US citizens and permanent residents are subject to US estate tax on worldwide assets regardless of location. Bitcoin on a Singapore hardware wallet, Swiss exchange, or Colombian bank: all US taxable estate. No territorial exceptions. The only exit is citizenship renunciation — which triggers an exit tax treating assets as sold the day before expatriation.

What is the exit tax for US citizens who renounce with Bitcoin?

IRC §877A treats covered expatriates as having sold all assets at FMV the day before expatriation. $5M Bitcoin with $500K basis = $4.5M capital gains recognized on the spot. Covered expatriate criteria: average net income tax over $190K for 5 preceding years OR net worth over $2M. Plan carefully before treating renunciation as a Bitcoin tax strategy.

What is a QDOT trust for expat Bitcoin families?

A Qualified Domestic Trust required when passing assets to a non-citizen surviving spouse. Without QDOT, the unlimited marital deduction is unavailable — estate tax is due immediately at death. QDOT allows Bitcoin to pass to the non-citizen spouse without immediate estate tax, deferred until distributions or the surviving spouse's death.

Does holding Bitcoin on a foreign exchange avoid US estate tax?

No. Foreign exchange jurisdiction does not determine tax treatment of a US citizen's assets. Foreign accounts above $10,000 also trigger FBAR reporting (FinCEN 114). FATCA may apply to accounts above $50K–$200K depending on filing status. Willful FBAR violations: penalties up to $100,000 per violation.

The Bottom Line for Expat Bitcoin Families

The bitcoin digital nomad estate planning challenge is not that the US tax system is uniquely punitive toward Bitcoin — it is that the worldwide reach of US taxation, combined with Bitcoin's borderless portability, creates a situation where many expats radically underestimate their ongoing US tax and reporting obligations. The hardware wallet in your pocket travels without friction. Your US tax nexus travels with it.

Effective planning for US expat Bitcoin families begins with clarity about the worldwide estate tax exposure, proceeds to structural solutions — US-sited irrevocable trusts, QDOT for non-citizen spouses, systematic annual gifting, FBAR/FATCA-compliant custody arrangements — and requires the ongoing involvement of specialized counsel who can navigate the intersection of US estate tax, international tax, and digital asset succession law.

The families who get this right are not the ones who assume that distance from the US changes their tax position. They are the ones who understand the rules precisely — and plan within them with the same precision they bring to their Bitcoin custody architecture.
