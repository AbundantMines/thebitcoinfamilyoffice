Bitcoin Grantor Trust Rules: §§671–679, IDGT Powers, and the Tax Burn Strategy

The grantor trust rules are the mechanical foundation beneath GRATs, SLATs, IDGTs, and every other trust strategy that lets Bitcoin compound outside your estate while you pay the income taxes personally. Here's how they work — and how to use them deliberately.

📅 March 13, 2026 ⏱ 15 min read 🏷 Grantor Trust · IDGT · §671–679 · Tax Burn

Estate planning attorneys talk about GRATs, SLATs, and IDGTs as if they were distinct structures. They are — but they all share a common technical foundation: the grantor trust rules of IRC §§671–679. Understanding these rules is the difference between following a blueprint and understanding the engineering. For Bitcoin families deploying any of these strategies, the grantor trust rules determine income tax treatment, planning flexibility, and the magnitude of the "tax burn" wealth transfer advantage.

This guide covers the complete grantor trust framework: what grantor trust status means, which powers trigger it, how the IDGT is built to be intentionally defective for income tax while being outside the estate, the tax burn mechanics, toggle trusts, and the §678 non-grantor power that extends these rules to trust beneficiaries.

1. What Grantor Trust Status Means

Under IRC §671, if a grantor (or a non-grantor person holding a power under §678) is treated as the owner of any portion of a trust, the grantor includes the items of income, deduction, and credit attributable to that portion in computing their own taxable income.

Practically, this means:

  • The trust does not file a separate income tax return (or files a simplified grantor trust return)
  • All Bitcoin capital gains inside the trust flow to the grantor's Form 1040
  • The grantor pays income tax on trust income — including Bitcoin gains — at their individual rates
  • The trust's Bitcoin compounds without any income tax drag (the tax is paid by the grantor from outside assets)

Grantor trust status is an income tax classification. It has nothing to do — directly — with estate tax inclusion. A trust can be a grantor trust for income tax purposes while being completely outside the grantor's taxable estate for estate tax purposes. This divergence is the core of IDGT planning.

2. The §§671–679 Framework: What Triggers Grantor Trust Status

Grantor trust status is triggered when the trust contains one or more of the powers enumerated in §§671–677. The most important for Bitcoin planning:

§675 — Administrative Powers

§675 creates grantor trust status when the grantor or a non-adverse party has any of several administrative powers inconsistent with independent trust administration:

  • §675(1): Power to deal with trust property for less than adequate consideration
  • §675(2): Power to borrow from the trust without adequate interest or security
  • §675(3): Grantor has actually borrowed trust funds without adequate interest or security and hasn't repaid
  • §675(4)(A): Power to vote stock of a controlled corporation
  • §675(4)(B): Power to control investment of trust assets for the grantor's benefit
  • §675(4)(C): Power to reacquire trust assets by substituting assets of equivalent value

The substitution power under §675(4)(C) is the workhorse of Bitcoin IDGT design. The grantor retains the right to exchange personal assets for trust assets of equivalent value. This is enough to make the entire trust a grantor trust — without causing estate tax inclusion, as confirmed by Rev. Rul. 2008-22 (the IRS ruled that a substitution power exercisable in a non-fiduciary capacity does not cause estate tax inclusion under §§2036 or 2038).

💡 The Substitution Power in Practice

A Bitcoin IDGT grantor might exercise the substitution power by exchanging a portfolio of lower-basis Bitcoin (with large embedded capital gains) for equivalent FMV cash or Treasury bonds from personal funds. The trust ends up holding lower-gain assets; the grantor holds the high-gain Bitcoin personally — potentially to hold until death for the §1014 step-up. The trust's lower-gain assets compound tax-free (to the trust); the grantor takes the step-up. This is a sophisticated basis optimization strategy built on the substitution power.

§677 — Income for Benefit of Grantor

§677(a) creates grantor trust status when trust income may, in the discretion of the grantor or a non-adverse party, be:

  • Distributed to the grantor or the grantor's spouse
  • Held or accumulated for future distribution to the grantor or grantor's spouse
  • Applied to pay premiums on life insurance on the grantor's or grantor's spouse's life

A SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust) triggers §677(a) because trust income may be distributed to the grantor's spouse. This makes the SLAT a grantor trust — the grantor pays income tax on SLAT income — and creates the "tax burn" benefit while the trust is outside the grantor's estate.

§674 — Power to Control Beneficial Enjoyment

§674(a) creates grantor trust status when the grantor or a non-adverse party has the power to change the beneficial enjoyment of trust income or corpus without the consent of an adverse party. Many broad discretionary distribution powers held by the grantor as trustee can trigger §674.

The exceptions in §674(b) and (c) are important: certain powers held by an independent trustee, limited to an ascertainable standard (health, education, maintenance, support), or subject to other restrictions do not trigger §674. This is why using an independent trustee for Bitcoin trusts designed to be grantor trusts is often unnecessary — the grantor trust status can be achieved through a specific §675 power without needing the grantor to serve as trustee at all.

§676 — Power to Revoke

§676 creates grantor trust status when the grantor has the power to revoke the trust and return the assets to themselves. A revocable living trust is a grantor trust under §676. The grantor reports all income on their Form 1040; the trust itself is completely ignored for income tax purposes.

§678 — Power Held by a Person Other Than the Grantor

§678 extends grantor trust rules to beneficiaries. A person who holds a power to vest trust income or corpus in themselves is treated as the owner of that portion of the trust — and pays income tax on it. This is the Crummey withdrawal power mechanism: if a beneficiary has a lapsing right to withdraw trust contributions (a "Crummey power"), that beneficiary is technically a §678 grantor trust owner for income tax purposes during the lapse period, though the practical implications are usually minimal for small annual exclusion gifts.

3. The Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT)

The IDGT exploits the divergence between the income tax grantor trust rules and the estate tax inclusion rules. A properly designed IDGT is:

  • Outside the estate for estate tax: Funded with completed gifts (removing the Bitcoin from the taxable estate) and containing no powers that trigger §§2036–2038 estate tax inclusion
  • Inside the grantor's income tax return: Containing one or more §§671–677 trigger powers so that all trust income is taxable to the grantor personally

The "defect" is the income tax grantor trust status — intentionally retained because it creates the tax burn advantage.

4. The Tax Burn: Mechanics and Magnitude

The tax burn is the central economic advantage of the IDGT structure for Bitcoin families. Here's how it works mechanically:

  1. Grantor creates an IDGT, funds it with Bitcoin (a completed gift — removes Bitcoin from the estate)
  2. Bitcoin appreciates inside the trust, or the trust receives installment sale payments that include interest income
  3. The grantor pays income tax on all trust income from personal (non-trust) funds
  4. The IRS ruled in Rev. Rul. 2004-64 that the grantor's income tax payment on behalf of a grantor trust is not a taxable gift
  5. The trust's assets compound without income tax drag
  6. The grantor's taxable estate shrinks each year by the income tax paid on trust income

💡 Tax Burn Magnitude Example

A Bitcoin IDGT with $5M of Bitcoin generates $500,000 of capital gains in Year 1 (Bitcoin appreciated 10%). The grantor pays $119,000 in income tax (23.8% LTCG + NIIT) on trust gains from personal funds. This payment: (a) reduces the grantor's taxable estate by $119,000 with no gift tax — effectively a tax-free wealth transfer; (b) leaves the trust's $5M Bitcoin intact, compounding without any income tax drag. Over 20 years with consistent appreciation and tax burn payments, the cumulative tax-free transfers can substantially exceed the original trust funding amount. The IDGT is a perpetual wealth transfer machine powered entirely by the grantor trust rules.

Rev. Rul. 2004-64: The Legal Basis

Rev. Rul. 2004-64 is the foundational IRS guidance confirming that a grantor's payment of income tax on behalf of a grantor trust is not a taxable gift. The IRS reasoned that the grantor is simply satisfying a legal obligation — the income tax is the grantor's liability under §671, not the trust's. Paying one's own legal obligations is not a gift.

The ruling also addresses estate tax: if the grantor has a right to be reimbursed by the trust for income taxes paid, the reimbursement right (or the trust's obligation to reimburse) could cause estate tax inclusion under §§2036 or 2038. Properly drafted IDGTs either (a) give the trustee discretionary reimbursement power (not mandatory) or (b) prohibit reimbursement entirely, ensuring the tax burn is permanent and no estate tax inclusion risk is created.

5. Grantor Trust and Installment Sales: The IDGT Combination

The most powerful Bitcoin planning structure combines the IDGT with an installment sale. The grantor sells Bitcoin to the IDGT in exchange for a promissory note bearing interest at the applicable federal rate (AFR). The sale is recognized as a sale — but because buyer and seller are the same person for income tax purposes (grantor trust = ignored), no gain is recognized on the sale.

The result:

  • Bitcoin moves to the trust (outside the estate) with no gift tax and no capital gains tax on the sale
  • The trust holds Bitcoin; the grantor holds the note (the note's FMV is included in the estate, but future Bitcoin appreciation is not)
  • Interest payments from the IDGT to the grantor are also ignored (grantor paying interest to themselves)
  • If Bitcoin appreciates at a rate exceeding the AFR, the excess appreciation passes to trust beneficiaries tax-free
  • The tax burn continues for as long as the trust is a grantor trust

6. Toggle Trusts: Turning Grantor Status On and Off

A toggle trust is drafted to allow grantor trust status to be switched on or off, typically by the trust protector or grantor:

Turning Grantor Status Off

If the grantor's income becomes burdensome (retirement, large passive income, health limitations), the trust protector can release the grantor trust trigger power. The trust becomes a non-grantor trust going forward — the trust pays its own income tax (at compressed trust brackets), and the grantor is no longer responsible for trust income taxes.

Turning Grantor Status On

Conversely, if the trust has accumulated significant Bitcoin that is about to be sold (generating large capital gains), the grantor may want to reestablish grantor trust status — especially if the grantor is in a lower effective rate bracket than the trust's compressed brackets at that point. The trust protector adds a new grantor trust trigger power, and the grantor absorbs the tax at their individual rate.

⚠️ Toggle Trust Complexity

Toggle trusts are sophisticated instruments that require careful drafting to ensure the on/off mechanism doesn't inadvertently create estate tax inclusion, gift tax events, or other unintended consequences. The grantor trust toggle must be a power held in a non-fiduciary capacity. Changes to grantor trust status mid-trust may require tax counsel to analyze whether the change is a taxable event. Do not attempt toggle trust drafting without experienced estate planning counsel.

7. Grantor Trust Termination: What Happens at the Grantor's Death

At the grantor's death, grantor trust status automatically terminates (there is no longer a living grantor). The trust converts to a non-grantor trust — it becomes a separate taxpayer filing Form 1041. For Bitcoin in the trust, this means:

  • Post-death Bitcoin gains are taxed to the trust at the compressed trust brackets (not the grantor's individual rates)
  • The trust must begin filing Form 1041
  • If the trust is not in the grantor's estate (standard IDGT design), there is no §1014 step-up at the grantor's death — the trust's Bitcoin retains its carryover basis from when the grantor sold it to the trust
  • The basis is the installment sale price (if sold to the IDGT) or the gift date FMV (if gifted) — not the date-of-death FMV

⚠️ No Step-Up for IDGT Bitcoin at Grantor's Death

This is the most important trade-off in IDGT planning. Bitcoin inside the IDGT does not receive a §1014 step-up when the grantor dies because it is not in the grantor's estate. The trust holds Bitcoin at the original installment sale price — which may be far below the date-of-death FMV if Bitcoin has appreciated significantly over the trust's life. Beneficiaries who sell IDGT Bitcoin pay capital gains on the full appreciation from the purchase price. The estate tax savings typically far exceed the forgone step-up for large, fast-appreciating positions — but this trade-off must be modeled for each family's specific situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a grantor trust for Bitcoin estate planning purposes?

A grantor trust is a trust in which the grantor is treated as owner for federal income tax purposes. All income, deductions, and credits — including Bitcoin capital gains — are reported on the grantor's Form 1040. The trust may still be outside the grantor's estate for estate tax purposes, creating the core IDGT opportunity: outside the estate for estate tax, transparent for income tax, with the grantor paying income taxes on trust gains as a free additional wealth transfer.

What is an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT)?

An IDGT is a trust designed to be (a) outside the estate for estate tax — an irrevocable trust with a completed gift or installment sale — while (b) a grantor trust for income tax — containing one §§671-679 trigger power. The "defect" (grantor trust status) is intentional because it causes the grantor to pay income taxes on trust income personally, which is a tax-free additional wealth transfer under Rev. Rul. 2004-64.

What powers trigger grantor trust status for a Bitcoin trust?

The most commonly used IDGT trigger is the substitution power under §675(4)(C) — the grantor can exchange personal assets for trust assets of equivalent value. This creates grantor trust status without causing estate tax inclusion (Rev. Rul. 2008-22). Other common triggers: loan power (§675(2)), spousal income power (§677(a) — used in SLATs), and power to add charitable beneficiaries (§674(b)(4)). Any one trigger is sufficient to make the entire trust a grantor trust.

What is the tax burn and how does it benefit Bitcoin trusts?

The tax burn is the grantor's payment of income tax on trust income from personal funds. Under Rev. Rul. 2004-64, this payment is not a taxable gift. Economically: the grantor's estate shrinks by the tax paid, the trust's assets compound without tax drag, and additional wealth transfers to beneficiaries each year at no gift tax cost. For a Bitcoin trust generating $500K of capital gains per year, the annual tax burn transfers ~$119K in value gift-tax-free.

Can grantor trust status be turned on and off?

Yes — a toggle trust includes a mechanism for the trust protector or grantor to enable or disable grantor trust status by releasing or adding a trigger power. Turning grantor status off shifts income tax to the trust (compressed brackets, 37% at ~$15,650). Turning it on shifts tax back to the grantor (individual rates). Toggle trusts require careful drafting to avoid estate tax, gift tax, or other unintended consequences from the switch.

Does grantor trust status cause estate tax inclusion?

Not necessarily. The grantor trust rules are income tax rules, not estate tax rules. An IDGT uses §675(4)(C) substitution power — which Rev. Rul. 2008-22 confirmed does not cause estate tax inclusion under §§2036 or 2038. Powers that do cause estate tax inclusion (§676 revocation power, §677 retained income) are not used in IDGTs designed to be outside the estate. The substitution power is the cleanest trigger: grantor trust for income tax, no estate inclusion for estate tax.
