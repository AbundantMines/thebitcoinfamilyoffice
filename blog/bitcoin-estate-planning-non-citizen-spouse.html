Bitcoin Estate Planning for Non-Citizen Spouses: The QDOT Solution

The unlimited marital deduction — the rule that lets US citizen spouses inherit everything tax-free — does not apply to non-citizens. If your spouse holds a green card, a work visa, or foreign citizenship, Bitcoin you leave them outright can trigger estate tax at the first death rather than the second. For international Bitcoin families, this is the most commonly missed planning gap. Here is what to do about it.

The Problem: Why Non-Citizen Status Changes Everything

The unlimited marital deduction under IRC §2056 is one of the most powerful provisions in US estate tax law. It allows a US citizen to leave any amount to a US citizen spouse completely free of estate tax. No limit. No threshold. Everything deferred until the surviving spouse's estate is settled.

Congress created this deduction on the assumption that the surviving spouse is a US person subject to US jurisdiction — meaning the IRS will eventually collect when that spouse dies. With a non-citizen surviving spouse, Congress was unwilling to make the same assumption. A non-citizen spouse can take the inherited assets and leave the United States, potentially removing them from the IRS's reach permanently.

The result: the unlimited marital deduction does not apply to transfers to a non-citizen spouse. Section 2056(d) of the Internal Revenue Code explicitly restricts the marital deduction for non-citizen spouses.

The estate tax accelerates to the first death. For a US citizen with $20M in Bitcoin married to a non-citizen spouse: if the US citizen dies first and leaves everything outright to the spouse, estate tax is owed immediately on the amount above the $15M OBBBA exemption — $5M taxable at 40% = $2M in estate tax due within nine months of death. Without the marital deduction deferral, the surviving spouse may need to sell Bitcoin to pay the tax bill.

Who Is Affected: More Families Than You Think

The Bitcoin community is disproportionately international. Early adopters came from technology, finance, cryptography, and economics communities — fields with high concentrations of immigrants, expatriates, and international couples. The profile that most commonly encounters this issue:

  • Tech founders and engineers who immigrated to the US on H-1B or O-1 visas, became permanent residents (green card), and have not yet naturalized — often due to travel restrictions, backlog delays, or simple inattention to the timeline
  • US citizens married to foreign nationals who live in the US on a spousal visa (IR-1) or green card but retain their foreign citizenship by choice or by the difficulty of naturalization
  • Dual-income professional couples where one spouse's immigration status is tied to an employer petition and naturalization is logistically deferred
  • Bitcoin-wealthy individuals who met international partners — Bitcoin's global community means international marriages are common among early adopters
  • Families with one spouse who has no desire to naturalize — permanent residents who intend to return to their home country eventually

Importantly: a green card does not fix this problem. A green card holder is a lawful permanent resident — not a US citizen. The unlimited marital deduction applies only to US citizens. A non-citizen permanent resident spouse receives no unlimited marital deduction. The same planning requirement applies whether the surviving spouse is on a green card, a long-term visa, or a foreign national living abroad.

The QDOT Solution: How It Works

Congress created the Qualified Domestic Trust (QDOT) under IRC §2056A as a mechanism to restore the marital deduction deferral for non-citizen spouses — under controlled conditions that ensure the IRS can collect the estate tax eventually.

The basic structure: rather than leaving Bitcoin directly to the non-citizen spouse (which triggers immediate estate tax), the US citizen's estate plan directs the Bitcoin into a QDOT. The estate receives the marital deduction for assets in the QDOT — estate tax is deferred, not eliminated. The non-citizen surviving spouse:

  • Receives all income from the QDOT for life (taxed as ordinary income to the spouse)
  • May receive principal distributions at the trustee's discretion — but each principal distribution triggers estate tax at the time of distribution
  • Has the full QDOT assets taxed in the deceased first spouse's estate when the surviving spouse dies

The QDOT defers the estate tax — it does not eliminate it. The tax that would have been owed at the first death is instead paid piecemeal as principal is distributed, and in full when the surviving spouse dies. The benefit is time: the surviving spouse can use the Bitcoin income for decades before the full tax comes due.

QDOT vs outright bequest — the comparison: A $20M Bitcoin estate, $15M exemption, non-citizen spouse. Outright bequest: $5M taxable immediately at 40% = $2M due in nine months. QDOT: $2M deferred for potentially 20–30 years while the Bitcoin compounds inside the trust. At a conservative 10% annualized Bitcoin appreciation, the $20M grows to $134M over 20 years — substantially changing what the eventual tax is levied on, but providing the surviving spouse decades of income in the meantime and the ability to plan further.

QDOT Requirements — Including for Bitcoin

A trust qualifies as a QDOT only if it meets specific statutory requirements under IRC §2056A. Several of these have direct implications for Bitcoin custody:

Requirement 1: At Least One US Citizen or US Corporate Trustee

The QDOT must have at least one trustee who is either a US citizen individual or a US domestic corporation (such as a US trust company or bank). This requirement exists to ensure the IRS has a US person responsible for withholding and remitting estate tax on principal distributions.

For Bitcoin families, this means the trustee must be someone capable of holding and managing digital assets. A US institutional trustee who refuses to hold Bitcoin creates a conflict with the family's custody preferences. Options:

  • A US family member or trusted advisor as individual co-trustee alongside the non-citizen spouse (the non-citizen spouse can be a co-trustee but cannot be the sole trustee)
  • A US institutional trustee with digital asset custody capabilities — increasingly available through qualified custodians
  • A Wyoming or South Dakota trust company with Bitcoin custody authorization

Requirement 2: Trustee Must Have Right to Withhold Estate Tax on Principal Distributions

The trustee must have the authority to withhold the applicable estate tax before distributing any principal from the QDOT. This right must be explicit in the trust document — it cannot be overridden by the surviving spouse. The estate tax rate applied to principal distributions is the first spouse's marginal estate tax rate (the rate that would have applied had the marital deduction not been elected).

Requirement 3: The $2M Threshold — Corporate Trustee or Security Required

For QDOTs holding assets with a fair market value exceeding $2 million, additional requirements apply:

  • A US bank or domestic trust company must be trustee (a US individual is insufficient), or
  • The trustee must furnish a bond or letter of credit to the IRS equal to 65% of the QDOT's fair market value

For Bitcoin families, most QDOTs will exceed $2M. This practically requires either a qualified institutional custodian as trustee or significant security posted to the IRS — both of which create administrative overhead and cost.

Requirement 4: The QDOT Election Must Be Made on the Estate Tax Return

The QDOT election is made on Form 706 (the federal estate tax return), due nine months after the date of death (15 months with extension). Assets must be in the QDOT before the estate tax return is filed, or the surviving spouse must irrevocably assign the inherited property to the QDOT by that deadline.

This means the QDOT can be set up in the will in advance (the cleanest approach), or the surviving spouse can post-death assign inherited assets into a newly created QDOT before the return deadline. Both approaches work, but pre-planning avoids the time pressure and risk of post-death execution errors.

QDOT and Bitcoin Custody: The Practical Problem

Bitcoin's unique self-custody model creates friction with QDOT requirements. The QDOT must hold the Bitcoin — meaning the trustee must control the keys or use a qualified custodian. Options:

  1. Multi-signature setup with US trustee as required co-signer: A 2-of-3 multisig arrangement where the US trustee holds one key, the surviving spouse holds one key, and a backup key is in geographically distributed cold storage. Principal distributions require the US trustee's co-signature — satisfying the withholding requirement.
  2. Institutional custodian (Coinbase Prime, Fidelity Digital Assets, Anchorage Digital): The QDOT holds the Bitcoin at an institutional custodian, with the US trustee as the authorized account controller. The custodian enforces distribution controls. Cleanest for large estates; adds counterparty dependency the family may not want.
  3. Wyoming or South Dakota digital asset trust company: Specialized trust companies in these states have statutory authority to hold digital assets as trustee, understand Bitcoin custody, and can satisfy the corporate trustee requirement for the $2M+ threshold.

QDOT Tax Mechanics: When the Tax Is Paid

The estate tax in a QDOT is triggered by three events:

Trigger Event Tax Consequence Who Pays Timing
Principal distribution to surviving spouse Estate tax at first spouse's rate on amount distributed QDOT trustee withholds and remits April 15 following the year of distribution
Death of surviving non-citizen spouse Estate tax on remaining QDOT assets at first spouse's rate QDOT trustee; included in surviving spouse's estate return 9 months after surviving spouse's death
QDOT ceases to qualify Immediate estate tax on all QDOT assets QDOT trustee Immediately
Surviving spouse naturalizes as US citizen No further QDOT requirements — marital deduction fully applies — QDOT terminates; assets distribute to spouse free of further deferred tax

The income from the QDOT — Bitcoin mining proceeds, staking rewards, or managed payout distributions — is distributed to the surviving spouse and taxed as ordinary income in their hands, the same as income from any trust. This is not a tax-free flow; it is simply income tax rather than estate tax.

The hardship exception: Distributions of principal for hardship — immediate financial need for health, maintenance, education, or support — may qualify for a QDOT hardship exception, avoiding the estate tax trigger on that distribution. The hardship standard is narrow, but it provides a safety valve for genuine financial emergencies. Trustees administering Bitcoin QDOTs should document hardship distributions carefully.

The Elevated Annual Gift Exclusion for Non-Citizen Spouses

While you are alive, the annual gift tax exclusion for gifts to a non-citizen spouse is substantially higher than the standard exclusion. In 2026, the elevated exclusion is approximately $185,000 (indexed annually from the $100,000 base established in 1988). This compares to the standard $19,000 annual exclusion for all other recipients, including citizen spouses.

Congress provided this elevated exclusion to allow non-citizen spouses to receive meaningful annual transfers — comparable in spirit to the unlimited marital deduction for citizens — while still maintaining a cap that ensures some US tax jurisdiction over the assets.

Using the Elevated Exclusion Strategically

For Bitcoin families, the elevated $185,000 annual exclusion represents a significant systematic transfer opportunity:

  • A US citizen spouse can transfer approximately $185,000 in Bitcoin to a non-citizen spouse each year without gift tax and without using any lifetime exemption
  • Over 10 years: approximately $1.85M in Bitcoin transferred tax-free (at current values — if Bitcoin appreciates, the transferred value grows in the non-citizen spouse's hands, outside the US citizen's taxable estate)
  • The Bitcoin transferred takes a carryover basis in the non-citizen spouse's hands (gift basis rules apply — not the stepped-up basis available at death)
  • If the non-citizen spouse is not a US person for income tax purposes, the transferred Bitcoin may not be subject to US capital gains tax on eventual sale — depending on their residency and tax treaty status

The carryover basis trap: Gifts carry over the donor's basis. If you gift low-basis Bitcoin to a non-citizen spouse (and they remain a US tax resident), they inherit your embedded capital gain — not the stepped-up basis they would receive by inheriting through your estate. The gift may reduce estate tax but increase income tax on eventual sale. For high-appreciation Bitcoin, the hold-until-death strategy may be more tax-efficient than lifetime gifting to a non-citizen spouse — unless the step-up would be eroded by estate tax anyway.

The Naturalization Strategy: Solve the Problem Before It Arises

The simplest and most complete solution to the non-citizen spouse estate tax problem is naturalization. A non-citizen spouse who becomes a US citizen before the first spouse's death receives the full unlimited marital deduction — no QDOT required, no principal distribution tax, no corporate trustee requirement.

Additionally, even after death, if the surviving non-citizen spouse naturalizes before the federal estate tax return is filed (due nine months after death, extendable to 15 months), the IRS has allowed the QDOT requirement to be waived. This post-death naturalization window provides a critical contingency if the non-citizen spouse was in the process of naturalizing when the first spouse died unexpectedly.

The Naturalization Timeline

Naturalization typically requires:

  • Five years of continuous permanent residence (three years if married to a US citizen and living together)
  • Physical presence requirement (30 months of actual US physical presence in the 5-year period)
  • N-400 application, biometrics, interview, and citizenship test
  • Total timeline: 18–36 months from application under current USCIS processing times (varies by field office and case complexity)

For Bitcoin families where one spouse has been a green card holder for several years, naturalization is often a straightforward process that simply hasn't been prioritized. Making the naturalization timeline explicit — and building it into the estate plan as the primary solution — can eliminate the entire QDOT framework and the complexity that comes with it.

The planning priority: If your non-citizen spouse is eligible to naturalize, the most effective estate planning action for an international Bitcoin couple is often not drafting a QDOT — it is scheduling the N-400 application. The QDOT remains as a backstop in case naturalization is delayed or the first death occurs before citizenship is granted. But naturalization is the cleanest, most complete solution.

The OBBBA $15M Exemption and Non-Citizen Spouses

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) increased the federal estate tax exemption to $15M per individual (approximately $30M for a married couple using portability). For Bitcoin families with estates below $15M, the non-citizen spouse issue may be entirely academic — if the US citizen's estate is under $15M, there is no taxable estate even without the marital deduction.

The planning implications vary by estate size:

Gross Estate Size Non-Citizen Spouse Issue? Primary Recommendation
Under $15M No — full exemption covers the estate Standard estate plan; QDOT not needed for current estate. Monitor if Bitcoin appreciates significantly.
$15M–$30M Yes — taxable at first death without marital deduction QDOT for assets above exemption; naturalization as primary solution if feasible; elevated gift exclusion to systematically reduce estate.
$30M–$60M Significant exposure at first death QDOT + dynasty trust combination; GRAT to remove appreciation; naturalization accelerated; life insurance trust (ILIT) to fund estate tax if needed.
Over $60M Material estate tax at first death Full structural planning: QDOT, dynasty trust, GRAT program, IDGT, life insurance, possible SLAT pre-death transfers. Engage estate planning counsel immediately.

An important caveat: Bitcoin's price trajectory means an estate that is comfortably under $15M today can cross the threshold within a few years. Bitcoin families who are "fine for now" need annual reassessment — the window for planning (particularly naturalization) requires lead time that an unexpected Bitcoin price surge may eliminate.

Alternatives and Complements to the QDOT

The QDOT is the primary mechanism for non-citizen spouse estate planning, but several alternatives and complementary strategies apply depending on the family's situation:

Dynasty Trust for Children (Bypass the Spouse)

Rather than leaving Bitcoin to the non-citizen spouse (triggering the QDOT requirement), some families structure their estate plan to leave Bitcoin directly to a dynasty trust for children and grandchildren. The non-citizen spouse receives income from the trust for life (and potentially principal for hardship), but the Bitcoin technically passes to the dynasty trust rather than to the spouse.

This approach bypasses the marital deduction question entirely — the dynasty trust uses the US citizen's $15M exemption rather than relying on the marital deduction. The trade-off: the Bitcoin does not belong to the surviving spouse in the same way — they are a beneficiary of a trust rather than an outright owner. For families with strong intergenerational wealth transfer goals, this trade-off is often acceptable.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT)

A SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust) allows the US citizen spouse to make a completed gift of Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust for the non-citizen spouse's benefit during life. The Bitcoin is removed from the US citizen's taxable estate. The non-citizen spouse benefits from the trust income and permissible distributions.

The SLAT requires the US citizen to use lifetime exemption to fund the transfer — but the transfer happens now, at current Bitcoin values, removing all future appreciation from the estate. For families with meaningful remaining lifetime exemption and a long time horizon for Bitcoin appreciation, the SLAT can be significantly more efficient than the QDOT.

Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) to Fund Estate Tax

If estate tax at the first death is unavoidable — the estate is large, naturalization isn't feasible, and QDOT administration is acceptable — an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) holding a survivorship life insurance policy can be used to fund the eventual estate tax liability. The life insurance proceeds flow estate-tax-free to the trust, providing liquidity to pay the tax without forcing a Bitcoin sale at a potentially inopportune price.

Bitcoin Custody and Key Management for International Families

Non-citizen spouse situations create unique Bitcoin custody challenges beyond the QDOT structure. Several scenarios to plan for explicitly:

What Happens to the Keys If the US Citizen Spouse Dies First?

If the US citizen spouse controls a self-custody Bitcoin wallet and dies unexpectedly — before the QDOT structure is executed, or before keys are distributed to the trustee — the surviving non-citizen spouse may face both a key recovery challenge and a QDOT funding deadline simultaneously. This is a genuine risk for any Bitcoin family that has not separated key management from personal control.

The solution: key access and recovery documentation should be held by the estate's executor or a designated custodian, completely independent of whether the surviving spouse is a citizen. The keys — or access instructions — should be transferable to a QDOT trustee within the nine-month deadline.

International Travel and Regulatory Risk

Non-citizen spouses may travel internationally more frequently than citizen spouses. Bitcoin carried across borders — even on hardware wallets in luggage — can encounter customs, anti-money laundering, or capital controls issues depending on the jurisdiction. For families with significant Bitcoin holdings, the intersection of international travel and custody should be explicitly addressed in the family's investment policy statement: what can be transported, what stays in cold storage, and who manages access if the traveling spouse is unable to return.

8-Item Planning Checklist for Bitcoin Families With a Non-Citizen Spouse

  • Determine citizenship status precisely. Green card holder is not a citizen. Dual citizen is a citizen for estate tax purposes. E-2 or other non-immigrant visa holder is not. Confirm your spouse's exact citizenship and immigration status with an immigration attorney before relying on any estate planning assumptions.
  • Calculate your current estate against the $15M exemption. If your Bitcoin + other assets are comfortably under $15M, the non-citizen spouse issue is currently moot — but monitor annually given Bitcoin's appreciation trajectory. Document the calculation and revisit after any significant price move.
  • Assess naturalization eligibility and timeline. If your non-citizen spouse has been a permanent resident for 3 years (married to US citizen) or 5 years, they may be eligible to naturalize now. If so, prioritize the N-400 application before any complex QDOT planning — naturalization eliminates the problem entirely.
  • Draft a QDOT as the backstop. Regardless of naturalization plans, include a QDOT in the US citizen's estate plan as a contingency for a death that occurs before naturalization is complete. The QDOT can be drafted to terminate automatically if the surviving spouse naturalizes within a specified period.
  • Select a QDOT trustee capable of Bitcoin custody. Identify a US citizen co-trustee (a family member, a trusted US advisor, or a Wyoming digital asset trust company) who can competently hold Bitcoin and satisfy the §2056A trustee requirements. Institutional custodians with digital asset capabilities work for larger estates.
  • Use the elevated $185,000 annual gift exclusion now. If you have not been making annual gifts to your non-citizen spouse, start. $185,000/year in Bitcoin transferred to the non-citizen spouse reduces the US citizen's taxable estate systematically. Document each transfer and file a gift tax return if the value exceeds the exclusion.
  • Integrate Bitcoin key access into estate documents. Ensure your will or trust instruments include provisions for transferring Bitcoin key access to the QDOT trustee. Key access documentation should be held independently of the US citizen's personal control — in an estate planning vault with the executor.
  • Review the plan annually or after any significant Bitcoin price move. A couple with $12M in Bitcoin today crosses the $15M exemption threshold after a 25% Bitcoin price increase. The QDOT and naturalization timeline become urgent at that point — not when the price has already moved. Build an annual review into your calendar.

The Bottom Line

The non-citizen spouse estate tax issue is not obscure — it is one of the most common structural gaps in Bitcoin estate planning, and it is entirely fixable with the right planning in place. The unlimited marital deduction's absence is a real and significant tax exposure, but the solutions — QDOT, elevated annual gift exclusion, naturalization, dynasty trust structures — are well-established and effective.

For most international Bitcoin families, the optimal path is: naturalize as soon as eligible, use the elevated annual gift exclusion while the process is ongoing, and maintain a QDOT as the drafted backstop in the estate plan. For families where naturalization is not feasible or not desired, the QDOT combined with a properly structured Bitcoin custody arrangement provides deferral — not elimination, but deferral that can be worth millions over decades of Bitcoin appreciation.

The families who fail here are not those who tried the wrong strategy — they are those who never discovered the problem. If your spouse is not a US citizen and you hold significant Bitcoin, this conversation with your estate planning attorney is not optional. It is the first item on the agenda.
