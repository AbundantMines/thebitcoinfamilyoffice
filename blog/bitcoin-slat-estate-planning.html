Bitcoin SLAT: Spousal Lifetime Access Trust Strategy for Married Bitcoin Holders

Hal Turner  ·  The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  11 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin SLAT Estate Planning

For married Bitcoin holders with substantial unrealized appreciation, the Spousal Lifetime Access Trust — commonly called a SLAT — offers one of the most compelling structures in modern estate planning. It removes Bitcoin from your taxable estate permanently while preserving your household's indirect access to those assets through your spouse. It is not a perfect structure. It carries meaningful risks that must be understood before funding. But for the right family, it can lock in today's Bitcoin value outside the estate and allow every dollar of future appreciation to pass to heirs estate-tax-free.

This guide covers the mechanics of bitcoin SLAT estate planning in full: how the structure works, why Bitcoin is particularly well-suited to it, the risks that can unwind its benefits, how to fund a SLAT with a Bitcoin position, and how it compares to the other primary vehicles in a Bitcoin estate plan.

What Is a SLAT?

A Spousal Lifetime Access Trust is an irrevocable trust established by one spouse — the grantor — for the benefit of the other spouse and, typically, their descendants. The grantor transfers assets to the trust, using their federal lifetime gift and estate tax exemption (currently $13.99 million per individual in 2025). Because the gift is complete and the assets are in an irrevocable trust, they are removed from the grantor's taxable estate: all future appreciation on those assets occurs outside the estate, permanently.

The key feature that distinguishes a SLAT from a standard irrevocable trust is the beneficiary designation: the grantor's spouse retains access to trust distributions. The trustee can — and typically does — make distributions to the beneficiary spouse for health, education, maintenance, and support. Because married couples share finances, the grantor indirectly benefits from those distributions even though they no longer legally own the trust assets. This "backdoor access" is what makes the SLAT palatable for families who are not yet comfortable locking assets away without any access mechanism.

The trust is typically structured as a grantor trust for income tax purposes, meaning the grantor continues to pay income tax on trust earnings. This is a feature, not a bug: it allows the trust assets to compound without being reduced by income tax, which is effectively an additional tax-free gift from the grantor to the trust each year.

Why Bitcoin Is Particularly Well-Suited to a SLAT

The SLAT is most powerful when funded with assets that are expected to appreciate substantially — because all appreciation that occurs after the funding date is outside the grantor's estate forever. Bitcoin's long-term appreciation thesis makes it an ideal candidate for SLAT funding.

Consider the alternative transfer vehicle most commonly compared to the SLAT: the grantor retained annuity trust (GRAT). A GRAT transfers only the appreciation above the IRS's Section 7520 hurdle rate. In a rising interest rate environment, that hurdle can be substantial — the IRS essentially requires the trust to "beat" the risk-free rate before any assets pass to heirs tax-free. For Bitcoin, which may compound at rates far above any 7520 threshold, GRATs can still work well. But SLATs have no rate hurdle at all: any appreciation above zero, from the day of funding forward, belongs to heirs free of estate tax.

There are additional structural advantages for Bitcoin specifically:

  • All future appreciation is estate-tax-free. If Bitcoin doubles, triples, or increases tenfold after the SLAT is funded, that entire increase passes to heirs without estate tax. You are locking in today's basis for estate tax purposes.
  • No annuity payment structure required. Unlike a GRAT, the SLAT does not require the grantor to receive annuity payments back from the trust. This simplifies the structure and avoids re-inclusion risk from annuity payments returning assets to the estate.
  • The beneficiary spouse retains meaningful access. Bitcoin holders often resist irrevocable transfers because they fear losing access entirely. The SLAT preserves indirect access through the spouse's distribution rights, making it psychologically and practically easier to fund.
  • Grantor trust status allows tax-free compounding. Because the grantor pays income tax on trust earnings, the Bitcoin inside the trust compounds without being reduced by tax drag at the trust level.
Funding a SLAT with Bitcoin today means all future appreciation — potentially across decades of a long-term holding thesis — occurs permanently outside the taxable estate. The exemption used today captures today's price; what happens next benefits only your heirs.

The Reciprocal Trust Doctrine: A Critical Risk to Manage

The SLAT's most frequently exploited planning opportunity — and its most significant legal risk — arise from the same source: the ability for both spouses to create SLATs for each other. Each spouse can establish a SLAT naming the other as beneficiary, effectively giving both spouses access to trust assets while removing both positions from both taxable estates. It sounds elegant. But the IRS has a well-established doctrine designed to prevent exactly this type of arrangement: the reciprocal trust doctrine.

Under the reciprocal trust doctrine, if two trusts are structured as substantially identical "mirror images" of each other — same terms, same trustee, same funding date, same asset class — the IRS may collapse them. When trusts are uncrossed under the doctrine, each grantor is treated as having created a trust for their own benefit, which means the trust assets are pulled back into each grantor's taxable estate. The entire planning benefit is lost.

Avoiding the reciprocal trust doctrine requires meaningful differentiation between the two SLATs. The trusts should be executed on different dates (ideally separated by several months or more), funded with different assets or different amounts, governed by different terms (different distribution standards, different trustee succession provisions), and administered by different trustees. Cosmetic differences are not sufficient — the IRS and courts look to the economic substance of the arrangements.

Practical planning considerations include: funding one SLAT with Bitcoin and the other with different assets; giving one trust more discretionary distribution language while the other uses a standard health, education, maintenance, and support (HEMS) standard; and appointing independent trustees who are not related to each other. The goal is a genuine structural distinction, not a form-over-substance variation.

The Divorce Problem

The SLAT's design feature — indirect access through the beneficiary spouse — is also its central vulnerability. If the couple divorces, the grantor loses all indirect access to the trust assets. The trust is irrevocable; it cannot be unwound. The beneficiary spouse retains access to trust distributions, and depending on how the trust is drafted, may retain that access indefinitely — even if they subsequently remarry. The grantor's Bitcoin is now permanently supporting a former spouse's financial life.

This is not an abstract risk. Divorce rates remain significant, and a trust that was funded during a marriage may be administered over decades during which circumstances change materially. The grantor must be comfortable with the possibility — however remote they consider it — that the trust assets will permanently benefit the beneficiary spouse even if the marriage ends.

Several drafting strategies can mitigate the divorce risk:

  • Automatic suspension of distributions on divorce. The trust document can specify that the beneficiary spouse's distribution rights terminate automatically upon divorce. After divorce, only descendants remain as beneficiaries. This eliminates the risk of funding a former spouse's post-divorce life but also eliminates the grantor's indirect access going forward.
  • Trustee discretion over distributions. Giving the trustee broad discretion — rather than mandatory distributions — means the trustee can respond to changed circumstances. An independent trustee with knowledge of the family situation can naturally reduce distributions in the event of marital difficulty.
  • Trust protector provisions. A trust protector can be given authority to modify the trust in response to changed circumstances, including the ability to remove the beneficiary spouse as a beneficiary following divorce.

None of these provisions is a perfect solution. The SLAT is most appropriate for couples with a strong, stable marriage and a shared planning horizon. It is not a structure to enter lightly when a marriage is uncertain.

The Death of the Beneficiary Spouse

A less discussed but equally important risk: if the beneficiary spouse predeceases the grantor, the grantor loses all indirect access to the trust. The SLAT was designed around the assumption that the beneficiary spouse would survive to receive distributions; when that assumption fails, the grantor is left with assets permanently outside the estate and outside their practical reach.

Mitigating strategies include:

  • Naming descendants as backup beneficiaries. When the beneficiary spouse dies, the trust continues for the benefit of children and grandchildren. The grantor has no access, but the assets continue compounding for the family.
  • Survivor annuity provisions. Some SLATs include a provision that, on the beneficiary spouse's death, a portion of the trust distributes back to the grantor as an annuity — effectively a built-in return mechanism for extreme scenarios. This requires careful drafting to avoid estate inclusion.
  • Life insurance coordination. A survivorship or second-to-die life insurance policy can replace the trust assets at the surviving grantor's death, providing liquidity for the estate even if the SLAT's assets are permanently in trust.

How to Fund a SLAT with Bitcoin

Funding a SLAT with Bitcoin requires careful attention to both the legal transfer mechanics and the Bitcoin custody architecture.

The Gift Transfer

Bitcoin is transferred to the SLAT as a completed gift. This uses the grantor's federal lifetime gift and estate tax exemption — currently $13.99 million per individual, though this amount is scheduled to sunset to approximately $7 million (inflation-adjusted) after 2025 absent Congressional action. Funding a SLAT before any potential sunset date locks in today's higher exemption permanently.

The transfer requires a qualified appraisal of the Bitcoin at the time of gift (typically straightforward given Bitcoin's liquid market), completion of a gift tax return (Form 709), and documentation that the transfer to the trust was a completed gift with no retained strings. The grantor must give up all control over the transferred Bitcoin; any retained rights risk bringing the assets back into the estate.

Custody Architecture Inside the SLAT

Once transferred, the Bitcoin is owned by the SLAT — specifically, by the trustee in their capacity as trustee. The practical implications for custody are significant:

  • Hardware wallet titling. The hardware wallet holding the trust's Bitcoin should be titled in the name of the trustee in their fiduciary capacity (e.g., "Jane Smith, as Trustee of the [Trust Name]"). The private keys belong to the trust, not to the individual trustee personally.
  • Letter of Instruction. The trust should have a Letter of Instruction that specifies the location of hardware wallets, the seed phrase storage protocol, multisignature architecture if used, and the procedure for transferring custody to a successor trustee.
  • Trust document provisions. The trust document itself should address Bitcoin-specific custody issues: who has authority to approve transactions, what custody standard applies, whether the trustee may use third-party custodians, and how trustee succession works in the context of digital assets. Standard trust documents drafted without Bitcoin expertise are often silent on these issues — and silence creates dangerous ambiguity.
  • Trustee competency. The trustee has a fiduciary duty to the beneficiaries. A trustee who does not understand Bitcoin custody — and who loses keys through negligence — may be personally liable for the resulting loss. The trust document should specify the custody standard applicable to digital assets, or appoint a co-trustee or investment advisor with demonstrated Bitcoin custody expertise.

In practice, the SLAT and the dynasty trust are not mutually exclusive. Many families structure a SLAT to hold a portion of their Bitcoin position — allowing the beneficiary spouse to retain access — while funding a dynasty trust with the balance for pure generational transfer with no access requirement. The SLAT handles the "we might need this" portion of the estate; the dynasty trust handles the "this is for our grandchildren" portion.

The Sunset Window: Why Timing Matters

The current federal estate and gift tax exemption of $13.99 million per individual ($27.98 million per married couple) is the result of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which doubled the prior exemption. That doubled exemption is scheduled to revert to approximately $7 million per individual (inflation-adjusted) on January 1, 2026, unless Congress acts to extend or make it permanent.

For Bitcoin holders, this creates a significant planning window. Funding a SLAT now — using the current elevated exemption — locks in that exemption permanently, even if the law reverts. The IRS has issued regulations confirming that gifts made using the higher exemption will not be "clawed back" if the exemption later decreases. Waiting until after a sunset event means a reduced exemption available for SLAT funding — and Bitcoin that continues to appreciate in the meantime remains in the estate.

The combination of a potentially expiring exemption and Bitcoin's long-term appreciation thesis creates a particularly strong case for acting before any legislative change takes effect.

Implementation: What the Process Looks Like

Establishing a Bitcoin SLAT is not a do-it-yourself project. It requires an estate planning attorney with specific experience in irrevocable trust drafting and ideally some familiarity with digital assets. The process typically includes:

  1. Strategy session with your advisory team. Define the size of the SLAT, which Bitcoin positions to fund it with, the trustee selection, and whether you will create one or two SLATs (and how to differentiate them if both spouses are funding).
  2. Trust drafting. Your attorney drafts the trust document with provisions for Bitcoin custody, distribution standards, trustee succession, and the grantor trust provisions that ensure income tax remains with the grantor.
  3. Trustee selection. Select an independent trustee — an individual or corporate trustee — who is not the grantor and who has the fiduciary capability to manage Bitcoin custody. The trustee should be willing to engage with the Letter of Instruction for digital asset management.
  4. Bitcoin transfer. Transfer the Bitcoin to a wallet controlled by the trustee. Document the transfer with clear chain-of-custody records. File Form 709 reporting the gift and allocating the applicable lifetime exemption.
  5. Custody documentation. Prepare the Letter of Instruction covering hardware wallet location, seed phrase storage, key management protocol, and trustee succession procedures for the Bitcoin.
  6. Ongoing administration. The trustee administers the trust according to its terms, maintains the Bitcoin custody architecture, files any required trust income tax returns, and makes distributions to the beneficiary spouse as appropriate.
