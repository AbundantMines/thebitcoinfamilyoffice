Bitcoin Estate Planning in New Jersey

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in New Jersey Est. 10 min read

New Jersey imposes the most aggressive state estate tax in the United States — a $675,000 exemption that has not been indexed for inflation and has remained essentially unchanged for decades. For Bitcoin families domiciled in New Jersey, this is not a peripheral planning concern. It is the dominant structural issue in every estate plan. A Bitcoin holder with even a modest position — say, 3–4 BTC at current prices — has already exceeded New Jersey's estate tax threshold. Every dollar of Bitcoin held in a New Jersey taxable estate above $675,000 is subject to state estate tax at rates rising to 16%.

This guide covers the New Jersey estate tax in full technical detail, the trust and LLC structures available to New Jersey Bitcoin families, the state's Uniform Trust Code framework, and the practical actions that allow families to protect accumulated Bitcoin wealth from an extraordinarily low exemption threshold. Bitcoin estate planning New Jersey is a critical specialty — the interaction between a near-negligible state exemption, trust formation mechanics, and digital asset succession requires precise, coordinated planning.

The New Jersey Estate Tax: The Lowest Exemption in the Country

New Jersey's estate tax — formerly imposed under N.J.S.A. 54:38-1 et seq. — was significantly restructured in 2018. The state repealed its estate tax effective January 1, 2018, for residents dying on or after that date. However, New Jersey retained its inheritance tax, which operates differently from an estate tax and creates its own planning traps for Bitcoin families.

The $675,000 figure referenced in planning discussions is the pre-2018 estate tax exemption that applied before repeal. As of 2018, New Jersey no longer levies a separate estate tax on decedents' estates. This is a significant — and frequently misunderstood — update. New Jersey Bitcoin families should verify with counsel which regime applies to their specific planning timeline.

What remains very much in force is New Jersey's inheritance tax — one of only six states in the country that still imposes one. The inheritance tax applies based on the relationship between the decedent and the beneficiary, not the total size of the estate. It does not exempt estates; it taxes the transfers themselves based on beneficiary class:

  • Class A beneficiaries (surviving spouse, domestic partner, children, grandchildren, parents): Exempt — no New Jersey inheritance tax
  • Class C beneficiaries (siblings, sons-in-law, daughters-in-law): Taxed at 11–16% on amounts above $25,000
  • Class D beneficiaries (all others — nieces, nephews, friends, domestic partners not qualifying as Class A): Taxed at 15–16% with no exemption on first dollar
  • Class E beneficiaries (qualified charities): Exempt
New Jersey's inheritance tax has no minimum estate size threshold for Class D beneficiaries. A Bitcoin transfer of any size to a friend, sibling's child, or non-qualifying partner can trigger tax at 15–16%.

Why New Jersey's Inheritance Tax Hits Bitcoin Families Hard

The inheritance tax creates acute planning urgency for Bitcoin families whose estate plans contemplate transfers to anyone outside Class A. Consider these common scenarios:

Business partners and key personnel: Many Bitcoin-focused entrepreneurs want to transfer Bitcoin to business partners or key employees at death. Under New Jersey's Class D rates, that transfer is taxed at 15–16% from the first dollar — with no exemption.

Unmarried partners: Domestic partners who are not legally recognized under New Jersey's domestic partnership statute (a narrow category) are Class D beneficiaries. A long-term unmarried partner receiving Bitcoin at death faces the full inheritance tax rate.

Nieces, nephews, and extended family: Many high-net-worth individuals want to transfer wealth to siblings' children. In New Jersey, those transfers are Class D — 15% on the first dollar. There is no family sentiment discount in the inheritance tax code.

Charitable giving strategies: Bitcoin holders who want to benefit both family (Class A) and charitable organizations can structure transfers to use Class E exemption for the charitable portion — but this requires careful coordination of the estate plan to maximize the charitable deduction and avoid wasting New Jersey exemptions.

Trust Structures for New Jersey Bitcoin Families

New Jersey adopted a version of the Uniform Trust Code, codified at N.J.S.A. 3B:31-1 et seq. The NJ UTC provides a modern statutory framework that supports the structures Bitcoin families need:

  • Irrevocable trusts and inheritance tax avoidance: Transferring Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust during lifetime removes the assets from the taxable transfer at death — but only if the grantor retains no "incidents of ownership" or control that would cause the assets to be pulled back into the estate. Properly structured lifetime transfers to irrevocable trusts avoid New Jersey inheritance tax entirely on the transferred assets.
  • Dynasty trusts: New Jersey abolished the Rule Against Perpetuities for trusts of personal property in 2016. NJ trusts can now potentially last for multiple generations, holding Bitcoin for descendants without triggering a taxable transfer at each generation. The GST (generation-skipping transfer) tax at the federal level still applies, but dynasty trust structures allow families to minimize transfer tax across multiple generations.
  • Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs): Bitcoin transferred to an irrevocable trust for the benefit of a spouse is not subject to New Jersey inheritance tax at the grantor's death (the trust, not the surviving spouse, holds the asset). The spouse's access is through trust distributions rather than outright ownership, preserving the inheritance tax planning benefit.

Wyoming Trust Situs for New Jersey Families

New Jersey families are not required to use New Jersey trust law. A trust's governing law can be established in Wyoming even if the grantor and beneficiaries are New Jersey residents. Wyoming offers directed trust authority, perpetual dynasty trust capability, and Bitcoin-specific statutory recognition — all accessible through a Wyoming-domiciled corporate trustee.

For New Jersey inheritance tax purposes, the key variable is whether the trust assets are includible in the decedent's taxable estate — not where the trust is sited. A Wyoming-sited trust where the New Jersey grantor has retained no taxable power removes those assets from the New Jersey inheritance tax calculation. Our detailed analysis of Wyoming trusts and LLCs for Bitcoin covers the full mechanics of siting a trust outside your home state.

New Jersey LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

New Jersey LLCs (governed by the New Jersey Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act, N.J.S.A. 42:2C-1 et seq.) provide effective Bitcoin holding vehicles with several planning advantages specific to the NJ inheritance tax environment:

Inheritance tax planning through lifetime transfers: LLC interests transferred during lifetime remove those interests from the New Jersey inheritance tax calculation at death. Annual exclusion gifts of LLC interests — up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2024 federal limit) — can systematically transfer Bitcoin value out of the taxable estate over time.

Valuation discounts: Minority interests in a properly structured family LLC qualify for valuation discounts of 20–35% for lack of marketability and minority interest. This allows more Bitcoin value to be transferred per dollar of gift tax annual exclusion or lifetime exemption — a powerful strategy for New Jersey families facing the inheritance tax.

Operating agreement governance: The LLC's operating agreement serves as the governance document for Bitcoin custody — specifying multi-signature requirements, key-person succession, transaction authorization, and restrictions on voluntary transfers of membership interests.

Domicile Planning: Should You Leave New Jersey?

For high-net-worth Bitcoin families, New Jersey's tax environment — high income tax (up to 10.75%), inheritance tax, and the overall cost of living — creates a strong incentive to consider domicile change. States like Florida, Texas, Nevada, or Wyoming offer zero income tax, no inheritance or estate tax, and strong asset protection frameworks.

Domicile change for New Jersey residents requires genuine, documented relocation — not merely establishing a nominal out-of-state address. New Jersey actively audits high-income taxpayers who claim to have changed domicile, and the standards require actual change in primary residence, economic connections, social ties, and intent. Bitcoin families considering this strategy should work with counsel experienced in New Jersey domicile audits.

Digital Asset Succession in New Jersey

New Jersey has not enacted digital-asset-specific succession legislation, though the legislature has considered versions of the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act. In the absence of a specific statute, New Jersey courts apply general property and fiduciary law to Bitcoin succession. This makes explicit estate plan documentation critical: powers of attorney, trust instruments, and will provisions must explicitly authorize fiduciaries to access, manage, and transfer Bitcoin.

Every New Jersey Bitcoin estate plan should include: explicit fiduciary authority to access digital assets in every governing document, a separate technical access document (not containing private keys, but containing the information needed to locate and access custody systems), and a tested succession protocol coordinated with the actual custody architecture. For multi-signature arrangements, the succession plan must address key-holder succession as a separate operational matter.

Practical Priorities for New Jersey Bitcoin Families

  1. Map your beneficiary classes — identify every intended recipient of Bitcoin at death and determine whether they are Class A (no inheritance tax), Class C (11–16%), or Class D (15–16%). This mapping drives the structure of every downstream planning decision.
  2. Transfer Bitcoin to irrevocable trust structures during lifetime — lifetime transfers remove assets from the inheritance tax calculation at death. The earlier this happens, the greater the compounding benefit of removing Bitcoin's potential appreciation from the taxable estate.
  3. Establish a New Jersey LLC as Bitcoin holding vehicle — with minority interest gifting to trusts for descendants at discounted values, reducing the inheritance tax exposure on remaining assets systematically over time.
  4. Evaluate Wyoming trust situs for the family trust holding the LLC interests — capturing Wyoming's directed trust and perpetuity advantages while maintaining New Jersey compliance on residency-based tax obligations.
  5. Document Bitcoin custody explicitly in all fiduciary instruments — New Jersey has no statutory safety net for digital asset succession, making explicit documentation the only protection available.
  6. Model domicile change if your Bitcoin position is significant — the combined income tax, inheritance tax, and cost-of-living advantage of relocating to Florida, Texas, or Nevada can be substantial for families with large Bitcoin positions.

Bitcoin estate planning New Jersey requires advisors who understand both the inheritance tax's beneficiary-class mechanics and the technical realities of Bitcoin custody succession. A general estate attorney unfamiliar with digital assets, or a Bitcoin-native advisor unfamiliar with New Jersey's inheritance tax, will each leave significant value on the table. The intersection of both disciplines is where the real planning lives.
