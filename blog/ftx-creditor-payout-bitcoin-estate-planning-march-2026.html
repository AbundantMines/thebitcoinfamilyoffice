The Tax Clock: What Happens the Moment FTX Funds Arrive

Before estate planning, the tax question. When Bitcoin from the FTX estate lands in your wallet, the IRS treatment depends on three variables: the form of your original deposit, the form of your distribution, and whether you are an original creditor or a claim purchaser.

Original Creditors Receiving Crypto

If you deposited 10 Bitcoin on FTX before the collapse and receive 10 Bitcoin (or equivalent value) back, the IRS generally treats this as a return of your original property. Your cost basis carries forward from your original acquisition date and price. If you bought those 10 Bitcoin at $20,000 each and receive them back now at $66,700, you have not realized a gain — the gain is unrealized until you sell or dispose of the coins.

However, if the FTX estate pays you more than your original deposit value (measured at the petition date), the excess may be treated as ordinary income. Some creditor classes have received above-petition-date valuations due to the estate’s recovery performance. This creates an immediate tax liability on the excess.

Original Creditors Receiving USD

If your original deposit was crypto but you receive USD, the distribution is likely treated as a disposition of the underlying crypto at the distribution value. This triggers capital gains or losses based on the difference between your original cost basis and the USD value received. If you deposited Bitcoin at $50,000 and receive a USD distribution valued at $66,700 per coin equivalent, you have a $16,700 per coin capital gain.

Claim Purchasers

If you bought FTX claims on the secondary market at a discount — say, 40 cents on the dollar — the entire difference between your purchase price and the distribution value is likely taxable. The character (ordinary vs. capital) and holding period depend on the specifics of your claim purchase. This is a high-complexity tax situation that requires professional guidance before the distribution arrives, not after.

Critical timing note: Regardless of your tax treatment, the cost basis of Bitcoin received in the distribution is established at the moment of receipt. If Bitcoin is at $66,700 when your distribution arrives and you later transfer it to an irrevocable trust at $70,000, your gift tax valuation is $70,000 per coin. The $3,300 gap is appreciation that happened inside your taxable estate. This is why speed matters.

The 48-Hour Estate Planning Window

Estate planners refer to the period immediately after a sudden wealth event — an inheritance, a business sale, a litigation settlement, a crypto distribution — as the “planning window.” It is the narrow period when the recipient has received value but has not yet allowed that value to appreciate inside their taxable estate.

For FTX creditors receiving significant Bitcoin distributions in Q1 2026, the 48-hour planning window is unusually powerful for three reasons:

Reason 1: The price is compressed. Bitcoin at $66,700 means every coin transferred into an irrevocable trust consumes $66,700 of your $15,000,000 lifetime exemption. That same coin at $100,000 consumes $100,000 of exemption — 50% more. The exemption is a finite resource that does not replenish. Using it at a 47% discount to all-time highs is the most efficient possible deployment.

Reason 2: The appreciation upside is asymmetric. From $66,700, Bitcoin reaching its prior all-time high of $126,000 represents 89% appreciation. Every dollar of that 89% move that occurs inside an irrevocable trust is permanently outside the taxable estate. Every dollar that occurs outside the trust — because the recipient waited — adds to the eventual estate tax liability at 40%.

Reason 3: The tax basis is fresh. The distribution establishes a new cost basis reference point. If you transfer Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust within 48 hours of receiving it, the fair market value for gift tax purposes and the cost basis for income tax purposes are nearly identical. There is minimal embedded gain to complicate the planning calculus.

“The FTX payout is a second chance. Not just a second chance to hold Bitcoin, but a second chance to hold it in the right structure. The families who lost access to their crypto in 2022 now get to decide: does this Bitcoin go back into a taxable estate, or does it go into a trust that protects it for generations?”

Five Structures for FTX Payout Proceeds

Not every family needs the same structure. The optimal approach depends on the distribution size, current exemption usage, family dynamics, and investment thesis. Here are the five most relevant structures for FTX distribution proceeds, ranked by planning impact.

1. Irrevocable Trust Transfer (Direct)

The simplest and most impactful move. Transfer Bitcoin received from the FTX distribution directly into an existing or newly established irrevocable trust. The transfer is a completed gift, valued at fair market value on the date of transfer. All subsequent appreciation occurs outside the grantor’s taxable estate.

2. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

A GRAT is a trust that pays the grantor a fixed annuity stream, with any appreciation above the IRS §7520 hurdle rate passing to beneficiaries gift-tax-free. The current §7520 rate (approximately 4.4%) sets the bar that Bitcoin must clear.

For FTX distribution proceeds, a GRAT is attractive because the entry price is compressed. A 2-year GRAT funded with 50 BTC at $66,700 ($3,335,000) has a hurdle of approximately $3,335,000 × 1.044² ≈ $3,635,000. If Bitcoin reaches $100,000 during the term, the trust holds $5,000,000 and approximately $1,365,000 passes to beneficiaries tax-free. If Bitcoin reaches $126,000, approximately $2,965,000 passes tax-free.

The zeroed-out GRAT structure — where the annuity payments are designed to return the full initial value to the grantor, leaving a zero gift tax liability — allows families to attempt this transfer with no exemption cost. If Bitcoin declines during the GRAT term, nothing passes and no exemption is wasted. If Bitcoin appreciates, the excess is a free transfer to the next generation.

3. Dynasty Trust with GST Exemption

For families receiving larger distributions ($1M+) who want multi-generational protection, a dynasty trust in a trust-friendly jurisdiction (Wyoming, Nevada, South Dakota) provides perpetual protection. Bitcoin placed in a dynasty trust with properly allocated Generation-Skipping Transfer (GST) tax exemption passes through unlimited generations without estate tax, gift tax, or GST tax at each generational transfer.

The FTX distribution is an ideal dynasty trust funding event because the compressed price means the GST exemption — which is also $15,000,000 per individual under OBBBA — covers more Bitcoin at $66,700 than it would at higher prices. A family can place 224 BTC into a dynasty trust using their full GST exemption at current prices. At $126,000 per coin, that same exemption covers only 119 BTC.

4. Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

For creditors receiving distributions with significant embedded gains — particularly claim purchasers who bought at deep discounts — a Charitable Remainder Trust can defer capital gains tax while providing income and an estate planning benefit. The trust sells the Bitcoin without immediate tax, reinvests the proceeds, pays the donor an annuity, and the remainder passes to charity at death. The donor receives an upfront charitable deduction.

This is particularly relevant for claim purchasers who acquired FTX claims at 30–50 cents on the dollar. Without a CRT, the gain on disposition is fully taxable. Inside a CRT, the gain is deferred across the trust’s payout term.

5. Annual Exclusion Gifts ($19,000 per Recipient)

The simplest immediate action for any FTX creditor: gift $19,000 worth of Bitcoin to each child, grandchild, or other recipient. No gift tax return required. No exemption consumed. A family with four children and eight grandchildren can transfer $228,000 of Bitcoin immediately with zero tax friction. If both spouses gift, $456,000.

At $66,700 per coin, $19,000 buys approximately 0.285 BTC per gift. If Bitcoin reaches $126,000, each annual exclusion gift appreciates to approximately $35,850 — an 89% return outside the taxable estate, accomplished with a form that takes five minutes to execute.

The Custody Architecture You Need Before Funds Arrive

The most common mistake FTX creditors will make this week is receiving their distribution without a custody plan. After losing access to their crypto on a centralized exchange for 40 months, the reflex will be to move funds to self-custody immediately. That instinct is correct — but self-custody without an estate plan is only marginally better than exchange custody without an estate plan.

Here is the custody architecture a Bitcoin-wealthy family should have established before the FTX distribution arrives:

Pre-Distribution Custody Checklist

  1. Hardware wallet or multi-signature arrangement ready. If receiving Bitcoin, have a Coldcard, Trezor, or Ledger device initialized and tested. For amounts exceeding $500,000, a multi-signature setup (2-of-3 or 3-of-5) with geographically distributed keys is strongly recommended.
  2. Irrevocable trust with digital asset provisions drafted and signed. The trust document should specifically address: what constitutes “control” of digital assets, who holds signing authority, how keys are stored and transferred, and what happens if the trustee becomes incapacitated. New York’s UCC Article 12 amendments taking effect June 3, 2026, make “control” language especially important.
  3. Directed trust agreement (if using Wyoming PFTC). A directed trust separates investment authority from administrative authority, allowing a family-controlled investment advisor to direct Bitcoin transactions while a corporate trustee handles compliance and reporting.
  4. Qualified custodian account (if trust requires institutional custody). Coinbase Custody, Fidelity Digital Assets, or similar institutional custodian accounts take 2–6 weeks to establish. If you don’t have one yet, you’re already late for this distribution — but start now for the next one.
  5. Letter of instruction for key access. A document (kept with your estate attorney, not on a computer) detailing: where hardware wallets are located, what multi-sig quorum is required, how to access backup seed phrases, and who to contact for each key. This document is the difference between your heirs accessing your Bitcoin and your Bitcoin becoming a digital tombstone.

Why FTX Creditors Are the Highest-Risk Demographic for Estate Planning Mistakes

This is uncomfortable but necessary. FTX creditors receiving distributions this week are at elevated risk of making estate planning errors, for reasons specific to their history:

Recency bias from the collapse. After losing access to their crypto for 40 months, many creditors will prioritize personal control above all else. “I’m never letting anyone else hold my crypto again.” The instinct is understandable but counterproductive. Self-custody in a personal wallet protects against exchange collapse. An irrevocable trust protects against estate tax, creditor claims, divorce proceedings, and heir disputes. The threats are different; the solutions should be too.

Unfamiliarity with the current landscape. The crypto custody, regulatory, and estate planning landscape has changed dramatically since November 2022. The SEC has issued new crypto securities guidance. New York’s UCC Article 12 takes effect in June. The CLARITY Act is advancing through Congress. OBBBA changed the exemption framework. A creditor who froze their planning at the 2022 collapse date is operating with outdated assumptions.

Sudden wealth syndrome. For creditors who had written off their FTX holdings as a total loss, receiving a $500K+ distribution is psychologically equivalent to a windfall. Research on lottery winners, inheritance recipients, and litigation settlement recipients consistently shows that sudden wealth is the moment when the most expensive financial mistakes are made — precisely because the emotional processing of “I have this money again” overwhelms the analytical discipline of “what structure should hold this money.”

The HODL trap. Many early Bitcoin holders have an identity built around “never selling.” Transferring Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust is not selling. It is moving your Bitcoin from a structure where the government takes 40% when you die to a structure where 100% passes to your heirs. The HODL thesis is about conviction in the asset. The estate plan is about conviction in the structure. They are complementary, not contradictory.

“FTX creditors got a second chance to own Bitcoin. The question is whether they’ll make the same structural mistake twice — holding a generational asset in a structure designed for a single lifetime.”

The Historical Parallel: What Happened After Prior Bankruptcy Distributions

FTX is not the first major crypto bankruptcy to distribute funds during a fear-driven market. The Mt. Gox trustee distributed Bitcoin to creditors in 2024 during a period of elevated uncertainty. The key lesson from Mt. Gox distributions: creditors who immediately sold their recovered Bitcoin into the market captured the recovery price. Creditors who held — and structured their holdings inside trusts — captured the subsequent appreciation.

The FTX distribution arrives at a moment when Bitcoin is at $66,700. The last time Bitcoin traded at this level was before its run to $126,000. The market has already priced in Q1’s worst-quarter performance, geopolitical uncertainty, and rate hike fears. What it has not priced in is the potential recovery.

For estate planning purposes, this is the fulcrum moment. The distribution forces a decision: does this Bitcoin go into a personal wallet (taxable estate) or into a trust (protected estate)? The decision made this week determines the tax treatment of every dollar of future appreciation — for the rest of the grantor’s life and beyond.

Seven-Step Playbook: FTX Distribution Week

What to Do Before, During, and After Your Distribution Arrives

  1. Before distribution (now): Confirm your KYC status with your distribution agent (BitGo, Kraken, or Payoneer). Verify your receiving wallet address. If you don’t have a hardware wallet, order one today — 2-day shipping is your friend.
  2. Day of distribution: Document the exact time of receipt and the price of Bitcoin at that moment. This is your cost basis reference. Screenshot the transaction and the price. This is your contemporaneous record for the IRS.
  3. Within 24 hours: Contact your estate attorney about an irrevocable trust transfer. If you don’t have an estate attorney experienced with digital assets, start the search now. The American College of Trust and Estate Counsel (ACTEC) maintains a directory.
  4. Within 48 hours: Execute annual exclusion gifts ($19,000 per recipient) to children, grandchildren, or other beneficiaries. These require no gift tax return, consume no lifetime exemption, and can be completed immediately via wallet transfer.
  5. Within 7 days: Complete irrevocable trust transfer. The fair market value on the date of transfer determines exemption usage. Every day of appreciation between your distribution and your transfer is appreciation that happens inside your taxable estate.
  6. Within 30 days: File IRS Form 709 (gift tax return) if irrevocable trust transfers exceed annual exclusion amounts. Your tax advisor should prepare this concurrently with the transfer, not after.
  7. Ongoing: Establish custody architecture for trust-held Bitcoin. Multi-sig, geographic distribution of keys, successor trustee access protocol, letter of instruction. The trust structure is only as good as the custody implementation.

What You Should Not Do

Equally important: the mistakes to avoid during FTX distribution week.

  • Do not leave Bitcoin on the distribution exchange. You lost access to your Bitcoin on a centralized exchange once. The lesson is obvious. Move to self-custody or trust custody immediately.
  • Do not sell into the panic. Bitcoin is at $66,700 during extreme fear. Selling here locks in the worst Q1 on record. If you need to reduce your position, do it methodically via dollar-cost averaging out, not in a single fear-driven liquidation.
  • Do not wait for “the right time” to do estate planning. There is no right time. There is now, when Bitcoin is down 47% from its all-time high and your exemption covers more coins per dollar. And there is later, when Bitcoin may be higher and your exemption covers fewer coins.
  • Do not assume your 2022 estate plan is still current. OBBBA changed exemption amounts. The SEC issued new crypto guidance. UCC Article 12 takes effect in June. Your trust documents need to reflect the 2026 legal landscape, not the 2022 one.
  • Do not discuss your distribution publicly. Two California teenagers drove 600 miles to Scottsdale to rob $66 million in Bitcoin from a known holder. Physical security starts with information security. Your FTX distribution is private. Keep it that way.
