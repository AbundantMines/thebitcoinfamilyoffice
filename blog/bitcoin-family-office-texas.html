Bitcoin Family Office in Texas: The Complete 2026 Setup Guide

By Hal Franklin · February 26, 2026 · ~19 min read

Texas has become the de facto center of Bitcoin wealth in America. It hosts more Bitcoin mining operations than any other state, has passed the most significant Bitcoin-related legislation outside Wyoming, and has seen a sustained influx of high-net-worth Bitcoin holders relocating from Bitcoin family office in California, New York, and other high-tax jurisdictions. If you hold meaningful Bitcoin wealth and live in — or are considering moving to — Texas, understanding how to structure that wealth within Texas's specific legal environment is essential.

This guide covers the full landscape: Texas's tax advantages, its unique community property implications for Bitcoin, its trust and LLC structures, and the practical steps for establishing a Bitcoin family office in the Lone Star State. Section 1: Why Texas for Bitcoin Families

The case for Texas begins with the obvious: no state income tax. Texas is one of nine states that levy no personal income tax, meaning Bitcoin capital gains realized by Texas residents are subject only to federal tax. For a Bitcoin holder who purchased early and is sitting on significant unrealized appreciation, this is not a minor detail — it is potentially millions of dollars in savings relative to California (13.3% capital gains rate) or New York (up to 10.9% for NYC residents).

But Texas's appeal for Bitcoin families goes beyond simple tax efficiency. The state has made a series of deliberate legislative and policy choices that signal deep institutional alignment with Bitcoin as a legitimate form of wealth.

No State Income Tax

Texas's no-income-tax status is enshrined in the Texas Constitution, which prohibits a personal income tax unless approved by voter referendum. This constitutional protection makes Texas's zero-income-tax status significantly more durable than that of states whose income tax levels are set by ordinary legislation. For a Bitcoin family planning 10, 20, or 50-year wealth transfer strategies, the stability of this tax environment matters as much as the current rate.

Texas does impose a franchise tax on business entities (also called the Texas margin tax), but it is structured as a tax on gross margin — not on income or capital gains — and applies rates of 0.75% (most businesses) or 0.375% (qualifying businesses). Properly structured personal holding entities often fall below the franchise tax's "no tax due" threshold of $2.47 million in Texas gross receipts.

No State Estate or Inheritance Tax

Texas has no state estate tax and no state inheritance tax. When a Texas resident dies holding Bitcoin — whether directly or through a family trust — only federal estate tax applies. This simplifies planning significantly: Texas families need to structure around the federal exemption and federal rate structure, without simultaneously navigating state-specific exemptions, marital deduction rules, or state-level portability elections.

Texas HB 1666 — Bitcoin Legal Tender Support

Texas House Bill 1666 established the state's position that Bitcoin should be recognized as a valid medium of exchange and articulated protections for Texas residents' right to hold and use Bitcoin. While full "legal tender" status involves federal monetary law preemption questions that Texas cannot unilaterally resolve, HB 1666 sent a clear signal to Bitcoin businesses and holders: Texas is an affirmatively welcoming jurisdiction, not a hostile one. The practical effect has been to encourage Bitcoin-native businesses, advisors, attorneys, and custodians to establish Texas operations — creating an ecosystem that supports sophisticated Bitcoin wealth management.

Texas Senate Bill 21 — Strategic Bitcoin Reserve

Texas Senate Bill 21, passed in 2025, directed the Texas comptroller to establish a Bitcoin strategic reserve fund — making Texas one of the first US states to formally hold Bitcoin as a state reserve asset. This is more than symbolism. SB 21 signals that Texas's legislative leadership views Bitcoin as a legitimate store of value worthy of institutional holding, not a speculative asset to be taxed, regulated, or discouraged. For Bitcoin families evaluating where to domicile, a state whose government holds Bitcoin is a materially different operating environment than one that views it with regulatory suspicion.

Energy Abundance and Bitcoin Mining Capital

Texas is the largest Bitcoin mining state in the US, accounting for a significant share of North American hashrate. This concentration exists for structural reasons: Texas has abundant, affordable energy (wind, solar, and natural gas), a deregulated electricity market (ERCOT) that allows miners to negotiate directly with generators, and a political environment that has actively courted mining operations. For Bitcoin families who integrate mining into their wealth strategy — using mining operations to accumulate Bitcoin at cost basis with significant tax deductions — Texas's mining infrastructure is unmatched. The state's abundance of operational Bitcoin mining companies also creates a robust local market for mining advisory services, hosting arrangements, and mining-specific legal and tax expertise.

Pro-Bitcoin Governor and Legislature

Texas's political leadership has been consistently and publicly supportive of Bitcoin. Governor Greg Abbott has made statements endorsing Bitcoin and Texas's role as a Bitcoin hub. Lieutenant Governor Dan Patrick has supported Bitcoin-related legislation. The Texas Republican Party platform has included provisions supporting Bitcoin. While political winds can shift, the legislative track record since 2019 suggests that Texas's pro-Bitcoin stance reflects genuine institutional commitment rather than opportunistic posturing.

Section 2: Texas Community Property Rules for Bitcoin Holders

Texas is a community property state. This has profound implications for Bitcoin holders that are often misunderstood or overlooked — including a significant federal tax advantage that is not available in common law property states.

Bitcoin Acquired During Marriage Is Community Property

Under the Texas Family Code, all property acquired during a marriage is presumed to be community property — owned equally by both spouses — unless it can be traced to a separate property source. This applies to Bitcoin. If a Texas resident purchases Bitcoin during their marriage using marital funds (wages, investment income, or other community property), that Bitcoin is community property regardless of whose name is on the wallet, account, or exchange.

This matters for estate planning in several ways. Community property is not automatically available for transfer to a trust or other entity by one spouse alone — both spouses must consent to the transfer of community property. If you are attempting to fund a dynasty trust with Bitcoin that is community property, you will need both spouses to execute the trust agreement and the asset transfer documents.

Separate Property Tracing for Pre-Marriage Bitcoin

Bitcoin acquired before marriage is separate property and remains the separate property of the acquiring spouse during and after the marriage — provided it is properly documented and segregated. The challenge for Bitcoin holders is proving the separate property character when Bitcoin may have been held in the same wallet before and after marriage, or when additional Bitcoin was purchased from both separate and community funds.

Proper tracing requires meticulous documentation: on-chain transaction records showing when Bitcoin was purchased, records of the funding source for each purchase, and documentation that no commingling occurred with community property funds. Bitcoin's on-chain transparency actually makes tracing easier than for cash accounts — every UTXO has a verifiable origin — but it requires proactive record-keeping from the outset.

Community Property Step-Up: A Powerful Federal Tax Advantage

The most significant federal tax advantage of Texas community property law for Bitcoin holders is the full step-up in basis at death. Under IRC Section 1014(b)(6), all community property — including the deceased spouse's half and the surviving spouse's half — receives a stepped-up basis to fair market value at the first spouse's death. In a common law state, only the deceased spouse's half gets a step-up; the surviving spouse's half retains its original cost basis.

Illustration: Community Property Step-Up

Scenario: A Texas married couple holds 10 BTC as community property. They acquired it during marriage at an average cost basis of $20,000 per BTC (total basis: $200,000). At the time of the first spouse's death, the BTC is worth $500,000 per BTC (total value: $5,000,000).

Texas (community property): All 10 BTC receive a step-up to $500,000 per BTC ($5M total basis). The surviving spouse can sell all 10 BTC with zero capital gains tax on the $4.8M of appreciation that accrued during the marriage.

New York (common law): Only 5 BTC (the deceased spouse's half) receive a step-up. The surviving spouse's 5 BTC retain the original $100,000 basis. Capital gains tax applies on approximaterially $2.4M of gain.

The federal tax difference — solely from community property versus common law property treatment — can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars for a meaningful Bitcoin estate.

Prenuptial Agreements for Bitcoin in Texas

Texas enforces prenuptial agreements (called premarital agreements under the Texas Family Code) that address the characterization of property acquired during marriage. A properly drafted Texas premarital agreement can provide that Bitcoin purchased during the marriage will be treated as separate property of the purchasing spouse, or can establish specific protocols for how Bitcoin is to be titled, held, and transferred during the marriage. For couples entering a marriage where one or both partners hold significant Bitcoin, a prenuptial agreement addressing Bitcoin is increasingly standard practice in sophisticated estate planning.

How to Segregate Bitcoin Assets in Marriage

The most reliable way to maintain separate property status for Bitcoin in a Texas marriage is to hold separate property Bitcoin in separately maintained wallets funded exclusively from separate property sources. Never combine pre-marriage Bitcoin in the same wallet as Bitcoin purchased with community funds. Maintain a separate ledger or documentation file linking each Bitcoin purchase to its funding source. Consider holding separate property Bitcoin in a properly structured single-member LLC or trust to establish a clear legal boundary between it and community property assets.

Section 3: Texas Trust Law for Bitcoin

Texas has a well-developed trust code that provides solid — though not class-leading — tools for Bitcoin wealth preservation. Understanding both the strengths and limitations of Texas trust law is essential for choosing the right structure.

Texas Trust Code

Texas's trust law is codified in the Texas Trust Code (Chappropriater 112, Texas Property Code). It establishes rules for trust creation, trustee duties, beneficiary rights, modification, and termination. Texas has substantially modernized its trust code in recent years, adopting provisions of the Uniform Trust Code with Texas-specific modifications. The result is a trust law that is flexible and modern, though not as aggressively innovated as Wyoming's or South Dakota's statutes.

Texas Directed Trust Statute

Texas adopted a directed trust statute that allows trust documents to grant investment or distribution authority to an advisor separate from the trustee. This allows a Bitcoin family to designate a technically competent Bitcoin custody advisor as the investment director for a Texas trust, while a corporate trustee handles administrative functions. The directed trustee statute provides that a trustee following the direction of an authorized advisor is not liable for following those directions unless the trustee knew them to be a breach of trust — an important liability protection that encourages institutional trustees to accept Bitcoin trust appointments.

Dynasty Trusts in Texas — 360-Year Maximum

Texas allows dynasty trusts, but with a meaningful limitation: trusts are subject to the rule against perpetuities, and Texas has set the maximum trust duration at 360 years. This is far longer than most states and functionally adequate for most families. However, Wyoming and South Dakota allow truly perpetual trusts with no maximum duration. For a family that wants the absolute maximum dynasty trust flexibility — or that has philosophical reasons for wanting a trust that could theoretically hold Bitcoin across truly unlimited generations — Texas's 360-year cap is a reason to consider Wyoming or South Dakota as the trust situs, even if the family lives in Texas.

Qualified Spendthrift Trusts in Texas

Texas law recognizes spendthrift trusts that protect beneficiaries' trust interests from their creditors. A properly drafted Texas trust with a spendthrift clause prevents beneficiaries' creditors from reaching trust assets before distribution. This protection applies even in bankruptcy proceedings, subject to fraudulent transfer limitations and exceptions for certain creditor categories (child support, alimony, tort claims in some circumstances).

Texas Investment Managers Act and Digital Assets

Texas has adopted provisions allowing trust investment advisors to be recognized under the Texas Investment Managers Act. For Bitcoin specifically, this provides a pathway for Bitcoin-focused wealth advisors to serve as directed investment advisors for Texas trusts, bringing technical Bitcoin custody expertise into a fiduciary framework without requiring the corporate trustee to hold or manage Bitcoin directly.

Section 4: Bitcoin Holding Structures in Texas

Texas LLC for Bitcoin Holdings

Texas LLCs provide solid asset protection for Bitcoin holdings. Texas's Bitcoin family office in Nevada protection applies to both single-member and multi-member LLCs, protecting Bitcoin held in the LLC from the personal creditors of its members. The charging order limits creditors to receiving economic distributions — they cannot foreclose on the LLC interest or force the LLC to sell its Bitcoin holdings. Texas LLC formation through the Secretary of State costs $300 (compared to Wyoming's $100), but for most families the formation cost difference is immaterial relative to the protection provided.

Texas Series LLC — Strongest in the US, Codified

Texas was among the first states to adopt a Series LLC statute and has the most clearly codified Series LLC law in the country. The Texas Business Organizations Code explicitly provides that each series of a Texas Series LLC is legally protected from the liabilities of other series, creating compartmentalization within a single administrative entity. For a Bitcoin family office managing multiple categories of Bitcoin — operating Bitcoin, long-term hold Bitcoin, mining-derived Bitcoin, gifting-designated Bitcoin — a Texas Series LLC can hold each category in a separate protected cell without requiring multiple separate entity formations.

Family Limited Partnership in Texas

The Texas Family Limited Partnership (FLP) remains a popular vehicle for Texas high-net-worth families, particularly for estate planning purposes. A Texas FLP holding Bitcoin allows the general partner (typically the founding generation) to maintain control over Bitcoin while limited partnership interests can be gifted to children and grandchildren at a potential valuation discount (for lack of control and lack of marketability). This gifting strategy can reduce the taxable estate significantly. However, the IRS scrutinizes FLPs closely, and they must be structured and maintained with genuine business purpose and proper formality to survive challenge.

How to Title Bitcoin Holdings in Each Structure

Regardless of which structure holds Bitcoin, proper titling requires:

  • Generating wallet addresses in the name of the entity (or using a custodian who holds Bitcoin in the entity's name)
  • Documenting the transfer of Bitcoin from personal ownership to entity ownership with a formal contribution agreement
  • Recording the transfer on the entity's books as a capital contribution at fair market value on the date of transfer
  • Filing a gift tax return if the transfer involves a gift element (e.g., transferring Bitcoin to a family trust for less than fair market value consideration)
  • Maintaining custody protocols consistent with the entity's operating agreement or trust document

Section 5: Texas Bitcoin Family Office Setup — Step by Step

Step 1: Choose Your Entity Structure

The most common structures for Texas Bitcoin family offices are:

  • Texas trust + Wyoming LLC: A Texas dynasty trust (or Wyoming dynasty trust with Texas beneficiaries) owns a Wyoming LLC that holds Bitcoin. This captures Texas trust law advantages for beneficiaries while using Wyoming's superior LLC asset protection and digital asset legislation for the holding entity.
  • Texas Series LLC: A single Texas Series LLC holds multiple categories of Bitcoin in separate series. Simpler to administer, but lacks the dynasty trust's generation-skipping and beneficiary protection features.
  • Texas LLC + Texas FLP: An LLC holds operating Bitcoin assets; an FLP holds long-term Bitcoin for estate planning purposes. Allows both operational flexibility and FLP valuation discounts for gifting.

Step 2: Entity Formation with the Texas Secretary of State

Texas LLCs are formed by filing a Certificate of Formation with the Texas Secretary of State. The filing fee is $300. Texas Series LLCs require a designation in the Certificate of Formation that establishes the series structure. Texas does not require a registered agent per se, but domestic entities must maintain a registered office in Texas with an agent for service of process.

Step 3: Operating Agreement with Digital Asset Provisions

Like a Wyoming LLC, a Texas Bitcoin holding entity's operating agreement should address Bitcoin-specific provisions: custody protocol requirements, multisignature standards, authorized signatories, transfer restrictions, succession provisions, and valuation methodology. Do not use a generic operating agreement template — engage a Texas attorney with Bitcoin or digital asset experience to draft or review the digital asset provisions.

Step 4: Multi-Sig Custody Setup

For any family holding meaningful Bitcoin, a multisignature custody arrangement is the appropriate standard. A 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multisig configuration means no single key loss or theft can result in loss of Bitcoin. Keys should be geographically distributed, with at least one key held by a third-party custodian or trusted institution. Document the multisig configuration in the entity's records — including hardware wallet details, seed phrase storage locations, and the procedures for authorizing transactions — so that successors can manage the Bitcoin if the founding member becomes incapacitated or dies.

Step 5: Texas-Based Custodians and Advisors

Texas has developed a meaningful ecosystem of Bitcoin-focused service providers, including attorneys, tax advisors, custodians, and financial advisors with Bitcoin specialization. Austin and Dallas have the densest concentrations of Bitcoin-focused professional services. When selecting custodians and advisors, prioritize those with specific experience in digital asset trust administration and Texas community property law — general estate planning attorneys without Bitcoin experience are likely to miss critical issues.

Section 6: Texas-Specific Tax Planning

Tax planning for Texas Bitcoin families operates entirely at the federal level for income and estate tax purposes. But the specific strategies available — and their relative value — are shaped by Texas's community property rules and Bitcoin's characteristics as an asset.

Federal Estate Tax Exposure

Texas Bitcoin families with meaningful holdings — commonly $3–10 million or more in Bitcoin — face potential federal estate tax exposure if their total estate (Bitcoin plus other assets) exceeds the applicable exclusion amount. The federal estate tax rate on amounts above the exclusion is 40%. For a $10 million Bitcoin estate above the exemption, the federal estate tax liability is approximaterially $4 million. Proper estate planning — including dynasty trusts, GRATs, charitable strategies, and annual gifting — can dramatically reduce or eliminate this exposure, but requires proactive action before death makes these techniques unavailable.

Community Property Tax Advantages for Estate Planning

Beyond the step-up in basis advantage described above, Texas community property law creates additional estate planning flexibility. Community property held in trust at the first spouse's death can be structured to preserve the estate tax marital deduction while also providing creditor protection and dynasty trust benefits for future generations. Texas attorneys specializing in estate planning for married couples often structure Bitcoin holdings to optimize the interplay between the community property rules, the estate tax marital deduction, and the dynasty trust generation-skipping provisions.

Step-Up in Basis Strategies for Texas Bitcoin Families

In addition to the community property step-up at death, Texas Bitcoin families can pursue several strategies to optimize their basis position:

  • Charitable remainder trusts (CRTs): Donating appreciated Bitcoin to a CRT provides an immediate charitable deduction, eliminates capital gains on the donated Bitcoin, and provides the donor with an income stream. The CRT remainder passes to charity at the end of the trust term.
  • Installment sales to grantor trusts: Selling Bitcoin to an intentionally defective grantor trust (IDGT) in an installment sale "freezes" the Bitcoin's value for estate tax purposes at the sale price while allowing future appreciation to accumulate outside the estate.
  • Annual gifting: The annual gift tax exclusion (currently $18,000 per person per year) allows Bitcoin to be gifted to children and grandchildren each year without using the lifetime exemption. Direct gifts of Bitcoin use the donor's adjusted basis; gifts through annual exclusion trusts can be structured more efficiently.

Bitcoin Gifting in Texas

Gifts of Bitcoin in Texas must navigate both federal gift tax rules and Texas community property law. A gift of community property Bitcoin by one spouse requires the consent of both spouses. Each spouse can make separate annual exclusion gifts of their half of community property Bitcoin, effectively doubling the annual exclusion available to the couple without the donor spouse's half being treated as a taxable gift. For larger gifts using the lifetime exemption, both spouses must sign gift documentation and any required gift tax returns.

Section 7: Texas vs Wyoming for Family Office Jurisdiction

The honest answer is that Texas and Wyoming serve different roles, and the most sophisticated Texas Bitcoin families use both. Wyoming provides the best holding entity and trust structure; Texas provides the operational domicile and community property advantages. Here is a systematic comparison:

When to Choose Texas vs Wyoming vs Hybrid

Choose Texas domicile when: You live in Texas (or are moving there), you are married and want to benefit from community property step-up rules, you operate Bitcoin mining or business operations in Texas, or you need access to Texas's established professional services ecosystem.

Choose Wyoming as trust situs when: You want a truly perpetual dynasty trust, you want the strongest available directed trust protections, or you want Bitcoin held in a legal framework purpose-built for digital assets.

Use a hybrid structure when: You live in Texas, want to take advantage of Texas community property rules during your lifetime, and want to hold long-term Bitcoin in a Wyoming dynasty trust. This is the most common structure for Texas Bitcoin families with $5M+ in holdings.

Conclusion: Texas Is a Serious Bitcoin Family Office Jurisdiction

Texas is not Wyoming. Its trust law is less innovative, its digital asset legislation less comprehensive, and its LLC protections — while solid — not quite as strong as Wyoming's class-leading statutes. But Texas offers something Wyoming cannot: the community property double step-up in basis, a world-class Bitcoin mining and business ecosystem, and a large professional services community that is increasingly Bitcoin-fluent.

The strongest approach for a Texas Bitcoin family is not to choose between Texas and Wyoming, but to use both strategically: live in Texas, benefit from community property rules and the no-income-tax environment, participate in Texas's Bitcoin ecosystem — and hold long-term Bitcoin in a Wyoming dynasty trust through a Wyoming LLC, capturing Wyoming's superior structural protections for the asset itself.

This hybrid approach is not just theoretical. It is increasingly the default recommendation of sophisticated estate planning attorneys serving Bitcoin-focused high-net-worth families in Texas. Start with the correct structure now, before forced liquidations, adverse legal events, or estate circumstances make the options more limited and more expensive.
