Bitcoin Buy, Borrow, Die: The Ultimate Tax Strategy for Long-Term Holders

How ultra-wealthy Bitcoin families access millions in liquidity without triggering a single dollar of capital gains tax ‚Äî and how they pass their entire position to heirs completely tax-free.

Hal Turner ‚Ä¢ The Bitcoin Family Office ‚Ä¢ February 25, 2026 ‚Ä¢ 12 min read

The wealthiest families in America rarely sell their appreciating assets. They never have. The Rockefellers didn't sell Standard Oil shares to fund their lifestyle. The Waltons don't sell Walmart stock every time they need cash. They borrow against their holdings, live on the loan proceeds, and when they eventually die, their heirs inherit a stepped-up cost basis that erases a lifetime of untaxed gains.

This strategy ‚Äî called Buy, Borrow, Die ‚Äî is now fully available to Bitcoin holders. And for long-term holders sitting on life-changing gains, it may be the single most powerful tax strategy available to you today.

What Is Buy, Borrow, Die (BBD)?

Buy, Borrow, Die is a three-step wealth preservation strategy employed by ultra-high-net-worth families to hold appreciating assets indefinitely ‚Äî accumulating wealth across generations ‚Äî without ever triggering a capital gains tax event.

The strategy has three phases:

  • Buy: Acquire and hold an appreciating asset ‚Äî real estate, equities, or in the modern era, Bitcoin.
  • Borrow: When you need liquidity, use the asset as collateral to secure a loan. You receive cash (or stablecoins). The loan proceeds are not income ‚Äî they are debt. No tax event is triggered.
  • Die: Upon death, your heirs inherit the asset with a stepped-up cost basis equal to its fair market value at the date of your death. The IRS effectively erases a lifetime of unrealized gains. Your heirs can sell enough of the position to repay the outstanding loan and owe zero capital gains tax.

"The Buy, Borrow, Die strategy doesn't avoid taxes through a loophole ‚Äî it operates entirely within the law as written. The step-up in basis at death is a deliberate feature of the U.S. tax code, not an oversight."

For traditional assets like real estate and public equities, this strategy has been in use for decades. Bitcoin adds new dimensions ‚Äî and new risks ‚Äî that every serious holder needs to understand before implementing it.

How Buy, Borrow, Die Works for Bitcoin

Step 1: Buy ‚Äî Accumulate and Hold

The foundation is conviction in Bitcoin's long-term value and the discipline to hold through volatility. Most successful BBD implementers have a 5‚Äì10+ year time horizon. The longer you hold, the larger the spread between your cost basis and current market value ‚Äî and therefore the larger the capital gains tax bill that BBD can help you avoid.

This step requires nothing more than acquiring Bitcoin and securing it properly ‚Äî ideally in self-custody or a qualified custodian for very large holdings.

Step 2: Borrow ‚Äî Collateralized Loans Against Your Bitcoin

When you need liquidity ‚Äî to fund a business, pay living expenses, invest in real estate, or diversify ‚Äî you pledge your Bitcoin as collateral and borrow against it. The lender holds your Bitcoin (or in some cases accepts a lien on your self-custodied holdings) and extends you a USD or stablecoin loan.

Critical tax point: Loan proceeds are not taxable income under the Internal Revenue Code. You receive cash, your Bitcoin position remains intact, and the IRS does not receive a Form 1099 reporting a realized gain. The strategy's entire tax efficiency flows from this single legal distinction between income and debt.

The interest you pay on the loan may be deductible as investment interest expense (subject to net investment income limitations under IRC ¬ß163(d)), which partially offsets the cost of borrowing.

Step 3: Die ‚Äî The Step-Up in Basis

Under IRC ¬ß1014, assets inherited by your heirs receive a new cost basis equal to the asset's fair market value on the date of your death. If you purchased 10 BTC at $10,000 each and they're worth $950,000 at your death, your heirs inherit a $950,000 basis ‚Äî not your original $100,000 basis.

Your heirs can then sell a portion of the Bitcoin at the new stepped-up basis to repay any outstanding loans, and the net capital gains on that sale are zero. The entire lifetime appreciation evaporates from the tax ledger.

The Math: A Concrete Example

üìä BBD vs. Sell ‚Äî Side-by-Side Comparison

The Position: 10 BTC purchased at $10,000 each = $100,000 cost basis. Current value: $950,000. Unrealized gain: $850,000.

Scenario A: You Sell

Federal long-term capital gains tax (23.8% including NIIT): $850,000 √ó 23.8% = $202,300 in taxes owed

Net proceeds after tax: $747,700. Your Bitcoin position is now zero.

Scenario B: You Borrow (BBD)

Borrow $475,000 at 50% LTV against your $950,000 BTC position.

Interest rate: 8% annually = $38,000/year. Interest may be deductible as investment interest.

You keep all 10 BTC. You receive $475,000 in cash. Capital gains tax owed: $0.

At death: heirs inherit 10 BTC at a stepped-up basis of $950,000. They sell $475,000 worth to repay the loan. Gain on the sale: $0.

Tax savings vs. selling: $202,300. Plus your Bitcoin continues to appreciate.

The break-even analysis depends on how long you live after taking the loan, how much BTC appreciates, and your interest cost. For most long-term Bitcoin holders, the math strongly favors borrowing over selling ‚Äî particularly as the position size grows.

The Risks You Must Understand

BBD is not a free lunch. The risks are real and must be actively managed. Executing this strategy poorly can be worse than simply paying the capital gains tax.

‚ö† Margin Call Risk

If Bitcoin's price drops 50%, your collateral value may fall below the lender's minimum collateral ratio. The lender can liquidate your Bitcoin to repay the loan ‚Äî forcing the very taxable sale you were trying to avoid, at the worst possible price. Always maintain conservative LTV ratios (30‚Äì40%) and have a plan to add collateral or repay partially if needed.

‚ö† Interest Cost Drag

At 8‚Äì10% annual interest, borrowing is not free. On a $475,000 loan, you're paying $38,000‚Äì$47,500 per year. Over 10 years, that's $380,000‚Äì$475,000 in interest. This needs to be weighed against the tax savings and Bitcoin's expected appreciation. The strategy works best when you believe Bitcoin will outpace your interest cost.

‚ö† Counterparty Risk

When you pledge Bitcoin to a lender, they hold your collateral. If the lender goes bankrupt (see: BlockFi, November 2022), your Bitcoin becomes an unsecured claim in a bankruptcy proceeding. Use only well-capitalized, regulated lenders ‚Äî or explore multi-sig structures (Unchained) that maintain your keys.

‚ö† Estate Complications

Outstanding loans at death create complexity. Your heirs need to know the loans exist, have access to loan statements, and understand the order of operations: inherit ‚Üí establish stepped-up basis ‚Üí sell enough to repay loans ‚Üí keep the rest. Without proper estate documentation, heirs may inadvertently sell first and repay after ‚Äî still achieving zero gains, but with unnecessary complexity and potential executor liability.

Who Is BBD Right For?

The Buy, Borrow, Die strategy is not for every Bitcoin holder. It makes the most sense for:

  • Long-term HODLers with a 3+ year minimum time horizon. The strategy requires surviving periods of volatility without a forced sale. If there's any chance you'll need to liquidate your position in the next 1‚Äì2 years, BBD adds unnecessary risk.
  • Holders with meaningful unrealized gains. The tax savings need to be large enough to justify the interest cost. As a rough guideline, holdings under $250,000 in value rarely make sense for BBD; the economics become compelling above $500,000.
  • Individuals comfortable with leverage and margin management. You need to actively monitor LTV ratios and have a plan ‚Äî cash reserves, additional collateral ‚Äî to respond to Bitcoin price drops without being forced into a liquidation.
  • Those who need liquidity without wanting to lose exposure. If you're bullish on Bitcoin and need cash for a specific purpose ‚Äî a real estate investment, business funding, or wealth diversification ‚Äî BBD lets you access liquidity while maintaining full upside participation.

The strategy is not appropriate for anyone who lacks the volatility tolerance to hold through 40‚Äì60% drawdowns while carrying loan obligations against their position.

Estate Planning Integration: Getting the Documentation Right

The stepped-up basis at death is automatic under ¬ß1014 ‚Äî but the loan repayment logistics are not. Proper estate planning documentation is essential to ensure your heirs can actually execute the strategy as intended.

Every estate file should include:

  • A Letter of Instruction (LOI) that explicitly lists all outstanding Bitcoin-backed loans, including lender name, loan balance, interest rate, and contact information.
  • Current loan statements kept in a secure but accessible location (encrypted drive, estate attorney files, or a secure digital vault accessible to your executor).
  • Clear written instructions for the executor and heirs explaining that all Bitcoin positions should be valued first (establishing the stepped-up basis), then liquidated only to the extent necessary to repay outstanding loans.
  • Trustee or executor coordination ‚Äî ideally, your estate attorney should have a conversation with the lender in advance to understand the lender's process for handling borrower death. Some lenders have estate-specific teams; others do not.

If your Bitcoin is held in a trust structure, the trustee needs to be explicitly empowered to manage crypto collateral positions and understands the mechanics of the stepped-up basis interaction with trust accounting.

Alternative: Pledged Asset Lines (PAL)

For larger Bitcoin holders ‚Äî particularly those with $2M+ in assets ‚Äî Pledged Asset Lines (PALs) from regulated financial institutions may offer more stability than pure crypto lenders.

PALs are revolving credit facilities secured by investment portfolios. Some private banks and wealth management institutions are beginning to accept Bitcoin and digital asset custody accounts as collateral, though this remains far from universal.

Options worth exploring at institutional scale include:

  • Anchorage Digital (OCC-chartered bank) ‚Äî The most established regulated institution offering Bitcoin-collateralized credit to qualified institutions and family offices.
  • Prime Trust ‚Äî Digital asset infrastructure provider with custody and credit capabilities.
  • Family office and private bank desks ‚Äî An increasing number of wealth management institutions are beginning to accept crypto assets held at qualified custodians as supplementary collateral for traditional credit lines.

PALs typically carry lower interest rates than pure crypto lenders (often 5‚Äì8%) and operate within a more regulated, bankruptcy-remote framework. The tradeoff is higher minimum thresholds and more rigorous underwriting.

The Bottom Line

Buy, Borrow, Die is the wealthiest families' most powerful tool for intergenerational wealth transfer ‚Äî and Bitcoin's volatility and growth trajectory make it uniquely well-suited to the strategy. The math is compelling: avoid hundreds of thousands of dollars in capital gains tax, maintain full exposure to Bitcoin's upside, access liquidity for life's opportunities, and pass a stepped-up position to your heirs completely free of the gains you accumulated over a lifetime.

But the strategy demands respect. Margin call risk is real. Counterparty risk is real. Estate documentation must be precise. The holders who benefit most from BBD are those who implement it conservatively ‚Äî low LTV ratios, quality lenders, robust estate planning, and a volatility reserve to ride out drawdowns without being forced to sell.

Executed correctly, BBD transforms Bitcoin from a volatile asset into a multigenerational wealth machine. That's worth understanding.
