Bitcoin Family Office in New York: Navigating America's Most Complex Tax Jurisdiction

Estate Planning New York Updated February 26, 2026 ~18 min read

New York is the only state in America where a Bitcoin family can face income tax at three simultaneous levels — federal, state, and city — while also navigating a state estate tax with a brutal cliff provision that no other state replicates. If you hold significant Bitcoin in New York, this is the jurisdiction that demands the most sophisticated planning of any state in the country.

The stakes are not abstract. A Manhattan-based Bitcoin founder with a $20 million estate who does no planning could face effective wealth transfer rates exceeding 60% — a combination of the 40% federal estate tax and New York's additional 16% state estate tax. Add the capital gains taxes already paid on the way up, and the government's total claim on your Bitcoin fortune can approach or exceed two-thirds of everything you built.

This guide is written for Bitcoin families who are domiciled in New York, considering a move from New York, or structured as a family office managing multigenerational Bitcoin wealth. We cover every layer of New York's tax stack, the specific strategies that work for Bitcoin, and the planning tools that can dramatically change your family's financial trajectory.

Federal + State + City: The Triple Stack

For a Bitcoin family living in New York City, the income tax exposure on a Bitcoin sale is:

  • Federal ordinary income tax: 37% (for income above ~$609K)
  • Federal net investment income tax (NIIT): 3.8%
  • New York State income tax: 10.9% (top rate, applies above $25M for single filers)
  • New York City income tax: 3.876% (for NYC residents)

Combined on short-term capital gains: ~55.576%. That means a New York City Bitcoin holder who mines, receives Bitcoin as compensation, or holds Bitcoin for less than one year and then sells faces giving more than half of every dollar to various governments before they see a penny. Even long-term capital gains — which receive federal preferential rates of 23.8% — still face the full NYS and NYC rates, producing combined rates of approximately 37.7% for NYC residents.

New York State Estate Tax: The Cliff Explained

New York imposes its own estate tax completely separate from the federal estate tax. In 2026, the NY estate tax exemption is $6.94 million. Estates below this threshold owe no NY estate tax. Estates above this threshold face rates ranging from 3.06% to 16% on the amount above the exemption.

But here is where New York becomes uniquely dangerous: the cliff.

Unlike the federal system — which taxes only the amount above the exemption — New York's system eliminates the exemption entirely once your estate exceeds the exemption by more than 5%. This means:

  • Estate of $6.94M or less: zero NY estate tax
  • Estate of $7.00M: NY estate tax applies only to $60K above exemption
  • Estate of $7.287M (exactly 5.01% above the $6.94M exemption): NY estate tax applies to the ENTIRE $7.287M estate — not just the excess
  • Estate of $10M: NY estate tax applies to the full $10M at graduated rates

The 5% threshold in 2026 equals approximately $347,000 above the $6.94M exemption. This is the zone where New York's cliff bites hardest — if your estate lands just above it, the effective marginal rate on that last dollar of estate value can exceed 100%.

New York Gift Tax: The Missing Lever

Despite all this complexity, New York has one glaring gap that creates enormous planning opportunity: New York has no state gift tax. The federal gift tax annual exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2026, $36,000 for married couples) can be used to transfer Bitcoin annually with no New York gift tax consequence whatsoever. This is the single most underutilized tool in New York Bitcoin planning.

Section 2: The NY Estate Tax Cliff — Critical for Bitcoin Families

The cliff is New York's most dangerous feature for Bitcoin families, and it creates an urgency that most wealth planning timelines don't account for: Bitcoin's price appreciation can silently push your estate from "safely below the cliff" to "fallen off the cliff" in a matter of weeks during a bull run.

A Real-World Example: The $7.3 Million Estate

Consider a Bitcoin family with a taxable estate of approximately $6.5 million — safely below the NY exemption. They hold 10 Bitcoin, which at the time of planning are worth roughly $650,000 and are part of that $6.5M estate. No NY estate tax exposure.

Then Bitcoin appreciates 50%. Their 10 BTC is now worth $975,000. Their total estate is now $6.825M — still below the $6.94M cliff, still no NY estate tax. They feel fine.

Another appreciation cycle. Bitcoin doubles again. Their 10 BTC is now worth $1.3M. Their total estate is $7.3M — which is 5.27% above the $6.94M exemption threshold. They have fallen off the cliff.

At $7.3M, New York now imposes estate tax on the entire $7.3M, not just the $360K above the exemption. At New York's graduated rates, a $7.3M estate generates approximately $622,000 in state estate tax. If they had stayed at $6.94M (just below the cliff), the NY estate tax would be zero. The $360K of Bitcoin appreciation above the cliff threshold cost them $622,000 in estate taxes — an effective marginal rate of over 172% on those last dollars.

Bitcoin Volatility Makes the Cliff Unpredictable

Unlike real estate or private equity, Bitcoin can move 20-50% in a single quarter. A family whose estate is safely below the NY cliff today can be well above it before year end — without making a single decision or completing a single transaction.

Planning Strategies: Get Under or Get Well Above

Financial planners and estate attorneys who work with New York Bitcoin families typically recommend one of two approaches:

Strategy A: Get definitively below the cliff. Gift or transfer enough assets out of the estate that even with significant Bitcoin appreciation, the estate remains below $6.94M. This typically involves aggressive annual gifting, SLAT structures, or irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) funding. This approach works well for families whose estate is currently near the threshold.

Strategy B: Get definitively above the cliff. For larger estates, trying to stay below $6.94M is futile — Bitcoin appreciation will push them above it regardless. In this case, the better strategy is to ensure the estate is far enough above the exemption that paying NY estate tax is baked into the plan, and the focus shifts to using every available trust structure to reduce the taxable estate over time.

The danger zone is estates between $6.94M and approximately $9M — large enough to have fallen off the cliff but not large enough that the cliff is an accepted cost of doing business. These families need the most urgent attention.

Annual Gifts: The Cliff Avoidance Tool

Because New York has no state gift tax, annual exclusion gifting is the most accessible and powerful tool for cliff management. In 2026:

  • Each Bitcoin family member can give $18,000 per recipient annually with no gift tax
  • Married couples can give $36,000 per recipient through "gift splitting"
  • A family with three children can gift $108,000/year (married) with no tax implications
  • Bitcoin given away is removed from the taxable estate at the date-of-gift value, not at death value
  • The recipient inherits the donor's cost basis (potentially triggering capital gains later) but removes the asset from estate taxation

Over a decade of consistent gifting, a family can transfer $1M+ of Bitcoin wealth free of both NY gift tax and estate inclusion. The key is consistency — annual exclusion gifting must happen every year, and it works best when started early in the planning process.

Section 3: NYC Domicile and the 183-Day Rule

New York City imposes an income tax up to 3.876% on its residents — in addition to the state income tax of up to 10.9%. For a high-income Bitcoin family, eliminating NYC residency for tax purposes can save hundreds of thousands of dollars per year in ongoing income taxes.

But New York is not passive about high-income departures. The state has one of the most aggressive audit programs in the country for taxpayers who claim to have changed their domicile, and the New York State Department of Taxation and Finance has a dedicated team that scrutinizes these claims.

Domicile vs. Statutory Residence: Two Different Tests

New York uses two separate but related tests for residence:

Domicile test: Your domicile is your permanent home — the place you intend to return to after temporary absences. You can have only one domicile. If your domicile is New York, you are a New York (and NYC) resident regardless of how many days you spend elsewhere.

Statutory residence test: Even if your domicile is not New York, you are still treated as a New York resident if you maintain a "permanent place of abode" in New York AND spend more than 183 days in New York during the tax year.

For Bitcoin founders who keep a pied-à-terre in Manhattan after officially moving to Florida or Texas, the statutory residence test is the trap. Maintaining any apartment in New York City — even one you use only occasionally — counts as a permanent place of abode. If you spend 184 days in New York in a given year, you owe NY taxes as a full resident for that year, regardless of your official domicile.

Successful NYC Tax Exit: What It Requires

Successfully exiting New York City taxation requires meeting a high evidentiary standard:

  1. Clear intent to change domicile: Obtain a driver's license in the new state, register to vote there, update your will and estate planning documents to reference the new state, establish primary banking relationships there
  2. Spend fewer than 183 days in New York: Document every day with hotel receipts, credit card statements, phone records, and flight manifests
  3. Terminate (or restructure) New York permanent place of abode: This is the hardest part for NYC families — you may need to sell your apartment, convert it to a rental, or significantly limit your usage rights
  4. Demonstrate social and professional ties to new state: Join clubs, establish business relationships, move medical providers, show evidence of social life in the new state
  5. Maintain contemporaneous records: New York auditors can request detailed day-by-day calendars and corroborating evidence for 3-6 years after the claimed change of domicile
New York Audit Reality

New York's "nonresident audit" program specifically targets high-income taxpayers who claim to have moved. Anyone with $1M+ in income who files as a part-year or non-resident is at elevated audit risk. Work with a tax attorney who has specific New York domicile audit experience before executing a departure strategy.

Popular New Domicile Destinations for Bitcoin Families

Bitcoin families exiting New York most commonly establish new domicile in:

  • Florida: No state income tax, no state estate tax, strong homestead exemption, warm weather, established Bitcoin community in Miami
  • Texas: No state income tax, no state estate tax, Austin and Houston Bitcoin infrastructure
  • Puerto Rico: Act 60 offers 0% federal capital gains tax on BTC appreciation after becoming a resident — the most aggressive tax treatment available under U.S. law
  • Wyoming: No income tax, Wyoming-specific trust laws (perpetual dynasty trusts), DAO-friendly regulatory environment, strong crypto infrastructure
  • Nevada: No state income tax, favorable trust laws, proximity to western time zone operations

Section 4: ING Trust (Incomplete Non-Grantor Trust) — New York's Power Move

For New York Bitcoin families who cannot or choose not to leave New York, the Incomplete Non-Grantor (ING) trust is the most powerful state income tax reduction tool available. Used correctly, it can eliminate New York's 10.9% state income tax on trust income without triggering a federal gift tax.

How the ING Trust Works

An ING trust is established in a no-income-tax state — typically Nevada (Nevada Asset Protection Trust, or NAPT) or Delaware. The trust is structured with two key characteristics:

  1. Incomplete gift: The grantor (the New York Bitcoin family member) retains a power to substitute trust assets with assets of equivalent value. This makes the transfer an "incomplete gift" for federal gift tax purposes — no gift tax is triggered, and no lifetime exemption is used.
  2. Non-grantor status: Despite the retained substitution power, the trust is structured so that the grantor is NOT treated as the owner of the trust for income tax purposes. The trust — not the grantor — pays the income taxes, and because the trust is established in Nevada or Delaware (no income tax), the trust income is not subject to New York state income tax.

The IRS blessed this structure in Chief Counsel Advice 200923024, and while New York has challenged these structures (see Matter of Knappe, 2016), properly drafted Nevada ING trusts continue to be used by sophisticated NY taxpayers with the guidance of experienced counsel.

Bitcoin in an ING Trust: Custody Mechanics

For Bitcoin specifically, the ING trust structure requires careful attention to custody:

  • The trust must have an independent trustee located in Nevada or Delaware (not a New York trustee)
  • Bitcoin held in the trust should be custodied through an entity or custodian with no New York connections
  • Keys and access credentials must be controlled by the Nevada/Delaware trustee, not by the grantor
  • Mining income flowing into the trust must be structured so the trust (not the grantor) is the recipient
  • Sales of Bitcoin within the trust are taxed at the trust's state tax rate — zero, in Nevada

Estimated Savings: $200,000–$500,000 Per Year

Consider a New York Bitcoin family with $10 million of Bitcoin that appreciates 20% in a given year — generating $2 million of unrealized gain. If they sell $2M of Bitcoin outside the ING trust while living in New York City, they owe approximately $218,000 in NY state income taxes and $77,500 in NYC income taxes on that sale — nearly $300,000 in state and city taxes alone.

The same sale within a properly structured Nevada ING trust: $0 in New York state income taxes. Over time and at larger scales, the savings compound dramatically. For a family with $10M in BTC generating significant annual liquidity events, the ING trust can save $200,000 to $500,000 per year — making the setup and ongoing costs (legal fees, Nevada trustee fees) trivial in comparison.

ING Trust Caution

ING trusts are sophisticated structures that require experienced counsel. New York has challenged poorly structured ING arrangements, and the IRS regularly scrutinizes them. The structure must be implemented correctly from inception — errors are difficult or impossible to correct after the fact. This is not a DIY planning tool.

Section 5: Trust Structures for NY Bitcoin Families

New York has a comprehensive body of trust law under the Estates, Powers and Trusts Law (EPTL) — one of the oldest and most extensively interpreted estate law frameworks in the country. This creates both tools and constraints for Bitcoin family office planning.

New York's Rule Against Perpetuities: The Dynasty Trust Problem

One of New York's most significant limitations for multigenerational Bitcoin planning is its Rule Against Perpetuities. New York's version of the rule (EPTL § 9-1.1) generally limits trust duration to 21 years after the death of a life in being at the time of trust creation — effectively capping most New York trusts at somewhere between 70 and 110 years.

For Bitcoin families with genuinely multigenerational ambitions, this is a serious constraint. A dynasty trust — a trust designed to hold and compound Bitcoin wealth across many generations, potentially in perpetuity — cannot be established under New York law. This drives many NY Bitcoin families to establish their dynasty trusts in other states:

  • South Dakota: Abolished the rule against perpetuities entirely. Perpetual trusts with no termination date. Strong directed trust statutes, top-tier privacy protections.
  • Wyoming: Allows perpetual trusts (1000-year limit), Wyoming-specific asset protection, strong Bitcoin/DAO-friendly legal infrastructure
  • Nevada: 365-year maximum trust duration, strong asset protection, no state income tax, directed trust statutes
  • Delaware: Up to 110 years (with modification exceptions), mature trust infrastructure, favorable directed trust laws

Importantly, a New York resident can be the settlor of an out-of-state dynasty trust without changing their domicile. The trust is governed by the law of the state where it is established, and as long as the trust has genuine connections to that state (qualified trustee located there, administered there), New York typically must respect the foreign state's trust law.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) in New York

A Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) allows one spouse to make an irrevocable gift of assets — including Bitcoin — to a trust for the benefit of the other spouse during their lifetime, plus children or other beneficiaries. The gift is complete for gift tax purposes (using the donor spouse's federal lifetime exemption), removing the assets from the donor's taxable estate.

In New York, SLATs are effective for:

  • Moving Bitcoin out of the NY taxable estate using the federal lifetime exemption ($13.99M per person in 2026)
  • Retaining indirect family access through the beneficiary spouse
  • Potential NY estate tax savings if the SLAT is established in a state with no state estate tax
  • Locking in today's Bitcoin basis for estate planning purposes (though this creates carryover basis issues for heirs)

The primary risk with SLATs is divorce — if the marriage dissolves, the donor spouse loses indirect access to the trust assets, which now benefit only the beneficiary spouse and descendants. Careful drafting with experienced NY family law and estate planning counsel is essential.

Out-of-State Trusts for NY Residents

The good news: New York generally respects properly structured out-of-state trusts established by its residents. A New York domiciliary can establish a South Dakota dynasty trust, fund it with Bitcoin, and enjoy the SD trust law benefits — so long as:

  • The trust has at least one qualified trustee in South Dakota (not the NY settlor)
  • The trust is administered in South Dakota (meetings, records, distributions)
  • The trust is not subject to NY jurisdiction through other connections
  • The initial funding is structured as a completed gift (for estate exclusion) or has the proper incomplete gift structure (for ING purposes)

Section 6: New York BitLicense — What Family Offices Need to Know

New York's Department of Financial Services (NYDFS) created the BitLicense framework in 2015 — the first comprehensive state-level virtual currency business regulation in the United States. For Bitcoin families operating a family office structure in New York, understanding what the BitLicense does and does not require is essential.

What Requires a BitLicense

The BitLicense is required for any entity engaged in "virtual currency business activity" in New York, which includes:

  • Receiving, transmitting, or exchanging virtual currency for customers
  • Holding virtual currency in custody for third parties
  • Converting virtual currency to or from fiat currency
  • Buying or selling virtual currency as a business
  • Providing virtual currency exchange services

The Family Office Exemption

A family office that manages only its own Bitcoin wealth does not require a BitLicense. The NYDFS BitLicense regulations include an exemption for persons who manage virtual currency solely on behalf of a "family" — defined as a group of persons organized as a family office to manage their own wealth.

Specifically, Regulation 23 NYCRR Part 200 exempts:

  • Merchants and consumers who use virtual currency for personal or business purchases of goods and services
  • Persons who use virtual currency for personal use and not for commercial purposes
  • Activities incidental to or furnished in connection with the provision of other financial services

A properly structured Bitcoin family office — one that manages the family's own Bitcoin, makes investment decisions for family members, and does not provide services to unrelated parties — falls outside the BitLicense requirement.

Where the Line Gets Complicated

The family office exemption has limits. If a New York Bitcoin family office begins:

  • Managing Bitcoin for friends, employees, or unrelated investors
  • Operating a Bitcoin lending desk for third parties
  • Providing custody services for clients beyond the family
  • Running a trading operation that offers services to outside parties

...it has crossed from family office activities into virtual currency business activities, and a BitLicense (or a New York limited purpose trust charter) may be required. The NYDFS has been active in enforcement, and penalties for operating without a license can be severe. Any family office that is expanding its scope of activities should obtain a NYDFS compliance opinion before proceeding.
