Bitcoin Cross-Border Estate Planning: The Complete Framework for Multi-Jurisdiction Families

March 16, 2026 · 22 min read · International Tax & Estate Planning
Home / Blog / Bitcoin Cross-Border Estate Planning

Bitcoin was designed to be borderless. Estate law was not. That single tension defines the most complex — and most frequently mishandled — planning challenge facing international Bitcoin holders in 2026.

If you hold Bitcoin across multiple custodians in different countries, if your spouse holds a different passport than you do, if your children live on a different continent, or if you maintain residency in one jurisdiction while domiciled in another, your estate plan is almost certainly incomplete. Not because your attorney was negligent, but because the legal frameworks governing death, succession, and taxation were built for a world where assets had a physical location and families stayed in one country.

Bitcoin breaks both assumptions. A single private key can control $14.8 million in value (200 BTC at today's price of approximately $74,000) while being simultaneously "located" in no jurisdiction and every jurisdiction, depending on which country's tax authority is doing the asking. The result is a planning environment where four or five countries may each assert taxing authority over the same Bitcoin — and where the failure to plan correctly can result in aggregate effective tax rates exceeding 80%.

This guide provides the complete framework. We cover situs rules for digital assets, the US estate tax treatment of non-resident aliens, the major bilateral estate tax treaties (and their gaps), forced heirship regimes, cross-border trust recognition, executor reporting obligations, and the international custody structures that tie everything together. If you are building a comprehensive Bitcoin estate plan and your life crosses borders, this is your roadmap.

Why Bitcoin Crosses Borders but Laws Do Not

Traditional estate planning assumes that assets have a situs — a legal location that determines which country's laws govern their transfer at death. Real estate is located where the land sits. Bank accounts are located where the bank is chartered. Shares of stock are located where the corporation is incorporated or where the share certificates are physically held.

Bitcoin defeats every one of these rules. Self-custodied Bitcoin exists as a cryptographic key pair. There is no issuing corporation, no charter jurisdiction, no physical certificate. The Bitcoin network itself is distributed across nodes in over 100 countries. When a German national holding a Ledger hardware wallet in Singapore dies, where is the Bitcoin "located"?

The answer depends entirely on who is asking — and each country's answer creates a separate taxing claim:

  • Germany may treat the Bitcoin as located wherever the decedent was domiciled (Germany, based on nationality-based inheritance tax rules) or wherever the hardware wallet is physically situated
  • Singapore — which has no estate tax — would not assert a claim at all, but the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore may still require estate administration under Singaporean succession law
  • The United States, if the decedent held Bitcoin through a US-based custodian like Coinbase or Fidelity, may treat the Bitcoin as US-situs property subject to the compressed $60,000 NRA estate tax exemption
  • The country of any beneficiary's residence may impose an inheritance tax on the receipt of the Bitcoin, independent of where the decedent lived or where the asset is "located"

The core problem is that situs rules were never harmonized internationally for digital assets. Each country applies its own domestic law to determine where Bitcoin is located, and those determinations frequently overlap. Without treaty relief, the result is double, triple, or quadruple taxation of the same asset at death.

US Estate Tax on Non-Resident Aliens: The $60,000 Trap

For non-US persons holding US-situs property, the estate tax exposure is severe. While US citizens and residents receive a $15 million unified credit exemption in 2026 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), non-resident aliens (NRAs) receive an exemption of just $60,000. The top marginal rate is 40%, and it kicks in at transfers above $1 million.

The question that determines everything for international Bitcoin families: is their Bitcoin US-situs property?

Where Is Bitcoin "Located" for US Estate Tax?

The IRS has not issued definitive guidance on the situs of cryptocurrency for estate tax purposes. The analysis depends on classification:

  • If Bitcoin is "property" (the IRS's position under Notice 2014-21): Intangible personal property owned by an NRA is generally not US-situs property under IRC §2104, unless it constitutes a debt obligation of a US person. Under this analysis, self-custodied Bitcoin held by an NRA should not be subject to US estate tax regardless of where the hardware wallet sits.
  • If Bitcoin is held through a US custodian: The analysis changes. Bitcoin held in an account at Coinbase (a Delaware corporation), Fidelity Digital Assets, or any US-based exchange creates a custodial relationship. Some practitioners argue the custodial arrangement creates a US-situs debt obligation or that the custodied coins take the situs of the custodian — making them US-situs property subject to the NRA estate tax.
  • If Bitcoin is held through a US ETF: Shares of IBIT, FBTC, or other US-listed Bitcoin ETFs are shares of a US domestic corporation. They are unambiguously US-situs property. An NRA holding $10 million in IBIT at death faces a US estate tax bill of approximately $3.98 million (40% of $10M less the $60,000 exemption).

Critical Distinction

The difference between self-custody and US exchange custody can mean the difference between zero US estate tax and a 40% tax on the entire position. For NRA Bitcoin holders, custody structure is not a security question — it is a $4 million tax question.

Treaty Override Positions

Several bilateral estate tax treaties modify the NRA rules. The US currently has estate tax treaties with 16 countries. For the major Bitcoin-holding jurisdictions:

Treaty Partner NRA Exemption Treatment Bitcoin Situs Rule Key Benefit
United Kingdom Pro-rata share of full US exemption ($15M) Domicile of decedent for intangibles UK-domiciled NRA may pay zero US estate tax on Bitcoin
Canada Pro-rata share of full US exemption Residency-based; intangibles follow domicile Canadian residents get proportional US credit
Germany Pro-rata share of full US exemption Intangible property sited at domicile Avoids double taxation via mutual credit mechanism
Australia Pro-rata share of full US exemption Domicile-based for personal property Australia has no estate tax — treaty primarily benefits US-side
Singapore No treaty Default NRA rules apply None — must rely on domestic law structuring

The US-UK treaty is the most generous. Under Article 8, a UK-domiciled individual is entitled to a pro-rata share of the full US unified credit based on the proportion of their worldwide estate that constitutes US-situs property. For a UK-domiciled individual whose only US-situs exposure is Bitcoin held through a US custodian (say, 20% of their worldwide estate), the effective US exemption would be approximately $3 million — far more generous than the $60,000 statutory amount.

The absence of a US-Singapore treaty is particularly significant. Singapore has become a major hub for Bitcoin wealth, yet Singaporean residents holding Bitcoin through US custodians receive no treaty relief. They face the full $60,000 NRA exemption. For families like the one in our case study below, this gap drives the entire planning structure.

Foreign Death Taxes and the Stacking Problem

The US estate tax is only one layer. Many countries impose their own death taxes — inheritance taxes, succession duties, or capital gains deemed dispositions — and these stack on top of the US liability unless treaty relief applies.

How Major Jurisdictions Tax Bitcoin at Death

  • Germany (Erbschaftsteuer): Inheritance tax applies based on the relationship between decedent and heir. Rates range from 7% to 50%. Bitcoin is classified as "other property" (sonstiges Vermögen). A child inheriting from a parent receives a €400,000 exemption; a spouse receives €500,000. The tax applies to the worldwide estate of German-domiciled persons and to German-situs assets of non-residents.
  • United Kingdom (Inheritance Tax): 40% on estates above the nil-rate band (£325,000, or £500,000 with the residence nil-rate band). UK IHT is domicile-based — a UK-domiciled individual is taxed on worldwide assets. Bitcoin is treated as property for IHT purposes. The UK applies a "deemed domicile" rule: anyone resident in the UK for 15 of the preceding 20 tax years is deemed domiciled.
  • Canada: No estate or inheritance tax, but a deemed disposition at death triggers capital gains tax on all appreciated property, including Bitcoin. At current inclusion rates, this can result in an effective tax rate of approximately 27% on the appreciation of a large Bitcoin position.
  • France (Droits de succession): Rates range from 5% to 45% for direct-line heirs. France asserts jurisdiction based on either the decedent's domicile or the heir's domicile (if the heir has been French-resident for at least 6 of the past 10 years). Bitcoin held by a French-resident heir is taxable regardless of where the decedent lived.
  • Australia: No estate or inheritance tax. Like Canada, Australia imposes a deemed disposal for capital gains purposes, but the main residence exemption and cost base reset at death can eliminate much of the liability.
  • Singapore: No estate tax (abolished in 2008). No capital gains tax. Singapore is the cleanest jurisdiction for Bitcoin succession from a pure tax perspective.

The stacking problem arises when multiple countries tax the same Bitcoin. A German national residing in Singapore with Bitcoin held through Coinbase (US) could face: US estate tax (NRA rules, no treaty), German inheritance tax (worldwide taxation of German nationals), and potentially UK IHT if any beneficiary is UK-deemed-domiciled. Without careful structuring, the aggregate tax burden can consume more than half the Bitcoin estate.

Cross-Border Trust Recognition: Will Your Trust Be Respected?

A US-based dynasty trust or irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) is a cornerstone of domestic Bitcoin estate planning. But when beneficiaries live abroad — or when the grantor is not a US person — the question of whether the trust will be recognized by foreign jurisdictions becomes critical.

United Kingdom

The UK generally recognizes foreign trusts but subjects them to aggressive tax treatment. A US trust with UK-resident beneficiaries may be treated as a "relevant property trust" subject to periodic charges (up to 6% every 10 years) and exit charges when distributions are made to UK beneficiaries. The UK's anti-avoidance rules under the Inheritance Tax Act 1984 (as amended by the Finance Act 2006) can treat transfers into a US trust by a UK-domiciled person as immediately chargeable to IHT.

Canada

Canada recognizes foreign trusts but imposes the "21-year deemed disposition rule." Every 21 years, a trust is deemed to have disposed of all capital property at fair market value, triggering capital gains tax. A US dynasty trust holding Bitcoin for Canadian-resident beneficiaries will face this deemed disposition, regardless of whether any actual distribution occurs. This effectively prevents perpetual tax-deferred growth for Canadian beneficiaries.

Germany

Germany does not have a domestic trust concept equivalent to common-law trusts. German tax law treats foreign trusts as "transparent" — the trust assets are attributed to either the grantor (if living) or the beneficiaries for inheritance and income tax purposes. A US irrevocable trust holding Bitcoin for German-resident beneficiaries provides no German tax shielding. The beneficiaries are taxed as if they held the Bitcoin directly.

Singapore

Singapore recognizes foreign trusts and does not impose estate or capital gains tax on trust distributions. A US trust distributing Bitcoin to a Singapore-resident beneficiary generally creates no Singapore tax liability. This makes Singapore the most favorable beneficiary jurisdiction for US trust structures.

Planning Insight

Cross-border trust recognition is not binary. A trust may be "recognized" by a foreign jurisdiction but subjected to tax treatment that negates its planning benefits entirely. The question is never simply "will they respect my trust?" but rather "how will they tax it?" For offshore trust structures, the analysis must be jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction.

Forced Heirship Laws and Bitcoin

Several civil-law jurisdictions impose forced heirship rules — mandatory minimum shares of an estate that must pass to specific heirs (typically children and surviving spouses), regardless of the decedent's wishes as expressed in a will or trust.

Key Forced Heirship Regimes

  • France: Children are "reserved heirs." One child receives at least 50% of the estate; two children receive at least 66.7%; three or more receive at least 75%. The freely disposable portion (quotité disponible) shrinks with each child.
  • Germany (Pflichtteil): Disinherited children and spouses are entitled to a compulsory share equal to 50% of their intestate share, payable as a cash claim against the estate. The Pflichtteil cannot be avoided by trust or gift — it is calculated on the full estate including gifts made within 10 years of death.
  • Spain: Two-thirds of the estate is reserved for children (the "legítima"). One-third is the strict reserve (legítima estricta) divided equally; one-third can be distributed among children unequally; only one-third is freely disposable.
  • Quebec: While the rest of Canada follows common-law freedom of testation, Quebec's Civil Code imposes a "survival pension" obligation on the estate for the benefit of dependents, and the matrimonial regime can override testamentary dispositions regarding family patrimony.

Application to Bitcoin

Forced heirship creates a direct conflict with common-law trust planning. If a German national creates a US irrevocable trust and transfers 200 BTC into it, intending to benefit only one of three children, the disinherited children retain a Pflichtteil claim under German law. German courts have historically enforced Pflichtteil claims against foreign trust assets, treating the transfer into trust as a gift subject to the 10-year clawback period.

For Bitcoin specifically, forced heirship creates an additional practical problem: enforcement. If the Bitcoin is held in a self-custody trust structure with a US trustee, a German court's Pflichtteil judgment must be domesticated in a US court to be enforced against the trust assets. Whether a US court will enforce a foreign forced heirship claim against a US trust depends on the state — some states (Nevada, South Dakota, Alaska) have enacted statutes specifically refusing to recognize foreign forced heirship orders.

This creates a genuine planning opportunity for international Bitcoin families, but one that must be structured carefully. Moving assets into a trust in a forced-heirship-resistant jurisdiction after the forced heirship claim has crystallized (i.e., after children are born) invites challenges for fraudulent transfer.

FBAR, FATCA, and Form 8938: What Executors Must File at Death

The reporting obligations that applied during the decedent's lifetime do not terminate at death. Executors and estate administrators inherit these obligations — and the penalties for failure are severe.

FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)

Any US person (including the executor of a US person's estate) with a financial interest in or signature authority over foreign financial accounts exceeding $10,000 in aggregate at any point during the year must file an FBAR. If the decedent held Bitcoin through a foreign exchange (Bitstamp in Luxembourg, Kraken's non-US entity, a Swiss private bank custody account), the executor must file the decedent's final FBAR for the year of death and may need to file for prior years if the decedent failed to do so.

Whether a foreign cryptocurrency exchange account constitutes a "foreign financial account" for FBAR purposes remains formally unresolved, but FinCEN proposed regulations in 2020 that would explicitly include virtual currency accounts. Most practitioners advise filing defensively. The penalty for willful failure to file is the greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account balance — per year, per account.

FATCA and Form 8938

The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act requires US persons to report specified foreign financial assets on Form 8938 if they exceed the applicable threshold ($200,000 at year-end for domestic filers; $400,000 for foreign residents). This applies to the estate as a taxpayer for the period between death and final distribution. If the estate holds foreign-custodied Bitcoin above the threshold, Form 8938 must be attached to the estate's income tax return (Form 1041).

Form 3520 / 3520-A

If the decedent was a grantor or beneficiary of a foreign trust holding Bitcoin, the estate must file Form 3520 (Annual Return to Report Transactions With Foreign Trusts) and ensure the foreign trust files Form 3520-A. The penalty for late or incomplete filing is 35% of the gross value of trust distributions or 5% of trust assets — whichever produces the larger penalty.

Executor Liability

An executor who distributes estate assets before satisfying foreign reporting obligations becomes personally liable for the penalties. For dual-citizen and expatriate Bitcoin holders, the reporting requirements can exceed a dozen forms across multiple jurisdictions. A qualified international tax attorney should review reporting obligations before any estate distributions.

International Custody Structures for Bitcoin

The choice of custody structure is the single most consequential planning decision for international Bitcoin families. It determines situs for estate tax, controls which countries can assert jurisdiction, and defines the reporting obligations that apply at death.

BVI/Cayman Holding Entity

A common structure for high-net-worth international families involves holding Bitcoin through a company incorporated in the British Virgin Islands (BVI) or Cayman Islands. The entity holds the Bitcoin (either through self-custody or a non-US custodian), and the family holds shares in the entity. At death, the transfer is of shares in a BVI/Cayman company — not of Bitcoin directly — which clarifies situs and can invoke favorable treaty treatment.

Benefits include: clear situs determination (shares are located where the company is incorporated), avoidance of US-situs classification, access to BVI's zero-tax regime, and simplified succession (share transfer versus multi-custodian key management). The structure costs $5,000–$15,000 annually to maintain and requires proper substance and governance.

Swiss Private Bank Custody

Several Swiss banks (Sygnum, SEBA, Maerki Baumann) now offer institutional Bitcoin custody. Swiss custody provides: a non-US situs for NRA purposes, robust regulatory oversight under FINMA, multi-jurisdictional estate planning support, and automatic CRS reporting (but not FATCA reporting unless the account holder is a US person). Swiss custody costs are typically 0.25%–0.75% of assets under custody annually.

The primary disadvantage is regulatory reporting. Under the Common Reporting Standard (CRS), the Swiss bank will automatically report account information to the tax authority of the account holder's country of residence. There is no "secrecy" benefit — the structure is purely for situs planning and institutional-grade security.

The "Most Treaty-Favored" Structure

For international Bitcoin families, the optimal custody and entity structure should satisfy several criteria simultaneously:

  1. Avoid US-situs classification: No US custodian, no US ETF, no US-incorporated entity
  2. Invoke treaty relief where available: Hold through an entity in a treaty-partner jurisdiction or ensure the decedent is domiciled in a treaty-partner country
  3. Minimize forced heirship exposure: If the family includes civil-law jurisdictions, use a trust in a jurisdiction that does not recognize foreign forced heirship claims
  4. Centralize reporting: Use a structure where reporting obligations are clear and consolidated rather than scattered across multiple custodians and jurisdictions
  5. Maintain operational security: The structure must actually work for Bitcoin — key management, multisig governance, and succession access must be technically functional across borders

The typical solution for families with $5M+ in Bitcoin value involves: a BVI or Cayman holding company, shares held by a trust in a common-law jurisdiction (South Dakota, Nevada, Jersey, or Guernsey depending on the family's connections), custody through a non-US institutional custodian (Swiss or Singaporean), and carefully drafted trust terms that respect forced heirship obligations in relevant jurisdictions to prevent clawback claims.

Power of Attorney and Healthcare Directive Validity Across Borders

A power of attorney (POA) executed in the United States may not be recognized in Germany, Singapore, or the UK. Each jurisdiction has its own requirements for form, execution, notarization, apostille, and registration. For Bitcoin holders, this creates a life-threatening gap: if you are incapacitated while abroad, your agent may be unable to access or manage your Bitcoin because the local jurisdiction does not recognize your POA.

Best practices for international Bitcoin families:

  • Execute parallel POAs in each jurisdiction where you hold assets, maintain residence, or are likely to receive medical care. Each POA should be drafted by local counsel and executed in compliance with local formalities.
  • Include specific digital asset provisions in every POA. Many jurisdictions' standard-form POAs do not contemplate cryptocurrency. The POA should explicitly authorize the agent to access hardware wallets, communicate with custodians, execute transactions on exchanges, and manage private keys.
  • Register POAs where required. Germany requires registration with the Bundesnotarkammer (Federal Chamber of Notaries). The UK Lasting Power of Attorney must be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian before it can be used. Singapore's Lasting Power of Attorney must be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian under the Mental Capacity Act.
  • Healthcare directives (advance directives, living wills) are even less portable than financial POAs. A US advance directive has no legal force in Germany. Execute separate healthcare directives in every jurisdiction where you might receive treatment.

Case Study: The Hartmann-Chen Family

The 4-Jurisdiction Problem

Family profile: Klaus Hartmann (German national, age 58) and Lisa Chen-Hartmann (US citizen, age 54) reside in Singapore. They hold 200 BTC (~$14.8M at $74,000/BTC) across three custodians: 80 BTC self-custodied on a multisig hardware setup in Singapore, 70 BTC at Coinbase Institutional (US), and 50 BTC at Sygnum Bank (Switzerland). Their son Max (age 28) lives in Munich. Their daughter Sarah (age 25) lives in San Francisco.

The four jurisdictions in play:

  1. United States: Lisa is a US citizen — her worldwide estate is subject to US estate tax regardless of where she lives. Klaus holds 70 BTC through a US custodian — potentially US-situs property. Sarah is a US-resident beneficiary.
  2. Germany: Klaus is a German national — Germany's inheritance tax applies to his worldwide estate. Max is a German-resident beneficiary subject to German inheritance tax on his receipt. Klaus is subject to Pflichtteil — Max and Sarah each have a compulsory share claim.
  3. Singapore: The couple's country of residence. No estate tax, no capital gains tax, but Singaporean succession law (Intestate Succession Act for non-Muslims) governs if no will is probated in Singapore.
  4. Switzerland: 50 BTC held at Sygnum creates a Swiss-situs asset and CRS reporting to Singapore.

Unstructured exposure: If Klaus dies first without planning, the 70 BTC at Coinbase is potentially subject to US estate tax at NRA rates ($60,000 exemption — no US-Singapore treaty), German inheritance tax on his worldwide estate (Max's share taxed at 11%–30% depending on value above €400,000 exemption), and potential Swiss withholding on the Sygnum account transfer. Lisa inherits the Singapore-custodied Bitcoin tax-free as a Singapore resident, but her US citizenship means those assets enter her worldwide estate. Total estimated unstructured tax burden at Klaus's death: $2.8–$3.4 million.

The Structure That Resolves It

Step 1: Eliminate US-situs exposure. Move the 70 BTC from Coinbase to Sygnum Bank in Switzerland. Klaus is not a US person, so holding Bitcoin through a non-US custodian removes the US estate tax claim. Cost: minimal (custody fee differential). Tax savings at death: ~$2.05 million (avoiding 40% on ~$5.18M above $60K).

Step 2: Establish a South Dakota dynasty trust for Lisa's share. As a US citizen, Lisa can use her $15 million OBBBA exemption to transfer her share of the Bitcoin into an irrevocable trust. South Dakota provides: no state income tax on trust accumulations, no rule against perpetuities (perpetual trust), and statutory refusal to recognize foreign forced heirship claims. Lisa gifts her community-property share (~100 BTC, ~$7.4M) into the trust, well within her $15M exemption. The trust names Max and Sarah as beneficiaries with distribution standards that respect German Pflichtteil for Max's share to prevent challenge. This is standard planning for any non-citizen spouse situation.

Step 3: BVI holding company for Klaus's share. Klaus transfers his ~100 BTC into a BVI-incorporated company. The BVI company holds the Bitcoin at Sygnum. At Klaus's death, what transfers is BVI company shares — sited in the BVI, not the US or Switzerland. Under the US-Germany estate tax treaty, intangible property is sited at the decedent's domicile (Singapore/Germany depending on analysis). The BVI entity provides clean situs determination and avoids custodian-location arguments.

Step 4: Address the Pflichtteil. Klaus cannot eliminate Max and Sarah's compulsory share rights under German law. But the structure can accommodate them. The BVI company's articles include a mandatory distribution provision requiring that, upon Klaus's death, each child receives shares representing at least 50% of their intestate share (the Pflichtteil calculation). This voluntary compliance prevents litigation and the risk that a German court orders a larger clawback.

Step 5: Parallel estate documents. The family executes: (a) a Singapore will covering assets physically located in Singapore (hardware wallets, local bank accounts), (b) a German will (covering the BVI company shares, which may be subject to German succession law), (c) a US will for Lisa (covering any remaining US-situs assets and pouring over to the South Dakota trust), and (d) POAs in all three jurisdictions plus Switzerland (for Sygnum account access). Each will contains a conflicts clause deferring to the others for assets outside its scope.

Step 6: Annual gift program. Klaus and Lisa each use the $19,000 annual gift tax exclusion to transfer Bitcoin to Max and Sarah. For Klaus, gifts to US-resident Sarah require reporting on Form 3520 by Sarah but create no US gift tax (gifts by NRAs of non-US-situs intangible property are not subject to US gift tax). Lisa's gifts use her annual exclusion and reduce her remaining lifetime exemption only if she exceeds $19,000 per recipient.

Result: Estimated tax at Klaus's death drops from $2.8–$3.4 million to approximately $350,000–$500,000 (German inheritance tax on Max's share above the €400,000 exemption, reduced by treaty credits). Lisa's estate, when she eventually dies, is sheltered by the $15M US exemption and the dynasty trust structure. Total estimated savings: $4–6 million across both estates.

The International Bitcoin Estate Planning Checklist

For families whose Bitcoin holdings cross borders, the following planning elements are non-negotiable:

  1. Jurisdiction audit: Identify every country that may assert taxing authority over your estate — domicile, nationality, residence, custodian location, beneficiary residence, and asset situs. Most international Bitcoin families have 3–5 jurisdictions in play.
  2. Treaty analysis: For each US-connected jurisdiction, determine whether an estate tax treaty applies and what relief it provides. For non-US jurisdiction pairs (e.g., Germany-Singapore), determine bilateral treaty coverage separately.
  3. Custody restructuring: Move Bitcoin off US custodians if you are not a US person. Move Bitcoin off custodians in high-tax jurisdictions if lower-tax alternatives provide equal security. Document the reasons for each custodial choice.
  4. Trust and entity structuring: Establish trusts or holding entities in jurisdictions that provide the optimal combination of tax treatment, forced heirship protection, and operational functionality. Match the structure to the family — not the other way around.
  5. Parallel wills and POAs: Execute jurisdiction-specific documents in every relevant country. Ensure each document has a conflicts/scope clause that prevents overlap. Register POAs where required.
  6. Reporting compliance: Identify every form that must be filed — FBAR, FATCA, Form 3520, CRS, local equivalents — and create a compliance calendar. The executor must know these obligations exist before you die. Include a reporting inventory in your estate planning binder.
  7. Key management protocol: Ensure that Bitcoin access procedures work across borders. If your hardware wallet is in Singapore and your executor is in San Francisco, how does transfer happen? Multisig with geographically distributed signers, time-locked recovery mechanisms, and institutional custody with documented succession procedures are all viable approaches. The wrong answer is "we'll figure it out."
  8. Annual review: International tax law changes constantly. Treaties are renegotiated, exemptions are adjusted, and new reporting requirements emerge. Review the structure annually with counsel in each relevant jurisdiction. The OBBBA's $15 million exemption — and its potential future reduction — makes timing decisions particularly consequential.

Conclusion: Bitcoin Demands International Planning

The borderless nature of Bitcoin is one of its greatest strengths as a monetary asset and one of its greatest complications as an estate planning asset. A single Bitcoin position can create taxable events in four or five countries, invoke forced heirship claims across civil-law jurisdictions, trigger dozens of reporting obligations, and challenge the recognition of trust structures that work perfectly in domestic planning.

The families that navigate this successfully are the ones that treat international estate planning not as an afterthought to their domestic plan, but as the primary framework from which everything else follows. If your life crosses borders, your Bitcoin plan must cross borders too — not by accident, but by design.

For families building their first Bitcoin estate plan, or for those with existing plans that assumed a single-jurisdiction world, the time to internationalize your planning is now — while the OBBBA's $15 million exemption provides the most generous transfer opportunity in US estate tax history, and while treaty networks still provide meaningful relief from double taxation.
