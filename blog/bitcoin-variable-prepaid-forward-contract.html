Bitcoin Variable Prepaid Forward Contract: How to Monetize BTC Without Triggering a Sale

Published March 16, 2026 · The Bitcoin Family Office

The variable prepaid forward — Wall Street's favorite tool for concentrated stock positions — is now available for Bitcoin. Here's how it works, why it defers tax, and when it makes sense for families sitting on eight- and nine-figure BTC positions.

You own a substantial Bitcoin position. The unrealized gain is enormous. You need liquidity — for a business acquisition, real estate, trust funding, portfolio diversification, or simply because having 70% of your net worth in a single volatile asset keeps your wealth advisor awake at night.

The obvious move — selling — triggers an immediate capital gains tax event. At current long-term rates, that's 20% federal plus 3.8% net investment income tax, plus state tax if applicable. On a $50 million position with a near-zero cost basis, you're writing a check to the IRS for $12 million or more before you've deployed a single dollar.

There's a better path. The bitcoin variable prepaid forward contract — a structure Wall Street has used for decades on concentrated equity positions — lets you receive cash today, retain partial upside exposure to BTC, and defer the capital gains tax event for two to five years. No sale. No constructive sale. Just a contract and a collateral pledge.

This is the most sophisticated monetization tool available for concentrated Bitcoin holders. Here's exactly how it works.

What Is a Variable Prepaid Forward Contract?

A variable prepaid forward (VPF) is a privately negotiated contract between two parties — typically a Bitcoin holder and an investment bank or institutional counterparty. The mechanics:

  1. You pledge Bitcoin as collateral to the counterparty.
  2. The counterparty pays you cash upfront — typically 75% to 90% of the current market value of the pledged BTC.
  3. At a future settlement date (usually two to five years out), you deliver a variable number of Bitcoin to the counterparty based on where BTC price lands relative to predetermined floor and cap prices.

The word "variable" is doing all the work. Unlike a fixed forward — where you agree to deliver a set number of coins at a set price — a VPF adjusts the delivery quantity based on price movement. If BTC rises significantly, you deliver fewer coins. If BTC falls, you deliver more coins (up to the full collateral amount). This variable delivery mechanism is what separates a VPF from a plain sale, and it's what keeps the structure on the right side of the tax code.

Think of it as monetizing your position while retaining a band of upside exposure. You get cash now. You keep some of the ride up. You give up gains above a cap. And critically, you don't trigger a taxable event until settlement.

Why a VPF Defers Capital Gains Tax

The tax deferral is the entire point. Under the Internal Revenue Code, a taxable event occurs when you sell or exchange property, or when a transaction is treated as a constructive sale under §1259. A properly structured VPF is neither.

At contract inception, you haven't sold anything. You've entered into a forward contract — a promise to deliver property in the future. The cash you receive is treated as a loan advance or prepayment against the forward obligation, not as sale proceeds. You retain legal title to the Bitcoin throughout the term (though it sits in the counterparty's custody as collateral).

The taxable event occurs at settlement — the date you actually deliver Bitcoin to the counterparty. At that point, you recognize gain or loss based on the difference between your cost basis and the value of the BTC delivered. If you entered a three-year VPF in 2026, you don't recognize gain until 2029.

This deferral has compounding value. Three to five years of deferred tax means three to five years of deploying the full pre-tax proceeds. If you invest the VPF cash at 8% annually, deferring $12 million in tax for three years generates roughly $3.6 million in additional returns — returns you'd never have if you simply sold and paid tax immediately.

There's also optionality in the deferral. Tax rates change. Your income profile changes. You may settle in a year when your marginal rate is lower, or when you have offsetting losses. The VPF buys you time to optimize the eventual tax hit.

The §1259 Constructive Sale Analysis

Congress saw the forward contract loophole coming. In 1997, it enacted IRC §1259 — the constructive sale rule — specifically to prevent taxpayers from locking in gains through forward contracts, short sales, and other derivative positions without recognizing taxable income.

Under §1259, you have a constructive sale if you enter into a transaction that substantially eliminates both the risk of loss and the opportunity for gain with respect to an appreciated financial position. A textbook fixed forward — "I will deliver 100 BTC at $85,000 per coin in three years" — eliminates both risk and reward. That's a constructive sale. Tax is due immediately.

A VPF avoids constructive sale treatment because it does not eliminate the opportunity for gain. Here's why:

The Variable Delivery Mechanism Preserves Upside

In a VPF, the number of coins you deliver at settlement varies based on price. If BTC is between the floor price and the cap price at maturity, you deliver fewer coins as the price rises. If BTC exceeds the cap, you still deliver the minimum number of coins — but you've captured the appreciation between the floor and the cap.

This retained upside participation — the spread between floor and cap — is what distinguishes a VPF from a constructive sale. You haven't locked in a fixed price. You've retained a meaningful band of potential gain. The IRS has consistently treated this structure as falling outside §1259, provided the spread between floor and cap is wide enough to constitute genuine economic exposure.

How Wide Must the Spread Be?

There's no bright-line rule. The IRS has not published specific guidance on the minimum acceptable spread for Bitcoin VPFs. For equity VPFs, market practice has generally settled on a cap that's 20% to 40% above the floor. Tax counsel will stress-test the spread against the "substantially all" language in §1259 and relevant case law.

For Bitcoin, where annualized volatility routinely exceeds 50%, a wider spread is both easier to justify and economically rational. A floor-to-cap spread of 30% to 50% is common in Bitcoin VPFs, reflecting the asset's volatility profile and providing substantial comfort against §1259 recharacterization.

The Anschutz Case: Cautionary Precedent

The most instructive case on VPF tax treatment is Anschutz Co. v. Commissioner (T.C. Memo 2011-46). The Tax Court held that certain prepaid forward transactions by Philip Anschutz constituted constructive sales because the taxpayer had effectively eliminated all economic risk. The structure involved extremely tight collars and additional hedging that, in aggregate, left virtually no upside exposure.

The lesson: the VPF itself must retain genuine economic exposure between floor and cap. You cannot layer additional hedges on top of a VPF that collectively eliminate your remaining upside without risking §1259 recharacterization. Structure matters. The spread between floor and cap is not decorative — it must represent real risk retained by the holder.

The Floor and Cap: Understanding Your Payoff Structure

Every VPF is defined by three parameters: the prepaid amount (cash you receive), the floor price, and the cap price. Together, these determine your delivery obligation and economic exposure.

How the Floor Works

The floor price is the downside threshold. If BTC is at or below the floor at settlement, you deliver the maximum number of coins — typically the entire pledged collateral. Below the floor, you bear full downside risk, and the counterparty is made whole through maximum delivery.

Example: You pledge 100 BTC when BTC is at $100,000. The floor is set at $80,000. If BTC is at $70,000 at settlement, you deliver all 100 BTC. The counterparty receives $7,000,000 in Bitcoin against a prepayment of roughly $8,000,000 — the counterparty takes a loss, absorbed by the implicit discount in the prepaid amount.

How the Cap Works

The cap price is the upside threshold. If BTC exceeds the cap at settlement, you deliver the minimum number of coins — calculated as the prepaid amount divided by the cap price (adjusted for the contract terms). Above the cap, the counterparty captures all additional appreciation. This is the cost of monetization: you've sold your upside above the cap in exchange for cash today.

Example (continued): The cap is set at $130,000. If BTC is at $180,000 at settlement, you still deliver the minimum — roughly 61.5 BTC (the economic equivalent of $8M at the cap price). The remaining 38.5 BTC are yours. But you don't participate in the move from $130,000 to $180,000 on the delivered coins.

The Zone Between Floor and Cap

Between floor and cap, the delivery amount scales linearly. As BTC price rises from $80,000 toward $130,000, you deliver progressively fewer coins. This is where your retained upside lives — the economic exposure that keeps you outside §1259.

BTC Price at Settlement BTC Delivered (of 100 pledged) BTC Retained Your Outcome
$70,000 (below floor)1000Full collateral delivered; kept prepayment
$80,000 (at floor)1000Full collateral delivered; breakeven vs. prepayment
$100,0008020Retained 20 BTC + prepayment
$130,000 (at cap)61.538.5Retained 38.5 BTC + prepayment
$180,000 (above cap)61.538.5Same as at cap; upside above cap goes to counterparty

The cap is the trade-off. You're giving up tail upside in exchange for immediate liquidity and tax deferral. For a holder who needs cash today, this is rational. For a holder who believes BTC will 5x in two years, the cap is expensive.

Bitcoin-Specific VPF Mechanics

VPFs originated in the equity world — concentrated stockholders at publicly traded companies have used them for decades. Bitcoin VPFs share the same legal structure but differ in execution, custody, and counterparty landscape.

Who Offers Bitcoin VPFs

The market is still developing, but several categories of counterparty are active:

  • Tier 1 investment banks with crypto desks: Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and JP Morgan have all built digital asset capabilities. For clients with existing prime brokerage relationships and positions above $10M, these banks can structure Bitcoin VPFs through their derivatives desks.
  • Crypto-native institutional platforms: Coinbase Prime, Galaxy Digital, and similar firms offer structured products for institutional Bitcoin holders. These firms understand crypto custody and can move faster than traditional banks, though credit risk profiles differ.
  • Specialty structured product firms: Boutique firms focused on digital asset derivatives can structure VPFs for qualifying holders, often with more flexibility on terms but potentially higher spreads.

Minimum Size

VPFs are bespoke, privately negotiated contracts. The legal, compliance, and structuring costs make them uneconomical below roughly $5 million notional. Most active VPF counterparties prefer $10M+ and become materially more competitive at $25M+. At $50M and above, you have real negotiating leverage on the prepaid percentage and cap level.

Collateral and Custody

The counterparty requires possession of the pledged Bitcoin. This is non-negotiable — the BTC is the collateral securing your delivery obligation and the counterparty's advance payment. During the VPF term:

  • BTC moves to the counterparty's custody infrastructure (or a mutually agreed qualified custodian)
  • You retain legal ownership but not possession
  • The counterparty typically cannot rehypothecate or lend the collateral (though this is a negotiation point — read the fine print)
  • Staking income, forks, and airdrops during the term are addressed in the contract — typically these accrue to the holder, but specifics vary

Custody is the single biggest operational concern for Bitcoin VPFs. You're trusting a counterparty with your coins for years. The collapse of FTX in 2022, Celsius, BlockFi, and Voyager made custody risk viscerally real for every Bitcoin holder. Insist on segregated custody with a regulated, insured qualified custodian. Accept nothing less.

Prepaid Percentage

The advance payment — the cash you receive at inception — typically ranges from 75% to 90% of the pledged Bitcoin's current market value. The exact percentage depends on:

  • BTC volatility: Higher implied volatility = lower advance (the counterparty bears more risk)
  • Contract term: Longer term = lower advance
  • Floor-to-cap spread: Wider spread = lower advance (you're retaining more upside)
  • Counterparty's hedging cost: The bank needs to hedge its exposure; this cost passes through
  • Your creditworthiness: Although the BTC collateral is the primary security, counterparties still assess credit

At current Bitcoin volatility levels, expect 75% to 85% for a three-year term with a reasonable floor-to-cap spread. Tighter spreads (less retained upside) command higher advances.

Hedging During the VPF Term and Straddle Rules

Once you've entered a VPF, your remaining economic exposure to Bitcoin lives in the floor-to-cap zone plus any un-pledged BTC you continue to hold. Can you hedge that residual exposure?

Carefully.

The §1092 Straddle Problem

IRC §1092 defines a "straddle" as offsetting positions in actively traded property. If you hold Bitcoin (through the VPF's retained upside) and simultaneously hold a position that offsets that exposure — like a put option on BTC — the IRS may treat the combination as a straddle. Consequences:

  • Loss deferral: Losses on one leg of a straddle cannot be recognized until the offsetting position is also closed
  • Holding period suspension: The long-term capital gains holding period may be suspended while the straddle is open, potentially converting long-term gains to short-term
  • Interest capitalization: Carrying costs may need to be capitalized rather than deducted currently

The Broader §1259 Risk

Even more dangerous: additional hedges layered on top of a VPF could cause the entire structure to be recharacterized as a constructive sale under §1259. If you enter a VPF with a floor-to-cap spread of $80,000 to $130,000 and then buy a put option at $85,000, you've narrowed your effective economic exposure to the point where the IRS could argue you've eliminated substantially all risk and reward. The VPF that was safe in isolation becomes a constructive sale when combined with the hedge.

The rule of thumb: do not layer additional derivatives on the pledged Bitcoin position without specific tax counsel sign-off. The interaction between the VPF, any additional hedges, §1259, and §1092 is complex enough that a generic "buy some puts for protection" approach can destroy the entire tax deferral benefit.

If you hold un-pledged BTC beyond the VPF collateral, hedging that separate position is a different analysis — but the straddle rules may still apply if the IRS views the positions as offsetting.

Estate Planning Applications

For families with concentrated Bitcoin wealth, a VPF isn't just a monetization tool — it's an estate planning catalyst. The cash proceeds unlock strategies that require liquidity, and the deferred gain creates planning opportunities that don't exist in a simple sell-and-reinvest scenario.

Fund Trusts with VPF Proceeds

The cash received from a VPF is immediately deployable. Families use these proceeds to:

  • Seed a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT): Fund a zeroed-out GRAT with VPF cash, invested in a diversified portfolio. If the portfolio outperforms the §7520 hurdle rate, the excess passes to heirs gift-tax-free. Meanwhile, the Bitcoin collateral remains pledged under the VPF — you've effectively separated the liquidity event from the concentrated position.
  • Fund an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT): Use VPF proceeds to pay premiums on a second-to-die policy inside an ILIT. The death benefit provides estate liquidity — solving the classic problem of illiquid Bitcoin positions forcing fire sales at death. This is especially powerful for tech executives and founders whose net worth is dominated by a single asset.
  • Gift to dynasty trusts or irrevocable trusts: VPF cash can be gifted (within annual exclusion or lifetime exemption limits) to irrevocable trusts, removing the transferred value from your taxable estate while the underlying Bitcoin remains pledged and growing.

Transfer the VPF Obligation to a Trust

A more advanced move: transfer the VPF contract itself — including the delivery obligation — to an irrevocable grantor trust. The trust, not you personally, delivers Bitcoin at settlement. The gain recognized on delivery is reported on the trust's tax return (or on the grantor's return if the trust is a grantor trust for income tax purposes). This can be powerful when combined with trust-level planning strategies or when the grantor's individual income tax situation changes between inception and settlement.

The transfer must be carefully structured to avoid triggering acceleration of the VPF or a current tax event. The counterparty must consent to the assignment. Tax counsel experienced in both derivatives and trust law is essential.

Charitable Planning with VPF Proceeds

Families with philanthropic goals can direct VPF cash proceeds into a charitable remainder trust (CRT) or donor-advised fund. While the VPF itself doesn't generate a charitable deduction (you haven't donated Bitcoin — you've pledged it), the liquidity it unlocks enables charitable strategies that require cash funding.

Alternatively, if you hold un-pledged Bitcoin beyond the VPF collateral, donating that BTC directly to a CRT or public charity generates a fair-market-value deduction without recognizing gain — a complementary strategy to the VPF on a different portion of your holdings.

Step-Up at Death: The VPF Wrinkle

If the VPF holder dies during the contract term, the treatment depends on the contract terms and the holder's estate plan. In the best case, the estate can settle the VPF by delivering Bitcoin that receives a stepped-up basis at death under §1014, potentially eliminating the deferred gain entirely. This is the "hold-to-death" optionality embedded in a VPF — if the holder passes away, the tax deferral may become permanent tax elimination.

However, this outcome is not guaranteed. The counterparty contract may have acceleration clauses triggered by death. The estate may lack liquid assets to manage the settlement. And §1014 step-up may not apply cleanly to all components of the VPF position. Estate planning counsel should model the death-during-term scenario explicitly.

VPF vs. Alternatives for Bitcoin Concentration

A VPF is one tool in a broader toolkit for managing concentrated Bitcoin positions. Here's how it compares to the primary alternatives:

VPF vs. Exchange Fund

An exchange fund pools concentrated positions from multiple holders into a diversified fund. You contribute Bitcoin and receive a pro-rata interest in a diversified portfolio — achieving diversification without a taxable sale.

DimensionVPFExchange Fund
Immediate cashYes (75–90% of value)No
DiversificationNo (proceeds must be self-directed)Yes (built into fund structure)
Tax deferral2–5 years (until settlement)Indefinite (until fund redemption)
Lock-upContract term (2–5 years)7 years minimum
Upside retainedFloor-to-cap onlyPro-rata fund returns
Best forLiquidity-driven monetizationDiversification without cash need

If you need cash, a VPF wins. If you need diversification and can wait, an exchange fund is cleaner.

VPF vs. Margin Loan (Borrow Against Bitcoin)

A margin loan against Bitcoin generates cash without a taxable event — similar to a VPF. But the economics differ materially.

DimensionVPFMargin Loan
Cash upfront75–90% LTV40–65% LTV typically
Margin call riskNone (fixed delivery obligation)Yes — forced liquidation if BTC drops
Interest costImplicit (embedded in cap)Explicit (annual rate, currently 6–12%)
Upside participationFloor-to-cap bandFull (you own 100% of appreciation)
Tax eventAt settlement (2–5 years)Never (unless loan defaults/liquidation)
ComplexityHigh (bespoke derivative)Moderate (standard lending)

The margin loan preserves full upside and avoids any future tax event (as long as you repay the loan). The VPF provides higher LTV and eliminates margin call risk but caps your upside and creates a future tax liability. For holders who are confident BTC will appreciate significantly and can tolerate margin call risk, the loan is often preferable. For holders who want certainty — maximum cash today, no margin calls, and a defined delivery obligation — the VPF wins.

VPF vs. Charitable Remainder Trust

A CRT funded with appreciated Bitcoin avoids immediate capital gains, provides an income stream, and generates a charitable deduction. But it's irrevocable, requires charitable intent, and the remainder goes to charity.

A VPF provides more flexibility and higher liquidity but no charitable deduction and no income stream — just a lump sum of cash upfront. Families with genuine philanthropic goals and the ability to sacrifice the remainder may prefer the CRT. Those focused purely on monetization will favor the VPF.

VPF vs. Hold-to-Death

The simplest strategy: hold Bitcoin until death, receive a §1014 stepped-up basis, and pass BTC to heirs with zero capital gains tax. This is the most tax-efficient outcome if you can tolerate full concentration risk and don't need liquidity during your lifetime.

The problem: concentration risk is real. A family with $50M in Bitcoin and minimal liquid assets faces a genuine estate liquidity crisis if the holder dies when BTC is in a drawdown. Hold-to-death also provides no diversification, no current income, and no flexibility to fund trust structures, insurance premiums, or business opportunities.

A VPF is the pragmatic middle ground: monetize some of the position for liquidity, retain upside on the rest, defer the tax, and use the proceeds to shore up the estate plan. It doesn't have to be all-or-nothing.

Risks and Limitations

A VPF is not a free lunch. Every benefit comes with a corresponding cost or risk.

Counterparty Risk

Your Bitcoin sits in the counterparty's custody for the duration of the contract. If the counterparty fails — becomes insolvent, suffers a hack, or simply disappears — recovering your collateral may be difficult, delayed, or impossible. This is not theoretical. The crypto industry has a recent and painful history of custodial failures.

Mitigation: use a counterparty with a strong balance sheet and regulatory oversight. Insist on segregated custody with a regulated qualified custodian (not commingled on the counterparty's balance sheet). Negotiate contractual protections for insolvency scenarios. Understand the bankruptcy treatment of your collateral under applicable law.

Upside Cap

The cap price is the cost of the VPF. All appreciation above the cap goes to the counterparty. In a market where BTC has historically produced multi-hundred-percent rallies, the opportunity cost of the cap can be enormous. If you enter a VPF with a cap at $130,000 and BTC reaches $300,000 at settlement, you've left $170,000 per coin on the table on the delivered portion.

This is a known and accepted trade-off. You're paying for liquidity and tax deferral. The cap is the price. But model the scenarios. Understand what you're giving up in a bull case.

Maximum Delivery Below the Floor

If BTC collapses below the floor price, you deliver your entire collateral — and the cash you received upfront may be worth more than the Bitcoin you're delivering. In that scenario, you've effectively sold at the prepaid price, which may be below your basis, creating a loss. You've also lost the Bitcoin. This is the worst-case outcome and it's equivalent to having sold at inception — but with the "sale price" locked in at the VPF advance amount.

IRS Recharacterization Risk

The IRS could challenge the VPF structure and assert that it constitutes a constructive sale under §1259, particularly if:

  • The floor-to-cap spread is too narrow
  • Additional hedges are layered on top
  • The contract terms effectively eliminate economic risk
  • The structure is deemed abusive or lacking economic substance

This risk is manageable with experienced tax counsel but never zero. The IRS has been increasingly focused on digital asset transactions, and novel structures like Bitcoin VPFs will attract scrutiny. Maintain robust documentation, ensure the economic terms reflect genuine risk retention, and work with counsel who has specific experience in derivatives tax law.

Liquidity of the VPF Position

VPFs are illiquid. You cannot easily exit, unwind, or transfer the position mid-term. If your circumstances change — you need the Bitcoin back, you want to exit early, or you need to restructure — the counterparty may agree to an early termination, but likely at unfavorable terms. You're locked in for the contract duration.

Regulatory and Legal Uncertainty

Bitcoin VPFs exist in a rapidly evolving regulatory landscape. The IRS has not issued specific guidance on cryptocurrency VPFs. The SEC's position on crypto derivatives continues to develop. State-level regulations vary. A structure that is clearly viable today could face new restrictions or adverse regulatory treatment during the contract term.

Who This Is For

A bitcoin variable prepaid forward contract is not for every Bitcoin holder. It's a sophisticated, high-minimum, counterparty-dependent structure that makes sense for a specific profile:

  • Concentrated BTC position of $5M+ (practically, $10M+ to justify the structuring costs)
  • Near-zero or very low cost basis — the larger the embedded gain, the more valuable the tax deferral
  • Genuine liquidity need — business acquisition, trust funding, real estate, diversification capital, or estate planning funding
  • Willingness to cap upside — you've made your money on the way up and want to de-risk without triggering the tax
  • Tolerance for counterparty risk — you're comfortable with a bank holding your BTC for years
  • Sophisticated tax and legal counsel — this is not a DIY structure

The ideal candidate is a founder, executive, or early Bitcoin adopter sitting on a massive unrealized gain who needs to unlock liquidity for the next chapter — whether that's diversification, estate planning, business investment, or simply sleeping better at night — without writing a nine-figure check to the IRS this year.

For Bitcoin miners with substantial holdings, the VPF is particularly interesting when combined with ongoing mining operations. The mining depreciation deductions generated by active operations can offset the income recognized when the VPF eventually settles — effectively creating a tax shield around the monetization event.

Next Steps

If you're considering a VPF for a concentrated Bitcoin position, the path forward involves:

  1. Engage tax counsel with specific experience in §1259, derivatives taxation, and digital assets. This is not general tax planning — it's specialized derivatives tax law.
  2. Identify counterparties. Your existing prime brokerage or wealth management relationship is the starting point. If they don't offer Bitcoin VPFs directly, they can connect you with desks that do.
  3. Model the economics. Run scenarios across floor prices, cap prices, prepaid percentages, and BTC price outcomes. Understand your payoff in bull, bear, and base cases. Compare against a margin loan, exchange fund, and outright sale.
  4. Integrate with your estate plan. The VPF proceeds and the deferred gain both create planning opportunities. Your estate attorney should be at the table from the beginning.
  5. Execute with custody diligence. The counterparty's custody arrangement is the single most important operational detail. Segregated, insured, regulated. Non-negotiable.

The variable prepaid forward is not the only tool for concentrated Bitcoin positions, and it's not always the best one. But for holders who need cash now, want to defer the tax hit, and are willing to trade upside above a cap for certainty and liquidity — it's the most precise instrument available.
