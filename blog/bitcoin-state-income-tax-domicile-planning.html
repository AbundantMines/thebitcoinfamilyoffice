Bitcoin State Income Tax Planning: Move Before You Sell — and Do It Right

By The Bitcoin Family Office  |  Updated March 2026  |  Tax Strategy  •  Domicile Planning

California taxes Bitcoin capital gains at 13.3%. Oregon at 9.9%. New York at 10.9%. Wyoming, Nevada, Texas, and Florida charge zero. On a $10 million gain, that difference is $1.3 million that either goes to a state government or stays in your family. A properly executed domicile change — before you realize the gain — is one of the highest-ROI moves in Bitcoin tax planning. It is also one of the most frequently botched.

This guide covers the mechanics of establishing a new domicile, the documentation required to make it stick, how California and New York pursue departing residents across state lines, the trust planning strategies that can help even if you can't or won't move, and the timing rules that determine whether your gain is taxed in the old state or the new one.

What Is Domicile — and Why It's Different from Residence

Residency and domicile are related but distinct legal concepts. A person can have multiple residences (homes in multiple states) but only one domicile at any time. Domicile is the state you consider your permanent home — where you intend to remain indefinitely, or return to if temporarily elsewhere.

State income tax follows domicile. If your domicile is California on the date you sell Bitcoin, California taxes the gain — even if you were physically sitting in Wyoming when you hit the sell button. This is why simply spending 183 days out of California is not enough. You must actually change your domicile.

The elements of domicile that states look at:

  • Where you own or rent your primary home (size matters — courts look at which home is larger/more valuable)
  • Where your immediate family lives (spouse, minor children)
  • Where you are registered to vote
  • Where your driver's license is issued
  • Where your vehicles are registered
  • Where your doctors, dentists, lawyers, accountants, and financial advisors are located
  • Where you spend the most time (183-day rule is a floor, not a ceiling)
  • Where you maintain bank accounts, safe deposit boxes, and financial accounts
  • Where your professional, civic, religious, and social connections are centered
  • Where your business activities occur
  • Where you file your federal tax return (address on Form 1040)

No single factor is determinative. Domicile is evaluated on the totality of the facts. California, New York, and other aggressive states look at all of these factors together — and will challenge a domicile change if the facts don't consistently point to the new state.

California: The Most Aggressive State for Departing Residents

California's Franchise Tax Board (FTB) is the most aggressive state tax authority in the United States for pursuing departing residents. California imposes income tax on the worldwide income of its residents — including Bitcoin gains — and takes an expansive view of who qualifies as a California resident.

The 546-Day Rule

Under California Revenue and Taxation Code §17014, a California resident who moves to another state is still treated as a California resident if they spend more than 546 days in California during any consecutive 24-month period (the "safe harbor" is being present in California fewer than 546 days in 24 months). This rule catches people who maintain a California home and commute back for business or family regularly.

The FTB's "Closest Connections" Test

California looks beyond the 546-day rule to determine where your "closest connections" are. The FTB audit process involves reviewing:

  • Passport records and travel logs
  • Credit card transactions by location
  • Cell phone records showing location data
  • Social media geotags and posts
  • Professional engagements scheduled in California
  • The location of business interests and income sources
  • Where professional advisors are retained

The 10-Year Lookback on Stock Options and Deferred Compensation

California has a specific rule for equity compensation (stock options, RSUs) and installment sale notes: it taxes the California-source portion of these instruments even after the holder has moved out of state. For a Bitcoin installment sale note (SCIN or IDGT note) where part of the note term was earned while a California resident, California may claim a portion of the gain. This is less relevant for outright Bitcoin sales but critical for structured transactions.

⚠️ The FTB audits departing residents with large capital gains. If you file a part-year California return showing a large capital gain in the year you moved, expect an audit. California's audit selection algorithms specifically flag returns showing a change in residency status combined with a significant gain realization. The audit may focus on whether your domicile actually changed before the gain was recognized. Document everything — contemporaneously, not retroactively.

New York: Dual Residency and the "Permanent Place of Abode" Trap

New York's residency rules have their own distinctive trap: the "permanent place of abode" (PPA) rule. Under New York Tax Law §605(b)(1)(B), a person who maintains a PPA in New York and spends more than 183 days in New York is taxed as a full-year New York resident — even if their domicile is elsewhere.

A "permanent place of abode" is broadly defined: it includes any dwelling you maintain, whether owned or rented, year-round or nearly so — even a small apartment or a vacation home in the Hamptons. If you keep an apartment in Manhattan while ostensibly domiciled in Florida, and you spend more than 183 days in New York (for any reason — visiting family, attending events, business meetings), New York taxes your worldwide income including Bitcoin gains.

The fix: sell or lease out the New York property, and keep a careful day count. If you have a New York presence you cannot eliminate (business, family), the only solution is to spend fewer than 183 days per year in New York — and document each day meticulously.

The Mechanics of a Clean Domicile Change

A domicile change that will survive state tax audit requires concrete, contemporaneous action — not just intent. Here is the standard checklist:

Before Moving

  1. Purchase or lease a home in the new state — ideally larger or at least comparable in value to the home you are leaving.
  2. Engage a tax advisor in the new state. Transfer relationships with accountants, attorneys, and financial advisors to the new state where possible.
  3. Open bank accounts in the new state. Transfer primary banking relationships.
  4. Register to vote in the new state. Cancel prior voter registration.

At the Time of Move

  1. Obtain a driver's license in the new state (within 30–60 days of establishing residency, per most state laws).
  2. Register vehicles in the new state.
  3. File a Declaration of Domicile in the new state (available in Florida, Nevada, and others — not all states have this form, but filing one creates a dated record).
  4. Move meaningful personal property: furniture, art, family heirlooms, safe deposit box contents to the new state.
  5. Update your address on federal tax filings (Form 1040), investment accounts, retirement accounts, and estate documents.
  6. Update beneficiary designations on life insurance, retirement accounts, and transfer-on-death accounts to the new state address.
  7. Notify the old state's revenue department of your departure (some states require a formal notification; California does not).

After Moving — Ongoing

  1. Do not spend more than 183 days in the old state in any calendar year — and keep a written log of days in each state.
  2. Transfer primary social, civic, and religious memberships to the new state.
  3. Transfer professional affiliations and board memberships where possible.
  4. Obtain new state medical professionals (doctor, dentist, specialists) — and actually use them.
  5. File the final part-year return in the old state and a full-year return (or part-year return) in the new state for the year of the move.

Documentation is your defense. If the FTB or NYDTF audits your domicile change, they will look at contemporaneous records — credit card statements, phone records, gate tags, utility bills. Keep a daily travel log starting from your move date. Use your new-state credit card for day-to-day purchases. Your digital footprint should mirror your stated domicile.

Timing: When Must the Move Be Complete Before You Sell?

The key question: does the gain get taxed in the old state or the new one?

For Bitcoin sold outright (a single transaction), the gain is taxed in the state where you are domiciled on the date of the sale. If your California domicile was severed on January 15 and you sell Bitcoin on February 1, the gain is taxed in your new state — not California.

The critical timing rule: your domicile change must be completed — all the steps above taken — before you sell. A domicile change initiated in January but not completed until March, followed by a Bitcoin sale in February, may be challenged as incomplete at the time of sale.

In practice, advisors typically recommend waiting at least three to six months after taking all domicile-change steps before realizing a large gain — creating a clear separation between the domicile change and the sale, and allowing time to accumulate contemporaneous documentation of life in the new state.

What California and New York Cannot Tax: Bitcoin Appreciation Before the Move

Neither California nor New York can tax the appreciation that occurred while you were not a resident. This is the core of the planning opportunity.

If you bought Bitcoin in Wyoming in 2020 (not a California resident at the time), moved to California in 2022, and move back out of California in 2026 before selling — California can only tax the gain that accrued while you were a California resident (2022–2026 appreciation). The gain from 2020–2022 is not California-source income.

Documenting the fair market value of your Bitcoin at the time of each domicile change creates a clear record of which state's income tax applies to which portion of the gain. A contemporaneous record — screenshot of the Bitcoin price on the day you established California residency, and again on the day you left — is essential for this allocation argument.

Trust Planning When You Can't or Won't Move

Not everyone can or wants to change states. A spouse's career in California, children in school, a business that requires presence — these are real constraints. Trust planning offers a partial alternative, though with important limitations.

Nevada or Wyoming Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT)

A DAPT established in Nevada or Wyoming can hold Bitcoin in a state with no income tax. However, if the grantor (California resident) is also a beneficiary of the trust — and most DAPTs are structured this way for asset protection purposes — California will treat trust income as California-source income taxable to the beneficiary-resident. California does not allow residents to use out-of-state trusts to avoid state income tax simply by virtue of the trust's situs.

Non-Grantor Trust with Non-California Beneficiaries

If the trust is a non-grantor trust (grantor has no retained interest or control) and all beneficiaries are non-California residents, California may not tax the trust's income. But this requires a complete, irrevocable transfer — no retained access, no beneficial interest for the California resident. This effectively rules out personal liquidity from the transferred Bitcoin.

The Bottom Line on Trusts for State Tax

Trust planning can help in specific circumstances, but it is not a substitute for an actual domicile change for a California or New York resident who wants to eliminate state tax on Bitcoin gains. The most effective trust planning for state tax purposes is to establish the trust after the domicile change — taking advantage of Wyoming or Nevada trust law from a Wyoming or Nevada domicile.

Washington State: The Capital Gains Tax Trap

Washington deserves a specific warning. Washington has historically been one of the nine states with no income tax — a frequent destination for California and Oregon departures. In 2021, Washington enacted a 7% capital gains tax on long-term gains above $262,000 (2026, indexed), effective starting tax year 2022.

Bitcoin gains above the threshold are subject to Washington's 7% capital gains tax if the holder is a Washington domiciliary when the gain is realized. Washington is no longer a "zero tax" state for large Bitcoin gains — it is a "reduced tax" state. The 7% rate is significantly better than California's 13.3% or Oregon's 9.9%, but it is not zero. Holders specifically seeking zero state capital gains tax should prefer Wyoming, Nevada, Texas, Florida, or South Dakota over Washington.

The Part-Year Resident Allocation

In the year of a domicile change, you will file two state income tax returns: a part-year resident return in the old state, and either a full-year or part-year return in the new state. Each return covers income earned or realized while domiciled in that state.

Bitcoin gains realized after the domicile change date go on the new state's return. Bitcoin gains realized before the domicile change date go on the old state's return. The allocation is based on the date of the transaction — not where you physically were, and not how long you held the Bitcoin.

This is why timing the sale after the domicile change is essential. Even a single day's difference — selling Bitcoin on the day your domicile change is complete versus the day before — can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in state tax saved or lost.

Practical Timeline for a California → Wyoming Move

  1. Month 1: Identify Wyoming property; engage Wyoming attorney and tax advisor; open Wyoming bank accounts; establish Wyoming LLC (if desired for privacy/structure)
  2. Month 2: Purchase or lease Wyoming primary residence; physically move primary household items; obtain Wyoming driver's license; register to vote in Wyoming
  3. Month 3: Cancel California voter registration; transfer vehicle registrations to Wyoming; redirect mail and utilities; update address on all financial accounts
  4. Month 4–6: Establish Wyoming as primary presence — accumulate 183+ days in Wyoming; document days via credit card statements, receipts, utility bills; transfer professional relationships
  5. Month 6+: Consult with tax advisor to confirm domicile change is complete and documented; evaluate timing of Bitcoin sale; consider filing Declaration of Domicile in Wyoming
  6. Year-end: File final part-year California return; file Wyoming return (Wyoming has no income tax, but filing a federal return from a Wyoming address reinforces domicile)

Key Takeaways

  • State income tax on Bitcoin gains ranges from 0% (Wyoming, Nevada, Texas, Florida, South Dakota, Tennessee) to 13.3% (California) — a difference of up to $1.3M on a $10M gain.
  • Domicile, not residency, determines which state taxes your gain. Domicile is your permanent home — the state you intend to return to indefinitely. Multiple residences are possible; only one domicile exists at a time.
  • A domicile change requires concrete action: new primary home, driver's license, voter registration, banking, and professional relationships in the new state — plus spending fewer than 183 days in the old state.
  • California's FTB is the most aggressive state tax authority in the US. It audits departing residents with large gains, reviews travel records and credit card data, and pursues cases for up to four years after the return is filed.
  • New York's "permanent place of abode" rule can trap New Yorkers who keep even a small apartment in the state and spend more than 183 days there — regardless of their stated domicile.
  • The Bitcoin gain must be realized after the domicile change is complete to be taxed in the new state. The date of sale determines which state's tax applies — not where you were physically when you clicked sell.
  • Washington State now imposes a 7% capital gains tax on gains above $262K — it is no longer a zero-tax destination for large Bitcoin gains.
  • Trust planning does not substitute for domicile change for California or New York residents; trusts work best for state tax planning when established by a resident of a no-tax state.
