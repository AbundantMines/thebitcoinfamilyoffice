Bitcoin Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax: Why the $15M GST Exemption Is the Most Important Number in Multi-Generational Planning

By The Bitcoin Family Office  |  Updated March 2026  |  Multi-Generational Planning  •  Estate Tax

The estate tax takes 40% when wealth passes from parent to child. Most planners know this. What fewer appreciate is that the generation-skipping transfer tax (GSTT) applies an additional 40% when wealth passes from grandparent to grandchild — or to anyone more than one generation removed. Without proper planning, the same Bitcoin can be taxed three times across three generations: at 40% each time, leaving the great-grandchildren with less than 22 cents on the dollar. The GST exemption — $15M per person, $30M per couple in 2026 — is the mechanism that breaks this cycle. Allocated to a dynasty trust at funding, it shelters unlimited appreciation across unlimited generations from the GSTT forever.

Understanding the GSTT — how it works, who it applies to, and how to properly allocate the exemption — is not optional for Bitcoin families pursuing multi-generational wealth transfer. It is the difference between a dynasty trust that works and one that creates a catastrophic tax event for grandchildren decades from now.

What Is the Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax?

The GSTT was enacted in 1986 to close the "generation-skipping loophole" — the strategy of leaving assets in trust for grandchildren and great-grandchildren, bypassing the children's estates entirely to avoid estate tax at each generation. Congress responded by imposing a flat 40% tax (equal to the top estate tax rate) on any transfer that skips a generation.

The GSTT applies to three types of transfers:

  1. Direct skip: A transfer directly to a "skip person" — someone at least two generations below the transferor. The most common examples are a grandparent making a gift directly to a grandchild, or a grandparent's estate leaving assets directly to grandchildren. The transferor pays GSTT at the time of the transfer.
  2. Taxable termination: A trust interest held by a non-skip person (a child) terminates — due to death, lapse of a power, or otherwise — and after the termination only skip persons (grandchildren) have interests in the trust. GSTT is imposed at the time of termination on the value of the trust assets.
  3. Taxable distribution: A distribution from a trust to a skip person (grandchild or more remote descendant), when non-skip persons (children) also have interests in the trust. The skip person pays GSTT on the distribution.

The triple-tax scenario without planning: A $10M Bitcoin estate passes from grandparent to child (40% estate tax = $4M tax, $6M to child), then from child to grandchild (40% estate tax on $6M = $2.4M, $3.6M to grandchild), then from grandchild to great-grandchild (40% estate tax = $1.44M, $2.16M remains). Three generations of compounding 40% estate tax: $10M becomes $2.16M. Add GSTT at the grandchild level and the result is even worse.

Who Is a "Skip Person"?

The GSTT defines "skip persons" by generation assignment. The transferor is assigned a generation, and the transferee is "skipped" if they are two or more generations below the transferor.

For family members:

  • A grandchild is a skip person relative to a grandparent (two generations below)
  • A great-grandchild is a skip person (three generations below)
  • A child is a non-skip person (only one generation below)

For non-family members:

  • An unrelated person who is more than 37.5 years younger than the transferor is a skip person
  • An unrelated person 12.5–37.5 years younger is assigned to the transferor's children's generation (non-skip)

Trusts are skip persons if all interests in the trust are held by skip persons — typically a trust created solely for grandchildren or more remote descendants.

The "predeceased parent exception" applies when a child of the transferor predeceases the transferor — in that case, the grandchild "moves up" a generation and is treated as the transferor's child for GSTT purposes, preventing the grandchild from being taxed as a skip person for transfers that would otherwise have gone to the now-deceased parent.

The GST Exemption: $15 Million Per Person in 2026

Every US person has a lifetime GST exemption — a dollar amount of transfers that can be made to skip persons without triggering GSTT. Under the One Big Beautiful Budget Act (OBBBA) signed in 2026, the GST exemption was increased to $15,000,000 per individual (matching the estate and gift tax basic exclusion amount). Married couples can combine exemptions for a $30,000,000 total shelter.

The GST exemption is indexed for inflation after 2026.

When the GST exemption is allocated to a transfer or trust, the assets (and all future appreciation) pass to skip persons with zero GSTT. A $5M Bitcoin dynasty trust funded with full GST exemption today, worth $50M in 30 years, passes the entire $50M to grandchildren and great-grandchildren without any GSTT — ever.

The Inclusion Ratio: How GST Exemption Allocation Works

The "inclusion ratio" is the fraction of a trust's assets that are subject to GSTT. It is calculated as:

Inclusion ratio = 1 − (GST exemption allocated ÷ value of assets transferred)

An inclusion ratio of 0 means the entire trust is GST-exempt — no GSTT on any distributions or terminations. An inclusion ratio of 1 means the entire trust is subject to GSTT. A partial allocation creates a fractional inclusion ratio.

Practical examples:

  • Transfer $10M to dynasty trust; allocate $10M of GST exemption: inclusion ratio = 1 − (10/10) = 0. Fully exempt — no GSTT ever.
  • Transfer $10M to dynasty trust; allocate $5M of GST exemption: inclusion ratio = 1 − (5/10) = 0.5. 50% of distributions to skip persons subject to GSTT.
  • Transfer $10M to dynasty trust; allocate $0 of GST exemption: inclusion ratio = 1 − (0/10) = 1. All distributions to skip persons subject to GSTT at 40%.

The key planning insight: a partial inclusion ratio is administratively messy and creates ongoing GSTT exposure. Either fully exempt the trust (inclusion ratio 0) or accept the GSTT consequence. Do not leave a trust with a fractional inclusion ratio by accident.

Automatic Allocation Rules: What Happens If You Don't Elect

The GST exemption is allocated automatically in certain situations — and the automatic rules are not always what you want. Understanding them prevents expensive mistakes.

Automatic Allocation to Direct Skips

For outright gifts directly to skip persons (writing a check to a grandchild), the GST exemption is automatically allocated to the transfer up to the amount of the gift. No action required. The GST exemption is consumed, and the gift is exempt.

Automatic Allocation to "Indirect Skip" Trusts

For transfers to trusts that are not direct skips (because a non-skip person — a child — also has an interest), automatic allocation to the trust applies if the trust is an "indirect skip trust" — defined as a trust that could make distributions to skip persons during the term.

This is where accidental exemption consumption happens. If you fund a trust for your children that also permits distributions to grandchildren (a Crummey trust, an irrevocable trust for a broad class of descendants), GST exemption is automatically allocated to those transfers — even if you intended to save the exemption for larger, more deliberate transfers later.

⚠️ The Crummey trust GST trap. A classic Crummey trust used for life insurance funding often has grandchildren as potential beneficiaries (to include their Crummey withdrawal rights). Each annual gift to this trust may automatically consume GST exemption — depleting the lifetime $15M allocation on $19,000/year gifts before you've transferred the big Bitcoin position you actually wanted to shelter. Opt out of automatic allocation on Crummey trust gifts; reserve exemption for dynasty trust funding.

Opting Out of Automatic Allocation

A taxpayer can elect to not have GST exemption automatically allocated to a specific trust on Form 709 (Gift Tax Return). File Form 709 with the opt-out election, and the automatic allocation does not apply for that transfer. This is essential for preserving GST exemption for the dynasty trust funding while making annual gifts to Crummey trusts or similar vehicles.

When to Allocate GST Exemption to a Dynasty Trust

The best time to allocate GST exemption to a dynasty trust is at funding — when asset values are lowest and the allocation "buys" the most future appreciation. The same $15M of GST exemption applied to a $15M Bitcoin position today shelters $150M of Bitcoin value in 10 years if Bitcoin 10x's. Applied after appreciation occurs, $15M of exemption only shelters $15M of value.

This is the fundamental timing principle: allocate GST exemption when trust asset values are low; the exemption shelters all subsequent appreciation tax-free.

For Bitcoin specifically, this means:

  • Fund the dynasty trust during a Bitcoin price drawdown (current BTC at $70,406 — down from ATH)
  • Allocate full GST exemption at funding (Form 709 filed for the year of the gift)
  • All future Bitcoin appreciation — whether to $200K, $500K, or $1M per coin — passes through the trust to grandchildren and great-grandchildren with inclusion ratio 0 and zero GSTT

GST Exemption and GRATs: An Important Limitation

A GRAT — grantor retained annuity trust — is one of the most effective estate planning tools for Bitcoin, as covered in our GRAT strategy guide. However, GRATs have a specific GST limitation: under IRC §2642(f), GST exemption cannot be allocated to a GRAT until the GRAT term ends (the "estate tax inclusion period" rule).

This means that even if a GRAT successfully transfers $10M of Bitcoin appreciation to the remainder beneficiaries tax-free, that $10M arrives in the remainder trust without GST exemption attached — it will be subject to GSTT when distributed to or held for grandchildren.

The solution: the GRAT remainder trust should itself be a dynasty trust with GST exemption allocated at the time the GRAT term ends (when the GRAT is no longer included in the estate tax inclusion period). At that point, allocate GST exemption to the remainder trust based on its then-current value. This requires monitoring and timely election — it does not happen automatically.

The "Reverse QTIP Election" for Surviving Spouse Trusts

When a surviving spouse holds a QTIP trust (qualified terminable interest property trust), GST exemption normally cannot be allocated while the surviving spouse is alive — the QTIP inclusion in the surviving spouse's estate makes the assets part of the estate tax inclusion period. A "reverse QTIP election" under §2652(a)(3) allows the original transferor (the predeceased spouse) to be treated as the transferor for GST purposes, allowing GST exemption from the predeceased spouse's estate to be allocated to the QTIP trust.

For Bitcoin families with QTIP trusts, this election can preserve GST exemption from the first spouse's estate and apply it to the QTIP trust — allowing Bitcoin appreciation in the QTIP to pass to grandchildren without GSTT after the surviving spouse's death. It must be made on the predeceased spouse's estate tax return. Missing the election means the surviving spouse's estate is the GST transferor — potentially consuming more exemption or subjecting the trust to GSTT.

Dynasty Trust + GST Exemption: The Complete Structure

The optimal multi-generational Bitcoin transfer structure combines:

  1. Irrevocable dynasty trust in a no-rule-against-perpetuities state (South Dakota, Wyoming, Nevada, Delaware) — assets held in trust forever, outside every descendant's taxable estate
  2. Full GST exemption allocated at funding — inclusion ratio set to 0 on Form 709 for the year of the initial gift or transfer to the trust
  3. Bitcoin investment control retained via directed trust structure — ITD (investment trust director) directs the trustee on Bitcoin investment decisions without the trustee bearing fiduciary liability
  4. Trust protector — authority to adapt trust terms to changes in Bitcoin technology, custody practices, and law over decades
  5. Discretionary distribution provisions — distributions at trustee/distribution director discretion, shielding trust assets from beneficiaries' creditors and divorces

This structure, properly executed:

  • Removes Bitcoin from every descendant's taxable estate — no estate tax at each generation
  • Eliminates GSTT permanently via the inclusion ratio 0 — no 40% tax when grandchildren or great-grandchildren receive distributions
  • Protects Bitcoin from creditors and divorces at every generational level
  • Allows Bitcoin to compound for centuries without forced liquidation by any beneficiary

The compounding effect of permanent GST exemption: $15M of Bitcoin in a properly structured dynasty trust with inclusion ratio 0, growing at 15% annually for 50 years, becomes approximately $500M — passed entirely to the fifth generation without a single dollar of GSTT. The GST exemption does not "expire" — it protects the trust forever, on all future appreciation, not just the initial $15M transferred.

5-Step GST Exemption Action Plan

  1. Review existing trusts for inclusion ratio. Any irrevocable trust that might make distributions to grandchildren should have its inclusion ratio identified. If partial or full exemption was never allocated, consult with an estate planning attorney about whether allocation is still possible (it may be, using "late allocation" rules).
  2. Opt out of automatic allocation on Crummey trusts. If you are making annual gifts to trusts that include grandchildren as potential beneficiaries (even for Crummey withdrawal rights only), elect to opt out of automatic GST exemption allocation on Form 709. Reserve exemption for the dynasty trust.
  3. Fund the dynasty trust when Bitcoin values are low. The current price creates an opportunity to transfer Bitcoin to a dynasty trust at lower values, stretching the $15M exemption further. $15M of GST exemption buys more future protection when allocated at lower Bitcoin prices.
  4. Allocate full GST exemption at trust funding. File Form 709 for the year of the gift. Report the transfer and the GST exemption allocation — do not rely on automatic allocation for a trust that requires a deliberate exemption strategy.
  5. Monitor GRAT remainders for GST exemption allocation. When a GRAT term ends, the remainder trust exits the estate tax inclusion period. File Form 709 for that year to allocate GST exemption to the remainder trust based on its then-current value. Do not allow the GRAT's tax-free appreciation to arrive in a trust that is unprotected from GSTT.

Key Takeaways

  • The GSTT imposes a 40% flat tax on transfers to grandchildren or more remote descendants — on top of estate tax. Without GST exemption planning, the same Bitcoin can be taxed at 40% three times across three generations, leaving less than 22 cents per dollar.
  • The 2026 GST exemption is $15M per individual / $30M per married couple — indexed for inflation. Allocating the full exemption to a dynasty trust at funding sets the inclusion ratio to 0 and permanently eliminates GSTT on all future appreciation in that trust.
  • The inclusion ratio is the key number. Aim for inclusion ratio 0 on every dynasty trust — not 0.5, not 0.3. Partial allocations create ongoing administrative complexity and GSTT exposure at every distribution.
  • Automatic allocation rules can silently consume GST exemption on Crummey trust annual gifts. Opt out on Form 709 and reserve exemption for deliberate dynasty trust funding.
  • GRATs cannot receive GST exemption allocation until the GRAT term ends (estate tax inclusion period rule). Monitor GRAT remainder trusts and allocate exemption promptly when the term expires.
  • Fund dynasty trusts when Bitcoin prices are low — the same $15M of GST exemption shelters far more future value when allocated to a lower-priced position.
  • A dynasty trust is only complete when it has: (1) inclusion ratio 0, (2) directed trust investment control provisions, (3) trust protector for long-term governance, and (4) a no-RAP state situs. All four are required; the GST exemption allocation is the most easily overlooked.
