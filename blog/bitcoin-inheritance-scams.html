Bitcoin Inheritance Scams: How to Protect Yourself

The scams targeting Bitcoin heirs are sophisticated, emotionally calibrated, and devastating. Knowing the patterns before you need this information is the only real protection.

By Hal Turner February 25, 2026 10 min read

Financial fraud always finds its moment. Scammers who target Bitcoin heirs have found theirs: the period immediately following a death, when family members are grieving, unfamiliar with the technology they've inherited, and feeling the urgency to "do something" before the situation becomes more complicated.

Bitcoin inheritance scams are distinct from other financial fraud in two important ways. First, they exploit the specific confusion that comes with inheriting an asset most people don't fully understand — scammers position themselves as knowledgeable guides in a moment of genuine disorientation. Second, Bitcoin transactions are irreversible. Unlike a wire transfer to a bank, which may have a brief recall window, a Bitcoin transaction that leaves a wallet cannot be recalled, reversed, or recovered. Ever. The funds are simply gone.

The best protection against these scams is not technical sophistication — it is pattern recognition. If you can identify what these operations look like before you encounter one, you are almost impossible to victimize. This guide is that pattern recognition.

The Five Scams Targeting Bitcoin Heirs

Scam 1 of 5
Fake "Bitcoin Recovery" Services

These operations advertise themselves as services that help people regain access to locked or inaccessible Bitcoin wallets. They appear prominently in search results — often through paid advertising — and are designed to look professional, with testimonials, case studies, and polished websites.

Their method: they ask you to share your seed phrase, enter it into a website they provide, pay an upfront "recovery fee," or grant remote access to your computer so their "technicians" can "work on your wallet." In every case, the outcome is the same — the Bitcoin is transferred to them, and you have no recourse.

The reality: A seed phrase is mathematically the wallet. If you have the seed phrase, you can restore access yourself — or with the help of a legitimate, verifiable professional you have independently researched and engaged. If you don't have the seed phrase, no one can "recover" the funds, regardless of what they claim. There is no back door. There is no override. Recovery services that promise otherwise are lying.
Scam 2 of 5
Impersonators Posing as Estate Attorneys

This scam requires very little technical sophistication — only information and confidence. When a death occurs and estate proceedings become public record, scammers identify the deceased, research the family, and contact heirs directly — often by phone or email — claiming to be the attorney handling the estate.

They may know details gathered from public obituaries, probate records, or social media: the deceased's name, the family members involved, the approximate timeline. This specificity makes them seem credible. They request access to accounts, transfers of funds "to hold in escrow during probate," or sensitive documents to "process the estate."

The reality: Legitimate estate attorneys do not contact heirs cold. If you didn't retain them and they're calling you, verify before you say a word. Look up the firm's phone number independently — do not use a number they provided — call the firm directly, and confirm the attorney is who they claim to be. Any legitimate professional will welcome this caution.
Scam 3 of 5
Social Engineering After Obituaries

Obituaries are, by design, filled with personal information: the deceased's full name, surviving family members by name, dates, communities, affiliations, and relationships. To the people who love the deceased, this is a tribute. To a scammer, it is a targeting document.

Using information from an obituary, a scammer can cold-contact a family member with enough specific detail to appear legitimate — mentioning the deceased by name, referencing the right family members, using the right dates. They then build a relationship over days or weeks before making their ask: access to accounts, transfer of funds, or sensitive information about the estate's Bitcoin holdings.

The reality: The specificity of what they know about you is not proof of legitimacy — it is proof that obituaries are public. Treat any unsolicited contact related to an estate, no matter how detailed their knowledge, as unverified until you have confirmed their identity through independently sourced channels.
Scam 4 of 5
Fake Exchange "Transfer Assistance"

This scam targets heirs who believe the inherited Bitcoin is held on an exchange — Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, or others. The heir receives an email, text, or call from someone claiming to represent the exchange, informing them that the inherited account is "frozen," "under compliance review," or requires a "transfer fee" to release to the heir.

The fee is paid — sometimes hundreds, sometimes thousands of dollars, sometimes in Bitcoin itself. The "frozen" funds are never released. The fee disappears. The "representative" becomes unreachable.

The reality: Legitimate exchanges do not require fees paid in cryptocurrency to release inherited accounts. The process for inheriting exchange-held assets is administrative and legal — it involves death certificates, letters testamentary, and identity verification — not wire transfers or crypto payments. If you receive this contact, hang up and call the exchange directly using the number on their official website.
Scam 5 of 5
Phishing Wallets and Fake Interfaces

An heir who is unfamiliar with Bitcoin and trying to access inherited funds will often search for the wallet software used by the deceased. Scammers have built websites that mimic the interfaces of popular wallets — Electrum, Exodus, MetaMask, and others — specifically to appear in search results when confused or grieving heirs are looking for "how to access a [wallet name] wallet."

These sites ask the heir to "enter their recovery phrase to restore access." The moment the seed phrase is entered, the scammer's software uses it to sweep the wallet. The funds are gone within seconds — often before the heir realizes anything has gone wrong.

The reality: Only enter a seed phrase into hardware you physically own or software you have downloaded from a verified, official source — and only with guidance from a professional you have independently engaged. Never enter a seed phrase into a website found through a search engine. Never, under any circumstances, for any reason.

The Red Flags — Memorize These

Across all five of these scam categories, the same warning signals appear. These are not hypothetical cautions — they are the specific markers that distinguish fraudulent contact from legitimate professional engagement.

⚠ Red Flags — Stop and Verify
  • They contacted you first. You did not reach out to them. Legitimate professionals in Bitcoin custody, estate law, and financial services do not cold-contact grieving heirs. You find them — they don't find you.
  • Anyone asking for a seed phrase under any circumstance. There is no legitimate use case. Not for "verification." Not for "recovery." Not to "confirm ownership." A seed phrase request is a theft attempt, always.
  • Upfront fees to "release" or "unfreeze" funds. Inheritance processes involve legal fees, but they are paid to attorneys through standard invoicing — not to strangers via wire transfer or cryptocurrency.
  • Urgency and time pressure. "Your window to claim this is closing." "The account will be locked in 48 hours." "We need to act before the weekend." Real estate processes are slow. Scammers impose artificial deadlines to override your caution.
  • Inability to provide verifiable credentials. A legitimate attorney has a bar number you can look up. A legitimate financial professional has registrations you can verify. If they cannot provide these — or if the credentials don't check out when you look independently — trust your instinct.
  • Requests for cryptocurrency payments for services. No legitimate professional charges for services in Bitcoin. If someone asks you to pay in crypto, it is because crypto payments cannot be reversed when you discover the fraud.

What Legitimate Professionals Never Do

"The most powerful protection is slowness. A real professional will respect a 48-hour verification period. A scammer will not."

Understanding fraud patterns is valuable. Understanding legitimate professional behavior is equally important — because the contrast between the two is your clearest signal.

What Legitimate Professionals Never Do
  • They never ask for your seed phrase. Full stop. Under no circumstances. If a professional you have retained makes this request, they have either made a serious error or they are not who they claim to be.
  • They never request cryptocurrency payments for professional services. Attorney fees, CPA fees, and financial advisory fees are invoiced and paid through standard banking channels. Requests for crypto payments are a universal fraud signal.
  • They never contact you unsolicited after a family member's death. If you didn't find them — through a referral, a directory, or your own research — and they found you, start verifying before sharing anything.
  • They never promise guaranteed recovery of lost funds. If seed phrases are truly lost and no backup exists, the Bitcoin is inaccessible. This is a mathematical reality. Anyone who promises otherwise is misrepresenting what is possible in order to collect a fee.
  • They never pressure you to act before you are comfortable. Every legitimate attorney, CPA, and financial professional understands that Bitcoin inheritance is complex and deserving of care. If someone is pressuring you to move quickly, the pressure itself is the scam.

The 48-Hour Rule

If you take only one thing from this guide, take this: before you do anything — before you share information, transfer funds, pay a fee, or engage with any professional who has contacted you — wait 48 hours and verify independently.

Look up the firm's phone number on their official website. Call and ask to speak to the person who contacted you. Check their bar number or professional registration. Ask a trusted friend or family member to look at the situation with fresh eyes. Sleep on it.

A scammer will tell you there is no time for this. They will create urgency, manufacture consequences, and make waiting feel dangerous. A legitimate professional — an estate attorney who has worked with families for decades, a CPA who understands Bitcoin cost basis, a custody specialist you found through a referral — will have no difficulty with 48 hours. They will welcome your diligence. They will tell you to take your time.

That difference — the response to your request for time — is the most reliable test you have.
