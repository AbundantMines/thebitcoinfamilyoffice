What Is Bitcoin? A Plain-Language Guide for Heirs

Hal Turner  ·  February 2026  ·  7 min read

Home › Research › Heir Education › What Is Bitcoin? Est. 7 min read

Someone you loved held Bitcoin. Now, in some way, it may come to you. Before you do anything — before you touch a device, call a number, or make any decision — it helps to understand what you're actually dealing with.

This article is not an endorsement of Bitcoin. It is not investment advice. It is simply an honest explanation of what Bitcoin is, why thoughtful people choose to hold it, and what makes it fundamentally different from every other asset you might inherit. That understanding is the foundation for everything else.

The Simplest Accurate Description

Bitcoin is a fixed-supply digital bearer asset. Each of those words carries meaning.

Fixed supply means there will only ever be 21 million bitcoin in existence. This is enforced by the protocol — the code that runs Bitcoin — and cannot be changed by any government, company, or individual. New bitcoin are created through a process called mining, but the schedule is predetermined and the total is capped. This is different from government-issued currencies, which central banks can create in unlimited quantities.

Digital means Bitcoin exists entirely as entries on a shared ledger — called the blockchain — that is maintained simultaneously by tens of thousands of computers around the world. There is no physical coin. There is no central server. The ledger is public and verifiable by anyone.

Bearer asset is the most important phrase for heirs. A bearer asset is one where possession equals ownership. Physical cash is a bearer asset: whoever holds the bills controls the money. Gold is a bearer asset: whoever holds the metal holds the wealth. Bitcoin works the same way, but the "possession" is cryptographic rather than physical. Whoever holds the private key — the secret mathematical proof of ownership — controls the Bitcoin. There is no third party who can override this, reverse a transaction, or restore access to a lost key.

"Whoever holds the key controls the Bitcoin. No institution stands behind it to recover access or validate your claim."

Why Did Your Family Member Choose to Hold It?

People who hold Bitcoin as a long-term store of value — as opposed to trading it or speculating on price — generally do so for a specific set of reasons. Understanding those reasons helps you understand what they were trying to accomplish and why they cared so much about the custody details.

The most common reasoning goes something like this: all currencies held in bank accounts are subject to inflation, meaning their purchasing power erodes over time as governments and central banks create more money. Gold has historically served as a hedge against this erosion, but gold is difficult to store, transport, and verify. Bitcoin was designed to have similar properties — scarcity, durability, portability — but in a form that can be held with complete certainty of quantity, transmitted anywhere in the world, and verified by anyone without trusting a third party.

People who hold Bitcoin this way often think of it the way earlier generations thought of real estate or gold: something to hold for decades, not to trade frequently. They accepted the volatility as a feature of an early asset finding its place in the world, not as a sign of speculation. They were interested in what it might be worth to their children and grandchildren, not to themselves next year.

Why Long-Term Bitcoin Holders Think Differently About Volatility

Short-term price swings concern traders. Long-term holders focus on the properties that don't change: the fixed supply, the resistance to seizure, the ability to hold value without counterparty risk. Your family member likely didn't watch the price daily. They were focused on the decades-long picture — and on making sure you could access and protect what they'd built.

Bitcoin Is Not "Crypto"

You will hear the word "crypto" used as if it describes a single category of thing. It does not. There are thousands of cryptocurrencies, most of which were created to raise money, enable specific applications, or simply capitalize on speculation. Most have little or no fundamental utility. Many have failed. Most will.

Bitcoin is different in one critical way: it is the only cryptocurrency with a credible claim to fixed supply and genuine decentralization. Ethereum, Solana, and virtually every other cryptocurrency have teams, companies, or foundations that make decisions about the protocol. Bitcoin has no CEO, no company, and no one who can change the supply cap. Changes to Bitcoin require overwhelming consensus from tens of thousands of independent participants — a process that has, in practice, proven nearly impossible to coerce.

This does not mean Bitcoin is risk-free. It means that the specific risks of Bitcoin are different from the specific risks of other cryptocurrencies. Your family member almost certainly understood this distinction and chose Bitcoin deliberately. If they left you a hardware wallet or a seed phrase, it is almost certainly Bitcoin — not a diverse portfolio of digital tokens.

When you see news about a cryptocurrency exchange collapsing or a token losing 90% of its value, do not assume it applies to Bitcoin. The stories are usually about very different things.

Why It's Stored Differently From Stocks

When you inherit a brokerage account, the process is managed by the institution. You present a death certificate and letters testamentary. The institution verifies your identity, transfers the account, and generates new statements in your name. The securities are held in a system that is specifically designed to protect against unauthorized access and facilitate orderly transfers.

Bitcoin held in self-custody — meaning the owner held their own private keys rather than leaving them with an exchange — has no such system. The Bitcoin exists on the global ledger. Access is controlled entirely by the private key. If you have the key, you have access. If you don't, you don't. No one can intervene on your behalf.

This means your family member almost certainly stored something physical: either a hardware wallet (a small electronic device that holds the keys offline) or a written seed phrase (the 12 or 24 words that can regenerate all keys). These physical items are the inheritance. Without them, there is nothing to claim.

It also means the process of transferring the Bitcoin to you is a technical act, not an administrative one. You will not fill out a transfer-on-death form and wait for the institution to process it. You will — at the right time, with proper legal guidance — use the physical device or seed phrase to move the Bitcoin to an address you control. This is why the custody article in this series is so important, and why rushing is so dangerous.

What You Do Not Need to Decide Right Now

You do not need to decide whether you agree with your family member's decision to hold Bitcoin. You do not need to decide whether you want to keep it or sell it. You do not need to understand every technical detail before taking your first step.

What you need to understand, right now, is that this asset behaves like physical property in ways that most financial assets do not. It rewards patience and careful action. It punishes haste and mistakes. The person who left it to you was deliberate. The best thing you can do in their memory — and in your own interest — is to approach this with the same deliberateness.
