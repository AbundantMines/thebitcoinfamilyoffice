Someone you loved held Bitcoin. Now, in some way, it may come to you. Before you do anything — before you touch a device, call a number, or make any decision — it helps to understand what you're actually dealing with.

This article is not an endorsement of Bitcoin. It is not investment advice. It is simply an honest explanation of what Bitcoin is, why thoughtful people choose to hold it, and what makes it fundamentally different from every other asset you might inherit. That understanding is the foundation for everything else — the tax questions, the custody process, and the decisions you'll need to make.

The Simplest Accurate Description

Bitcoin is a fixed-supply digital bearer asset. Each of those words carries meaning.

Fixed supply means there will only ever be 21 million bitcoin in existence. This is enforced by the protocol — the code that runs Bitcoin — and cannot be changed by any government, company, or individual. New bitcoin are created through a process called mining, but the schedule is predetermined and the total is capped forever. This is fundamentally different from government-issued currencies, which central banks can create in unlimited quantities at will.

Digital means Bitcoin exists entirely as entries on a shared ledger — called the blockchain — maintained simultaneously by tens of thousands of computers around the world. There is no physical coin. There is no central server. The ledger is public and verifiable by anyone.

Bearer asset is the most important phrase for heirs. A bearer asset is one where possession equals ownership. Physical cash is a bearer asset: whoever holds the bills controls the money. Gold is a bearer asset: whoever holds the metal holds the wealth. Bitcoin works the same way, but the "possession" is cryptographic rather than physical. Whoever holds the private key — the secret mathematical proof of ownership — controls the Bitcoin. There is no third party who can override this, reverse a transaction, or restore access if the key is lost.

"Whoever holds the key controls the Bitcoin. No institution stands behind it to recover access or validate your claim."

Why Your Family Member Held It

People who hold Bitcoin as a long-term store of value — as opposed to trading it speculatively — generally do so for a specific set of reasons rooted in monetary history and economic philosophy. Understanding those reasons helps you understand what they were trying to accomplish and why they cared so much about the custody details.

The most common reasoning goes something like this: all currencies held in bank accounts lose purchasing power over time as governments and central banks create more money. This is inflation — measured in the United States at 3–8% per year in recent years, and historically higher in other countries. Over decades, inflation erodes wealth in ways that are invisible day-to-day but devastating over a lifetime.

Gold has historically served as a hedge against this erosion. But gold is difficult to store at scale, expensive to transport, hard to verify (counterfeit gold exists), and slow to move internationally. Bitcoin was designed to have similar scarcity properties — a fixed cap, no government control, no centralized issuer — but in a form that can be held with mathematical certainty of quantity, transmitted anywhere in the world within minutes, and verified by any computer on earth.

People who hold Bitcoin this way think of it the way prior generations thought of land or gold: something to hold for decades, not to trade weekly. They accepted Bitcoin's price volatility as a feature of an early asset establishing its place in the world — not as a sign they were speculating. Many were thinking explicitly about what it would be worth to their children and grandchildren. That is probably why you are reading this now.

Why Long-Term Bitcoin Holders Think Differently About Volatility

Short-term price swings concern traders. Long-term holders focus on the properties that don't change: the fixed supply, the resistance to seizure, the ability to hold value without counterparty risk. Your family member likely didn't watch the price daily. They were focused on the decades-long picture — and on making sure you could access and protect what they'd built.

Bitcoin Is Not "Crypto"

You will encounter the word "crypto" as if it describes a single category. It does not. There are thousands of cryptocurrencies, most created to raise money, enable specific applications, or simply capitalize on speculation. Most have little fundamental utility. Many have failed. Most will eventually fail.

Bitcoin is different in one critical way: it is the only cryptocurrency with a credible claim to fixed supply and genuine decentralization. Ethereum, Solana, and virtually every other cryptocurrency have teams, companies, or foundations that can make decisions about the protocol. Bitcoin has no CEO, no company, and no one who can change the 21-million cap. Changes to Bitcoin require overwhelming consensus from tens of thousands of independent participants worldwide — a process that has, in practice, proven nearly impossible to coerce or corrupt.

This does not mean Bitcoin is risk-free. It means the specific risks of Bitcoin are different from the specific risks of other cryptocurrencies. Your family member almost certainly understood this distinction and chose Bitcoin deliberately. When you see news about a cryptocurrency exchange collapsing or a token losing 90% of its value overnight, do not assume it applies to Bitcoin. The stories are usually about very different things with very different risk profiles.

If your family member left you a hardware wallet or a written seed phrase, it almost certainly holds Bitcoin — not a portfolio of different tokens. But confirm this early in the process.

Why It's Stored Differently From Stocks

When you inherit a brokerage account, the process is institutional. You present a death certificate and letters testamentary. The brokerage verifies your identity, transfers the account, and generates statements in your name. The securities are held in a custodial system specifically designed to facilitate orderly transfers and protect against unauthorized access.

Bitcoin held in self-custody — meaning your family member held their own private keys rather than leaving them at an exchange — has no such institutional system. The Bitcoin exists on the global ledger. Access is controlled entirely by the private key. If you have the key, you have access. If you don't, you don't. No institution can intervene on your behalf to restore access or validate your claim.

This means your family member almost certainly stored something physical: either a hardware wallet (a small electronic device that holds the keys offline, resembling a USB drive) or a written seed phrase (a set of 12 or 24 words that can regenerate all keys associated with that wallet). These physical items are the inheritance. Without them, there is nothing to claim.

It also means that transferring the Bitcoin to you is a technical act, not merely an administrative one. You will not fill out a transfer form and wait for an institution to process it. At the right time, with proper legal guidance, you will use the physical device or seed phrase to move the Bitcoin to an address you control. This is why the custody guide for heirs is the essential next article in this series.

What Bitcoin Is NOT: Five Common Misconceptions

Heirs frequently arrive with misconceptions about Bitcoin that lead to poor decisions. Understanding what Bitcoin is not is as important as understanding what it is.

1. Bitcoin is not stored "in a wallet" the way cash is stored in a wallet. The Bitcoin is on the blockchain — the global ledger. The "wallet" is just a device or software that holds the private key needed to authorize transactions involving your Bitcoin. If the wallet is destroyed, the Bitcoin is still on the blockchain. If you have the seed phrase, you can recreate access from any device. If you have neither, the Bitcoin is permanently inaccessible — not destroyed, but unspendable by anyone.

2. Bitcoin is not "attached" to an account that can be frozen or seized. Unlike a bank account, there is no account number associated with an identity in any central database. Bitcoin is controlled by a cryptographic key. Governments can compel exchanges to freeze accounts, but they cannot freeze Bitcoin held in self-custody unless they physically obtain the key.

3. Bitcoin transactions cannot be reversed. When Bitcoin is sent to an address, the transaction is permanent. There is no dispute process, no chargeback, no bank to call. This is why security is so critical: once Bitcoin is stolen or accidentally sent to the wrong address, it is gone.

4. Bitcoin is not anonymous. All transactions are visible on the public blockchain. Sophisticated analysis can often trace Bitcoin movements and associate them with identities. Your family member's transactions may be visible to anyone who knows their wallet addresses. This is a privacy consideration that may be relevant in the estate administration process.

5. Bitcoin is not "gone" if the hardware fails. The hardware wallet is just a key-holder. If the device fails but you have the seed phrase — the 12 or 24 words — you can access the Bitcoin from any compatible hardware or software wallet. The seed phrase is the master key to everything. Protect it accordingly.

Understanding the Price — Why It Goes Up and Down

Bitcoin's price is volatile by the standards of most assets most heirs encounter. Swings of 20–50% in a single year are normal. This can be alarming if you're not expecting it. Understanding why the price moves the way it does helps you avoid panic-driven decisions.

Bitcoin's price is determined entirely by supply and demand in global markets. The supply is fixed and known — approximately 21 million coins, with roughly 19.8 million already mined as of 2026. The demand fluctuates based on: macroeconomic conditions (inflation fears, currency crises, and financial instability typically increase Bitcoin demand), regulatory developments (new regulations or legal clarity affect institutional participation), institutional adoption (when major asset managers, pension funds, or corporations add Bitcoin, demand rises), and market sentiment (speculative cycles of fear and greed drive short-term price movements).

The long-term trend since Bitcoin's creation in 2009 has been dramatically upward — from fractions of a cent to over $100,000. But the path has included multiple 50–80% drawdowns, each followed by recovery to new all-time highs. Long-term holders generally view these drawdowns as the price of admission for an asset that no institution controls and no government can inflate away.

As an heir, the price on the day you inherit is not especially important in isolation. What matters is understanding that price volatility is a feature of this asset — not evidence that something is wrong with the specific Bitcoin you inherited. Your family member almost certainly knew this and held through previous volatility without selling.

Your Taxes as an Heir: The Step-Up Benefit

Here is the good news, and it is genuinely significant: when you inherit Bitcoin, you likely owe zero capital gains tax on all the appreciation that occurred during your family member's lifetime.

This is the "step-up in cost basis" under IRC Section 1014. When an asset passes through an estate at death, the heir receives a new cost basis equal to the fair market value on the date of death. Your family member may have bought Bitcoin at $500 per coin. At their death, it was worth $95,000 per coin. You inherit it with a cost basis of $95,000 per coin. The $94,500 of appreciation during their lifetime is permanently erased for capital gains tax purposes.

This means: if you sell the Bitcoin immediately after inheriting it, at the same price it was worth at death, you owe zero capital gains tax. If you hold it and it appreciates further, you only owe capital gains tax on the appreciation above your stepped-up basis ($95,000 in this example). And inherited assets are automatically treated as long-term capital gains assets regardless of how long you've held them — meaning you pay the lower 0–20% long-term rate, not the higher short-term rate, even if you sell one week after inheriting.

There is one important exception: Bitcoin held inside a traditional IRA does not receive a step-up in basis. IRA distributions are taxable as ordinary income. If your family member held Bitcoin in a Self-Directed IRA, consult a CPA before taking any distributions — the tax treatment is fundamentally different from directly held Bitcoin.

For a complete explanation of the step-up and how to document it, see our guide for heirs on the step-up in basis.

Important: Document the Bitcoin price on the exact date of death — this is your stepped-up cost basis. Screenshot the price from a major exchange (Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini). The IRS uses the average of the high and low price on the date of death. Save this documentation permanently.

Scam Warning: You Are a Target

The moment a Bitcoin inheritance becomes known, you may become a target for sophisticated fraud. This is not hypothetical — Bitcoin inheritance scams are well-documented and growing in frequency. Common patterns include:

Common scam patterns targeting Bitcoin heirs:

  • "Recovery services" that claim to retrieve lost Bitcoin: No legitimate service can access self-custodied Bitcoin without the private key or seed phrase. Anyone claiming otherwise is a scammer. Bitcoin recovery scams are one of the fastest-growing categories of cryptocurrency fraud.
  • Impersonators posing as the exchange or custodian: Scammers call or email claiming to be the exchange where the Bitcoin is held, asking for "verification" of account details or seed phrases. No legitimate exchange will ever ask for your seed phrase. Ever. For any reason.
  • Estate attorneys or advisors who claim unusual familiarity with "your family member's account": Anyone who reaches out to you proactively about the Bitcoin inheritance — rather than you finding them — should be treated with extreme skepticism.
  • Offers to "move the Bitcoin to a safe wallet" for you: Anyone who offers to handle the technical transfer for you, without being a previously established relationship of your family member, is likely attempting theft.
  • Social media or community contacts who knew your family member: Bitcoin forums, groups, and communities sometimes have members who identify and target grieving heirs.

The rule: never share a seed phrase with anyone, under any circumstances, for any reason. Not with an exchange. Not with an attorney. Not with a "recovery service." Not with a Bitcoin advisor. The seed phrase is the Bitcoin — whoever has it controls everything. Our full guide on Bitcoin inheritance scams covers every pattern and how to identify legitimate vs. fraudulent contacts.

The Emotional Dimension

It is worth saying plainly: you are probably reading this in the middle of grief, or in anticipation of it. The practical questions are real and pressing — but they are not the most important thing.

Your family member made a deliberate choice to hold Bitcoin. They thought carefully about it. They likely read extensively, understood the risks, and formed a considered view about why it was the right thing for their family. They took steps — however complete or incomplete — to make sure you could access it.

The fact that they left you something like Bitcoin — unconventional, complicated, potentially valuable — says something about how they thought about you. They believed you could handle it. They thought it might matter to your future, or your children's future. They didn't leave you a puzzle without a reason.

Take the time you need. Bitcoin has no expiration date. The price may move while you gather information and make decisions — that is unavoidable and acceptable. What matters most right now is that you do not make irreversible decisions under pressure, that you protect the physical custody devices and seed phrases from loss, and that you find advisors you genuinely trust before taking any technical action.

What Not to Do Right Now

  • Do not share the seed phrase with anyone. Not family members, not attorneys, not "helpers." The seed phrase is the entirety of the access. Keep it physically secure and tell only one trusted person where it is stored.
  • Do not attempt to move the Bitcoin before you understand the process. Technical mistakes — sending Bitcoin to the wrong address, using an untrusted computer, entering a seed phrase on a compromised device — result in permanent loss with no recourse.
  • Do not sell immediately out of fear or unfamiliarity. The step-up in basis means you owe zero capital gains tax on inherited appreciation. You have time. Make the decision with full information and proper tax advice.
  • Do not engage anyone who contacts you proactively about the Bitcoin. Legitimate professionals are found; they do not find you through social media or cold outreach.
  • Do not assume the Bitcoin is "safe" at an exchange indefinitely. Exchanges fail, freeze accounts, and impose withdrawal restrictions. If Bitcoin is on an exchange, understand the process for accessing it under the estate.

Your Next Steps

You do not need to understand every technical detail before taking your next step. Here is the right sequence:

  1. Secure the physical items: Find and physically secure any hardware wallets, seed phrase backups, and written notes your family member left about their Bitcoin. If you don't know where they are, that search is the first priority — before anything else.
  2. Document the date-of-death price: Find Bitcoin's price on the exact date of death from a major exchange. This is your stepped-up cost basis.
  3. Find the Letter of Instruction (if it exists): Your family member may have left a document explaining custody details, exchange accounts, and who to contact. Check with the estate attorney and look through their papers and home safe.
  4. Consult a Bitcoin-literate attorney and CPA: Before touching anything technically, get the legal and tax picture clear. The step-up benefit is valuable; do not inadvertently compromise it through hasty action.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should an heir do first when they inherit Bitcoin?

Do nothing irreversible for at least 30 days. Don't sell, move, or guess passwords. Contact the estate attorney, locate hardware wallets and seed phrases, and read the Letter of Instruction if one exists. Scammers specifically target Bitcoin heirs — be skeptical of anyone contacting you claiming to help recover or manage Bitcoin.

Is inherited Bitcoin taxable?

Inherited Bitcoin in a taxable account receives a step-up in cost basis to FMV at the date of death — all prior capital gains are eliminated. Inherit Bitcoin worth $500K and sell immediately: zero capital gains on prior appreciation. Any gain after the date of death is taxable using the stepped-up basis.

How do I access Bitcoin I inherited?

Depends on how it was held: Exchange accounts — contact the exchange with death certificate and legal authority. Self-custody hardware wallets — you need the seed phrase; without it, access may be permanently impossible. Multisig — need the quorum of keys and wallet software instructions. Start with estate documents and any Letter of Instruction before taking any other steps.

Why is Bitcoin stored differently from other assets?

Bitcoin ownership is determined by cryptographic private keys, not account records at a financial institution. The private key IS ownership. A will or trust establishes your legal right to the Bitcoin; the seed phrase or exchange credentials give you the actual ability to access it. Both are required — legal right without technical access gets you nowhere.
