Bitcoin Mining Tax Strategy: Section 179, Bonus Depreciation, and Entity Structure

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  12 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Of all the ways to acquire Bitcoin, mining may have the most favorable tax treatment — if structured correctly. The depreciation rules that apply to business equipment, when applied to ASIC mining hardware, create a mechanism for acquiring Bitcoin with pre-tax dollars and converting ordinary income into a capital asset with a known cost basis. This is not a tax loophole. It is the straightforward application of long-standing business tax principles to a new type of business asset.

But "favorable tax treatment" is conditional. The advantages are real only for taxpayers in the right income profile, with the right entity structure, who understand how the multiple layers of tax treatment interact with each other. Structured incorrectly, mining can create tax complexity with limited benefit — or inadvertently trigger adverse consequences through misclassified income, incorrect basis tracking, or self-employment tax exposure that wasn't anticipated.

This article explains the tax mechanics of Bitcoin mining from first principles: how mining income is taxed, how the depreciation rules work, how entity structure affects the outcome, and how mining operations fit within broader family office tax planning frameworks.

How Bitcoin Mining Income Is Taxed

The IRS treats Bitcoin as property, not currency. This classification has specific consequences that are different from both traditional asset purchases and traditional business income.

When miners produce Bitcoin, the IRS takes the position — established in Revenue Ruling 2023-14 and consistent with prior guidance — that mined Bitcoin is gross income at the time of receipt, valued at its fair market value on the date received. This is ordinary income, not capital gains. The distinction matters significantly.

If you purchase Bitcoin on an exchange, no taxable event occurs at acquisition. Your cost basis equals the purchase price, and you owe no tax until you dispose of the Bitcoin. If you mine Bitcoin, the fair market value at the time of receipt is recognized as ordinary income immediately — regardless of whether you sell the Bitcoin or hold it. You owe ordinary income tax on that value in the year the Bitcoin is mined, even if the Bitcoin is still in your wallet at year-end.

This creates a specific tax planning requirement: miners must track the fair market value of every unit of Bitcoin on the day it is received, recognize that value as income, and establish cost basis records accordingly. For operations producing Bitcoin daily, this requires systematic tracking infrastructure — either accounting software integrated with pricing APIs or a robust manual process. Inadequate records create both tax risk and audit exposure.

Practical Note on Mining Income Timing

Bitcoin mining income typically accrues through pool mining, where small amounts of Bitcoin are credited to your pool account continuously as your machines contribute to solving blocks. The taxable event is when the Bitcoin is credited to you — not when you withdraw it from the pool. Many miners inadvertently defer income tracking to the withdrawal date, which creates a recordkeeping mismatch with the IRS's position. Use cost basis tracking software that records pool credits at the time they are received.

The Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation Advantage

The favorable tax treatment of Bitcoin mining comes primarily from the capital expenditure side, not the income side. ASIC mining hardware qualifies as five-year property under MACRS (the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System), which would normally allow 20% annual depreciation over a five-year recovery period. Two provisions accelerate this significantly:

Section 179 of the Internal Revenue Code allows a business to elect to deduct the full cost of qualifying business equipment in the year it is placed in service, rather than depreciating it over its MACRS recovery period. For tax year 2025, the Section 179 deduction limit is $1,160,000, with a phase-out beginning at $2,890,000 of total equipment placed in service. ASIC miners purchased for an active mining business clearly qualify as Section 179 property. A business that purchases $600,000 of ASIC miners in 2025 and places them in service before December 31 can deduct the entire $600,000 against business income in 2025.

Bonus depreciation operated similarly under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, which allowed 100% first-year bonus depreciation for qualified property placed in service between September 27, 2017 and December 31, 2022. Bonus depreciation has been phasing down: 80% in 2023, 60% in 2024, 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026, and zero thereafter under current law — though extension or reinstatement remains legislatively possible. Bonus depreciation differs from Section 179 in that it can create a net operating loss, while Section 179 generally cannot exceed business income.

The combined effect of these provisions creates what practitioners refer to as "negative-basis" Bitcoin acquisition. Consider the structure:

  • Year 1: Purchase $500,000 of ASIC miners. Deduct $500,000 against ordinary income (via Section 179 or bonus depreciation). Net tax position: significant tax reduction in year one.
  • Years 1-5: Miners produce Bitcoin. Each Bitcoin received is ordinary income at fair market value, with that value establishing cost basis. But the miners themselves have been fully expensed — there is no remaining depreciable basis in the hardware.
  • Year 5: Sell miners for salvage value. This creates ordinary income (the hardware was already fully deducted; any sale proceeds recapture the deduction).

The Bitcoin produced throughout this period accumulates with a cost basis equal to its fair market value at time of mining — which is itself ordinary income. But the full purchase cost of the machinery that produced that Bitcoin was already deducted in year one. The effective cost of the Bitcoin, from a net-of-tax perspective, is substantially below its face acquisition cost on the market.

The equipment deduction converts pre-tax dollars into mining capacity. That capacity then produces Bitcoin with a cost basis set by market value at time of receipt — a fundamentally different economic structure than market acquisition.

Entity Structure for Mining Operations

The entity structure for a Bitcoin mining operation affects the self-employment tax, state tax exposure, income pass-through characteristics, and how the operation interacts with the family's broader legal and tax architecture. There is no universal answer — the optimal structure depends on scale, income profile, and the family's existing structure.

Sole proprietorship / single-member LLC (disregarded entity). The simplest structure. All mining income flows directly to the individual's Schedule C, subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on the first $160,200 of net earnings in 2024, 2.9% above that). Self-employment tax applies to net mining income — revenue minus deductible expenses, but before the depreciation that may zero out the income in year one. In subsequent years when the hardware is no longer depreciable, the full net mining income is subject to SE tax. This structure is appropriate for very small operations or initial testing, but carries meaningful SE tax exposure at scale.

S-Corporation. An S-Corp allows the owner to split compensation between a reasonable salary (subject to payroll taxes) and a distribution (not subject to SE tax). For a mining operation producing significant income, this can meaningfully reduce SE tax relative to sole proprietorship operation. The owner-operator receives a salary commensurate with the services actually performed, with remaining profits distributed without payroll tax exposure. S-Corps have restrictions on ownership structure (limited to 100 shareholders, no foreign shareholders, no non-individual shareholders) that may conflict with complex family trust structures.

C-Corporation. For very large mining operations where substantial reinvestment is planned, a C-Corp may be advantageous. Mining income retained in the corporation is taxed at the 21% flat corporate rate — significantly below the top individual ordinary income rate for high earners. Retained earnings can be reinvested in hardware purchases with the favorable depreciation treatment at the corporate level. The disadvantage is double taxation when profits are ultimately distributed as dividends. C-Corp structure is most logical when the intention is to grow the mining operation rather than extract cash flow, and when the scale justifies the additional administrative complexity.

Wyoming LLC with appropriate tax election. Wyoming LLCs are often the preferred legal entity for Bitcoin mining operations, independent of the tax election. Wyoming's LLC statutes provide strong charging order protection, clear digital asset property rights, and business-friendly administration. The Wyoming LLC can be taxed as a disregarded entity, partnership, S-Corp, or C-Corp depending on the election made — the legal entity and the tax classification are separate decisions. For most family office mining operations, a Wyoming LLC taxed as an S-Corp combines the legal advantages of Wyoming's entity law with the SE tax efficiency of S-Corp status. Cost Basis on Mined Bitcoin

Basis tracking for mined Bitcoin is operationally demanding and legally significant. Getting it wrong creates both tax risk and audit exposure.

The rule is straightforward in principle: the fair market value of Bitcoin at the time it is received as mining income equals both the amount of ordinary income recognized and the cost basis established in that Bitcoin. When the Bitcoin is subsequently sold or disposed of, the gain or loss is calculated from that basis — not from zero.

In practice, this means maintaining records of:

  • The date and time each Bitcoin (or fraction of Bitcoin) was received as mining income
  • The fair market value of Bitcoin at that specific time (typically the closing price on a major exchange, or a time-weighted average price)
  • The ordinary income recognized (which equals the fair market value)
  • The cost basis established (which also equals the fair market value)

The holding period for capital gains purposes begins on the date the Bitcoin is mined — not on any subsequent date. Bitcoin held for more than one year from the mining date qualifies for long-term capital gains treatment upon sale. Bitcoin sold within one year of mining is short-term capital gains — taxed at ordinary income rates, which creates no net tax benefit from holding vs. immediate sale.

The practical implication: the long-term tax optimization strategy for mined Bitcoin is to hold it for at least one year from each production date before disposal, converting future appreciation from ordinary income treatment to long-term capital gains treatment. The combination of ordinary income treatment at mining and long-term capital gains treatment on subsequent appreciation is one of the most favorable tax structures available for Bitcoin accumulation.

Electricity and Operating Expenses as Deductible Business Costs

In addition to hardware depreciation, all legitimate operating expenses of the mining business are deductible against mining income. For a hosted mining arrangement, this primarily means:

  • Hosting fees: The per-kWh or flat monthly fees paid to the hosting facility are ordinary and necessary business expenses, fully deductible.
  • Management fees: If you engage a manager or consultant to oversee the mining operation, their fees are deductible.
  • Insurance: Equipment insurance premiums for the mining hardware are deductible business expenses.
  • Professional services: Accounting, legal, and tax advisory costs attributable to the mining operation are deductible.

For self-operated mining (where the family controls the physical infrastructure), electricity costs are the largest operating expense and fully deductible. At scale, the difference between $0.04/kWh and $0.07/kWh power can be the difference between a profitable operation and a break-even one — and all of those electricity costs flow through as business deductions.

The Interaction with Family Office Structures

Bitcoin mining operations exist within a broader family legal and tax architecture, and the way they interact with that architecture requires careful planning.

Mining LLC within a family structure. A mining LLC is typically owned by the family's operating structure — either directly by the individual or through a holding entity. The relationship between the mining LLC and any existing trusts, holding companies, or investment entities requires deliberate design. Passive activity rules may limit the deductibility of mining losses against non-mining income for certain taxpayers, though miners who materially participate in the business can typically treat mining as an active trade or business.

Can a family trust own a mining LLC? Yes, with important qualifications. A grantor trust (including many common estate planning trusts) is treated as the grantor for income tax purposes — so mining income flows to the grantor's individual return, and the grantor's participation may satisfy the material participation test. Non-grantor trusts are taxed as separate entities, with their own tax rates and their own material participation analysis. For mining LLCs held through trust structures, the legal and tax analysis must be done specifically, not by analogy to simpler arrangements. This is an area where qualified professional counsel is not optional.

Self-directed IRA mining. Self-directed IRAs can, in theory, own a mining LLC — but the rules governing prohibited transactions, UBTI (unrelated business taxable income), and the specific requirements for IRA-owned operating businesses make this a complex, high-risk structure that should only be pursued with specialized ERISA and tax counsel. Mined Bitcoin in an IRA would be subject to UBTI tax at trust rates, eliminating much of the tax advantage. We mention it only to note that it exists and that the implementation complexity is substantial.

Structuring from the Start

Bitcoin mining's tax advantages are real. The combination of first-year equipment deductions, ongoing operating expense deductibility, and the cost basis treatment that converts subsequent appreciation to long-term capital gains creates a tax profile that is genuinely superior to market acquisition for the right taxpayer profile.

But those advantages require proper structure from the beginning. Retroactive restructuring of a mining operation — changing entity type, reallocating ownership, or changing the tax accounting method after operations have begun — introduces complexity and risk that proper initial planning avoids entirely. The entity structure, basis tracking methodology, and tax accounting elections should all be in place before the first machine is deployed.
