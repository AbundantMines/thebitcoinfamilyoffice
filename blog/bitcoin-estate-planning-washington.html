Bitcoin Estate Planning in Washington State

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Washington State is often described as a no-income-tax state, and technically that's accurate — Washington has no personal income tax. But Bitcoin families in Washington face two significant state-level tax issues that many residents don't fully appreciate until they've realized a large gain or passed away with a significant estate: a 7% capital gains tax on gains above $262,000, and a state estate tax with rates reaching 20% on large estates. Understanding these two taxes — and the planning strategies that address them — is essential for Bitcoin wealth in Washington.

Washington's Capital Gains Tax: The 2022 Surprise

In 2022, Washington enacted a 7% excise tax on long-term capital gains exceeding $262,000 (adjusted annually for inflation) — officially characterized as an "excise tax on the sale or exchange of capital assets" rather than an income tax, in an effort to avoid the state constitution's prohibition on graduated income taxes. The Washington Supreme Court upheld the tax in Quinn v. State of Washington (2023), rejecting challenges under the state and federal constitutions.

For Bitcoin families in Washington, the capital gains excise tax (codified at RCW Chapter 82.87) applies to net long-term capital gains above the annual threshold. The $262,000 threshold (approximately, as adjusted) sounds significant but is modest relative to any meaningful Bitcoin position. A holder selling $500,000 of Bitcoin at a $400,000 long-term gain pays 7% on approximately $138,000 — roughly $9,660 in Washington state tax on top of federal tax. For larger realizations, the tax is more significant.

Several exemptions exist: gains from real estate transfers are exempt (Washington has a separate Real Estate Excise Tax). Gains from sales of small businesses (under $6 million gross revenue) have an exemption. Retirement account distributions are exempt. But for Bitcoin gains specifically, no exemption exists — 7% applies to net long-term gains above the threshold for Washington residents.

Washington calls it an "excise tax on capital asset sales" rather than an income tax — but for Bitcoin families realizing large gains, the 7% above $262K lands the same way regardless of the label.

Washington State Estate Tax: Rates Up to 20%

Washington imposes a standalone state estate tax (RCW Chapter 83.100) with an exemption of $2,193,000 (2024, indexed for inflation) and rates ranging from 10% to 20% on taxable amounts above the exemption. Unlike the federal estate tax exemption of $13.99 million, Washington's $2.19 million threshold catches a much broader range of Bitcoin families — any family whose total estate exceeds that threshold faces Washington estate tax.

The Washington estate tax has no federal state death tax credit offset (the federal credit was eliminated in 2005), so Washington families pay both federal estate tax (if the estate exceeds the federal threshold) and Washington estate tax (if the estate exceeds $2.19 million) independently. For a Bitcoin family with a $5 million estate entirely below the federal threshold, there is no federal estate tax but potentially $660,000 or more in Washington state estate tax.

The Washington estate tax applies to all property included in the federal gross estate under IRC §2031, including Bitcoin held by the decedent or in revocable trusts. Washington-specific deductions include the marital deduction (passing to a surviving spouse eliminates Washington estate tax at first death) and deductions for Washington qualified family-owned business interests — though Bitcoin held in an LLC or operating entity may potentially qualify if structured appropriately.

Strategies to Address Washington Estate Tax

  • Marital deduction planning: Maximize the marital deduction by ensuring Bitcoin passes to a surviving spouse at first death — through a revocable trust or direct bequest — deferring Washington estate tax until the second death. At second death, estate equalization planning, gifting, and irrevocable trust funding can reduce the taxable estate.
  • Annual exclusion gifting: Washington does not have a gift tax. Gifts of Bitcoin during lifetime are not subject to Washington estate tax — they reduce the gross estate dollar for dollar. Systematic gifting programs ($18,000 per recipient annually, or larger amounts using the federal lifetime exemption) reduce the Washington taxable estate.
  • Irrevocable trust funding: GRATs, SLATs, and dynasty trusts funded with Bitcoin during lifetime remove those assets (and future appreciation) from the Washington gross estate. Washington has no gift tax, making lifetime giving particularly valuable.
  • Charitable giving: Charitable gifts reduce the Washington taxable estate. Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) can allow Bitcoin to be sold without immediate capital gains recognition while providing an income stream and charitable deduction that reduces the taxable estate.

Washington Trust Act (RCW Chapter 11.98)

Washington's Trust and Estate Dispute Resolution Act and the Washington Trust Act (RCW Chapter 11.98) provide the statutory framework for trust administration in the state. Washington has directed trust provisions that allow trust instruments to assign investment direction authority to parties other than the trustee — relevant for Bitcoin trusts where custody expertise and administrative competence need to be separated.

Washington is not among the leading trust situs states — it doesn't have Wyoming's DPTA, Nevada's asset protection trust statute, or South Dakota's dynasty trust depth. However, for Washington-resident Bitcoin families with modest estates below the federal threshold, a Washington-sited revocable trust that avoids probate and clearly specifies Bitcoin custody succession is a reasonable starting point.

For larger estates or families seeking the most favorable trust law, a Wyoming or Nevada trust situs provides significant advantages. Washington's capital gains tax does not impose a residency-based rule for trusts — trust income is not subject to the Washington capital gains excise tax if the trust is not a Washington-resident trust, providing some planning flexibility for families willing to establish an out-of-state trust situs.

Washington LLCs and Community Property

Washington is a community property state (RCW §26.16.030). Bitcoin purchased with community funds during marriage is community property, providing the same double step-up benefit at first death as Texas, California, and other community property states — both halves of the community estate receive a stepped-up basis at the decedent's death under IRC §1014(b)(6). For Washington families with large, long-held Bitcoin positions, this step-up can be worth more than the estate planning benefit of an irrevocable trust that forfeits it.

Washington LLCs (RCW Chapter 25.15) provide standard charging order protection for multi-member LLCs. As with Colorado, Wyoming or Nevada LLC formation provides stronger protection for families who want maximum creditor shielding for their Bitcoin holding entity.

Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
