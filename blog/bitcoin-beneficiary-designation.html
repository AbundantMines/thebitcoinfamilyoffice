Bitcoin Beneficiary Designation: What Works, What Doesn't, and What to Use Instead

Hal Franklin February 2026 Updated 2026-02-27 ~20 min read

You can name a beneficiary on your 401(k), your life insurance policy, and your brokerage account. Many Bitcoin holders assume the same simple designation applies to their Bitcoin — and discover too late that the reality is far more complicated. Whether a beneficiary designation works for your Bitcoin depends entirely on where and how you hold it. Here is the complete guide to what works, what doesn't, and what actually protects your heirs.

The Short Answer: It Depends on Custody Structure

Whether a beneficiary designation works for your Bitcoin depends entirely on where and how your Bitcoin is held. The mechanism is radically different across custody types.

Custody Type Beneficiary Designation? Avoids Probate? Best Succession Mechanism
Major exchange (Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini) Limited / varies Possibly, if true TOD Register account in trust name; or true TOD if available
Hardware wallet (self-custody) No Not unless held in trust Revocable living trust + Letter of Instruction
Software wallet (self-custody) No Not unless held in trust Revocable living trust + Letter of Instruction
Revocable living trust Yes — governed by trust terms Yes This is the succession mechanism
Bitcoin SDIRA (Self-Directed IRA) Yes — standard IRA beneficiary form Yes IRA beneficiary form; 10-year rule applies to most heirs
Bitcoin Roth IRA Yes — standard IRA beneficiary form Yes IRA beneficiary form; 10-year rule applies; distributions tax-free

The fundamental reason self-custody Bitcoin has no beneficiary designation mechanism: the Bitcoin blockchain responds only to cryptographic signatures from whoever holds the private keys. There is no institution to receive or honor a legal designation — no bank to call, no account manager to verify your heir's identity. The Bitcoin is controlled by whoever has the keys, period.

Exchange-Held Bitcoin: The TOD Question

A Transfer-on-Death (TOD) designation allows assets to pass directly to a named beneficiary at death, bypassing probate. Traditional brokerage accounts routinely support TOD. Bitcoin exchanges have been slower to build equivalent functionality — and those that offer succession processes often do not offer true TOD.

The distinction between a true TOD and a "deceased account process" matters enormously. A true TOD designation automatically transfers the account to the named beneficiary with minimal documentation — similar to how a brokerage TOD works. A "deceased account process" requires your executor to contact the exchange, provide a death certificate, letters testamentary or successor trustee documentation, and wait weeks to months for processing. It is effectively just estate administration by another name.

Before relying on an exchange's succession process as part of your estate plan, contact the exchange directly and ask two questions: "Does my account support a TOD beneficiary designation that passes outside probate?" and "What exact documentation will my heirs need to access this account after my death?" The answers determine whether the exchange account is part of your estate plan or simply another asset your executor will need to claim through the normal process.

Exchange risk: Even if your exchange offers a beneficiary designation or succession process today, exchange policies change. Exchanges are acquired, reorganized, or shut down. A revocable trust or will should always serve as a backstop, independent of any exchange's succession process. Do not build your Bitcoin estate plan on the assumption that any given exchange policy will remain stable.

Self-Custody Bitcoin: No Designation Mechanism Exists

If your Bitcoin is held in a hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor, Coldcard, Passport) or a software wallet (Electrum, Sparrow, Bitcoin Core), no beneficiary designation mechanism exists. The blockchain does not know who you are, who your heirs are, or what your legal wishes are. It responds only to valid cryptographic signatures from whoever controls the private keys.

When you die with self-custodied Bitcoin, the succession works entirely through two documents: your estate planning instruments (will or trust) and your Letter of Instruction.

  • Your will or revocable trust gives your executor or successor trustee the legal authority to manage and transfer the Bitcoin on behalf of your estate or trust
  • Your Letter of Instruction tells them where the hardware wallet is located, where the seed phrase backup is stored, what the passphrase is (if any), and how your multisig arrangement works (if applicable)
  • The executor or trustee uses both documents to access and transfer the Bitcoin to heirs per your wishes

Without a Letter of Instruction, even a perfect will is functionally insufficient for self-custodied Bitcoin. The executor has legal authority but no operational access — the Bitcoin is inaccessible. This is why the LOI is as important as the trust document for Bitcoin holders.

The Revocable Trust: The Best "Beneficiary Designation" for Bitcoin

For self-custodied Bitcoin, the closest functional equivalent to a beneficiary designation is a revocable living trust. Here is how it works and why it outperforms a simple designation for most holders.

You create a revocable trust, name yourself as the initial trustee, and document your Bitcoin holdings in the trust's Letter of Instruction. At your death, your named successor trustee steps in immediately — without court involvement, without probate, without a public disclosure of your holdings. The successor trustee has legal authority to manage the Bitcoin per the trust terms, and the LOI provides operational access.

Advantages over a simple exchange beneficiary designation

A simple beneficiary designation on an exchange account passes the Bitcoin to one (or more) named individuals at death — immediately and outright, with full control, no conditions. A revocable trust is significantly more flexible:

  • Conditional distribution: You can specify that Bitcoin distributes to your adult children at 25, 30, and 35 in thirds — rather than an 18-year-old receiving $500,000 in Bitcoin outright
  • Bitcoin-literate trustee oversight: Your successor trustee can manage the Bitcoin with professional guidance rather than leaving a potentially untechnical heir to manage custody and selling decisions alone
  • Creditor and divorce protection: Bitcoin held in a discretionary trust is protected from a beneficiary's creditors and from equitable distribution in a divorce — something an outright designation cannot provide
  • Unified succession for all Bitcoin: The trust governs both self-custodied Bitcoin and exchange-held Bitcoin (if exchange accounts are registered in the trust's name) under a single set of instructions
  • Step-up in basis preserved: Bitcoin in a revocable trust at your death receives a full step-up in basis to the date-of-death fair market value — the same benefit as directly held Bitcoin

IRA-Held Bitcoin: Beneficiary Designations Work — With Tradeoffs

If your Bitcoin is held inside a Self-Directed IRA (SDIRA), the IRA does have a standard beneficiary designation form — and it works. When you die, the named beneficiary inherits the IRA outside of probate, directly from the IRA custodian. This is straightforward and reliable.

But the IRA beneficiary designation comes with significant tax tradeoffs that make it different from a simple brokerage account designation:

No step-up in basis

Bitcoin held outside an IRA receives a step-up in cost basis at death — erasing all embedded capital gains permanently. Bitcoin inside an IRA receives no step-up. Every dollar distributed from a traditional SDIRA is taxable as ordinary income at the heir's marginal rate, regardless of how long the Bitcoin was held or how much it appreciated. For large positions with decades of appreciation, the IRA structure can be significantly less tax-efficient at death than direct ownership.

The 10-year rule

Under the SECURE Act 2.0, most non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit a traditional IRA must distribute the entire account within 10 years of the original owner's death. For an inherited traditional Bitcoin SDIRA, this means the heir must take distributions — and pay ordinary income tax — over a 10-year window, regardless of market conditions. If Bitcoin doubles in years 3–8 of the distribution window, the heir faces escalating ordinary income tax bills as they withdraw at increasingly higher values.

Surviving spouses have more flexibility: they can roll the inherited IRA into their own IRA, restarting the distribution clock based on their own age. Spouses also have the option to treat the inherited IRA as their own, potentially deferring distributions much longer.

Roth IRA is better for beneficiaries

An inherited Roth IRA (including a Roth SDIRA holding Bitcoin) is also subject to the 10-year rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries — but distributions are tax-free if the Roth has been held for at least 5 years. This makes Roth SDIRA Bitcoin significantly more favorable for heirs than traditional SDIRA Bitcoin. Converting to Roth before death — even partially, during low-income years or Bitcoin price corrections — can substantially reduce the tax burden your heirs face.

Per Stirpes vs. Per Capita: Which Designation Structure to Use

When naming multiple beneficiaries, the designation structure — per stirpes or per capita — determines what happens if one of your named beneficiaries predeceases you. This choice is critical and frequently misunderstood.

Per stirpes ("by the branch") means that if a named beneficiary predeceases you, their share passes to their descendants. If you name your three children equally per stirpes, and one child dies before you leaving two grandchildren, those grandchildren split their parent's share equally. The total distribution remains within your family tree.

Per capita ("by the head") means that if a named beneficiary predeceases you, their share is redistributed equally among the surviving named beneficiaries. If you name your three children equally per capita and one dies, the two surviving children split the full account equally. The deceased child's descendants receive nothing.

Recommendation for most Bitcoin holders: Use per stirpes. It ensures that Bitcoin stays within your family tree even if a beneficiary predeceases you — and prevents the potentially large per-capita windfall that surviving siblings would receive at the expense of a deceased sibling's children. Per stirpes is the standard recommendation of most estate attorneys for primary beneficiary designations.

Per capita is sometimes appropriate when you specifically want only living named individuals to benefit — for example, when naming charities or non-family members where "descendants" is not meaningful. For family beneficiary designations on Bitcoin assets, per stirpes is almost always the right choice.

Primary and Contingent Beneficiaries

All beneficiary designation forms — exchange accounts (where available) and IRAs — distinguish between primary and contingent beneficiaries. Primary beneficiaries receive the asset first. Contingent beneficiaries receive only if all primary beneficiaries predecease the account holder.

For Bitcoin holders, always designate both primary and contingent beneficiaries. Without a contingent designation, if all primary beneficiaries predecease you, the asset falls back into your estate — goes through probate, and distributes according to your will (or intestacy law if you have no will). All the probate-avoidance benefit of the beneficiary designation is lost.

Common contingent beneficiary structures for Bitcoin holders:

  • Children as primary, grandchildren per stirpes as contingent: Most common multi-generational structure
  • Spouse as primary, children per stirpes as contingent: Standard spousal first structure — simple and appropriate for most married holders
  • Revocable trust as primary or contingent: For maximum flexibility — the trust governs distribution per your trust terms (see below)

Naming Minors as Beneficiaries: Special Rules

Naming a minor child directly as a Bitcoin beneficiary — on an exchange account or IRA — creates significant legal complications that most holders do not anticipate. Minors cannot legally own property in most states. If a minor inherits Bitcoin outright, a court must appoint a guardian of the property (a "property guardian" or "conservator") to manage the Bitcoin on the minor's behalf until they reach the age of majority (18 in most states, 21 in some). This conservatorship process is:

  • Court-supervised — requiring ongoing court approval for major decisions about the Bitcoin
  • Expensive — legal fees and court costs can be substantial
  • Inflexible — the conservator must distribute all assets to the child at the age of majority, regardless of the child's financial maturity
  • Potentially damaging — an 18-year-old receiving $200,000+ in Bitcoin outright is a well-documented recipe for poor outcomes

The solutions, in order of preference:

  • Name a revocable trust as beneficiary: Your trust holds the Bitcoin for the minor's benefit with distribution terms you control (age-based, discretionary, etc.) — no court involvement
  • Name a testamentary trust as beneficiary: A trust created by your will that comes into existence at your death; slightly more complex but avoids outright distributions to minors
  • UTMA/UGMA custodianship: Some states allow a custodian under the Uniform Transfers to Minors Act to manage inherited assets for a minor. Less flexible than a trust — all assets distribute at 18 or 21 with no conditions — but simpler to establish
  • Do not name minors directly on exchange accounts or IRAs without professional legal guidance

Why Naming Your Estate Is Almost Always a Mistake

Some Bitcoin holders, uncertain about whom to name, designate "my estate" as the beneficiary of an exchange account or IRA. This is almost always the wrong choice, for three reasons:

  1. Probate is guaranteed: If your estate is the beneficiary, the Bitcoin must go through probate before distributing to heirs. You lose the probate-avoidance benefit entirely — the most significant practical benefit of a beneficiary designation.
  2. IRA creditor protection is lost: IRAs with individual or trust beneficiaries receive creditor protection in many states. IRAs payable to an estate lose that protection — all IRA assets become available to your estate's creditors.
  3. Tax acceleration for IRAs: When an estate is the beneficiary of a traditional IRA, the entire IRA must often be distributed within 5 years (not 10), accelerating the tax bill significantly.

If you are uncertain whom to name as beneficiary, name your revocable trust as the contingent beneficiary as a backstop. An estate attorney can help you identify the optimal primary and contingent beneficiary structure for your specific situation.

Spousal Designations in Community Property vs. Common Law States

In community property states (California, Texas, Arizona, Washington, Nevada, Idaho, Louisiana, New Mexico, Wisconsin), special rules govern how you can name beneficiaries on community property assets — including Bitcoin acquired during marriage that is community property.

In community property states, both spouses have equal ownership interests in community property. You generally cannot designate a non-spouse beneficiary for your half of community property without your spouse's written consent. In Texas, for example, a spouse must execute a written consent to allow community property to pass to a non-spouse beneficiary — even on an IRA.

In common law states (including Florida, New York, and most other states), spouses do not automatically co-own separately titled assets. Bitcoin held in your name alone is generally your separate property, and you can name any beneficiary without spousal consent — subject to your state's elective share statute (which allows a surviving spouse to claim a minimum percentage of your estate regardless of your designations).

For Bitcoin holders who have relocated between community property and common law states, the community property character of assets acquired in a community property state generally follows the assets to the new state — requiring specific attention to whether spousal consent is needed for beneficiary designations on those assets.

Naming Charities as Bitcoin Beneficiaries

For Bitcoin holders who are charitably inclined, naming a qualified charity as a beneficiary of Bitcoin assets is one of the most tax-efficient giving strategies available. The charity receives the Bitcoin at full fair market value, pays no capital gains tax on the appreciation, and the donor's estate receives a charitable deduction for the full FMV — reducing estate taxes.

For exchange-held Bitcoin with a beneficiary designation: name the charity directly (or through a donor-advised fund sponsor like Schwab Charitable, Fidelity Charitable, or a Bitcoin-focused DAF like The Giving Block) as a primary or partial beneficiary. When the charity receives the Bitcoin, no capital gains tax is triggered — the entire appreciated value goes to the charitable purpose.

For IRA-held Bitcoin: naming a charity as IRA beneficiary is particularly effective. An IRA payable to a charity escapes both income tax and estate tax — 100% of the IRA passes to the charitable purpose. By contrast, an IRA payable to individual heirs is subject to ordinary income tax on every distribution. The optimal strategy for charitably inclined Bitcoin holders: name charities as IRA beneficiaries (where income tax applies), and leave directly held Bitcoin to individual heirs (where the step-up eliminates capital gains).

Naming Your Trust as Beneficiary

Naming your revocable living trust as beneficiary of exchange accounts and IRAs is a powerful strategy that gives your trust document's distribution instructions control over all Bitcoin assets — creating a single, unified succession plan rather than fragmented designations across multiple accounts.

For exchange accounts with beneficiary designations: naming the trust is straightforward. At your death, the exchange transfers the Bitcoin to the trust; your successor trustee manages it per the trust terms.

For IRAs: naming a trust as IRA beneficiary is more complex. For the trust to qualify as a "see-through trust" — allowing the IRA to use the individual beneficiaries' life expectancies for distribution purposes — the trust must meet specific IRS requirements under Treasury Regulation §1.401(a)(9)-4. The key requirements: the trust must be valid under state law, irrevocable at death, the beneficiaries must be identifiable from the trust document, and the trustee must provide a copy of the trust document (or a list of beneficiaries) to the IRA custodian by October 31 of the year following death. Work with a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney and CPA before naming a trust as IRA beneficiary — the rules are technical and the penalties for non-compliance are significant.

Annual Review: The Most Neglected Step

Beneficiary designation errors are the most common estate planning mistake — and the most preventable. The error almost always occurs not when the designation is first made, but when life changes and the designation is not updated.

Life changes that require a beneficiary designation review:

  • Marriage or divorce: A divorce does not automatically revoke an ex-spouse's beneficiary designation in all states. In some states, a divorce revokes the designation by operation of law; in others, the ex-spouse inherits if not explicitly removed.
  • Birth or adoption of children: Children born after a designation was made are not automatically included as beneficiaries unless the designation uses "my children, per stirpes" language that sweeps in future children
  • Death of a named beneficiary: If a primary beneficiary predeceases you and you haven't updated the designation, the contingent beneficiary (or your estate, if no contingent was named) receives the Bitcoin
  • New Bitcoin holdings: A new exchange account requires a new beneficiary designation; new self-custody holdings require an updated Letter of Instruction
  • Changes in exchange policy: Exchanges update their succession policies; verify annually that your exchange still offers the succession mechanism you are relying on
  • Trust amendments: If you amend your revocable trust, verify that the trust still qualifies for any see-through trust benefits and that the amendment doesn't create conflicts with existing beneficiary designations

Action Checklist: What to Do This Week

  • Inventory all Bitcoin custody locations: Exchange accounts, hardware wallets, software wallets, SDIRAs. List each and its current succession status.
  • Contact each exchange: Ask whether your account supports a beneficiary designation or TOD that passes outside probate. Get the answer in writing or documented from their support team.
  • Update or establish exchange beneficiary designations: Where available, name your revocable trust as beneficiary (or name individuals per stirpes, with contingent beneficiaries).
  • Create a revocable living trust: If you hold self-custodied Bitcoin and do not have a revocable trust, engage a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney. This is your primary succession mechanism.
  • Write or update your Letter of Instruction: Ensure it covers all current wallet locations, custody configurations, and access protocols — and is updated whenever your custody arrangement changes.
  • Confirm SDIRA beneficiary designations: Log into your SDIRA custodian's portal or call them to verify current primary and contingent beneficiary designations. Update if needed.
  • Evaluate per stirpes vs. per capita: Confirm your existing designations use per stirpes if family continuity is your goal. Ask your attorney to review.
  • Review minor beneficiary designations: If any named beneficiary is or may become a minor, restructure to route Bitcoin through a trust rather than outright to the minor.
  • Remove "my estate" as any beneficiary: Replace with trust or named individuals. Never let Bitcoin default to your estate unless probate is intentional.
  • Set an annual calendar reminder: Review all beneficiary designations each year and after any major life event. This is the most important maintenance action in Bitcoin estate planning.
