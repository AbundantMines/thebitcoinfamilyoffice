Bitcoin Beneficiary Designation: What Works, What Doesn't, and What to Use Instead

By Hal Turner February 25, 2026 ~2,100 words

You can name a beneficiary on your 401(k). You can name one on your Bitcoin Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust policy. Many Bitcoin holders assume the same simple designation process applies to their Bitcoin — and discover too late that the reality is far more complicated. Here is what beneficiary designations actually do and don't do for Bitcoin, and what options genuinely work.

The Short Answer: It Depends on Where Your Bitcoin Is

Whether you can name a beneficiary for your Bitcoin depends entirely on custody structure. Bitcoin held on an exchange (custodial) operates under that exchange's rules and policies. Bitcoin held in self-custody (hardware wallet, software wallet) has no institutional mechanism for beneficiary designations at all — the blockchain responds only to private keys, not to legal designations.

Exchange-Held Bitcoin: The TOD Question

A Transfer-on-Death (TOD) designation allows assets to pass directly to named beneficiaries at death, bypassing probate. Brokerage accounts routinely offer TOD. Most Bitcoin exchanges do not — at least not in the same seamless form.

The distinction matters: a true TOD designation automatically transfers the account to the named beneficiary with minimal documentation. A "deceased account process" requires your executor to contact the exchange, provide a death certificate, Letters Testamentary or successor trustee documentation, and wait weeks to months for processing — effectively the same as any other asset going through estate administration.

Before relying on an exchange's succession process as your plan, contact the exchange directly and ask: "Does my account have a beneficiary designation that passes outside of probate?" and "What documentation will my heirs need to access this account?" The answers will determine whether an exchange account is part of your estate plan or simply an asset your executor will need to claim.

⚠ Exchange Risk: Even if your exchange offers a beneficiary designation or simplified succession process today, that policy can change. Exchanges are acquired, reorganized, or shut down. Relying solely on exchange succession as your Bitcoin estate plan is inherently fragile. A revocable trust or will should always be in place as a backstop.

Self-Custody Bitcoin: No Designation Mechanism Exists

If your Bitcoin is in a hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor, Coldcard) or a software wallet (Electrum, Sparrow, Bitcoin Core), there is no institution to receive or enforce a beneficiary designation. The blockchain does not know your name. It does not know who your heirs are. It responds only to valid cryptographic signatures from whoever holds the private keys.

When you die with self-custodied Bitcoin, the succession works entirely through your estate documents and your Letter of Instruction:

  1. Your will or trust gives the legal authority to your executor or successor trustee
  2. Your Letter of Instruction tells them where the hardware wallet is, where the seed phrase backup is, and any passphrase details
  3. Your executor uses both documents to access and transfer the Bitcoin to heirs

Without a Letter of Instruction, even a perfect will is insufficient — the executor has legal authority but no operational access. This is why the LOI is as important as the will for Bitcoin holders.

The Revocable Trust: The Best "Beneficiary Designation" for Bitcoin

For self-custodied Bitcoin, the closest functional equivalent to a beneficiary designation is a revocable living trust. Here's why it works better than trying to name beneficiaries through other mechanisms:

How the trust functions as a succession mechanism

You create a revocable trust, name yourself as trustee, and fund it with your assets (including documenting your Bitcoin custody in the trust's Letter of Instruction). At death, you become incapacitated, or choose to step down — your named successor trustee steps in immediately. No court involvement. No probate. The successor trustee has legal authority to act on Bitcoin-related decisions per the trust terms, and the LOI tells them operationally how to access the Bitcoin.

Advantages over a simple beneficiary designation

A beneficiary designation passes assets to one (or more) named people at death. A trust is more flexible: you can specify conditions for distribution (age Bitcoin family office minimum requirementss for children, instructions to hold Bitcoin long-term rather than immediately liquidate, per-stirpes provisions if a beneficiary predeceases you), assign a Bitcoin-literate trustee to oversee the administration, and protect the Bitcoin from an heir's creditors or a bad divorce settlement.

Avoiding the "outright to heirs" problem

A pure beneficiary designation gives the heir the Bitcoin outright — immediately, with full control, no conditions. For a teenager or young adult inheriting significant Bitcoin, this can be catastrophic. A trust allows you to specify: "distribute 25% at age 25, 25% at age 30, remainder at age 35" or "hold Bitcoin; only distribute gains" — instructions a simple beneficiary designation cannot encode.

IRA-Held Bitcoin: Beneficiary Designations Work — But With Tradeoffs

If your Bitcoin is held inside a Self-Directed IRA (SDIRA) via a Bitcoin SDIRA custodian, the IRA does have a beneficiary designation — the standard IRA beneficiary form. This designation passes the IRA outside probate directly to the named beneficiary. This works. But it comes with significant tax tradeoffs:

No step-up in basis inside an IRA

Direct Bitcoin (outside an IRA) receives a step-up in cost basis at death — your heir's basis is the date-of-death value, eliminating all capital gains. Bitcoin inside an IRA receives no step-up. Every dollar distributed from the IRA is taxed as ordinary income at the heir's marginal rate. For large Bitcoin positions with decades of appreciation, the IRA structure may actually be less tax-efficient at death than direct ownership.

The 10-year rule for non-spouse heirs

Under the SECURE Act, most non-spouse heirs must fully distribute (and pay income tax on) inherited IRA assets within 10 years of the original owner's death. For a Roth IRA Bitcoin position, this is less punitive (Roth distributions are tax-free). For a traditional IRA Bitcoin position, 10 years of forced distributions can create enormous tax bills for heirs — especially if Bitcoin appreciates significantly during the distribution period.

The "Name Your Trust as Beneficiary" Strategy

For exchange accounts that do offer a beneficiary designation, one effective approach is to name your revocable trust as the beneficiary rather than an individual. This means:

  • The exchange account passes to your trust (outside probate, if the exchange honors the designation)
  • The trust distributes per your trust terms (with all the flexibility and protection a trust provides)
  • One plan governs all your Bitcoin — exchange and self-custody alike

Note: naming a trust as IRA beneficiary is more complex (see a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney and CPA before doing this — there are specific trust qualification requirements for IRA beneficiaries, known as "see-through trust" rules).

What to Do This Week

The practical takeaway from all of this:

  1. Self-custody Bitcoin: Create a revocable living trust (work with a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney) and write a Letter of Instruction. This is your "beneficiary designation" equivalent.
  2. Exchange Bitcoin: Contact each exchange and ask specifically whether they offer TOD beneficiary designations or successor account policies. Update or add designations. Then name your trust as beneficiary if you have one.
  3. Bitcoin SDIRA: Confirm your beneficiary designation is current, understand the 10-year rule implications, and consult a Bitcoin CPA on optimal distribution strategy for heirs.
  4. Review annually: Custody arrangements change. Exchanges update policies. New wallets get created. The Letter of Instruction is only useful if it's current.
