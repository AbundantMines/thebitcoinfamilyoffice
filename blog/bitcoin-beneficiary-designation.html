Bitcoin Beneficiary Designation: The Form That Overrides Your Will

Your beneficiary designation on a Bitcoin IRA or exchange account is a legally binding contract that overrides everything in your will. Name your spouse correctly and they avoid RMDs for years. Name a trust incorrectly and your heirs lose up to 37% of the IRA to ordinary income tax in 10 years. Leave it blank and the Bitcoin goes to your estate — triggering probate, delays, and potentially the worst tax outcome of all.

By The Bitcoin Family Office Research Team  ·  Updated March 2026  ·  13 min read

Most Bitcoin estate planning conversations focus on wills, trusts, and tax strategies. The beneficiary designation form — the simple one-page document you fill out when you open an IRA or exchange account — gets treated as an afterthought. That's a mistake.

Beneficiary designations are not part of your estate. They pass outside of probate, outside the will, and outside the trust — directly to the named beneficiary at death. This is powerful when done correctly: immediate transfer, no probate delay, no public court record. It is catastrophic when done incorrectly: an ex-spouse named 20 years ago inherits your IRA. A blank form sends your Bitcoin IRA through probate with your estate. A trust named without the right IRS language forces a 5-year payout and 37% ordinary income tax on assets that could have stretched 10 years.

This guide covers every type of Bitcoin account where beneficiary designations matter, who to name in each situation, the specific language required for trust beneficiaries, and the common mistakes that destroy Bitcoin inheritance value.

Where Beneficiary Designations Apply for Bitcoin

Not all Bitcoin accounts have beneficiary designations. Understanding which do — and which pass through your estate or trust instead — is the starting point:

The Hierarchy: Primary vs. Contingent Beneficiaries

Every account with a beneficiary designation should have both a primary beneficiary and a contingent beneficiary:

  • Primary beneficiary: Receives the account first. If they predecease you, the contingent beneficiary receives it.
  • Contingent beneficiary: Receives the account if the primary beneficiary is deceased or disclaims the inheritance. The backup.
  • Per stirpes vs. per capita: If naming multiple beneficiaries, specify whether a deceased beneficiary's share passes to their children (per stirpes) or is divided among surviving named beneficiaries (per capita). Per stirpes is almost always the right choice for families.
⚠️ No Beneficiary = Probate + Worst Tax Outcome

If you die with no beneficiary named on an IRA, the account passes to your estate. Your estate is not a "designated beneficiary" under IRS rules. The IRA must be fully distributed within 5 years of death (or by December 31 of the year containing the 5th anniversary). Five years of forced distributions instead of 10 years under the SECURE Act — each year's distribution taxed as ordinary income. Plus probate delays, court supervision, and public record. A blank beneficiary form is the worst possible outcome.

Naming Your Spouse: The Best Bitcoin IRA Beneficiary

For married Bitcoin holders with traditional IRAs, a surviving spouse as primary beneficiary is almost always optimal. Here's why:

Spousal Rollover — The Most Powerful IRA Tool

A spouse who inherits an IRA can roll it into their own IRA — treating it as if they had owned it all along. This provides:

  • RMD deferral: The surviving spouse uses their own age to calculate RMDs, not the deceased spouse's age. A 60-year-old surviving spouse doesn't need to take RMDs until age 73 under current law — potentially 13 years of continued tax-deferred compounding.
  • Flexibility: The surviving spouse can name new beneficiaries on the rolled-over IRA, allowing the family to update designations based on current family circumstances (new grandchildren, changed relationships)
  • 10-year rule avoidance: Spouses are "eligible designated beneficiaries" — they are NOT subject to the SECURE Act's 10-year mandatory distribution rule that applies to most adult children

When NOT to Name Your Spouse as Primary

If your estate is above the federal exemption ($15M individual, $30M married under 2026 OBBBA), naming your spouse as primary may not be optimal. Adding the IRA to an already-taxable estate may create estate tax exposure at the second death. In this case, consider naming a bypass trust or a charitable remainder trust as primary beneficiary to remove the IRA from the taxable estate. This is a high-net-worth exception — for most families, spouse as primary beneficiary is correct.

Naming Children as Beneficiaries: The 10-Year Rule Trap

Under the SECURE Act (2019) and SECURE 2.0 (2022), most non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit an IRA must withdraw the entire balance within 10 years of the original owner's death. This applies to adult children — the most common non-spouse beneficiary.

The 10-year rule doesn't require equal distributions each year — but the entire IRA must be empty by December 31 of the 10th year following death. For a large Bitcoin IRA, this means:

  • 10 years of ordinary income recognition on withdrawals, regardless of Bitcoin's performance
  • If Bitcoin appreciates significantly during the 10-year window, later distributions push heirs into higher brackets
  • No ability to "stretch" distributions beyond 10 years (the pre-2020 "stretch IRA" strategy is gone for most beneficiaries)

The exception: "Eligible designated beneficiaries" — surviving spouses, minor children (until age of majority), disabled individuals, chronically ill individuals, and individuals not more than 10 years younger than the deceased — can still use the old life expectancy stretch rules. Adult children over 10 years younger than the deceased are subject to the 10-year rule.

Naming Minor Children: Handle With Care

A minor child named as IRA beneficiary can use the life expectancy stretch — but only until they reach the age of majority. After that, the 10-year rule kicks in. For Bitcoin IRAs, this means a complicated transition at majority: the minor's guardian manages distributions until majority, then the adult child must clear the entire IRA within 10 years. For most families, naming a trust as beneficiary (with the trust distributing to the child per the trustee's discretion) is preferable to naming minor children directly.

Naming a Trust as IRA Beneficiary: The Right Way and the Wrong Way

Many Bitcoin families want to name a trust as their IRA beneficiary — for control over distributions, spendthrift protection, or to ensure the IRA doesn't fuel destructive behavior in an heir. This is possible, but the trust must be structured correctly to avoid collapsing the tax benefits.

The "See-Through Trust" Requirements

For a trust named as IRA beneficiary to use the same distribution rules as if individual beneficiaries were named directly, the trust must be a "see-through trust" (also called a "look-through trust") meeting four IRS requirements:

  1. The trust must be valid under state law
  2. The trust must be irrevocable at the owner's death
  3. The trust's beneficiaries must be identifiable from the trust document
  4. A copy of the trust must be provided to the IRA custodian by October 31 of the year following the owner's death

If these requirements are met, the IRS looks through the trust to the individual beneficiaries to determine the distribution period. The oldest beneficiary determines the applicable distribution period.

Conduit Trust vs. Accumulation Trust

Trust Type How It Works Tax Treatment Asset Protection Best For
Conduit Trust IRA distributions must pass through immediately to individual beneficiaries — trust cannot accumulate IRA income Distributions taxed at beneficiary's individual rates — often lower than trust rates Weak — assets leave trust immediately on distribution Financially responsible adult heirs; lower tax bracket beneficiaries
Accumulation Trust Trust can retain IRA distributions rather than passing them out immediately Retained distributions taxed at trust rates — which reach 37% at just $15,650 of income (2026). Devastating for large distributions. Strong — assets remain in trust, protected from creditors, divorce, poor decisions Beneficiaries with creditor exposure, divorce risk, or spending concerns; but tax cost is severe
⚠️ The Accumulation Trust Tax Trap

An accumulation trust that retains IRA distributions pays tax at trust rates. Trusts hit the 37% bracket at just $15,650 of income (2026). A $500,000 IRA distribution accumulated in a trust pays approximately $185,000 in federal income tax. The same distribution made to an individual child earning $150,000 would be taxed at their marginal rate — potentially much lower. For Bitcoin IRAs, accumulation trusts are generally a poor choice unless the beneficiary's personal income tax rate is high enough that trust rates are no worse.

The CRT as IRA Beneficiary

For charitably inclined Bitcoin families, naming a Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) as the IRA beneficiary is one of the most powerful strategies available. Here's why:

  • The CRT is a tax-exempt entity — it receives the IRA distribution without paying income tax
  • The CRT pays an annuity to the named family beneficiaries over the trust term
  • The income from the CRT is taxable as distributions are made — but spread over many years at typically lower rates
  • At the CRT's termination, the remainder passes to the named charity
  • Net result: a "charitable stretch" that effectively eliminates the 10-year rule's brutal compression effect

This strategy works particularly well for Bitcoin IRAs where the holder is charitably inclined and the family does not need full access to the IRA balance immediately. See the full mechanics: Bitcoin Charitable Remainder Trust Guide.

Exchange Account Beneficiary Designations

Most Bitcoin exchange accounts do not have beneficiary designation features — meaning Bitcoin on exchanges passes through your estate (probate) at death unless it's titled to a trust. This is one of the strongest arguments for either:

  1. Moving exchange Bitcoin to self-custody within a revocable trust, or
  2. Using exchanges that do offer beneficiary designation or Transfer on Death (TOD) registration

Exchanges With Beneficiary / TOD Features (as of 2026)

  • Coinbase: Offers a "Legacy Contact" feature for some users — not a full TOD but allows a trusted contact to initiate account recovery. Not equivalent to a legal beneficiary designation.
  • Fidelity Crypto: As part of Fidelity's broader brokerage account, may offer TOD registration in some cases — check account settings.
  • Kraken, Gemini, Binance.US: No formal beneficiary designation as of 2026. Family must present estate documents (letters testamentary or trust certification) to access accounts at death.

For Bitcoin on exchanges without TOD/beneficiary features, the cleanest solution is to title the exchange account to your revocable trust while you're alive. The exchange account is then governed by the trust at death — no probate, no beneficiary form required. This requires the exchange to accept trust-owned accounts (most major exchanges do with proper documentation).

TOD Registration for Non-IRA Brokerage Bitcoin

For Bitcoin ETF holdings (IBIT, FBTC) in a regular taxable brokerage account, Transfer on Death (TOD) registration is the simplest beneficiary mechanism. TOD registration:

  • Names beneficiaries who receive the account directly at death, bypassing probate
  • The beneficiary receives the Bitcoin ETF shares with a stepped-up basis (unlike IRA beneficiaries who face IRD)
  • Available at virtually all major brokerages (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, TD Ameritrade)
  • Can be changed at any time while alive
  • Overrides the will for the specific account

Note: TOD registration on a non-IRA account does not prevent the assets from being included in your taxable estate for estate tax purposes — it only avoids probate. If estate tax is a concern, the assets should be in a trust rather than subject to TOD.

Seven Beneficiary Designation Mistakes That Destroy Bitcoin Inheritance Value

Mistake 1: Naming a Minor Child Directly

A minor child cannot legally receive an IRA distribution. The court appoints a guardian to manage the inherited IRA on the child's behalf until majority — adding court supervision, costs, and inflexibility. Name a trust for the child's benefit instead.

Mistake 2: Never Updating After Life Events

Divorce, remarriage, births, deaths — any of these should trigger an immediate beneficiary designation review. An ex-spouse named 15 years ago still inherits if the form was never changed. Most states have a revocation-on-divorce statute for wills — but many of those statutes explicitly do NOT apply to beneficiary designations on retirement accounts. Federal law (ERISA) preempts state divorce revocation rules for 401(k)s.

Mistake 3: Naming "My Estate" as Beneficiary

Never name your estate as an IRA beneficiary. The estate is not a designated beneficiary, so the 10-year rule does not apply — the IRA must be distributed within 5 years. Plus, the IRA goes through probate. Plus, the estate is subject to creditors' claims on the IRA before it reaches heirs. Naming a trust is almost always preferable to naming the estate.

Mistake 4: Naming a Trust Without IRS See-Through Compliance

A trust that doesn't meet the four see-through requirements is treated as having no designated beneficiary — triggering the 5-year payout rule. Verify that your trust was specifically drafted for IRA beneficiary purposes, with the required provisions for conduit or accumulation treatment.

Mistake 5: Not Naming a Contingent Beneficiary

If the primary beneficiary predeceases you and there's no contingent, the account passes to your estate — probate, 5-year rule, creditor exposure. Always name a contingent.

Mistake 6: Naming Equal Shares Without Considering Tax Brackets

If you have multiple children in vastly different income tax brackets, leaving equal shares of a traditional IRA may not be the most tax-efficient outcome. A child in the 12% bracket keeps much more of an IRA distribution than a child in the 37% bracket. Consider whether directing the IRA primarily to lower-bracket beneficiaries — while leaving other assets to higher-bracket ones — produces better after-tax results for the family overall.

Mistake 7: Forgetting the ERISA Spousal Consent Requirement

For 401(k) plans (including Solo 401(k)s), ERISA requires that a married participant obtain their spouse's written, notarized consent before naming anyone other than the spouse as primary beneficiary. This rule does not apply to IRAs — IRA owners can name anyone. Failing to obtain spousal consent on a 401(k) designation makes the designation invalid, and the spouse inherits regardless of what the form says.

The Annual Beneficiary Designation Audit

Beneficiary designations degrade over time. A designation that was correct five years ago may be wrong today. The annual audit takes 30 minutes and should check:

  1. Every IRA and 401(k): Log in, find the beneficiary section, confirm current designations match intent. Print or screenshot for your letter of instruction.
  2. Every brokerage account: Confirm TOD registration is active and named correctly. If no TOD — consider registering or titling to a trust.
  3. Exchange accounts: Confirm any beneficiary or legacy contact features are current. Consider whether titling to a trust makes more sense.
  4. Life insurance policies: Not Bitcoin, but equally important — confirm beneficiaries match the estate plan.
  5. Coordinate with trust: Confirm that the beneficiary designations are consistent with the trust's distribution provisions. If the trust is the beneficiary of an IRA, confirm the trust's see-through provisions are in place.

The best time to do this audit: when you update your letter of instruction — typically annually and after any significant life change.

The Bottom Line

Beneficiary designations are the unsexy, underappreciated cornerstone of Bitcoin estate planning. They're simple forms that take five minutes to fill out and can save — or cost — hundreds of thousands of dollars depending on whether they're done correctly.

For Bitcoin in IRAs: name your spouse as primary, children per stirpes as contingent, and consider a CRT for large estates above the exemption. Understand that the 10-year rule makes IRA Bitcoin inheritance tax-inefficient for adult children — Roth conversion is the structural fix, not the beneficiary form.

For Bitcoin in exchange accounts: most exchanges don't have beneficiary forms — title the account to your revocable trust while alive instead.

For Bitcoin ETF in taxable brokerage: TOD registration combines the step-up in basis advantage with probate avoidance — the most tax-efficient inheritance structure available for this asset type.

And review the forms every year. The beneficiary designation form you filled out when you were 35 may be running your estate when you die at 75. Give it the attention it deserves.
