Bitcoin Cost Basis Methods: FIFO, HIFO & Specific ID Explained (2026)

Most Bitcoin investors default to FIFO — and pay more tax than they have to. Choosing the right lot selection method is one of the simplest, highest-impact tax optimizations available. Here's how each method works, what the IRS requires, and which approach minimizes your tax bill in every scenario.

If you've accumulated Bitcoin through multiple purchases over time — a common pattern for any investor who DCA'd through multiple cycles — you own multiple "lots" of Bitcoin, each with a different cost basis and acquisition date. When you sell a portion of your Bitcoin, the tax outcome depends entirely on which lots you're considered to have sold.

This choice — called your cost basis method or lot identification method — is one of the most underappreciated levers in Bitcoin tax planning. The difference between the best and worst method can amount to tens of thousands of dollars on a modest sale, and hundreds of thousands on large positions. Understanding the four methods, their IRS requirements, and when to use each is essential for any serious Bitcoin investor.

What Is Cost Basis and Why Does It Matter?

Your cost basis in Bitcoin is the amount you paid to acquire it — the purchase price plus any transaction fees. When you sell, your taxable gain (or loss) equals proceeds minus basis. Lower basis = higher gain = more tax. Higher basis = lower gain = less tax.

For a single purchase, basis is simple: you bought 1 BTC at $30,000, you sell at $80,000, your gain is $50,000. But most Bitcoin investors have made many purchases at many prices. When you sell "some" Bitcoin, which purchases are you selling from? That's the cost basis method question — and different answers produce dramatically different tax outcomes on the same sale.

The Four Lot Selection Methods

1. FIFO — First In, First Out (IRS Default)

Sell your oldest Bitcoin first. If you've been accumulating since 2017, your 2017 purchases are sold before your 2023 purchases. In a bull market where early purchases have the lowest basis, FIFO maximizes your reported gain — and your tax bill. FIFO is the IRS default if you don't make a specific identification election.

Best for: Investors who want to hold their lowest-basis lots indefinitely (until death for §1014 step-up) while recognizing gains on newer, higher-basis purchases. FIFO automatically sells the oldest lots — which in a long-term portfolio might be exactly the ones you want to keep for step-up planning.

Worst for: Active traders who want to minimize current-year gains.

2. LIFO — Last In, First Out

Sell your most recently acquired Bitcoin first. In a rising market, your newest purchases likely have the highest basis — meaning LIFO produces the smallest gain on each sale. However, LIFO can inadvertently trigger short-term capital gains treatment if the most recent purchases were made within the last 12 months.

Best for: Markets where recent prices are higher than older prices, and you have no recent short-term lots to worry about.

Worst for: Long-term investors with recent purchases — LIFO will sell your newest (potentially short-term) lots first, converting what could be long-term gains into ordinary income. Usually a poor default choice for Bitcoin investors.

3. HIFO — Highest In, First Out

Sell the lots with the highest cost basis first, regardless of acquisition date. This minimizes the recognized gain on every sale by always applying the maximum available basis. HIFO is not explicitly named as a method in the Internal Revenue Code, but it is a form of specific identification — you are identifying specific lots (the highest-basis ones) to sell.

Best for: Investors who want to minimize current-year taxable gains across all their lots. HIFO is the tax-optimal default for active sellers in most market conditions.

Requires: Specific identification documentation — same requirements as any specific ID election. Must be documented at time of sale, not retroactively applied.

4. Specific Identification (Spec ID)

Manually select which exact lots to sell for each transaction. This gives maximum flexibility — you can optimize each sale individually for the best combination of low gain, long-term treatment, or loss harvesting. Requires contemporaneous documentation: you must identify the lots being sold at or before the time of sale.

Best for: Sophisticated investors making large, strategic sales where the combination of basis, holding period, and other gains/losses makes specific lot selection highly valuable.

Requires: Accurate lot-level records and documentation practice. Most crypto tax software supports specific ID selection at the transaction level.

FIFO Lot A (oldest) $42,000 $48,000 gain Long-term $11,424
LIFO Lot D (newest) $95,000 ($5,000) loss Short-term loss $0 (saves $1,890)
HIFO Lot D (highest basis) $95,000 ($5,000) loss Short-term loss $0 (saves $1,890)

In this example: FIFO produces an $11,424 tax bill. LIFO and HIFO both select the highest-basis lot (Lot D) and generate a $5,000 short-term loss instead of a gain — $0 tax plus a usable loss carryforward. Optimal specific ID selects Lot C for a $5,000 long-term gain at $1,190 in federal tax — if you specifically want to realize a small long-term gain rather than a loss (e.g., to fill a low-income year bracket).

The right answer depends on your broader tax situation: do you have other short-term losses to use? Is this a good year to realize gains? Is Lot D's loss more valuable than a small long-term gain? Specific ID lets you answer all of these questions optimally for every individual sale.

IRS Rules: What's Required for Each Method

Method IRS Approval Documentation Required Retroactive Application?
FIFO ✅ Default — no election needed Standard purchase records Yes — FIFO applies automatically without action
LIFO ✅ Permitted as specific identification Must document which lots at time of sale No — must identify lots at or before sale
HIFO ✅ Permitted as form of specific identification Must document which lots at time of sale No — contemporaneous documentation required
Specific ID ✅ Explicitly permitted under IRS guidance Must identify specific lots at or before time of sale; record which lots in writing No — IRS requires contemporaneous identification

The retroactive problem: The IRS requires that specific identification be made at or before the time of sale. You cannot look at December's sales in April and retroactively assign the most favorable basis. If you use crypto tax software (Koinly, TaxBit, CoinTracker), the software's lot selection at the time you record/confirm the transaction effectively serves as contemporaneous documentation — but you must use it consistently throughout the year, not just at tax time.

How to Execute Specific Identification Correctly

Specific identification requires a two-part process: adequate records of each lot and contemporaneous designation when selling.

Adequate Records for Each Lot

For each Bitcoin purchase, maintain:

  • Date of acquisition (trade date for exchange purchases)
  • Amount of Bitcoin acquired
  • Purchase price per BTC and total cost
  • Any fees included in basis
  • Exchange or wallet source
  • A unique lot identifier (your tax software will assign these automatically)

Contemporaneous Designation at Sale

When you sell Bitcoin, you must be able to document:

  • Which specific lot(s) you are selling
  • The lot's acquisition date and original cost
  • That this designation was made at or before the time of sale

Practically, the cleanest approach is to use a crypto tax platform (Koinly, TaxBit, CoinTracker, TokenTax) that lets you select specific lots at the time you record or import each sale. Most major platforms support HIFO and specific ID and generate a compliant audit trail automatically.

For large OTC or direct Bitcoin sales (not through a standard exchange), prepare a written designation document before or at the time of sale specifying: "I am selling [X] BTC acquired on [date] at [price], Lot [identifier], with cost basis of [amount]." Keep this document with your tax records.

Which Method to Use in Each Scenario

Your Situation Recommended Method Why
You have lots at many price levels and want to minimize current-year gain HIFO or Specific ID (select highest-basis lots) Maximizes basis applied to sale, minimizing reportable gain
You want to harvest losses for year-end tax management Specific ID (select losing lots) Identify exactly the lots underwater; sell those first to realize deductible losses
You want to ensure long-term capital gains treatment Specific ID (select oldest lots >12 months) Manually select only lots held >12 months; avoid accidentally selling short-term lots
You plan to hold your lowest-basis lots until death (§1014 step-up) FIFO or Specific ID (avoid oldest lots) FIFO sells oldest lots first — if those are your lowest basis lots, this aligns with step-up strategy. Alternatively, use Specific ID to sell higher-basis newer lots and preserve oldest lots.
You are filling a low-income year's 0% LTCG bracket Specific ID (select long-term, low-basis lots) Select LT lots that generate gains just up to the 0% bracket limit — effectively realizing gains tax-free
You DCA and want simplicity without optimization FIFO (default) No documentation needed; automatically applied; acceptable if tax optimization isn't a priority

Cost Basis and the Step-Up at Death

Your lot selection method during your lifetime has an important interaction with the §1014 step-up in basis at death. When you die, your heirs receive a stepped-up basis equal to the fair market value of your Bitcoin on your date of death — regardless of what basis you had accumulated during your lifetime.

This means: for Bitcoin you intend to hold until death, the basis of those lots is irrelevant from a capital gains perspective (your heirs will get a fresh basis regardless). The lot selection question only matters for Bitcoin you intend to sell during your lifetime.

The practical implication for estate planning:

  • Lots you plan to sell: Use HIFO/Specific ID to minimize gain on those lots
  • Lots you plan to hold until death: Basis optimization is irrelevant — the step-up eliminates all embedded gain regardless
  • Lots you're transferring to a trust: The carryover basis rules apply during the grantor's lifetime; step-up may apply on distribution depending on trust structure

If your intention is to hold most of your Bitcoin until death and sell only a small portion during your lifetime, apply HIFO/Specific ID aggressively to the small portion you do sell, and don't worry about basis optimization on the rest.

Bitcoin Mining: The Tax Strategy That Resets Your Basis

Mined Bitcoin always has a fresh cost basis equal to fair market value at the time of mining — no legacy low-basis lot problem. For investors with large low-basis Bitcoin stacks, mining provides a pathway to acquire additional Bitcoin at current-price basis, enabling more granular lot management and harvesting flexibility.

The difference lies in how well they handle edge cases: mining income, DeFi transactions, NFTs, staking, and cross-chain activity. For straightforward Bitcoin investors (exchange purchases and sales, self-custody transfers), any of the major platforms provide adequate lot tracking.

Key workflow: Import all transactions at the beginning of the year (not just at tax time). Run the gain/loss report periodically throughout the year to see your real-time tax position. Make lot selection decisions on large planned sales before executing — not after. The software generates your Form 8949 automatically; the human judgment is in which lots to select for each sale.

8-Item Cost Basis Checklist

  1. Import all transactions into crypto tax software by end of January. Don't wait until tax season. Load all 2025 transactions in January so you have a clean baseline for 2026 planning. Verify the software correctly identifies all lots with accurate dates and prices.
  2. Confirm your default method setting in your software. Most platforms default to FIFO. If you want HIFO, change the setting at the beginning of the year — before any sales. Note that some exchanges (Coinbase, for example) use their own internal lot tracking that may differ from your software's calculation; reconcile regularly.
  3. Use HIFO as your default for active sales unless you have a specific reason not to. HIFO minimizes current-year gain in most market conditions. The only exceptions: when you specifically want to realize a long-term gain (to fill a 0% bracket), or when managing lots for step-up planning.
  4. Document specific ID selections in writing at time of sale. For large sales (six figures or more), create a simple written record: "Selling [X] BTC from Lot [ID], acquired [date] at [price], basis [amount]." Keep with your year-end tax records. Your software's lot selection report also serves as documentation.
  5. Review the holding period of each lot before selling. Never sell a lot without knowing whether it's short-term or long-term. One accidental sale of a short-term lot when you intended to sell a long-term lot can cost tens of thousands in unnecessary tax.
  6. Run your tax projection in October. Using your crypto tax software, run a projected gain/loss report for the year. Identify opportunities to harvest losses before year-end, realize gains in a low-income year, or defer gains to next year by timing your sales carefully.
  7. Identify lots you intend to hold until death and segregate them mentally. For these lots, basis optimization is irrelevant — the step-up eliminates all gain. Focus your lot selection effort on the Bitcoin you actually plan to sell during your lifetime.
  8. Reconcile your software's calculation against exchange 1099-DA forms when received. Exchanges report gross proceeds to the IRS. If the proceeds don't match your software's records, identify the discrepancy before filing. Cost basis mismatches between your records and the IRS's information are the most common source of CP2000 notices for Bitcoin investors. The Bottom Line

Cost basis method selection is the most overlooked Bitcoin tax optimization — and one of the easiest to implement correctly with modern software. FIFO is the default; HIFO is usually better for active sellers; Specific Identification gives maximum control for strategic planning.

The rules are straightforward: document your lots, make your selection at the time of sale, and use software that maintains a consistent audit trail. Done right, lot selection is a passive, ongoing source of tax savings that compounds over your Bitcoin investment lifetime.

Combined with Bitcoin's no-wash-sale advantage, holding period discipline, and systematic year-end harvesting, cost basis management is one of the core pillars of the tax layer in any serious Bitcoin wealth strategy.
