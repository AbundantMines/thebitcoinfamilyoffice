Can Bitcoin Go in a Living Trust?

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  7 min read

Home › Research › Can Bitcoin Go in a Living Trust? Est. 7 min read

The short answer is yes. Bitcoin can be placed in a revocable living trust, and doing so is one of the most important steps a Bitcoin holder can take to avoid probate, simplify succession, and ensure heirs can actually access their inheritance. But the mechanics of placing Bitcoin in a trust are meaningfully different from how you would transfer a brokerage account or a piece of real estate — and getting it wrong can create serious problems.

This article explains exactly how Bitcoin trust ownership works, what your trust documents need to say, where the common mistakes occur, and when a revocable living trust is — and isn't — the right structure for your Bitcoin holdings.

Why a Living Trust Matters for Bitcoin

A revocable living trust is an estate planning tool that holds assets during your lifetime and transfers them to your named beneficiaries at death — without going through probate court. Probate is the court-supervised process of validating a will and distributing assets, and it is time-consuming (typically 9–18 months or longer), expensive (attorney fees commonly run 3–5% of gross estate value), and public (probate records are a matter of public record in most states).

For Bitcoin holders, the probate risk is compounded: if Bitcoin is not properly accounted for in your estate plan, heirs may not even know it exists. And if they do know, the court process required to establish their authority to access the wallet can be lengthy enough that practical access becomes complicated. A properly structured trust bypasses all of this.

The trust also allows you to specify, in legally binding terms, exactly how your Bitcoin should be managed, accessed, and distributed — including custody requirements, multi-signature arrangements, and trustee responsibilities that courts are not equipped to adjudicate efficiently.

How Bitcoin Is "Transferred" to a Trust

Here is where Bitcoin differs fundamentally from other assets. When you transfer a brokerage account to a trust, you update the account registration at the custodian. When you transfer real estate, you execute and record a deed. Bitcoin has no central custodian and no registry.

To "place" Bitcoin in a living trust, you do not literally move the coins to a new wallet or change any on-chain address. Instead, you execute a trust assignment document — a legal instrument declaring that your Bitcoin holdings (identified by wallet address, hardware wallet, or custody account reference) are now held by you as trustee of your trust, for the benefit of the trust. The trust becomes the legal owner of the beneficial interest in those Bitcoin holdings.

If your Bitcoin is held at a qualified custodian (e.g., Coinbase Custody, Gemini), you can often formally update the account registration to reflect trust ownership — similar to a brokerage account. If you hold Bitcoin in self-custody (a hardware wallet), the trust assignment document and your estate plan documentation establish the trust's ownership interest even though no on-chain transfer occurs.

Placing Bitcoin in a trust is a legal act, not a technical one. The trust documents establish ownership — the coins themselves don't move.

What the Trust Documents Must Say

Standard revocable living trust templates drafted before 2015 or so frequently contain no provisions addressing digital assets. This is a serious gap. Your trust document — or an amendment to it — should include explicit provisions covering:

  • Digital asset authority. The trust should expressly authorize the trustee to hold, manage, transact with, and distribute digital assets including Bitcoin. Without this authority, a successor trustee may be uncertain whether they have legal authority to act.
  • RUFADAA compliance language. The Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), adopted in most U.S. states, gives fiduciaries (including trustees) legal access to digital assets when the governing document or online tool of a service explicitly grants that access. Your trust should include express authorization consistent with RUFADAA to ensure your successor trustee has clear legal footing.
  • Custody instructions by reference. The trust document can reference a separate Letter of Instruction — kept outside the public record — that details the specific custody arrangement, wallet addresses, hardware device locations, and access procedures. Do not put seed phrases or private keys in the trust document itself.
  • Trustee technical authority. Consider whether your named successor trustee has the technical capability to manage Bitcoin custody. If not, the trust can authorize the trustee to engage a qualified Bitcoin custodian, a co-trustee with technical expertise, or an investment advisor with authority over Bitcoin holdings.

What NOT to Do: The Seed Phrase Mistake

Critical Warning: Never write seed phrases, private keys, or wallet passwords directly into your trust document, will, or any other legal document that will be filed with a court or held by an attorney. Trust documents can be amended, are sometimes filed in probate proceedings, and are accessible to multiple parties. A seed phrase in a legal document is a security catastrophe waiting to happen.

The correct approach: keep seed phrases and access credentials in a physically secure location (a home safe, safety deposit box, or purpose-built inheritance vault service), referenced only by location and access instructions in your Letter of Instruction — not in the trust document itself. The Letter of Instruction is not a legal document; it is a private operational document that the trustee can access at need without it entering any public record.

RUFADAA and Trustee Authority

RUFADAA, enacted in some form in most U.S. states, provides the legal framework for fiduciaries — including successor trustees — to access and manage digital assets after incapacity or death. Under RUFADAA, a trustee has authority to manage digital assets if: (1) the trust document expressly grants that authority, or (2) the decedent used an online tool provided by the digital asset custodian to grant fiduciary access.

For self-custodied Bitcoin — where there is no "online tool" — the trust document's express grant of authority is the primary mechanism establishing the trustee's legal right to act. This is why the trust's digital asset provisions matter so much: without them, your successor trustee may encounter legal uncertainty even if they have the technical ability to access the wallet.

In states that have not yet fully adopted RUFADAA, or where the state's version is modified, additional planning may be needed. An attorney familiar with your state's digital asset fiduciary laws should review your trust to confirm adequate trustee authority.

When a Revocable Trust Is the Right Structure — and When It Isn't

A revocable living trust is the right primary structure for Bitcoin when:

  • Your estate is below the federal estate tax exemption ($13.99M per person in 2025) and you have no state estate tax exposure
  • Your primary goals are probate avoidance and controlled succession to named beneficiaries
  • You want to retain full control of your Bitcoin during your lifetime
  • You want the step-up in basis to apply to your Bitcoin at death (revocable trusts preserve this)

A revocable trust is not sufficient — or may not be the right primary vehicle — when:

  • Your estate exceeds or approaches the federal estate tax exemption, and you need irrevocable trust structures for estate tax reduction
  • You want asset protection from creditors (revocable trusts offer essentially no creditor protection during your lifetime)
  • You want to establish a dynasty trust for multigenerational Bitcoin holding (requires an irrevocable structure with perpetuity provisions)
  • You need income-shifting or charitable planning structures (CRTs, GRATs, etc.) — these require irrevocable trusts

For most Bitcoin holders below the estate tax threshold, a revocable living trust — properly drafted with digital asset provisions — is the essential first layer of Bitcoin estate planning. It is not the only layer, but it is where most families should start.
