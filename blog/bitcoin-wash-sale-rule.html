Bitcoin Wash Sale Rule 2026: Why It Doesn't Apply (Yet)

Stock investors must wait 30 days before rebuying after a loss sale or forfeit the deduction. Bitcoin investors face no such restriction. The wash sale rule under IRC §1091 applies only to securities — and Bitcoin is property. Here's what that means, the important gray areas, and the legislative risk every Bitcoin investor must monitor.

Every year, stock investors engage in "tax-loss harvesting" — selling losing positions to realize capital losses, then waiting 31 days before buying back to avoid the wash sale rule. The wait is frustrating: you're out of the market for a month, potentially missing a rebound, and your deduction disappears if you accidentally buy "substantially identical" securities too soon.

Bitcoin investors don't have this problem. The wash sale rule under IRC §1091 applies only to "stock or securities." Bitcoin is neither — it is property under Revenue Ruling 2014-21. There is no 30-day window, no substantially identical rule, no rebuy restriction. Sell Bitcoin at a loss at 9am, buy it back at 9:01am at the same price, and claim the full capital loss deduction. No rule prohibits this.

This is one of the most powerful and underutilized tax advantages available to Bitcoin investors. Understanding exactly how it works, where the gray areas lie, and when the legislative landscape might change is essential for any serious Bitcoin wealth strategy.

What Is the Wash Sale Rule?

The wash sale rule under IRC §1091 disallows a capital loss deduction when a taxpayer sells stock or securities at a loss and, within 30 days before or after the sale, purchases "substantially identical" stock or securities. The 61-day window (30 days before + day of sale + 30 days after) is designed to prevent taxpayers from claiming paper losses without actually changing their economic position.

When a wash sale is triggered, the disallowed loss is added to the cost basis of the newly purchased shares. The tax benefit isn't permanently lost — it's deferred until the replacement position is eventually sold outside the wash sale window. But the timing deferral can be significant: if you planned to use the loss to offset gains in the current tax year, a wash sale eliminates that benefit.

What Counts as "Substantially Identical"

For stocks, the substantially identical rule applies to the exact same security (same CUSIP) and generally to options or contracts to acquire the same security. The IRS has not provided comprehensive guidance on every edge case, but some examples are clear:

  • Selling Apple stock and buying Apple stock 5 days later → wash sale
  • Selling an S&P 500 index fund and buying a nearly identical S&P 500 ETF → likely wash sale (substantially identical)
  • Selling one tech stock and buying a different tech stock → generally not wash sale (different securities)

The substantially identical concept doesn't apply to Bitcoin at all — because §1091 doesn't apply to property, period.

Why Bitcoin Is Exempt — The Property Classification

IRC §1091 applies specifically to "stock or securities." The Internal Revenue Code defines "securities" in various contexts, but for wash sale purposes, the relevant framework comes from §1091 itself and the broader securities law definitions that inform it.

Bitcoin is property, not a security. The IRS established this definitively in Revenue Ruling 2014-21: "Virtual currency is treated as property for U.S. federal tax purposes. General tax principles applicable to property transactions apply to transactions using virtual currency." The ruling did not classify Bitcoin as a security, a currency, or anything other than property.

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) classifies Bitcoin as a commodity. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has consistently declined to classify spot Bitcoin as a security (though it has classified certain other crypto assets as securities). The IRS, Treasury, CFTC, and SEC have all reached the same conclusion for their respective purposes: Bitcoin is not a security.

Since §1091 applies only to stock or securities, and Bitcoin is property (not a security), the wash sale rule by its plain text does not apply to Bitcoin. This is not a loophole — it's the direct application of the statute's scope as written.

No IRS challenge: As of March 2026, the IRS has not issued any guidance, ruling, or enforcement action treating Bitcoin wash sales as disallowed under §1091. The IRS has been explicit that Bitcoin is property. The professional tax community broadly agrees the wash sale rule does not currently apply to Bitcoin. This doesn't mean it can't change — Congress can extend §1091 to cover property — but the current legal position is clear.

What This Means in Practice

For a Bitcoin investor, the absence of the wash sale rule means:

  • Immediate repurchase: You can sell Bitcoin at a loss and immediately rebuy the same amount of Bitcoin at the same (or any) price. The loss is fully deductible. Your economic position is unchanged.
  • No waiting period: Unlike stock investors who must wait 31 days to re-establish a position without triggering wash sale, Bitcoin investors face zero mandatory wait time.
  • No "substantially identical" concern: Since the rule doesn't apply, there's no need to worry about whether a different cryptocurrency constitutes "substantially identical" property to Bitcoin. (Though each crypto-to-crypto exchange is its own taxable event.)
  • Year-end flexibility: You can harvest Bitcoin losses on December 31, immediately repurchase on January 1, and enter the new year with the same Bitcoin position but a deducted loss applied to the prior year's return.
  • Basis reset: Each time you harvest and repurchase, your new cost basis is the repurchase price. Future gains or losses are calculated from the new basis.

Step-by-Step Harvest Example

Scenario: Bitcoin Tax-Loss Harvest in Action

November 1: You purchased 2 BTC at $90,000 each ($180,000 total basis).

December 15: Bitcoin price drops to $70,000. Your 2 BTC is worth $140,000. You have a $40,000 unrealized loss.

December 15, 10:00 AM: You sell 2 BTC at $70,000 = $140,000 proceeds. Realized loss: $40,000. Short-term loss (held < 12 months).

December 15, 10:05 AM: You buy 2 BTC at $70,000 = $140,000. New basis: $70,000/BTC.

Tax result: $40,000 short-term capital loss claimed on your 2026 return. Offsets $40,000 of other short-term gains (or long-term gains, or up to $3,000 of ordinary income). Economic position: unchanged — still own 2 BTC. New basis: $70,000/BTC (lower than original $90,000).

If Bitcoin recovers to $100,000 and you sell later: New gain = $100,000 - $70,000 = $30,000/BTC. You've effectively shifted $20,000 of gain per BTC from the current year (when you harvested) to the future year (when you sell). The harvest provided a real deferral of $20,000 × 2 BTC × 23.8% = $9,520 in deferred tax.

The tax benefit from harvesting is most powerful when:

  • You have other capital gains in the same year to offset (up to dollar-for-dollar offset)
  • Bitcoin's price recovers significantly after the harvest (the deferred gain gets pushed further into the future)
  • You're in the highest marginal bracket (23.8% federal LTCG rate — each dollar of deferred gain saves $0.238 in current taxes)

The Bitcoin ETF Gray Area

The launch of spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024 — iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT), Fidelity Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC), ARK 21Shares Bitcoin ETF (ARKB), and others — created a potentially significant complication for Bitcoin tax-loss harvesting.

The question: If you sell actual Bitcoin at a loss and immediately purchase a spot Bitcoin ETF (like IBIT), have you triggered a wash sale?

The answer is unresolved under current IRS guidance, but the analysis matters:

  • Direct Bitcoin = property. No wash sale rule applies to the sale.
  • Spot Bitcoin ETF = likely a security (registered investment company shares traded on an exchange). The wash sale rule almost certainly applies to ETF shares as securities.
  • The substantially identical question: Is Bitcoin "substantially identical" to a spot Bitcoin ETF that holds Bitcoin? If yes, selling Bitcoin at a loss and buying IBIT within 30 days could trigger the wash sale rule — because IBIT is a security and Bitcoin is the underlying. The IRS has not issued guidance on this specific question.

The conservative approach: if you harvest a Bitcoin loss, wait 31 days before purchasing any spot Bitcoin ETF. If you need Bitcoin exposure during the 31-day window, hold direct Bitcoin (buy it back immediately — no wash sale) or buy a Bitcoin futures ETF (different underlying, less likely to be substantially identical to spot Bitcoin, though still unsettled).

Safe harbor approach: Sell Bitcoin → immediately rebuy Bitcoin. No wash sale issue. Do not buy a spot Bitcoin ETF within 30 days of a Bitcoin loss sale until the IRS clarifies the substantially identical question. The direct Bitcoin rebuy is unambiguously outside the wash sale rule. The ETF rebuy is a gray area.

Legislative Risk: What Congress Has Proposed

The most important thing to understand about Bitcoin's wash sale exemption is that it exists because Congress wrote §1091 to cover securities, not property — and they haven't updated it yet. Several proposals to extend wash sale rules to cryptocurrency have been introduced:

Proposal Year Status What It Would Have Done
Build Back Better Act 2021 Failed in Senate Extended wash sale and constructive sale rules to all commodities and digital assets
Lummis-Gillibrand RFIA 2022, 2023 Not enacted Would have created de minimis exemption but also formalized wash sale applicability to crypto
Various budget proposals 2022–2024 Not enacted Multiple proposals included crypto wash sale extension as revenue-raiser
Current status (2026) 2026 No wash sale rule for Bitcoin No enacted legislation extending §1091 to cryptocurrency as of March 2026

The pattern is clear: Congress has repeatedly attempted to extend wash sale rules to crypto as a revenue offset in larger legislative packages. These proposals have consistently failed — largely due to the broader crypto industry's lobbying efforts and the difficulty of passing comprehensive crypto legislation. But the proposals have never gone away. The JOINT Committee on Taxation has scored crypto wash sale extension as a multi-billion-dollar revenue raiser, giving it recurring appeal as a pay-for mechanism in future legislation.

What this means for investors: The no-wash-sale advantage exists today. It may not exist in five years. Bitcoin investors should use this window aggressively while it remains open — both for current-year harvesting and for building a multi-year loss bank that offsets future gains even if the rule changes prospectively.

State Wash Sale Rules

Most states conform to federal tax law, including the property classification of Bitcoin that keeps it outside wash sale rules. However, states have some independent authority over their own tax codes, and a few nuances apply:

  • Most states: Follow federal characterization — Bitcoin is property, wash sale doesn't apply at state level either.
  • California: Historically aggressive in asserting tax claims; no specific CA wash sale rule extending to crypto as of 2026, but watch for state-level action as federal proposals continue to be introduced.
  • New York: Follows federal treatment for capital gains; no separate NY wash sale rule for crypto currently.
  • Note: If Congress enacts a federal crypto wash sale rule, most states with rolling conformity to federal law would automatically adopt it. States with static or selective conformity would need separate legislation.

The Step-Up Trade-Off: When NOT to Harvest

The no-wash-sale rule is powerful, but it interacts in a counterintuitive way with the §1014 stepped-up basis strategy. If you plan to hold Bitcoin until death — passing the entire position to heirs with a basis step-up to fair market value at the date of death — then aggressive tax-loss harvesting during your lifetime may be counterproductive.

Here's why: every time you harvest a loss and repurchase, your basis resets to the repurchase price. When you die, the step-up goes to fair market value at death — regardless of your accumulated basis. The harvested loss you carried forward is only useful if you use it against gains before you die.

If you harvest a $200,000 loss and carry it forward for 20 years without ever using it, then die with the Bitcoin position, the $200,000 loss carryforward disappears — it cannot be used by your heirs. Your heirs inherit the position at the step-up basis (current FMV at death) and have no capital gains tax regardless. The harvested loss served no purpose.

Harvest if you plan to sell; don't harvest if you plan to hold until death. For a Bitcoin position you intend to hold for decades and pass to heirs, the step-up strategy and the harvesting strategy are mutually exclusive. Choose one framework and stick with it — mixing them creates accounting complexity without tax benefit.

For investors who will sell during their lifetime — either for diversification, spending, or charitable giving — harvesting is clearly additive. Every loss captured reduces current-year tax or creates a carryforward that offsets future gains.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term: Which Losses to Harvest First

Not all capital losses are equal. The tax code's netting rules require you to match losses against gains in a specific order — and the order matters for how much tax you actually save:

  1. Short-term losses first offset short-term gains (taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37%)
  2. Long-term losses first offset long-term gains (taxed at preferential rates up to 23.8%)
  3. Excess short-term losses can offset long-term gains
  4. Excess long-term losses can offset short-term gains
  5. Net capital losses (after all netting) offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year, with unlimited carryforward

The strategic implication: short-term losses are more valuable than long-term losses when you have short-term gains to offset. A $10,000 short-term loss offsetting a $10,000 short-term gain saves $3,700 (at 37%). The same $10,000 long-term loss offsetting a $10,000 long-term gain saves $2,380 (at 23.8%). Harvest short-term losses preferentially when you have short-term gains; long-term losses when you have only long-term gains.

How to Document Your Harvest Correctly

A Bitcoin wash sale harvest is only as good as its documentation. The IRS may question the loss if records are incomplete. Here's what you need:

  1. Exchange trade confirmations: Timestamp of the sale, BTC amount, price, proceeds. Export from your exchange immediately — don't rely on memory.
  2. Purchase records for the repurchase: Same — timestamp, BTC amount, price, cost. This establishes your new basis.
  3. Cost basis for the sold position: Your original purchase date, price, and lot allocation method (FIFO, HIFO, specific ID). This determines the loss amount. For specific ID, document which lots you're selling at the time of sale, not retroactively.
  4. Form 8949 reporting: Every Bitcoin sale — harvest or otherwise — is reported on Form 8949. Column (d) = proceeds, Column (e) = cost basis, Column (h) = gain or (loss). Short-term gains/losses on Part I; long-term on Part II.
  5. Crypto tax software: Use your platform (Koinly, TaxBit, CoinTracker, etc.) to import all transactions and generate Form 8949. The software will automatically calculate gains and losses by lot, apply your preferred basis method, and flag any anomalies.
  6. Retain records for 7 years minimum (10 years if substantial omissions are possible) — exchange CSV exports, hardware wallet logs, and tax software reports.

8-Item Wash Sale Harvesting Checklist

  1. Confirm your holding strategy first. If you intend to hold this Bitcoin until death and pass via §1014 step-up, skip harvesting — the loss serves no long-term purpose and creates accounting complexity for no benefit.
  2. Identify all unrealized Bitcoin losses annually by November. Review each lot — purchase date, original price, current price. Calculate the loss per lot. Prioritize short-term losses over long-term losses if you have short-term gains to offset.
  3. Match losses to gains before executing. Determine what other capital gains you have (stock sales, real estate, other crypto) and how much loss offsets them at what tax rate. Harvest the losses with the highest marginal benefit first.
  4. Sell the Bitcoin position. Specify which lot(s) you're selling if using specific identification. Export the trade confirmation immediately. Timestamp matters.
  5. Immediately repurchase direct Bitcoin (not an ETF). Buy Bitcoin within seconds of the sale. This eliminates market exposure gap and eliminates any wash sale question. Do NOT purchase a spot Bitcoin ETF (IBIT, FBTC, ARKB, etc.) within 30 days — gray area risk.
  6. Record the new basis. Your repurchase price becomes the new cost basis for these coins. Update your tax software immediately.
  7. File Form 8949 accurately. Report both the sale and the repurchase. The sale generates the loss; the repurchase creates the new lot. Both must be on your tax return. Missing either transaction creates an audit trigger.
  8. Monitor legislative developments. Set a calendar reminder to check for crypto wash sale legislation before each year-end harvest cycle. If Congress extends §1091 to crypto during the year, adjust your strategy accordingly. A new law would typically apply prospectively, but confirm with your tax advisor.

The Bottom Line

Bitcoin's no-wash-sale status is one of the clearest tax advantages it holds over traditional securities. Stock investors must choose between maintaining exposure and claiming the loss. Bitcoin investors don't have to choose. The loss and the position can both be maintained, simultaneously, with zero waiting period.

The value of this advantage compounds over a Bitcoin investor's lifetime. Every year with significant volatility is a harvesting opportunity. Every bear market is a loss-banking event. A decade of disciplined harvesting builds a loss carryforward that offsets gains in bull markets at full rates — essentially a tax-free fund that deploys in the moments when it's most valuable.

Use it while it lasts. Monitor the legislative calendar. And integrate it with a broader Bitcoin tax strategy that includes zero-tax structures, NIIT reduction, and multi-generational estate planning — because the wash sale advantage is one piece of a larger puzzle, not the complete picture.
