Bitcoin Step-Up in Basis at Death: The Tax Benefit Most Holders Miss

By Hal Turner February 25, 2026 ~2,200 words

If you've held Bitcoin since 2015, you're sitting on a gain of roughly 8,000%. When you sell, you'll owe capital gains tax on every dollar of that appreciation. When you die, your heirs inherit at current market value — and owe nothing. This is the step-up in basis, and for long-term Bitcoin holders, it may be the single most valuable tax planning tool available. Most holders either don't know about it, or don't understand how to use it strategically.

What the Step-Up in Basis Actually Is

Under IRC Section 1014, assets transferred at death receive a new cost basis equal to the fair market value on the date of death. This is called the "step-up in basis" (or more precisely, the "stepped-up basis" — because the basis steps up to current market value, erasing all embedded appreciation).

For Bitcoin, the mechanics are straightforward:

The $435,000 gain your heirs would have owed tax on — permanently eliminated. The heir who inherits these 5 BTC can sell immediately at $95,000 each and pay zero capital gains tax. Or they can hold, and their new basis is $95,000 — they only owe capital gains on appreciation above $95,000 per BTC from that point forward.

Why This Matters More for Bitcoin Than Almost Any Other Asset

The step-up in basis applies to all appreciated property — stocks, real estate, art, business interests. But it matters more for Bitcoin for one specific reason: the magnitude of appreciation is unlike anything else in the investment universe. Bitcoin has appreciated roughly 150,000% since 2012. A holder who bought 10 BTC at $100 each in 2013 has a $1,000 cost basis in assets worth $950,000. The embedded gain is $949,000. The capital gains tax on that gain, at long-term rates, is approximately $190,000-$226,000.

If that holder dies with the Bitcoin still in their possession, that entire $949,000 gain disappears. Their heirs inherit with a $950,000 cost basis. The government never taxes the appreciation that occurred during the holder's lifetime.

The Planning Implication: Think Twice Before Selling Low-Basis Bitcoin

The step-up in basis creates a powerful planning principle: low-basis Bitcoin is best held until death. Every year you hold without selling, you defer capital gains. At death, those deferred gains are permanently forgiven — not merely deferred. This changes the calculus for older holders with large Bitcoin positions acquired early.

Hold Low-Basis Bitcoin; Spend/Gift High-Basis Bitcoin

If you have a mix of old Bitcoin (low basis) and recently acquired Bitcoin (high basis), prioritize spending or gifting the high-basis coins. The low-basis coins are the most valuable at death because the step-up eliminates the largest tax liabBitcoin Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusty. When you gift Bitcoin during life, the recipient takes your original basis — the step-up is lost. Don't gift your oldest, most appreciated Bitcoin.

Use HIFO While Alive; Let the Rest Accumulate for the Step-Up

When you do sell Bitcoin to fund living expenses or rebalance, use the HIFO (Highest In, First Out) cost basis method — it sells your highest-basis coins first, minimizing current gains. Your lowest-basis coins (the ones acquired earliest, with the largest embedded gains) accumulate and will receive the step-up at death. HIFO is an election — you must be using proper Bitcoin tax software (CoinTracker, Koinly, TaxBit) and consistently applying HIFO across all transactions.

Spend IRA Assets Before Self-Custodied Bitcoin

Bitcoin held in a traditional IRA does NOT receive a step-up in basis at death. Every dollar distributed from a traditional IRA is taxable as ordinary income — including all the appreciation. By contrast, Bitcoin held in a taxable account (hardware wallet, exchange) DOES receive the step-up. The optimal spending sequence for older holders: spend traditional IRA assets first (take RMDs, then additional distributions in low-income years), preserve self-custodied Bitcoin for heirs who will receive the step-up.

What the Step-Up Doesn't Cover

The step-up in basis has limits that are important to understand:

Bitcoin in IRAs: No Step-Up

Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and SDIRA (Self-Directed IRA) assets do not receive a step-up in basis at death. The IRA structure removes the asset from the step-up rules. For a traditional IRA, heirs pay ordinary income tax on every dollar distributed. Even for a Roth IRA (which passes tax-free), the basis question is moot because Roth distributions are already tax-free — but the key point is that you're not getting an additional step-up benefit.

Gifted Bitcoin: Basis Transfers, No Step-Up

When you gift Bitcoin during life, the recipient takes your original cost basis. If you give your child 1 BTC that you bought at $10,000 when it's worth $95,000, they inherit your $10,000 basis. If they sell at $95,000, they owe capital gains on $85,000. The step-up that would have occurred at your death is gone. This is why gifting your oldest, most appreciated Bitcoin is usually a poor strategy from a tax perspective — save those for the step-up.

Carried Interest and Income Tax Items

The step-up applies to capital gains embedded in an asset. It does not eliminate ordinary income items — for example, if you were a Bitcoin miner, the FMV of Bitcoin at the time of mining was already taxable income. The step-up erases capital gains appreciation that occurred after the asset entered your estate, not income that was already taxable during your lifetime.

Planning for the Step-Up: What to Do Now

If you hold Bitcoin acquired more than a few years ago at prices significantly below current market value, the step-up is part of your estate plan whether you've formalized it or not. Here is how to plan around it:

  1. Inventory your cost basis by acquisition date. Know which coins are "old" (large embedded gain) and which are "new" (small or no gain). Your Bitcoin tax software can produce a lot-by-lot report.
  2. Apply HIFO when you sell. Sell your highest-basis lots first; preserve your lowest-basis lots for the step-up at death.
  3. Document everything for your executor. The date-of-death value must be captured accurately for your heirs' new basis. Your executor needs to know about all wallets and the price on the exact date of death.
  4. Hold Bitcoin outside of IRAs for the step-up. Direct self-custody or exchange accounts qualify; IRAs do not.
  5. Brief your estate attorney and CPA. The step-up interacts with estate tax, GRAT strategy, charitable giving, and gifting decisions in complex ways. It should be part of your comprehensive estate plan, not an afterthought.
