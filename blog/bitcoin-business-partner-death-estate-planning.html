Bitcoin Estate Planning When a Business Partner Dies: Buy-Sell Agreements, Key Person Insurance, and Succession

When a business partner dies and the company holds Bitcoin, three crises hit simultaneously: the deceased partner's heirs inherit an interest in a volatile asset they may not want, the surviving partners lack liquidity to buy them out, and no one agrees on what 150 BTC is "worth" when the price moved 8% between the death certificate and the first attorney call. Standard buy-sell agreements were designed for businesses with predictable revenue and stable balance sheets. Bitcoin businesses have neither. This guide covers the legal structures, valuation mechanics, insurance strategies, and operational procedures that prevent a partner's death from destroying the company — and the family wealth embedded in it.

Why Bitcoin Businesses Are Different When a Partner Dies

In a traditional professional services firm or manufacturing company, a partner's death triggers a well-understood sequence: the buy-sell agreement defines the price, life insurance provides the liquidity, and the estate receives a check. The timeline is measured in weeks, maybe months. The valuation is rarely disputed because the business has relatively stable earnings and tangible assets.

Bitcoin businesses break every assumption in that sequence. Consider the complications:

  • Volatile treasury. A company holding 150 BTC on its balance sheet has a treasury that can swing $1 million or more in a single week. The "value" of the deceased partner's interest changes materially between the date of death, the date of the first estate meeting, and the date the buy-sell actually closes.
  • Custody complexity. If the business uses multisig custody — and any well-run Bitcoin business should — the deceased partner held one or more signing keys. Those keys don't transfer through probate. If the surviving partners can't access the treasury without the deceased's key, the business may be operationally frozen.
  • Heir misalignment. The deceased partner's spouse may have zero interest in maintaining Bitcoin exposure. They want dollars, not sats. But the surviving partners don't have the cash to buy them out at current BTC prices — because their wealth is also in Bitcoin.
  • Regulatory gray zones. Bitcoin mining operations involve energy contracts, equipment leases, hosting agreements, and hash rate commitments that don't transfer cleanly. A partner's death can trigger change-of-control provisions in hosting contracts the surviving partners didn't even know existed.

The core problem is liquidity asymmetry: the business's most valuable asset (Bitcoin) is liquid on a 24/7 global market, but the partners' ability to purchase that interest is constrained by fiat banking hours, insurance claim timelines, and legal processes that move at bureaucratic speed. This mismatch is where partnerships fracture.

Buy-Sell Agreement Structures for Bitcoin Businesses

A bitcoin buy-sell agreement is the foundational document that determines what happens to a deceased partner's interest. Three primary structures exist, and each interacts differently with Bitcoin treasury holdings.

Cross-Purchase Agreement

Each partner personally agrees to purchase the deceased partner's interest. In a three-partner Bitcoin mining company, Partner A and Partner B each buy half of deceased Partner C's interest.

Advantages for Bitcoin businesses: The purchasing partners receive a stepped-up basis in the acquired interest — critical when the underlying Bitcoin has appreciated significantly since the partnership acquired it. The surviving partners' outside basis increases by the amount paid, which can reduce future capital gains on Bitcoin dispositions.

Disadvantages: In a multi-partner Bitcoin business, the insurance and funding logistics become complex. With three partners, you need six separate life insurance policies (each partner owns a policy on each other partner). With four partners, you need twelve. The premium burden falls on individual partners, not the entity — and partners in a Bitcoin mining operation may have wildly different personal liquidity profiles.

Entity Redemption (Stock Redemption)

The business entity itself purchases the deceased partner's interest. The LLC or partnership buys back the membership interest using entity funds — which, in a Bitcoin business, likely means selling BTC to generate the cash needed for the buyout.

Advantages for Bitcoin businesses: Simpler structure — the entity owns one policy on each partner. Only three policies for a three-partner company instead of six. The entity controls the timing and funding.

Disadvantages: No basis step-up for surviving partners. The entity's inside basis in the Bitcoin treasury remains unchanged. If the partnership holds 150 BTC with a basis of $15,000 per coin and BTC is trading at $100,000, the surviving partners inherit the entity's low-basis Bitcoin problem. Additionally, if the entity must sell BTC to fund the redemption, it triggers taxable gains at the entity level — compounding the cost of the buyout.

This is the critical distinction most business attorneys miss when drafting buy-sell agreements for Bitcoin companies: an entity redemption that requires selling appreciated Bitcoin to fund the buyout creates a tax event that erodes the very treasury the surviving partners are trying to preserve.

Wait-and-See (Hybrid) Agreement

The entity has the first option to redeem the deceased partner's interest. If it declines (or can only partially redeem), the surviving partners cross-purchase the remainder. This structure provides maximum flexibility for Bitcoin businesses because it allows the entity to evaluate whether selling BTC at current prices is advantageous or whether the surviving partners should fund the purchase personally — perhaps using life insurance proceeds held outside the entity.

For most Bitcoin partnerships, the wait-and-see structure is optimal. It preserves optionality in a volatile asset environment and allows the surviving partners to make the cross-purchase vs. redemption decision based on market conditions at the time of death — not conditions that existed when the agreement was drafted three years earlier.

Bitcoin Valuation for Buy-Sell Agreements

The single most contested element in any bitcoin buy-sell agreement is valuation. Bitcoin trades 24/7/365 on dozens of exchanges in multiple currencies. There is no "closing price" in the traditional sense. Three valuation approaches are used, each with significant implications for Bitcoin businesses.

Fixed Price

Partners agree on a fixed dollar value for each partner's interest and update it annually. This is the simplest approach — and the most dangerous for Bitcoin businesses. A fixed price set when BTC was $40,000 becomes absurdly inadequate when BTC hits $100,000. Partners rarely remember to update the schedule. The deceased partner's estate will almost certainly challenge a stale fixed price, and courts tend to agree that outdated valuations are unconscionable.

Fixed price valuation has no place in a Bitcoin business buy-sell agreement. The volatility makes any fixed number obsolete within months.

Formula-Based

The agreement specifies a formula: for example, the business's net asset value (including BTC treasury at the volume-weighted average price on the five major U.S. exchanges over the 30 calendar days preceding the date of death) multiplied by the deceased partner's ownership percentage, plus a premium for goodwill or minus a discount for minority interest.

Formula-based valuation works well for Bitcoin businesses, but the formula must address several Bitcoin-specific issues:

  • Which price? Spot price at moment of death, daily close, 7-day VWAP, 30-day VWAP? Each produces materially different results. A 30-day VWAP smooths volatility but may disadvantage the estate if BTC surged in the days before death. The IRS uses the "fair market value on the date of death" — which for Bitcoin typically means the average of the high and low on that date, consistent with public securities valuation under Revenue Ruling 59-60.
  • Which exchange? Coinbase, Kraken, Bitstamp, and CME futures all quote slightly different prices. The agreement should specify a composite or primary exchange to avoid disputes.
  • Unrealized gains discount. If the entity holds BTC with a basis of $15,000 and current price of $100,000, selling to fund the buyout triggers $85,000 per coin in gains. Should the valuation reflect a discount for the embedded tax liability? In traditional business valuation, the Eisenberg and Jelke lines of cases support discounting for built-in gains in C-corporations. For pass-through entities, the treatment is less settled — but the economic reality is the same.
  • Alternate valuation date. IRC §2032 allows the estate to elect an alternate valuation date six months after death for estate tax purposes. If BTC drops 30% in those six months, the estate's tax bill decreases — but the buy-sell valuation may be locked at the date-of-death price. The buy-sell agreement should address whether the alternate valuation election affects the purchase price.

Independent Appraisal

Each side selects an appraiser, and if they disagree, a third appraiser breaks the tie. This is the gold standard for traditional businesses but introduces timing problems for Bitcoin. Appraisals take 60-90 days. Bitcoin's price can move 40% in that window. The appraisal may reflect a market that no longer exists by the time the buyout closes.

The optimal approach for most Bitcoin businesses: formula-based valuation with a 14-day VWAP from a specified composite of exchanges, applied to the date of death, with an independent appraisal as the dispute resolution mechanism if either party challenges the formula result by more than 10%.

Key Person Life Insurance Funded with Bitcoin Gains

Life insurance is the engine that funds buy-sell agreements. Without it, the surviving partners must either liquidate Bitcoin treasury (triggering gains), take on debt (introducing leverage into an already volatile business), or pay the estate over time (creating a creditor relationship with the deceased partner's heirs that poisons the business). None of these outcomes is acceptable for a well-structured Bitcoin business.

The optimal structure uses an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) to own the policies. Here's why this matters specifically for Bitcoin partnerships:

The ILIT Structure for Bitcoin Business Partners

Each partner creates an ILIT. The ILIT purchases a life insurance policy on the partner's life. When the partner dies, the ILIT receives the death benefit — outside the deceased partner's taxable estate. The ILIT then uses the proceeds to purchase the deceased partner's business interest from the estate (in a cross-purchase arrangement) or lends the proceeds to the surviving partners for that purpose.

For Bitcoin business owners whose combined business interest and personal Bitcoin holdings may approach or exceed the federal estate tax exemption — currently $15 million per person under the 2026 framework — keeping insurance proceeds outside the estate is not optional. It's essential. A partner with a $5 million business interest and $12 million in personal Bitcoin is already over the exemption. Adding a $5 million insurance death benefit to the estate pushes the tax exposure higher. The ILIT eliminates this problem entirely.

Funding Premiums with Bitcoin Gains

Bitcoin business partners can fund ILIT premiums by taking distributions from the partnership, selling small amounts of BTC personally, or using partnership income from mining operations. The critical constraint: annual gifts to the ILIT must stay within the $19,000 annual gift exclusion per beneficiary (2026) to avoid consuming lifetime exemption. With Crummey withdrawal rights and multiple beneficiaries, a well-designed ILIT can absorb $100,000+ in annual premiums without gift tax consequences.

The insurance amount should be calibrated to the buy-sell price — which means it needs periodic adjustment as BTC appreciates. A policy sized for a $3 million buyout when BTC was $40,000 may be woefully inadequate when the same partnership interest is worth $8 million at $100,000 per coin. Build an annual review requirement into the buy-sell agreement: every January, partners recalculate the estimated buyout value and adjust coverage if the gap exceeds 15%.

The "First Offer" Right for Bitcoin Holdings

Beyond the buy-sell agreement for the business interest itself, Bitcoin partnerships should include a separate right of first offer for the entity's Bitcoin treasury. This provision requires the deceased partner's estate to offer the partnership's BTC holdings to the surviving partners before selling on the open market.

Why this matters: if the buy-sell agreement is structured as an entity redemption and the entity sells 50 BTC to fund the buyout, the remaining partners may want to purchase that BTC personally — at the entity's basis — rather than have the entity dump it on Coinbase. The first-offer right gives surviving partners the option to absorb the BTC at the entity level (maintaining the treasury) while funding the redemption through alternative means.

The first-offer right should specify:

  • A response window (72 hours is standard — fast enough to limit price risk, slow enough for partners to arrange funding)
  • The price formula (matching the buy-sell valuation methodology)
  • Whether partial exercise is permitted (a partner can buy 30 of 50 BTC being offered)
  • Transfer mechanics (on-chain transfer to a specified address vs. exchange-mediated transfer)

LLC Operating Agreement Provisions for Digital Assets

The LLC operating agreement is where crypto business succession planning lives or dies. Standard operating agreements address capital accounts, profit allocation, and management authority. Bitcoin LLCs need additional provisions that most business attorneys don't think to include.

Custody Transfer Procedures

The operating agreement should specify exactly how custody of Bitcoin transfers upon a partner's death. If the entity uses multisig (e.g., 2-of-3 keys), the agreement must address:

  • Key rotation protocol. Within 48 hours of a partner's death, the surviving key holders initiate a key rotation — transferring all BTC to a new multisig address controlled by the surviving partners and a designated successor key holder. This isn't optional. A deceased partner's key, if compromised through estate proceedings or physical access to their hardware wallet, is a security vulnerability.
  • Successor key holder designation. Each partner designates a successor key holder in the operating agreement — typically their estate planning attorney, a trusted family member who has been trained, or a qualified custodian. The successor's role is temporary: they participate in the key rotation and then relinquish the key once the new multisig is established.
  • Emergency access provisions. If the deceased partner held the only key to a single-sig wallet (which they shouldn't, but it happens), the operating agreement should include a provision requiring each partner to deposit encrypted key material with a designated escrow agent — a corporate trustee, an attorney, or a specialized digital asset custodian.

Signing Authority Succession

For Bitcoin businesses that interact with exchanges, custody platforms, or DeFi protocols, the operating agreement should specify signing authority succession. Who can authorize exchange withdrawals if the partner who managed the entity's exchange accounts dies? Which partner has authority to interact with mining pool operators, hosting providers, and energy contract counterparties?

Document every platform, every account, every API key — and designate primary and secondary authorized signers for each. This information should be maintained in a secure digital vault that the surviving partners can access without the deceased partner's credentials. A trust protector can serve as a neutral third party with authority to facilitate access transitions during the succession process.

The Bitcoin Mining Partnership Problem

Bitcoin mining partnerships present the most complex bitcoin business partner death estate planning scenario because the business contains at least four distinct asset categories, each requiring different valuation approaches:

1. Mining Equipment (ASICs)

Depreciable tangible personal property. Valued at fair market value — which for used ASICs depends on hash rate efficiency, age, condition, and the current BTC price (which determines profitability and therefore demand for used machines). A fleet of S19 XPs has a different value at $100,000 BTC than at $40,000 BTC because the machines' profitability — and therefore resale value — is price-dependent.

2. Energy Contracts

Below-market power purchase agreements are often the most valuable asset in a mining operation — and the hardest to value. A 5-year contract for power at $0.04/kWh when market rates are $0.08/kWh has substantial value, but it may contain assignment restrictions or change-of-control provisions that limit transferability. The buy-sell agreement must address whether energy contracts are valued at their intrinsic value (the spread between contract and market rates times expected consumption) or at a transfer-restricted discount.

3. Hash Rate and Operational Capacity

The ongoing revenue-generating capacity of the operation: hash rate deployed, uptime history, pool relationships, and operational efficiency. This is the "going concern" value of the mining business — distinct from the equipment and energy contracts. Valued using a discounted cash flow model based on projected BTC price, network difficulty, and halving schedule.

4. BTC Treasury

The Bitcoin held on the entity's balance sheet — mined and accumulated over time. This is the simplest to value (market price times quantity) but the most tax-complex to distribute, because the basis of mined Bitcoin is the fair market value on the date it was mined. A treasury of 150 BTC mined over three years has 150 different basis lots, each tied to the BTC price on the day that coin was mined. Tracking and allocating these basis lots in a buy-sell context requires detailed mining records.

These advantages compound when structured correctly within a partnership. If your business involves mining, understanding the tax mechanics is foundational to every succession decision.

§736 Payments to a Deceased Partner's Estate

When a partner in a partnership dies and the partnership makes payments to the estate for the deceased partner's interest, IRC §736 governs the tax treatment. This section is critical for Bitcoin partnerships and often misunderstood.

§736(b) Payments: Property Payments

Payments for the deceased partner's share of partnership property — including Bitcoin on the balance sheet, mining equipment, and other tangible and intangible assets. These payments are treated as distributions in exchange for the partnership interest. The estate recognizes capital gain or loss based on the difference between the payment received and the estate's basis in the partnership interest (which, thanks to the stepped-up basis at death, is typically fair market value at the date of death).

For Bitcoin partnerships, §736(b) payments are generally favorable to the estate because the stepped-up basis minimizes or eliminates gain recognition. The estate receives payments for the deceased partner's share of the BTC treasury at current market value, with a basis equal to that same market value. Little or no taxable gain.

§736(a) Payments: Income Payments

Payments that are not for partnership property — specifically, payments for the deceased partner's share of unrealized receivables or payments that constitute a guaranteed payment or distributive share of partnership income. §736(a) payments are ordinary income to the estate but deductible by the partnership.

In a Bitcoin mining partnership, the classification matters enormously. If the partnership has significant unrealized receivables (uncommon for miners, but possible if the entity provides consulting or services), those payments receive §736(a) treatment — ordinary income rates rather than capital gains rates. The operating agreement should clearly define which payments fall under §736(a) and §736(b) to prevent disputes with the IRS and between the estate and surviving partners.

The Interplay with Bitcoin Valuation

Here's the nuance: §736(b) payments are limited to the fair market value of the deceased partner's share of partnership assets. If the buy-sell agreement values the interest above the partner's proportionate share of asset value (perhaps including goodwill or a control premium), the excess may be characterized as §736(a) income. For Bitcoin businesses where the primary asset (BTC) is marked to market, the gap between §736(a) and §736(b) is typically narrow — but for mining operations with significant operational goodwill, it can be substantial.

Inside Basis Step-Up: The §754 Election

When a partner dies and their partnership interest receives a stepped-up basis under IRC §1014, that step-up applies to the outside basis — the estate's basis in the partnership interest. Without a §754 election, the partnership's inside basis in its assets (including Bitcoin) remains unchanged. This creates a mismatch.

Example: A three-partner Bitcoin mining LLC holds 150 BTC with an average inside basis of $20,000 per coin. BTC is trading at $100,000. Partner C dies. Under §1014, Partner C's estate receives a stepped-up outside basis in the partnership interest — fair market value of one-third of the partnership, including approximately $5 million in BTC value.

Without a §754 election, when the partnership sells Bitcoin, Partner C's estate is allocated its share of gain based on the partnership's inside basis of $20,000 per coin — meaning the estate pays tax on gain that was economically eliminated by the step-up. The §754 election fixes this by adjusting the inside basis of partnership assets with respect to the deceased partner's share, aligning inside and outside basis.

For Bitcoin partnerships with significant unrealized appreciation, the §754 election is not optional. Failing to make it forces the deceased partner's estate to pay tax on phantom gain — gain that doesn't represent real economic income because the basis step-up already accounted for the appreciation. Every Bitcoin partnership operating agreement should require a mandatory §754 election upon a partner's death.

The mechanics: the adjustment is made under §743(b), which increases (or decreases) the partnership's basis in its assets with respect to the transferee partner (the estate). The adjustment equals the difference between the estate's outside basis (stepped-up to FMV) and the estate's share of the partnership's inside basis. For a Bitcoin mining LLC with 150 BTC at $20,000 basis and $100,000 market value, the §743(b) adjustment for a one-third partner's estate would be approximately $4 million — representing the step-up that should flow through to the Bitcoin treasury.

The "Zombie Partner" Scenario: Incapacitation Without Death

Death is straightforward in one respect: it's a definitive event. Incapacitation is far worse for Bitcoin business partnerships. A partner who suffers a severe stroke, traumatic brain injury, or degenerative cognitive decline is neither dead (triggering the buy-sell) nor capable of fulfilling their management obligations. They're a zombie partner — present on the cap table but absent from operations.

Power of Attorney for Crypto

A standard durable power of attorney may not cover digital asset management. The agent named in the POA needs explicit authority to:

  • Access and manage cryptocurrency wallets and exchange accounts
  • Participate in multisig signing ceremonies
  • Execute key rotations and custody transfers
  • Vote on partnership matters involving digital asset strategy
  • Authorize Bitcoin transactions on behalf of the incapacitated partner

Without this explicit authority, the surviving partners face a nightmare: the incapacitated partner's agent (often a spouse or adult child with no crypto experience) holds a multisig key they don't know how to use, can't participate in operational decisions, and may not even know the partnership holds Bitcoin. The business grinds to a halt while the courts sort out guardianship and conservatorship.

Buy-Sell Triggers for Disability

The buy-sell agreement should define disability as a separate trigger event with its own terms. Standard practice: if a partner is unable to perform their material duties for 90 consecutive days (or 120 days in any 12-month period), the disability buyout provision activates. The valuation methodology and funding mechanism may differ from the death trigger — disability insurance replaces life insurance as the funding source, and the payout timeline is typically longer (24-36 monthly installments rather than a lump sum).

For Bitcoin businesses specifically, the disability provision should also address immediate operational concerns: within 48 hours of a partner's incapacitation being medically confirmed, the partner's designated POA agent must participate in a key rotation ceremony to transfer the incapacitated partner's signing authority to a successor. This protects the treasury from the security vulnerability of an uncontrolled key.

Case Study: The Blackwell & Torres Bitcoin Mining Partnership

Marcus Blackwell, Elena Torres, and David Park formed BTE Mining LLC in 2022 — a Wyoming LLC operating a Bitcoin mining facility in West Texas. By 2026, the operation had grown to 500 TH/s deployed hash rate, a 150 BTC treasury accumulated from mining rewards, and a below-market energy contract with a local wind farm operator at $0.035/kWh.

The partnership structure: equal one-third interests, Marcus as managing member responsible for operations and custody, Elena handling finance and energy procurement, David managing equipment purchasing and facility maintenance. The BTC treasury was held in a 2-of-3 multisig, with each partner holding one key.

On February 15, 2026, David Park died suddenly of a cardiac event at age 47. He was unmarried with two adult children (ages 22 and 24) who had no involvement in the business and no understanding of Bitcoin mining.

What Went Right

The partnership had a buy-sell agreement — a wait-and-see structure drafted by an attorney with digital asset experience. The agreement specified:

  • BTC treasury valued at the 14-day VWAP across Coinbase, Kraken, and Bitstamp ending on the date of death
  • Mining equipment valued by an independent appraiser specializing in ASIC hardware
  • Energy contract valued at the net present value of the below-market spread over the remaining contract term
  • Mandatory §754 election
  • ILIT-owned cross-purchase life insurance: $3 million policy on each partner's life

Each partner had designated a successor key holder in the operating agreement. David's successor was his attorney, James Chen, who had been trained on basic hardware wallet operations and held encrypted backup material in a secure vault.

What Happened Next

Day 1 (February 15): Marcus and Elena learned of David's death. They immediately contacted James Chen, David's designated successor key holder. Per the operating agreement, they initiated the key rotation protocol — transferring all 150 BTC from the existing 2-of-3 multisig to a new 2-of-3 multisig with Marcus, Elena, and a temporary institutional custodian as key holders. The rotation was completed within 36 hours.

Week 1: Marcus and Elena notified their respective ILITs' trustees. David's estate was valued. BTC was trading at approximately $98,000 on the date of death. The 14-day VWAP came to $95,400. David's one-third interest in the BTC treasury: approximately $4.77 million (50 BTC × $95,400). Total estimated partnership value including equipment ($2.1 million), energy contract NPV ($1.8 million), and operational goodwill: approximately $18.6 million. David's one-third interest: approximately $6.2 million.

Week 2-3: The ILITs held $3 million each in death benefits. Under the wait-and-see agreement, the entity had the first option to redeem. Marcus and Elena evaluated: entity redemption would require selling approximately 22 BTC at current prices to bridge the gap between insurance proceeds and the total buyout price. They elected instead to have each partner's ILIT fund a cross-purchase — each buying half of David's one-third interest for approximately $3.1 million each. The insurance proceeds covered the purchase almost exactly.

Month 2: The §754 election was filed with the partnership's 2026 tax return. David's estate received a stepped-up basis in the partnership interest of $6.2 million. The §743(b) adjustment allocated approximately $4.5 million of basis step-up across the partnership's assets — primarily the BTC treasury and mining equipment — with respect to the interests acquired by Marcus and Elena's ILITs.

Month 3: David's estate received full payment. His two children split the proceeds evenly. They had no desire to be involved in Bitcoin mining, and the buy-sell agreement ensured they didn't have to be. Marcus and Elena continued operating BTE Mining with adjusted 50/50 ownership, updated insurance, and a new buy-sell agreement reflecting the two-partner structure.

What Would Have Gone Wrong Without Planning

Without the buy-sell agreement and operational provisions, multiple cascading failures would have occurred:

  • Custody crisis. David's key would have been inaccessible — locked in a hardware wallet his children didn't know existed, protected by a PIN they didn't have. The 150 BTC treasury would have been frozen until probate court appointed someone with authority to search David's property for the device.
  • Valuation war. David's children would have hired their own appraiser. With BTC moving daily, the parties would have argued about the "right" price for months. Legal fees would have consumed six figures.
  • Forced liquidation. Without insurance funding, Marcus and Elena would have had to sell BTC from the treasury to buy out the estate — triggering capital gains on low-basis mined coins and depleting the very asset that made the business valuable.
  • Operational paralysis. David's children, as successor members of the LLC, would have had voting rights on partnership decisions — including whether to sell equipment, modify the energy contract, or change mining pool allocations. Two heirs with no mining knowledge making operational votes alongside two experienced operators is a recipe for deadlock.

The total cost of proper planning — buy-sell agreement drafting, ILIT creation, insurance premiums, operating agreement digital asset provisions — was approximately $85,000 over four years. The cost of not planning would have been measured in millions of lost value, months of operational downtime, and the probable dissolution of a profitable mining operation.

Implementation Checklist

For Bitcoin business partnerships, the following documents and provisions should be in place before they're needed:

  1. Buy-sell agreement with Bitcoin-specific valuation methodology (VWAP, exchange specification, embedded gains treatment)
  2. ILIT for each partner owning cross-purchase life insurance sized to the current estimated buyout value, reviewed annually
  3. Operating agreement digital asset addendum specifying multisig key rotation protocol, successor key holder designations, custody transfer procedures, and exchange account authority succession
  4. Disability buyout provision with defined trigger events, disability insurance funding, and immediate key rotation requirements
  5. §754 election commitment built into the operating agreement as a mandatory provision upon any partner's death or transfer
  6. First-offer right for Bitcoin treasury dispositions, with 72-hour response windows and specified price methodology
  7. Durable power of attorney for each partner with explicit digital asset management authority
  8. Annual review requirement — partners meet every January to recalculate estimated buyout values, adjust insurance coverage, update key holder designations, and confirm custody access procedures

The cost of implementing this framework is a fraction of the value it protects. For a Bitcoin mining partnership with $15-20 million in total value, the alternative to planning is an ugly, expensive, avoidable catastrophe that benefits no one except the attorneys litigating the aftermath.
