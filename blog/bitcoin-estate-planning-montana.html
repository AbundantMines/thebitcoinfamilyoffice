Bitcoin Estate Planning in Montana

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  8 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Montana Est. 8 min read

Montana occupies a distinctive position in the Bitcoin estate planning landscape. The state imposes no estate tax and no inheritance tax, creating a baseline environment that is friendly to intergenerational wealth transfer. But Montana's advantages run deeper than simple tax absence. The Montana Trust Code, enacted in 2019 and substantially updated since, provides modern infrastructure for directed trusts, trust decanting, and multigenerational planning. Montana's strong privacy laws protect trust and LLC ownership structures from public disclosure. And for families whose wealth spans ranching operations, mineral rights, and Bitcoin holdings simultaneously, Montana offers a unified legal framework that can accommodate all three asset classes within a single succession architecture.

Bitcoin estate planning Montana demands attention to the intersection of digital asset succession, hard asset succession, and privacy — three dimensions that rarely appear in standard estate planning curricula and that most general practitioners are not equipped to handle together. This guide covers the Montana-specific legal environment and the structures that work best for Montana Bitcoin families.

Montana's Tax Advantage: No Estate or Inheritance Tax

Montana is one of the majority of U.S. states that imposes neither a state estate tax nor an inheritance tax. There is no Montana estate tax to plan around, no inheritance tax rate schedule to navigate, and no state-level gift tax. The only tax dimension for Montana Bitcoin families is federal: the federal estate tax, which applies to estates exceeding the federal exemption (approximately $13.99 million per individual in 2025, scheduled to revert to roughly half that amount after 2025 unless Congress acts). Bitcoin families with holdings approaching or exceeding that threshold need a federal estate tax plan regardless of state of domicile, but Montana at least does not add a state-level layer on top.

This does not mean Montana families can defer planning. The absence of state estate tax simply removes one urgency driver — it does not remove the need for proper succession documentation, custody continuity, and trust infrastructure. Without a comprehensive plan, Bitcoin passes through probate at death, subjecting the estate to court oversight, public disclosure, and the practical risk of inaccessible keys.

The Montana Trust Code: Modern Infrastructure

Montana's Trust Code (Montana Code Annotated Title 72, Chapter 38) was significantly modernized in 2019 and provides a sophisticated framework for trust formation and administration. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families:

  • Directed trusts: Montana law explicitly authorizes directed trust arrangements where the trust instrument separates investment direction from administrative trustee responsibilities. For Bitcoin families, this means a family member or specialized Bitcoin custody advisor can direct custody decisions — including hardware wallet selection, multi-signature threshold configurations, and key rotation protocols — while a professional trustee handles tax reporting, distributions, and compliance.
  • Trust decanting: Montana trustees have statutory authority to decant trust assets into a new trust with modernized terms without court approval, subject to the trust instrument's terms. Bitcoin trusts established before digital asset succession became well-understood can be updated without litigation.
  • Privacy protections: Montana does not require trusts to be filed publicly. Unlike states where trust registration or probate creates public records, Montana trust structures can remain entirely private — a meaningful advantage for Bitcoin families who prefer that custody arrangements and beneficiary identities not be disclosed in public filings.
  • Rule against perpetuities: Montana allows trusts to continue for up to 100 years, providing multigenerational planning horizons that accommodate Bitcoin's long-term appreciation thesis.
Montana's combination of zero estate/inheritance tax, directed trust authority, and strong privacy protections makes it a natural environment for Bitcoin families with complex hard-asset estates.

Ranching, Mineral Rights, and Bitcoin: The Montana Wealth Profile

Many Montana Bitcoin families do not hold Bitcoin in isolation. Ranch land, water rights, grazing leases, oil and gas royalties, and mineral rights are common components of Montana family wealth — and each raises distinct succession planning considerations that interact with Bitcoin succession in non-obvious ways.

A family LLC is typically the most effective integration vehicle. A Montana LLC can hold Bitcoin, ranch real property, and mineral rights interests in a single entity, with an operating agreement that governs each asset class appropriately. For Bitcoin custody specifically, the operating agreement should designate who holds signing authority for on-chain transactions, the multi-signature threshold required for transfers above specified amounts, and succession protocols for key access when a member dies or becomes incapacitated. For ranch property, the operating agreement addresses management rights, encumbrance restrictions, and right-of-first-refusal provisions that keep the property within the family.

Mineral rights succession in Montana is governed by the Uniform Disposition of Community Property Act and Montana's deed recording system. Bitcoin, by contrast, passes through the custody architecture — not through deed recording. A comprehensive Montana estate plan must address both systems explicitly and ensure that fiduciaries have authority and access to act under each.

Investment Adviser Considerations in Montana

Montana-registered investment advisers are regulated by the Montana Commissioner of Securities and Insurance, with registration requirements that apply at lower asset thresholds than SEC registration. For Bitcoin families working with local advisers, this matters because many Montana-based wealth advisers are state-registered rather than SEC-registered. State-registered advisers' fiduciary duty to clients is governed by Montana law — and Montana has been slower than some states to explicitly incorporate digital asset guidance into its investment adviser regulatory framework.

The practical implication: when selecting advisers for Bitcoin estate planning Montana engagements, families should confirm whether their adviser has specific experience with digital asset succession, regardless of registration status. Montana's regulatory environment does not yet provide the same degree of digital-asset-specific guidance available in states like Wyoming or Nevada.

Digital Asset Succession in Montana

Montana has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), codified at Montana Code Annotated §§ 72-31-1001 through 72-31-1026. RUFADAA authorizes fiduciaries — trustees, executors, and agents under a durable power of attorney — to access digital assets belonging to a decedent or principal, subject to any express override in the governing documents or the platform's terms of service.

For Bitcoin specifically, RUFADAA's authorization is necessary but not sufficient. The statute authorizes legal access; it does not provide technical access. A trustee or executor with RUFADAA authority but without the private keys, seed phrases, or hardware wallet access required to move Bitcoin cannot actually administer the asset regardless of legal authority. Every Montana Bitcoin estate plan must therefore combine the legal authorization structure (RUFADAA-compliant trust language, powers of attorney, executor authority) with a technically sound key succession protocol — maintained separately from the trust instrument itself to avoid inadvertent disclosure.

Practical Steps for Montana Bitcoin Families

  1. Establish a Montana LLC as the unified holding vehicle for Bitcoin, ranch property, and mineral interests — with an operating agreement that addresses each asset class and designates succession authority explicitly.
  2. Fund a directed trust with LLC interests, designating a Bitcoin-knowledgeable investment trust adviser to oversee custody decisions while a Montana trust company or professional trustee handles administrative duties.
  3. Implement a key succession protocol — a technically sound multi-signature arrangement with documented access procedures — maintained separately from trust documents and tested periodically.
  4. Review federal estate tax exposure if total estate value approaches the federal exemption, particularly given the scheduled post-2025 reduction in the federal exemption amount.
  5. Leverage Montana's privacy protections — structure the LLC and trust to avoid public disclosure of Bitcoin holdings and custody architecture.
