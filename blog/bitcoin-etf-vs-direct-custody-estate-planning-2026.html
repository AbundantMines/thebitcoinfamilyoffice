The Wall Street Pitch — And What It Leaves Out

Morgan Stanley's Bitcoin Trust (MSBT) launched in late March 2026. Within seven days, it attracted over $100 million in inflows — the fastest first-week accumulation of any bank-sponsored Bitcoin ETF. Goldman Sachs, not to be outdone, has filed for a Bitcoin ETF with an income strategy overlay, signaling that the largest wealth management platforms on Earth are now competing for your Bitcoin allocation.

The pitch is compelling: familiar brokerage interface, regulatory clarity, no private key management, seamless integration with your existing portfolio. Your financial advisor can buy it alongside your S&P 500 index funds, and it shows up on the same statement.

For families managing generational Bitcoin wealth, that pitch is incomplete at best — and at worst, it's the most expensive convenience fee in modern estate planning.

Wall Street solved the access problem. They did not solve the estate planning problem. In fact, for families with positions above $500K, they may have made it worse.

This analysis breaks down the structural estate planning differences between holding Bitcoin through an ETF and holding it directly — in a trust, in multisig custody, under your family's sovereign control. We are not arguing that Bitcoin ETFs are bad products. We are arguing that for high-net-worth families with multigenerational planning horizons, the convenience of an ETF carries hidden costs that compound across decades and generations.

The Step-Up in Basis Myth

The most common misconception in Bitcoin ETF estate planning is that ETF shares and directly-held Bitcoin are treated differently at death for step-up in basis purposes. They are not.

Under IRC §1014, both Bitcoin ETF shares held in a taxable brokerage account and Bitcoin held directly receive a full step-up in cost basis to fair market value at the date of death. If you bought $50,000 of IBIT shares that are worth $500,000 when you die, your heirs inherit them with a $500,000 basis. If you bought $50,000 of Bitcoin directly and it's worth $500,000 at death, same result.

The step-up is not the issue. The issue is everything that happens before death — and everything that the step-up doesn't fix.

What the step-up doesn't address

  • Ongoing fee drag — the ETF charges an annual expense ratio that compounds against your heirs' inheritance for as long as the shares are held
  • Trust eligibility — the most powerful wealth transfer strategies (GRATs, SLATs, dynasty trusts with multisig governance) require assets you actually control, not securities held at a clearing corporation
  • Embedded fund gains — the ETF itself may carry unrealized gains on its underlying Bitcoin that can trigger capital gains distributions to shareholders, independent of any action the shareholder takes
  • Sovereign custody — ETF shares cannot be self-custodied, cannot be moved outside the financial system, and depend on a chain of intermediaries that your estate has no control over

The step-up in basis is a tax tool. Estate planning is an architecture. Confusing the two is how families end up with a $10 million Bitcoin position locked inside a product designed for retail convenience.

The Fee Drag Nobody Models Over Dynasty Horizons

Let's make this concrete. BlackRock's IBIT charges 0.25% annually. Morgan Stanley's MSBT charges 0.30%. These look small in any given year. They are not small across a dynasty trust's horizon.

These figures assume the Bitcoin position remains static — no appreciation. If Bitcoin appreciates at even modest rates over the next century, the dollar-value fee extraction is dramatically higher, because the 0.25% is charged on the growing NAV.

Direct Bitcoin held in a properly structured trust has zero ongoing custody fees beyond modest multisig infrastructure costs (hardware wallets, co-signer agreements, annual key ceremony). For a $5M position, the lifetime infrastructure cost is typically under $50,000. The ETF alternative extracts over $1.1 million at a flat NAV — and far more if Bitcoin appreciates.

The fee drag on a Bitcoin ETF is not a rounding error. Over a dynasty trust's horizon, it is the single largest quantifiable cost of choosing the wrong ownership structure.

Trust Strategies ETF Shares Cannot Support

This is where the estate planning implications get structural. The most powerful wealth transfer strategies for Bitcoin families require direct ownership of the underlying asset — not a brokerage position representing indirect exposure.

GRAT (Grantor Retained Annuity Trust)

A zeroed-out GRAT is one of the most effective tools for transferring Bitcoin appreciation to the next generation with minimal gift tax exposure. The grantor funds the GRAT with Bitcoin, receives annuity payments over the GRAT term, and any appreciation above the IRS 7520 rate passes to beneficiaries tax-free.

You can fund a GRAT with ETF shares. But the value of the GRAT strategy depends on the asset appreciating significantly — and every year, 0.25% of that appreciation is extracted by the fund manager before it reaches your beneficiaries. More fundamentally, the GRAT trustee holds ETF shares at a brokerage, subject to brokerage counterparty risk, rather than Bitcoin under sovereign custody with multisig governance.

Dynasty Trust with Multisig Governance

The most sophisticated Bitcoin estate plans use multi-signature custody arrangements where the trustee holds one key, a co-signer (family member, trust protector, or professional fiduciary) holds another, and a third key is held in geographic or institutional escrow. This architecture ensures no single party can unilaterally move the Bitcoin, while also ensuring the Bitcoin cannot be lost if any single key is compromised or unavailable.

ETF shares have no keys. They are book-entry positions at the Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC). You cannot distribute them across multiple custodians, cannot implement programmatic spending controls, and cannot design governance architecture around them. The trust holds a brokerage account. That's it.

SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust)

A SLAT allows one spouse to transfer Bitcoin into an irrevocable trust for the benefit of the other spouse and descendants, removing the Bitcoin from the grantor's taxable estate while preserving indirect access through the beneficiary spouse. With the OBBBA's $15M/$30M per couple exemption, SLATs are particularly powerful for Bitcoin families that expect significant future appreciation.

Funding a SLAT with direct Bitcoin gives the trustee sovereign custody and eliminates ongoing fee drag. Funding it with ETF shares introduces a permanent annual extraction that compounds for the life of the trust.

CRT (Charitable Remainder Trust)

A CRT funded with highly appreciated Bitcoin allows the grantor to avoid immediate capital gains tax, receive an income stream, and leave the remainder to charity — with a current-year charitable deduction. The in-kind transfer of Bitcoin to the CRT is the mechanism that defers the capital gains.

Transferring ETF shares to a CRT works mechanically, but the income stream is reduced by the fund's ongoing expense ratio. Over a 20-year CRT term on a $5M position, that's over $250,000 in fees extracted before the income stream or charitable remainder.

Counterparty Risk: The Estate Planning Dimension

Estate planning is, at its core, a multigenerational risk management exercise. The question is not whether your intermediaries will fail this year. The question is whether your estate plan should depend on a chain of intermediaries surviving and performing faithfully across 50, 75, or 100 years.

A Bitcoin ETF position depends on:

  • The fund manager (BlackRock, Morgan Stanley, Goldman, Fidelity) continuing to operate the fund
  • The ETF custodian (Coinbase Custody, Fidelity Digital Assets) remaining solvent and secure
  • The broker-dealer holding the shares in your brokerage account
  • DTCC's clearing and settlement infrastructure
  • The regulatory framework permitting spot Bitcoin ETFs to continue operating

Direct Bitcoin in a multisig trust depends on:

  • The Bitcoin network continuing to operate (it has been running continuously since January 3, 2009)
  • Your key management and backup procedures remaining intact

One of these is a chain of counterparties. The other is a protocol. For a dynasty trust designed to span multiple generations, the difference in structural resilience is not subtle.

When the ETF Actually Wins

Intellectual honesty matters. There are situations where a Bitcoin ETF is the correct estate planning choice:

  • Tax-advantaged accounts: If you're holding Bitcoin in a traditional IRA, Roth IRA, or 401(k), the ETF is the most operationally simple path. Self-directed IRA custodians for direct Bitcoin exist but add complexity and cost. For Roth IRAs especially — where assets grow tax-free and aren't subject to RMDs during your lifetime — IBIT or FBTC is a clean, defensible choice.
  • Positions under $100K: The cost of establishing a proper trust, multisig infrastructure, and legal documentation exceeds the planning benefit at smaller position sizes. The ETF's simplicity wins.
  • Trustee without digital asset expertise: If your successor trustee is a family member who will never learn Bitcoin custody, and you haven't appointed a professional digital asset fiduciary, the ETF may be the pragmatic choice. A lost key is worse than a fee drag.
  • Short-term tactical allocation: If you're using Bitcoin as a portfolio diversifier with a 1–5 year time horizon rather than a multigenerational asset, the ETF's simplicity is appropriate. Estate planning implications are minimal for short holding periods.

The dividing line is clear: ETFs for convenience and tax-advantaged accounts. Direct custody for multigenerational wealth.

The ETF-to-Direct-Custody Conversion Playbook

If you currently hold a significant Bitcoin ETF position in a taxable account and want to transition to direct custody inside a trust, the process is straightforward — but the tax implications require planning.

Step 1: Assess the tax cost

Selling ETF shares triggers capital gains tax on any appreciation since purchase. For long-term holdings (over one year), the federal rate is 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income, plus the 3.8% net investment income tax for high earners. If your ETF shares have significant embedded gains, the tax cost may be substantial.

Step 2: Evaluate tax-loss harvesting opportunities

If you hold ETF shares with losses or have other portfolio losses to offset, the conversion may be partially or fully tax-neutral. Bitcoin is not currently subject to wash sale rules under IRC §1091, though this may change — consult your tax advisor on current status.

Step 3: Establish the trust and custody infrastructure

Before purchasing Bitcoin directly, establish your irrevocable trust (dynasty trust, SLAT, or GRAT depending on goals), set up multisig custody (2-of-3 or 3-of-5 depending on complexity), and document key management procedures. This typically takes 4–8 weeks with a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney.

Step 4: Execute the conversion in tranches

Sell ETF shares and purchase Bitcoin in tranches to manage timing risk and spread the tax impact across calendar years if the position is large enough to warrant it. The trust purchases Bitcoin directly through an exchange or OTC desk and moves it to multisig custody.

Step 5: Document basis and provenance

Maintain meticulous records: the date and price of each ETF sale, the capital gains tax paid, the date and price of each Bitcoin purchase, the UTXO set held by the trust, and the multisig key distribution architecture. Your estate attorney, tax advisor, and successor trustee all need this documentation.

The May 2026 Action Checklist

If you are a high-net-worth family holding Bitcoin through an ETF — or considering buying into Morgan Stanley's MSBT or Goldman's forthcoming product — ask these questions before committing:

  1. What is your total Bitcoin exposure? If it's under $100K, the ETF is fine. If it's above $500K in a taxable account, direct custody in a trust deserves serious evaluation.
  2. What is your time horizon? One generation or multiple? ETFs are fine for one-generation wealth. Dynasty wealth needs direct custody.
  3. Does your trust document include digital asset investment authority? If not, your trustee may not have legal authority to hold Bitcoin directly, regardless of what you intended.
  4. Have you modeled the fee drag? Ask your advisor to project the cumulative cost of the ETF's expense ratio over your actual planning horizon. Compare it to the one-time cost of trust establishment and multisig infrastructure.
  5. Who is your successor trustee? If they cannot manage Bitcoin custody, you need either a professional digital asset fiduciary or a trust structure that separates investment management from distribution authority.
  6. Does your estate plan have a sovereign custody component? If your entire Bitcoin exposure is mediated by financial intermediaries, you don't own Bitcoin — you own a claim on an institution that owns Bitcoin. That distinction may not matter this year. It will matter across generations.

The Bottom Line

Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs are making Bitcoin ETFs easier to buy. That is not the same as making them better for your estate plan.

For families with meaningful Bitcoin positions and multigenerational planning horizons, direct custody inside a properly structured trust remains the superior architecture — by a significant margin — on fee drag, trust strategy eligibility, counterparty risk, and sovereign control.

The ETF is a product. Direct Bitcoin is property. Your estate plan should be built on property, not products.

The question is not whether your financial advisor can buy IBIT for you. The question is whether IBIT belongs in a trust designed to outlast the institutions that created it.
