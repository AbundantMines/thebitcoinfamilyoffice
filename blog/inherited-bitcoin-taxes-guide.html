Inherited Bitcoin Taxes: What You Owe (And What You Don't) in 2026

Just inherited Bitcoin? The tax picture is simpler — and more favorable — than most heirs expect. The stepped-up basis rule eliminates capital gains on your inherited BTC. Here's exactly what you owe, what documentation you need, and the timing decisions that matter most.

Published March 10, 2026 By The Bitcoin Family Office ~2,800 words · 11 min read

If you just inherited Bitcoin, the first question most heirs ask is: "How much of this do I have to give to the IRS?" The answer depends on a few variables — but the core news is better than most people expect.

Under US tax law, inherited Bitcoin benefits from the stepped-up basis rule (IRC §1014): your cost basis resets to the Bitcoin's fair market value on the date of the deceased person's death. Every dollar of appreciation that occurred during their lifetime simply disappears from the tax ledger. If they bought Bitcoin at $5,000 and it was worth $71,000 when they died, you inherited it at a $71,000 basis — and if you sell at $71,000, you owe zero capital gains tax.

That's the good news. The nuances — documentation requirements, trust inheritance rules, estate tax vs. income tax, and timing decisions — are what this guide addresses.

The Core Rule for Most Heirs

Inherited Bitcoin is NOT taxable income. It gets a stepped-up basis equal to fair market value at date of death. Sell immediately: zero capital gains tax. Hold and sell later: you owe capital gains only on appreciation above the inherited basis. Estate tax (if any) was the estate's problem, not yours.

The Two Taxes You Need to Understand: Estate Tax vs. Capital Gains Tax

Most heirs confuse these. They are entirely separate taxes, paid by different parties, at different times:

Estate Tax — Paid by the Estate, Not You

Federal estate tax (up to 40%) is owed by the estate on the total value of the deceased's assets if those assets exceed the applicable exemption — approximately $15 million per individual in 2026. If the estate owes estate tax, it is paid from estate assets before you receive anything. By the time Bitcoin reaches you as an heir, any estate tax has already been settled. You do not pay estate tax as an heir.

Capital Gains Tax — Potentially Paid by You, When You Sell

Capital gains tax applies when you sell inherited Bitcoin for more than your inherited basis. Your basis is the stepped-up value (Bitcoin's price on date of death). If you sell at that price or lower, no capital gains. If Bitcoin appreciates after you inherit it and you sell, you owe long-term capital gains tax (0%, 15%, or 20% + 3.8% NIIT depending on your income) on the gain above your inherited basis.

Calculating Your Tax on Inherited Bitcoin: Real Examples

✅ Scenario 1: Immediate Sale

You inherit 5 BTC. Bitcoin is priced at $71,000 on the date of death → your stepped-up basis is $71,000/BTC ($355,000 total).

You sell all 5 BTC immediately at $71,000 each.

Capital gains tax: $0. You sold at exactly your basis. The original owner paid $10,000/BTC ($50,000 total). That $305,000 in appreciation is permanently erased.

📈 Scenario 2: Hold and Sell at Higher Price

Same inheritance: 5 BTC at a $71,000 stepped-up basis.

You hold for 2 years and sell at $150,000/BTC ($750,000 total).

Taxable gain: $750,000 − $355,000 basis = $395,000.

Long-term capital gains tax at 20% + 3.8% NIIT (if applicable): roughly $94,810.

Note: Because all inherited assets qualify for long-term treatment automatically (IRC §1223(9)), the 2-year hold period doesn't change your rate — you'd pay the same long-term rate even if you sold after 1 week.

⚠️ Scenario 3: Sell at a Loss

You inherit 5 BTC at a $71,000 stepped-up basis ($355,000 total).

Bitcoin drops to $55,000/BTC. You sell at $275,000.

Capital loss: $275,000 − $355,000 = ($80,000) loss.

You can deduct up to $3,000/year against ordinary income, with the rest carried forward indefinitely. Or use the loss to offset other capital gains in the same year.

The Long-Term Rate Advantage for Inherited Bitcoin

One of the most valuable — and least-known — benefits of inherited Bitcoin is that it automatically qualifies for long-term capital gains rates, regardless of your holding period. Under IRC §1223(9), inherited property is always deemed to have been held for more than one year.

This means: even if you sell inherited Bitcoin the day after inheriting it, you pay the long-term rate (0-20% depending on income), not the short-term rate (up to 37% as ordinary income). For heirs in higher income brackets, the difference between long-term and short-term rates can be 17-22 percentage points — an enormous built-in advantage.

What Documentation You Need

The stepped-up basis is only valuable if you can prove it. Here's what you need to document:

1. Date of Death Value

Establish the Bitcoin price on the exact date of death. The IRS accepts the average of the high and low prices on a recognized exchange for that date, or the closing price on a major index (CoinDesk Bitcoin Price Index, CME reference rate). Your tax preparer will need this figure to report the basis on your Schedule D when you sell.

2. Estate Documentation

Keep copies of the estate tax return (Form 706) or, if no estate tax return was filed (estate below the exemption), the estate attorney's documentation establishing the inheritance and the Bitcoin value at death. This establishes your legal basis for the step-up.

3. Form 1099-DA Awareness

Beginning with 2025 transactions, brokers file Form 1099-DA reporting digital asset transactions. If you inherit Bitcoin on an exchange and the exchange processes the transfer, they may report the proceeds to the IRS when you eventually sell. Make sure your tax preparer reconciles the 1099-DA proceeds against your stepped-up basis — not the original owner's basis. An unsophisticated preparer who uses the wrong basis will generate a massive phantom tax bill.

4. Trust K-1 if Inherited Through a Trust

If the Bitcoin comes from a trust, you will receive a Schedule K-1 from the trust's tax return (Form 1041) documenting your share of trust income, gains, and deductions. Review this carefully — the trust may have already recognized a capital gain if it sold Bitcoin to fund your distribution in cash rather than distributing Bitcoin in-kind.

Inheriting Bitcoin From a Trust: The Different Rules

Not all inherited Bitcoin gets the stepped-up basis. If the Bitcoin comes from an irrevocable trust that was outside the decedent's gross estate, the rules are different:

  • Revocable trust → stepped-up basis: Bitcoin in a revocable living trust is included in the decedent's estate → heirs receive the step-up. Functionally identical to direct inheritance.
  • Irrevocable trust (dynasty trust, SLAT, IDGT) → carryover basis: Bitcoin in an irrevocable trust was removed from the estate during the decedent's lifetime. It does NOT get a stepped-up basis at death. The trust's original cost basis carries over. When the trust distributes Bitcoin to you or sells it, gains are calculated against the original (pre-step-up) basis.
  • Inherited through a GRAT remainder: Bitcoin that passes through a GRAT remainder trust carries the trust's original carryover basis — no step-up.

If you're unsure whether your inheritance came from a revocable or irrevocable structure, ask the estate attorney or review the trust document. This distinction can mean a significant difference in your tax bill when you sell.

Critical: Get the Basis Right Before You File

Many tax preparers who are not Bitcoin-specialized will default to the original owner's cost basis (which may be very low) rather than your stepped-up inherited basis. This creates a phantom taxable gain that you do not actually owe. Before filing, confirm with your preparer that they are using the fair market value at date of death — not the original purchase price — as your cost basis for inherited Bitcoin.

Your First 90 Days After Inheriting Bitcoin: Action Plan

  1. Locate and secure the Bitcoin

    Work with the executor or successor trustee to identify all Bitcoin holdings — exchange accounts, hardware wallets, ETF shares, custodial accounts. Follow the digital asset letter of instruction if one exists. Do not transfer or sell until you have legal authority to do so (letters testamentary or trust distribution documentation).

  2. Establish the date-of-death value

    Document Bitcoin's price on the date of death using a recognized exchange closing price or index. Save a screenshot with timestamp. This is your stepped-up basis figure and will be used on every future tax filing when you sell the inherited Bitcoin.

  3. Determine whether a step-up applies

    Confirm whether the Bitcoin was in the decedent's gross estate (revocable trust, direct ownership) or in an irrevocable trust outside the estate. Ask the estate attorney directly: "Does this Bitcoin receive a stepped-up basis?" The answer determines your entire tax picture.

  4. Transfer to your own custody or account

    Once you have legal authority, transfer the Bitcoin to your own wallet or exchange account. For exchange-based Bitcoin, each exchange has a deceased account transfer process (typically 30-60 days with death certificate and legal documentation). For hardware wallets, use the seed phrase per the letter of instruction.

  5. Decide: sell now or hold?

    You face no immediate tax pressure — the step-up means selling now is tax-free at the inherited basis. Your decision is purely financial: do you believe Bitcoin will appreciate further (hold), or do you need the liquidity (sell)? If you hold, document your basis immediately and don't let that record get lost.

  6. Engage a Bitcoin-literate tax preparer

    When you sell, your tax preparer needs to correctly report the inheritance and stepped-up basis on Schedule D. Provide them with: the date of death, the Bitcoin price on that date, documentation of the inheritance, and any Form 1099-DA you received from the exchange. Verify they are using your inherited basis — not the original owner's.

State Inheritance Tax: Six States Still Have It

The federal government does not impose an inheritance tax (only estate tax, paid by the estate). But six states impose their own inheritance tax on heirs: Iowa, Kentucky, Maryland, Nebraska, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania. These states tax you as the recipient of an inheritance — at rates that vary by your relationship to the decedent and the value inherited.

If you live in or the decedent lived in one of these states, check your state's inheritance tax rules. Close relatives (spouses, children) are often exempt or taxed at lower rates. The inheritance tax applies to the total value of what you receive — including Bitcoin — and is separate from and in addition to any federal estate tax.

What If You Inherit Bitcoin That's Currently Down?

If Bitcoin has fallen below its recent highs, your stepped-up basis may actually be lower than the decedent's original cost basis in some cases (if they bought near the top). This creates a "step-down" situation — you inherit at the current lower value, and if Bitcoin rises back up, you'll owe capital gains tax on the recovery.

There's nothing you can do about this after inheritance — the basis is what it is at date of death. But it's worth understanding: inheriting Bitcoin during a price drawdown means your tax-free foundation is lower than it would have been at a price peak.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do you pay taxes on inherited Bitcoin?

+
When you inherit Bitcoin, you generally do NOT owe capital gains tax on the appreciation during the deceased's lifetime — thanks to the stepped-up basis rule. Your cost basis resets to Bitcoin's fair market value on the date of death. If you sell immediately, you owe zero capital gains tax. Estate tax (if applicable) was already paid by the estate, not you.

What is my cost basis for inherited Bitcoin?

+
Your cost basis for inherited Bitcoin is the fair market value on the date of the decedent's death — the stepped-up basis. Document this price carefully (exchange closing price or recognized index). When you eventually sell, you owe capital gains tax only on the gain above this inherited basis. If you sell below it, you have a capital loss.

Is inherited Bitcoin considered income?

+
No. Inherited Bitcoin is not ordinary income to the heir. You do not report its value as income on your tax return. Only when you sell does a taxable event occur — and then only on gains above your stepped-up basis.

How long do I need to hold inherited Bitcoin before selling?

+
No waiting period required. Under IRC §1223(9), inherited property automatically qualifies for long-term capital gains rates regardless of how long you hold it. You pay the lower long-term rate (0-20%) even if you sell the day you inherit. This is a significant advantage — no need to hold for a year to avoid short-term treatment.

What happens if I inherit Bitcoin from a trust?

+
It depends on the trust type. Bitcoin from a revocable trust (in the decedent's estate) typically receives a stepped-up basis. Bitcoin from an irrevocable trust outside the estate generally does NOT — the trust's original carryover basis applies. Ask the estate attorney which type of trust the Bitcoin came from. Review the Schedule K-1 you receive for your share of any trust taxable income or gains.
