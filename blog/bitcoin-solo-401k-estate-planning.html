Bitcoin Solo 401(k): The Self-Employed Investor's Most Powerful Retirement and Estate Planning Tool

If you're self-employed — a Bitcoin entrepreneur, miner, consultant, or freelancer — you have access to a retirement account structure that dwarfs what employees get. The Solo 401(k) allows up to $70,000 per year in contributions, a Roth option with no income limits, and checkbook control to buy Bitcoin directly without custodian approval. Here's how it works — and how it fits into a multi-generational estate plan.

Educational Content Only: This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal, tax, or financial advice. Solo 401(k) rules are complex and vary by plan document. Consult a qualified CPA, ERISA attorney, and financial advisor before establishing or contributing to a Solo 401(k).

Why the Solo 401(k) Is the IRA's Superior — For Those Who Qualify

The traditional IRA allows $7,000 per year in contributions ($8,000 if 50+). The Solo 401(k) allows $70,000 ($77,500 if 50+). For a self-employed Bitcoin holder maxing both options, the Solo 401(k) alone contributes 10x more to tax-advantaged Bitcoin accumulation each year.

Beyond the contribution limits, the Solo 401(k) offers structural advantages that matter specifically for Bitcoin investors:

  • Checkbook control: With a properly structured plan, you as the trustee can purchase Bitcoin directly — no custodian approval, no per-transaction fees, no waiting period
  • Roth option without income limits: Any high-income self-employed person can make Roth contributions — there's no income phaseout for Solo 401(k) Roth contributions (unlike Roth IRA contributions, which phase out at $246,000 for married filers in 2026)
  • Loan provision: Solo 401(k)s can allow participant loans — you can borrow up to 50% of your vested balance (max $50,000) from yourself, useful for short-term liquidity without selling Bitcoin
  • No UBIT on Bitcoin gains: Unlike IRAs, Solo 401(k)s are generally exempt from Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) — relevant if your plan invests in debt-financed property, but less so for straightforward Bitcoin holdings

Who Qualifies

A Solo 401(k) — also called an Individual 401(k), Self-Employed 401(k), or i401(k) — is available to:

  • Sole proprietors (Schedule C income)
  • Single-member LLC owners
  • Partners in a business partnership with no non-owner employees
  • Independent contractors and consultants
  • Bitcoin miners who report mining income as self-employment income on Schedule C
  • Spouse of any of the above (a spouse who works in the business can also participate, effectively doubling the household contribution limit)

Disqualifying condition: If your business has even one full-time W-2 employee other than your spouse, you do not qualify for a Solo 401(k). You would need a SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, or a traditional 401(k) with a Third Party Administrator instead.

Contribution Limits: The Math

The Solo 401(k) has two contribution components:

Employee Elective Deferral

As the "employee" of your own business, you can defer up to $23,500 (2026) of your net self-employment income — or 100% of earned income if lower. If you're 50 or older, the catch-up contribution adds $7,500, for a total of $31,000.

This deferral can be designated as traditional (pre-tax) or Roth (after-tax), or any split between the two.

Employer Profit-Sharing Contribution

As the "employer," your business can make an additional profit-sharing contribution of up to 25% of your net self-employment income (after the SE tax deduction). The employer contribution is always traditional/pre-tax — Roth employer contributions are not currently permitted in Solo 401(k) plans.

2026 Contribution Example: Bitcoin Miner with $200K Net SE Income

  • Net SE income: $200,000
  • SE tax deduction (half of SE tax): $14,130
  • Compensation for plan purposes: $200,000 − $14,130 = $185,870
  • Employee deferral (Roth): $23,500
  • Employer profit-sharing (25% of $185,870): $46,468
  • Total 2026 contribution: $23,500 + $46,468 = $69,968 (near the $70,000 cap)
  • Roth portion: $23,500 (all future appreciation on this portion is tax-free)
  • Pre-tax portion: $46,468 (tax-deferred; ordinary income when distributed)

If both spouses work in the business, each can contribute up to the annual limit — potentially $140,000+ per year in combined tax-advantaged Bitcoin accumulation for a married self-employed couple.

Checkbook Control: Buying Bitcoin Directly

The standard Solo 401(k) offered by major brokerages (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab) does not support alternative assets like Bitcoin. To hold Bitcoin in a Solo 401(k), you need a self-directed Solo 401(k) with checkbook control — a structure where:

  1. The plan is established with a self-directed Third Party Administrator (TPA) — companies like Nabers Group, Broad Financial, or Rocket Dollar
  2. The plan establishes its own checking account (and optionally a brokerage account) in the plan's name
  3. You, as the trustee of the plan, direct investments directly from the plan account — including purchases of Bitcoin on exchanges, transfer to hardware wallets titled to the plan, or any other alternative investment
  4. No custodian approves or processes individual transactions — you act as trustee with full investment discretion

The checkbook control structure eliminates the per-transaction fees and delays of traditional custodial IRAs. For active Bitcoin accumulation — regular purchases, DCA strategies, or rapid repositioning — this is significantly more efficient.

Bitcoin Custody Inside a Solo 401(k)

When you purchase Bitcoin inside a checkbook-controlled Solo 401(k), the Bitcoin must be titled to the plan — not to you personally. This means:

  • Exchange accounts must be opened in the plan's name (e.g., "John Smith Solo 401(k) Plan" with the plan's EIN)
  • Hardware wallet seed phrases should be stored as plan assets — not commingled with personal wallets
  • Any co-mingling of plan and personal Bitcoin is a prohibited transaction and can disqualify the entire plan, triggering immediate taxation of all plan assets

The plan trustee (you) manages the Bitcoin in a fiduciary capacity — you have investment discretion but you're acting as trustee, not as a personal investor.

The Roth Solo 401(k): No Income Limit, Maximum Tax-Free Accumulation

This is the most powerful feature for high-income Bitcoin holders: Roth Solo 401(k) contributions have no income limit. If you earn $500,000 in self-employment income, you can still designate your entire $23,500 employee deferral as Roth — building a tax-free Bitcoin position without the income phaseout that blocks high earners from contributing to a Roth IRA.

Key insight: Roth Solo 401(k) accounts are subject to RMDs at age 73 — unlike Roth IRAs. However, this is easily solved: roll the Roth Solo 401(k) balance into a Roth IRA before age 73. Once in a Roth IRA, there are no RMDs for the owner's lifetime. Most Solo 401(k) holders execute this rollover when they reduce or cease self-employment activity.

Bitcoin Miners: The Solo 401(k) Is Built for You

Bitcoin miners who operate as sole proprietors or single-member LLCs and report mining income on Schedule C are ideal Solo 401(k) candidates. Here's why:

  • Mining income is self-employment income: Schedule C net profit qualifies as earned income for Solo 401(k) contribution purposes
  • Mining depreciation reduces SE income: Bonus depreciation and Section 179 deductions on mining equipment reduce your net SE income — and thus the employer profit-sharing contribution limit. Plan your depreciation timing carefully with a CPA to balance current deductions against retirement contribution room
  • Double benefit: Mining income funds Solo 401(k) contributions; depreciation on equipment reduces taxable income. Combined, you accumulate Bitcoin tax-advantaged while reducing the effective tax cost of the mining operation
  • Self-mined Bitcoin in the plan: You cannot contribute Bitcoin you mined directly to the Solo 401(k) — contributions must be in cash. However, you can contribute the cash equivalent of mining income and then purchase Bitcoin inside the plan with checkbook control

Estate Planning: How the Solo 401(k) Passes to Heirs

Beneficiary Designation — Not the Trust

Unlike personal Bitcoin held in a revocable trust, a Solo 401(k) passes to heirs through beneficiary designations — not through your will or trust. The plan documents specify who receives the account at your death, independent of your estate plan. This means:

  • Your trust does not automatically receive your Solo 401(k) — unless the trust is named as beneficiary
  • Beneficiary designations must be kept current — an ex-spouse named as beneficiary before divorce may still inherit if the designation was never updated
  • A surviving spouse has special rights: they can roll the inherited 401(k) into their own IRA, avoiding the 10-year forced withdrawal rule that applies to other beneficiaries

Surviving Spouse Rollover

A surviving spouse who inherits a Solo 401(k) can roll the entire balance into their own IRA — traditional or Roth — and treat it as their own retirement account. No 10-year forced withdrawal. No immediate income tax. The Bitcoin continues compounding inside the IRA on the spouse's timeline, subject only to RMDs beginning at age 73.

For Roth Solo 401(k) assets: the surviving spouse rolls to a Roth IRA — no RMDs during their lifetime, all future distributions tax-free. This is the most estate-tax and income-tax efficient outcome available for Solo 401(k) assets.

Non-Spouse Heirs: The 10-Year Rule

Non-spouse beneficiaries (adult children, grandchildren) must withdraw the entire inherited Solo 401(k) balance within 10 years of the original owner's death. There are no annual RMDs within those 10 years — they can take distributions in any pattern they choose (all in year 1, spread evenly, or all in year 10). For traditional (pre-tax) Solo 401(k) assets, each distribution is ordinary income. For Roth Solo 401(k) assets, each distribution is income-tax-free.

The estate planning implication: a large traditional Solo 401(k) creates a "tax time bomb" for heirs — they must recognize significant ordinary income within 10 years. Converting to Roth during your lifetime (via in-plan Roth conversion, if your plan allows it, or by rolling to a traditional IRA and then converting) defuses this time bomb.

Naming a Trust as Beneficiary

If you want your Solo 401(k) to benefit minor children or heirs under an incentive structure, you can name a conduit trust or accumulation trust as beneficiary. This is complex and requires specific trust drafting to comply with IRS beneficiary rules — a generic revocable trust named as beneficiary may not achieve the intended result. Work with an ERISA attorney if you're considering a trust beneficiary for your Solo 401(k).

Prohibited Transactions: What to Avoid

The IRS Solo 401(k) rules prohibit transactions between the plan and "disqualified persons" — yourself, your spouse, your lineal descendants, and entities you control. The most important prohibited transactions for Bitcoin investors:

  • ❌ Selling Bitcoin from your personal wallet to the plan (even at fair market value)
  • ❌ Using plan Bitcoin as collateral for a personal loan
  • ❌ Personally using the plan's hardware wallet or exchange account
  • ❌ Paying yourself fees for managing the plan's Bitcoin investments (beyond reasonable trustee compensation where permitted)
  • ❌ Contributing Bitcoin in-kind — contributions must be cash; Bitcoin is then purchased inside the plan

A prohibited transaction can trigger immediate disqualification of the entire plan — all assets become taxable in the year of disqualification. The penalties are severe. When in doubt, get a written opinion from a qualified ERISA attorney before any non-standard transaction.

Setting Up a Bitcoin Solo 401(k): The Steps

  1. Confirm eligibility: Verify you have self-employment income (Schedule C, partnership K-1, or S-corp W-2 with ownership) and no full-time non-spouse employees
  2. Choose a provider: Select a self-directed Solo 401(k) administrator that supports alternative assets and checkbook control — Nabers Group, Broad Financial, Rocket Dollar, or similar
  3. Adopt the plan document: The TPA provides an IRS-approved plan document. You sign as the employer and as the plan trustee. The plan adoption agreement specifies Roth option, loan provision, investment authority
  4. Obtain an EIN for the plan: The plan itself has its own EIN, separate from your business EIN
  5. Open plan accounts: A business checking account in the plan's name (for checkbook control) and/or a brokerage account. Exchange accounts for Bitcoin purchases go in the plan's name with the plan's EIN
  6. Fund the plan: Make your first employee deferral contribution from your business account. The plan document specifies the timing and mechanics
  7. Purchase Bitcoin: Using checkbook control, direct the plan to purchase Bitcoin on an exchange account titled to the plan. Transfer to hardware wallet titled to the plan if desired
  8. Name beneficiaries: Complete beneficiary designation forms — typically spouse as primary, children or trust as contingent. Keep designations current
  9. Annual compliance: Plans with assets over $250,000 must file Form 5500-EZ annually with the IRS. Plans below that threshold are exempt until they exceed it

The SEP-IRA is simpler to administer and may be preferable if the employer profit-sharing contribution alone gets you to the $70,000 cap without needing the employee deferral component.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I hold Bitcoin in a Solo 401(k)?

Yes — with a self-directed Solo 401(k) with checkbook control. The plan holds its own checking and/or brokerage account, and you as the trustee purchase Bitcoin directly without custodian approval. Exchange accounts must be titled to the plan (not to you personally). This structure lets you buy Bitcoin on any exchange, store on a hardware wallet titled to the plan, and manage the position exactly as you would personally — with the plan's tax benefits.

How much can I contribute to a Bitcoin Solo 401(k)?

In 2026: up to $70,000 per year ($77,500 if 50+). This combines an employee deferral ($23,500 max) and an employer profit-sharing contribution (up to 25% of net SE income). A miner or entrepreneur earning $200,000 can typically contribute nearly the full $70,000 annually — about 10x the IRA limit of $7,000. Both spouses who work in the business can each contribute up to the limit.

What is the Roth Solo 401(k) option?

A Roth Solo 401(k) lets you designate your employee deferral contributions ($23,500 max; $31,000 if 50+) as after-tax/Roth — all future growth and qualified distributions are permanently tax-free. Crucially, there is no income limit on Roth Solo 401(k) contributions — high earners who can't contribute to a Roth IRA can still make full Roth Solo 401(k) contributions. Roll to a Roth IRA before age 73 to avoid RMDs.

Who qualifies for a Solo 401(k)?

Any self-employed individual with no full-time W-2 employees other than a spouse: sole proprietors, single-member LLC owners, independent contractors, freelancers, and Bitcoin miners reporting Schedule C income. If you have even one full-time non-spouse employee, you don't qualify for a Solo 401(k) and need a different plan structure.

How does the Solo 401(k) pass to heirs?

Through beneficiary designations — not your will or trust. A surviving spouse can roll it to their own IRA (no 10-year rule; unlimited deferral). Non-spouse heirs must withdraw within 10 years of your death; Roth distributions are tax-free, traditional distributions are ordinary income. Keep beneficiary designations current — they override your will. For complex heir situations (minor children, incentive conditions), consult an ERISA attorney about naming a trust as beneficiary.
