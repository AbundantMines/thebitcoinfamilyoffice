What Just Happened — The Hormuz Blockade in Context

On Sunday, April 12, 2026, President Trump announced that the United States Navy would "begin the process of blockading any and all ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz." The order came hours after Vice President J.D. Vance confirmed that US-Iranian negotiators in Islamabad, Pakistan, had failed to reach an extended ceasefire after nearly 20 hours of talks.

The USS Frank Peterson and USS Michael Murphy transited the waterway as part of a mine clearance and freedom-of-navigation operation. Trump stated that all 28 of Iran's mine-laying boats had been destroyed. An estimated 2,000 ships — including six cruise liners and numerous oil tankers — remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, along with approximately 20,000 seafarers.

This is not a new crisis. It is the latest and most severe escalation in a conflict that began with US airstrikes against Iran in late February 2026. Iran responded by restricting Strait traffic from 138 vessels per day to as few as four or five. We covered the initial estate planning implications in our March 10 analysis of the oil shock and the Bitcoin-stock decoupling during the tariff panic.

What changed today: the situation moved from restricted passage to active naval blockade. Diplomacy failed. Military operations are underway. NATO allies — Germany, Italy, Japan, the United Kingdom — declined to participate. Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council resolution authorizing force to reopen the waterway.

The planning implication: If you were waiting for a ceasefire to crystallize before making estate planning decisions, that clarity just evaporated. The conflict has entered a new phase with no diplomatic off-ramp visible. For Bitcoin families, every week spent waiting for geopolitical resolution is a week where your planning window may be narrowing — not because of the conflict itself, but because of what it's doing to oil prices, inflation expectations, and the IRS 7520 rate.

Oil Above $100, the 7520 Rate, and Your GRAT Window

Here is the estate planning chain reaction that most Bitcoin families are not tracking:

Oil above $100 → inflationary pressure → Fed holds rates or hikes → Treasury yields stay elevated → IRS Section 7520 rate stays high or rises → your GRAT hurdle rate goes up → less value passes tax-free to beneficiaries.

Let's unpack each link.

The 7520 Rate Mechanics

The IRS Section 7520 rate is 120% of the applicable federal midterm rate, rounded to the nearest 0.2%. It's used to calculate the present value of annuities, life estates, and remainder interests — including Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs).

A higher 7520 rate means the annuity portion of a GRAT is worth more, which means the remainder — the part that passes tax-free to your beneficiaries — is worth less. In plain English: the higher the 7520 rate, the harder your GRAT has to work before your family gets the benefit.

With oil above $100 and the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, energy-driven inflation is not going away soon. The Fed, which had been signaling potential rate cuts, is now boxed in. If the 7520 rate moves from 5% to 7% over the next two quarters, a family running a $10 million Bitcoin GRAT loses $410,000 in tax-free transfer value compared to funding it today.

Time-sensitive: The 7520 rate is published monthly. You lock in the rate for the month in which you fund the GRAT. If you believe oil prices will remain elevated — and with the Strait blockaded, there is no near-term catalyst for them to fall — then every month you delay risks a higher hurdle rate. Consider whether funding a GRAT this month, at the current 7520 rate, is more favorable than waiting for geopolitical "resolution."

The "Waiting for Clarity" Trap — Why Inaction Is the Most Expensive Strategy

Since the Iran conflict began in February, we've heard the same refrain from advisors across the wealth management industry: "Let's wait until things settle down before making any big estate planning moves."

Let's examine what that advice has cost.

A family that decided to "wait for clarity" on March 1, when Bitcoin was at $68,000, has now watched:

  • Bitcoin oscillate between $65,000 and $74,000 for six weeks — never providing a clear "safe" entry point
  • The 7520 rate tick up, making GRATs marginally less efficient each month
  • A ceasefire come and go without permanent resolution
  • Peace talks collapse with no next meeting scheduled
  • The US Navy physically enter the Strait to clear mines — an escalation beyond anything in February or March

The family is exactly where it was on March 1. No trust funded. No GRAT initiated. No gifts made. Six weeks lost. And the situation is more uncertain, not less.

The fundamental error: "Waiting for clarity" assumes that a future state of clarity will arrive that makes the decision obvious. In geopolitics, that state almost never arrives. What arrives instead is a new normal — a baseline of permanent uncertainty that simply gets priced in. The families who act during uncertainty — transferring BTC at depressed prices, locking in today's 7520 rate, establishing custody infrastructure — capture the premium. The families who wait pay more for less.

This is the same pattern we identified during the extreme fear event in March when the Fear & Greed Index hit 23. The families who gifted at $66,845 during that panic captured a mathematically superior transfer. Every BTC gifted at $66K instead of $74K represents $8,000 of additional tax-free appreciation captured per coin.

The Hormuz blockade is not a reason to freeze. It is a reason to move — because the conditions that make Bitcoin "cheap" relative to its potential are the same conditions that make irrevocable transfers most efficient.

According to multiple reports, Iran has demanded payment of approximately $1 per barrel from ships transiting the Strait — with some demands for payment in cryptocurrency, including Bitcoin.

Set aside the geopolitics for a moment and consider what this represents: a sovereign nation under maximum economic sanctions is using Bitcoin as a settlement layer for international commerce.

This is not a sanctioned individual hiding money. This is a state actor using Bitcoin's properties — permissionless, censorship-resistant, no intermediary required — to conduct trade with counterparties who cannot or will not use the traditional banking system. Whether you approve or disapprove of Iran's actions is irrelevant to the technical signal: Bitcoin works as a sovereign-grade settlement layer, even — especially — when SWIFT and the dollar system are weaponized against a counterparty.

For estate planning families, this matters for two reasons:

  1. Long-term value thesis strengthened. Every real-world use case where Bitcoin functions as the settlement layer of last resort reinforces the network's value proposition. For dynasty trusts holding BTC across 50+ year horizons, these adoption signals compound into higher long-term valuations.
  2. Regulatory clarity accelerated. Iran's use of Bitcoin for sanction evasion will accelerate US regulatory frameworks — including the SEC's non-security classification, qualified custodian rules, and potentially new OFAC compliance requirements for institutional holders. Families need estate structures that can adapt to regulatory evolution without restructuring. A Wyoming PFTC governing a directed trust provides that flexibility.

7-Step Crisis-Mode Estate Planning Checklist for Bitcoin Families

This is not a theoretical framework. These are the seven steps that families with $1M+ in Bitcoin should be executing or verifying right now — this week — in light of the Hormuz blockade escalation.

  • Step 1: Review (or create) your Investment Policy Statement. An IPS is a written document that specifies exactly what you will and will not do during market stress. It removes emotion from the equation. If you already have one, open it now and follow it. If you don't, the fact that you're making estate planning decisions during a military blockade without one is itself the strongest argument for creating it. Your IPS should specify: rebalancing triggers, gifting thresholds, GRAT funding criteria, and liquidity floor requirements.
  • Step 2: Check your GRAT exposure and current 7520 hurdle rate. If you have an active GRAT, verify the hurdle rate you locked in. If you're considering a new GRAT, look up the current month's 7520 rate and model whether funding now — before oil-driven inflation potentially pushes the rate higher — is mathematically favorable. The rate is published monthly by the IRS; you lock it at the month you fund.
  • Step 3: Model an irrevocable trust transfer at today's $71K price. Run the numbers in the table above for your specific situation. How many BTC fit under your remaining exemption at $71K vs. $85K? What is the tax-free appreciation captured if BTC returns to $126K? Have your attorney or CPA produce the gift tax return calculation so you can act when you're ready — not scramble when the window opens wider.
  • Step 4: Verify your custody infrastructure survives extended market stress. Can you execute a transfer from self-custody to a trust-held custody solution during a period when exchanges may be overwhelmed with sell orders? Is your multisig setup documented so your trustee can access it if you're unavailable? Does your custody provider have a Business Continuity Plan for geopolitical disruptions? If any of those answers are uncertain, fix them before the next escalation.
  • Step 5: Assess your liquidity position. The worst-case scenario for a Bitcoin-holding family during a geopolitical crisis is being forced to sell BTC to cover lifestyle expenses during a drawdown. If you hold 80%+ of your net worth in Bitcoin with minimal fiat reserves, you are exposed to forced liquidation at the worst possible moment. The rule of thumb: maintain 12-24 months of living expenses in cash or cash-equivalents outside your Bitcoin position. Adjust up during active military conflicts.
  • Step 6: Review beneficiary designations and Powers of Attorney. A Hormuz blockade doesn't directly affect your POA. But military escalation is a reminder that incapacity planning — who manages your Bitcoin if you can't — is not theoretical. Review your Durable Power of Attorney, Healthcare Proxy, and any Digital Asset Directives. Ensure your crypto-specific POA includes authority over wallets, seed phrases, and exchange accounts. For more, see our guide on what happens to Bitcoin after death.
  • Step 7: Confirm your trustee succession plan doesn't depend on a single person or jurisdiction. If your trustee is a family member who also serves as your custody co-signer, you have a single point of failure. If your trust is governed by a state that could change its trust laws in response to federal regulatory shifts, you have jurisdiction risk. Consider a Wyoming Private Family Trust Company with multiple trust protectors and a directed trust structure that separates investment authority from distribution authority.

A note on timing: Every item above is action-ready today. None requires waiting for the Hormuz situation to resolve. None requires waiting for BTC to "stabilize." The checklist is designed for uncertainty — because that's the permanent state of the world, and the families who build their estate plans to function inside uncertainty are the ones who transfer the most wealth across generations.
