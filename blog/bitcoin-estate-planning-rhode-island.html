Bitcoin Estate Planning in Rhode Island: The $1.7 Million Threshold

By Hal Turner ¬∑ February 25, 2026 ¬∑ ~9 min read

Rhode Island has a problem ‚Äî and if you hold Bitcoin there, it may soon become your family's problem. The Ocean State imposes a state estate tax with an exemption of just $1.7 million, one of the lowest thresholds in the entire country. That figure has not been meaningfully adjusted for inflation in years. And at a Bitcoin price of $95,000, it takes just 18 coins to push a single person above it.

This is not a tax that affects only the ultrawealthy. It affects modestly successful early Bitcoin buyers, software engineers who held through multiple cycles, and small business owners who diversified into Bitcoin years ago. If you have 18 or more Bitcoin and live in Rhode Island, this article is essential reading.

Rhode Island's Estate Tax: The Basics

Rhode Island is one of a shrinking number of states that still levy a state estate tax independent of the federal system. Here is how it currently works:

  • Exemption: $1,733,264 (adjusted periodically but far below federal levels)
  • Tax rates: 0.8% to 16%, progressive on amounts above the exemption
  • No portability: Rhode Island does not allow the estate tax exemption to pass from one spouse to another upon death
  • No inheritance tax: Unlike Maryland, Rhode Island only imposes one transfer tax ‚Äî the estate tax. Beneficiaries do not pay a separate tax on what they receive

And unlike states such as Oregon, Rhode Island doesn't have the reputation of being known as a high-tax state, so many residents haven't thought carefully about estate tax exposure at all.

No Portability Between Spouses

One of the most consequential details of Rhode Island's estate tax law ‚Äî and one of the most frequently overlooked ‚Äî is that the exemption is not portable between spouses.

Under federal estate tax rules, when the first spouse dies, any unused portion of their federal exemption ($13.61 million) can be transferred to the surviving spouse through a portability election on the estate tax return. This effectively doubles the federal shelter for married couples without any trust planning.

Rhode Island offers no such relief. If you are married and the first spouse to die leaves everything outright to the surviving spouse (which qualifies for the marital deduction and avoids estate tax at that point), the first spouse's $1.73 million RI exemption is permanently lost. When the surviving spouse eventually dies, their estate is taxed with only one exemption ‚Äî not two.

üìå The Portability Gap

A married couple in Rhode Island who each own $1.5M in Bitcoin (total $3M combined) could theoretically shelter both amounts from RI estate tax with proper trust planning. Without it, the entire $3M is taxable from the surviving spouse's estate ‚Äî potentially triggering $100,000+ in state estate tax that a credit shelter trust would have eliminated entirely.

Rhode Island Is a Common Law Equitable Distribution State

Like most states outside the West and Southwest, Rhode Island is a common law state ‚Äî not a community property state. This means that assets are not automatically split 50/50 between spouses at death or divorce. Property belongs to whoever earned it, purchased it, or holds title to it.

For Bitcoin holders, this creates a straightforward ownership structure: Bitcoin you acquired with your own funds and stored in your own wallet belongs entirely to you. Your spouse has no automatic claim. At your death, the entire value of that Bitcoin counts toward your taxable estate.

This also means there is no automatic equalization of assets between spouses that might otherwise reduce the total estate tax burden. Deliberate planning ‚Äî titling, gifting, and trust structures ‚Äî is required to optimize how assets are distributed across both spouses' estates.

Rhode Island RUFADAA: Digital Assets Can Pass Through Your Estate

Rhode Island has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA). This is meaningful good news for Bitcoin holders: under RI law, your executor or trustee has legal authority to access, manage, and distribute your Bitcoin according to your estate plan or ‚Äî if you die without one ‚Äî under the rules of intestacy.

However, legal authority and practical access are two very different things. RUFADAA gives your executor the right to access your digital assets; it does not give them the private keys. Without documented access procedures ‚Äî a seed phrase stored securely, a multi-signature arrangement, or institutional custody ‚Äî your Bitcoin may be effectively irretrievable.

A complete Rhode Island Bitcoin estate plan includes both a legal structure (will, trust, durable power of attorney) and a technical access plan for your digital assets. The two must be coordinated.

Estate Planning Strategies for Rhode Island Bitcoin Holders

1. Credit Shelter Trust (Bypass Trust)

Given Rhode Island's lack of portability, the credit shelter trust is the single most important planning tool for married Bitcoin holders in the state. Here's how it works in practice:

When the first spouse dies, assets up to the RI estate tax exemption ($1.73 million) are placed into an irrevocable credit shelter trust (also called a "B trust" or bypass trust). The surviving spouse can benefit from the trust assets ‚Äî income, and in some cases principal ‚Äî during their lifetime, but the assets in the trust are not counted in the surviving spouse's taxable estate when they eventually die.

The result: both spouses' $1.73 million exemptions are preserved and utilized. For a married couple, this can shelter up to $3.46 million from Rhode Island estate tax ‚Äî instead of just $1.73 million if no planning is done.

For Bitcoin specifically, the credit shelter trust must address: how Bitcoin is held within the trust, who controls the private keys or custodial accounts, and what investment discretion the trustee has. Holding volatile digital assets in a trust requires careful drafting.

2. Wyoming or South Dakota Dynasty Trust

For Rhode Island Bitcoin holders with larger holdings ‚Äî say, 50+ BTC or significant total wealth ‚Äî an out-of-state dynasty trust can be a powerful tool for generational wealth transfer that removes assets from the Rhode Island estate entirely.

Wyoming and South Dakota are the two premier trust jurisdictions in the United States. Both offer:

  • No state income tax on trust income
  • No state capital gains tax
  • Perpetual or very long-duration trust rules
  • Robust digital asset laws (Wyoming in particular)
  • Strong asset protection features

A Rhode Island resident can establish a Wyoming-sited dynasty trust with a Wyoming trustee, fund it with Bitcoin (which is a taxable gift at the time of funding, using the lifetime gift tax exemption), and remove that Bitcoin and all future appreciation from their taxable estate permanently. The trust can benefit children, grandchildren, and beyond for generations ‚Äî without Rhode Island estate tax at each generation's death.

3. Annual Gifting Program

The federal annual gift tax exclusion allows each individual to give up to $18,000 per recipient per year without any gift tax consequences and without using any of their lifetime exemption (2024 figure, adjusted periodically for inflation). A married couple can combine to give $36,000 per recipient per year through gift-splitting.

For a Rhode Island Bitcoin holder with children and grandchildren, a systematic annual gifting program removes both the gifted Bitcoin and all its future appreciation from the taxable estate. At current prices, $18,000 per year represents approximately 0.19 BTC per recipient. Across multiple children and grandchildren over a decade, this compounds meaningfully.

One planning note: Bitcoin gifts for estate tax purposes are valued at the date of the gift, not at death. If Bitcoin appreciates substantially after the gift, that appreciation is entirely out of the estate.

4. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

A GRAT allows you to transfer assets into an irrevocable trust, retain an annuity payment for a fixed term, and pass any appreciation above the IRS hurdle rate (the Section 7520 rate) to beneficiaries completely free of gift or estate tax.

Bitcoin's volatility creates both opportunity and risk in a GRAT structure. A short-term GRAT (2-3 years) funded with Bitcoin during a period of low prices can transfer enormous appreciation to the next generation if Bitcoin rises during the GRAT term. If Bitcoin falls or stays flat, the GRAT "zeroes out" ‚Äî you get the assets back with no harm done except the trust costs.

The key risk: if you die during the GRAT term, the assets are pulled back into your taxable estate. Short-term GRATs mitigate this risk. For Rhode Island residents looking to remove Bitcoin appreciation from a taxable estate without triggering a current gift, a GRAT can be an excellent tool.

5. Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

For Rhode Island Bitcoin holders whose estate will owe tax but who want their heirs to receive full value, an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) can provide liquidity to pay the estate tax without forcing the sale of Bitcoin. The life insurance policy is held by the trust (outside your taxable estate) and pays out upon your death, providing heirs with cash to satisfy any estate tax liability while keeping the Bitcoin holdings intact.

The Urgency Is Real

Rhode Island's $1.73 million exemption is not indexed for inflation in any meaningful way. Bitcoin's price, on the other hand, has historically appreciated dramatically over multi-year periods. The math is inexorable: as Bitcoin rises, more Rhode Island holders cross the estate tax threshold without taking any action at all.

Consider: a holder with 30 BTC who bought years ago and is sitting on substantial unrealized gains has a current estate value (from Bitcoin alone) of $2.85 million. Add a modest home in Providence or Newport ‚Äî easily $500,000 to $1 million in today's market ‚Äî and a retirement account, and this person likely has $4+ million in total taxable estate. Their Rhode Island estate tax exposure, without planning, could exceed $200,000.

That same person, with a properly structured credit shelter trust and annual gifting program, could potentially eliminate their Rhode Island estate tax entirely. The difference is planning ‚Äî and time.

The earlier planning is implemented, the more flexibility and options you have. Trust structures take time to draft and fund. Gift programs take years to compound meaningfully. GRATs have minimum terms. Waiting until you're ill or until Bitcoin has appreciated further reduces your options and increases the cost of planning.

Practical Next Steps for Rhode Island Bitcoin Holders

  1. Count your coins and calculate your total estate: Include Bitcoin (at current fair market value), real estate, retirement accounts, taxable brokerage accounts, business interests, and life insurance death benefits. If you're above $1.73 million as a single person, or above $3.46 million as a married couple without trust planning, you have actionable exposure today.
  2. Audit your beneficiary designations: Retirement accounts and life insurance pass outside of your will. Make sure beneficiary designations are current and consistent with your overall estate plan.
  3. Evaluate a credit shelter trust: If you're married and your combined estate exceeds $1.73 million, discuss an A/B trust structure with a Rhode Island estate planning attorney familiar with digital assets.
  4. Document your Bitcoin access procedures: Regardless of what estate plan you implement, make sure your executor or trustee can actually access your Bitcoin. This is a technical problem as much as a legal one.
  5. Start annual gifting: If you have children or grandchildren, begin a systematic annual gifting program now. The compounding effect over time is substantial.
  6. Consult specialists: Rhode Island estate law combined with Bitcoin custody and valuation is a specialized area. Generalist estate planning attorneys often lack the Bitcoin-specific knowledge needed to draft effective plans for digital asset holders.
