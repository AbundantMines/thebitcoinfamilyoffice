Bitcoin Estate Planning in Rhode Island

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Rhode Island Est. 9 min read

Rhode Island has one of the lowest estate tax exemptions in the country — approximately $1.79 million, indexed annually for inflation. For Bitcoin families, this threshold creates an urgent planning imperative: a holder with even a modest Bitcoin position is likely within Rhode Island's estate tax scope. Unlike Massachusetts ($2M exemption) or Vermont ($5M exemption), Rhode Island's threshold means that ordinary wealth combined with a meaningful Bitcoin holding can quickly produce a six-figure state estate tax liability that families did not anticipate.

Rhode Island has no inheritance tax, so the planning focus is entirely on the estate tax imposed on the decedent's estate at death. The top Rhode Island estate tax rate is 16% — applied to amounts above the exemption on a graduated schedule. With no Rhode Island gift tax, lifetime transfers reduce the taxable estate without Rhode Island-level gift tax exposure, making lifetime planning strategies the cornerstone of Rhode Island Bitcoin estate planning.

Rhode Island's Estate Tax: The $1.79 Million Problem

Rhode Island imposes its estate tax under R.I. Gen. Laws § 44-22-1 et seq. The exemption, set at approximately $1.79 million as of 2025 (adjusted annually), is the second-lowest among states that impose an estate tax — only exceeded by Oregon's $1 million threshold in restrictiveness relative to federal exemption levels. The practical consequence for Bitcoin holders is stark:

  • A Bitcoin holder with a $1.5 million BTC position and $500,000 in other assets ($2 million total estate) faces Rhode Island estate tax on approximately $210,000 of taxable estate — not enormous, but entirely avoidable with basic planning.
  • A Bitcoin holder with a $5 million BTC position faces Rhode Island estate tax on approximately $3.2 million above the exemption — potentially $300,000–$450,000 in Rhode Island estate tax alone, before any federal estate tax.
  • Bitcoin price appreciation can push estates across the Rhode Island threshold during price rallies — and pull them below during corrections. This volatility argues for estate planning structures that are robust across a wide range of Bitcoin valuations, not just today's price.

Rhode Island's estate tax rate structure rises from approximately 0.8% on the first taxable amounts to 16% at the highest bracket. The effective rate on the entire estate above the exemption for mid-sized estates is typically in the 8–12% range.

At $1.79 million, Rhode Island's estate tax threshold is one of the lowest in the country. A Bitcoin holder who has crossed this threshold — even modestly — faces real estate tax exposure that demands immediate planning attention.

Lifetime Transfer Strategies: The Only Effective Remedy

Rhode Island has no state-level gift tax. Gifts made during the grantor's lifetime reduce the Rhode Island taxable estate at no Rhode Island gift tax cost. This asymmetry is the foundation of Rhode Island estate tax planning:

Annual exclusion gifts: The federal annual exclusion ($18,000 per recipient per donor in 2025) allows unlimited gifting to unlimited recipients without gift tax cost at either the federal or Rhode Island level. A couple can gift $36,000 per year to each child, grandchild, and trust. Systematic annual gifting of Bitcoin or LLC interests holding Bitcoin removes meaningful value from the Rhode Island taxable estate over time.

Gifts to irrevocable trusts: Outright gifts to irrevocable trusts — SLATs, dynasty trusts, GRATs — remove assets from the Rhode Island taxable estate at the time of the gift. No Rhode Island gift tax applies. Federal gift tax applies if the gift exceeds the annual exclusion and the lifetime exemption, but for estates below the federal threshold, gifts can be made entirely free of both federal and Rhode Island gift and estate tax.

Spousal Unlimited Marital Deduction: Rhode Island, like the federal system, allows an unlimited marital deduction for transfers to a surviving spouse. Bitcoin passing to a surviving spouse at death avoids Rhode Island estate tax in the first estate. However, this deferral — not elimination: the surviving spouse's estate will face Rhode Island estate tax above the $1.79M exemption when they die. Proper marital planning must address the second-death scenario, often through a Credit Shelter Trust (also called a Bypass Trust) that uses each spouse's Rhode Island exemption and avoids stacking the entire estate in the surviving spouse's taxable estate.

Credit Shelter Trusts: A Credit Shelter Trust is funded at the first spouse's death with the maximum amount sheltered by the Rhode Island estate tax exemption ($1.79M). This removes the exemption amount from the surviving spouse's taxable estate while allowing the surviving spouse to benefit from trust distributions. For a couple with a combined Bitcoin position, each spouse's Rhode Island exemption is worth approximately $1.79 million of estate tax shelter — meaning proper planning can shelter nearly $3.6 million from Rhode Island estate tax through the use of two Credit Shelter Trusts.

GRAT Strategies for Rhode Island Bitcoin Holders

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs) are particularly effective for Rhode Island Bitcoin families because they remove both the contributed Bitcoin and all subsequent appreciation from the Rhode Island taxable estate, with no Rhode Island gift tax on the transfer. A GRAT transfers Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust while the grantor retains annuity payments for a fixed term. If Bitcoin appreciates above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate during the GRAT term, the excess growth passes to beneficiaries — and heirs — free of both federal gift tax (if within the annual exclusion or exemption) and Rhode Island estate tax.

For Bitcoin's volatility profile, short-term rolling GRATs are typically preferable to a single long-term GRAT: if Bitcoin declines during the GRAT term, the position returns to the grantor (no worse off); if Bitcoin appreciates, the gain is captured and shifted to heirs. Multiple rolling GRATs allow families to repeatedly harvest appreciation above the hurdle rate while limiting downside to the returned principal.

Rhode Island Trust Law and Wyoming Situs

Rhode Island adopted the Uniform Trust Code and provides a functional trust law framework. Rhode Island allows directed trusts and trust decanting, though it has not enacted the premium directed trust provisions and perpetual trust rules that make Wyoming and South Dakota the preferred situs for multigenerational Bitcoin trusts. A Rhode Island-domiciled grantor can establish a trust sited in Wyoming — administered by a Wyoming corporate trustee — that holds Bitcoin LLC interests for the benefit of multiple generations of beneficiaries.

For Rhode Island's particularly low estate tax threshold, the Wyoming dynasty trust strategy is especially compelling: by transferring Bitcoin to an irrevocable Wyoming trust during the grantor's lifetime, the grantor removes the position from the Rhode Island taxable estate while establishing a trust that can hold Bitcoin perpetually for descendants without further estate tax at any generational level. Rhode Island LLC for Bitcoin Holding

Rhode Island LLCs (governed by R.I. Gen. Laws § 7-16-1 et seq.) provide a sound vehicle for Bitcoin holding. A family LLC allows systematic gifting of minority interests at discounted valuations — typically 20–35% discounts — reducing the Rhode Island estate tax value of transferred interests. The LLC operating agreement should explicitly address Bitcoin custody governance: multi-signature requirements, succession of key management authority, and technical documentation standards.

  1. Immediately assess whether your estate exceeds $1.79M — Rhode Island's threshold is low enough that many Bitcoin holders are already in estate tax scope without realizing it.
  2. Establish a Credit Shelter Trust plan for married couples that uses each spouse's Rhode Island exemption at death — avoiding stacking the entire estate in one spouse's taxable estate.
  3. Begin annual exclusion gifting of Bitcoin or LLC interests immediately — no Rhode Island gift tax applies, making this the most cost-effective way to reduce the taxable estate.
  4. Evaluate GRAT strategies for the core Bitcoin position — particularly effective given RI's low estate tax threshold and Bitcoin's appreciation potential.
  5. Consider Wyoming trust situs for a dynasty trust holding the Bitcoin position — removing it from the Rhode Island taxable estate while establishing a perpetual holding structure for descendants.
  6. Document Bitcoin succession explicitly — Rhode Island's RUFADAA adoption provides legal authority but not technical access; key succession must be addressed separately.
