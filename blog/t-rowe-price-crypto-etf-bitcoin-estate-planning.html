T. Rowe Price's Active Crypto ETF vs. Direct Bitcoin Ownership: What It Means for Estate Planning

March 17, 2026 • 22 min read

T. Rowe Price — the Baltimore-based asset manager overseeing $1.6 trillion — just filed an amended S-1 registration statement for its Active Crypto ETF. The filing details institutional-grade custody arrangements, an actively managed portfolio strategy, and something that caught every estate planner's attention: potential staking provisions.

This is not a passive Bitcoin tracker like BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) or Fidelity's Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC). T. Rowe is building something different — an actively managed crypto vehicle where portfolio managers make allocation decisions, rotate between digital assets, and may generate staking income. For the traditional wealth management client who trusts their advisor and wants crypto exposure through familiar channels, it sounds compelling.

But if you're a high-net-worth family — anyone holding more than $1 million in Bitcoin or considering a meaningful position — the estate planning implications of choosing an ETF wrapper over direct ownership are profound. And most advisors recommending the ETF path have not thought through what that choice actually costs their clients in terms of wealth transfer flexibility, privacy, control, and multi-generational planning.

Let me be direct: for families with serious Bitcoin wealth and serious estate planning goals, an ETF wrapper is usually the wrong tool. Not always. But usually. Here's why, and here's when I'm wrong.

What T. Rowe Price Is Actually Building

The amended S-1 reveals a fund that differs meaningfully from the spot Bitcoin ETFs that launched in January 2024. Those products — IBIT, FBTC, Grayscale's converted GBTC — are passive vehicles that simply hold Bitcoin and track its price. They do not trade around positions. They do not stake. They do not rotate between assets.

T. Rowe's Active Crypto ETF is structurally different in three ways:

Active management. Portfolio managers will make discretionary decisions about crypto allocations. This means the fund may hold Bitcoin, Ethereum, and potentially other digital assets in varying proportions. The allocation will shift based on the team's macro views, technical analysis, and risk assessment. Your exposure is not static — it's whatever T. Rowe's crypto desk decides it should be on any given day.

Institutional custody. The filing outlines custody arrangements through an institutional-grade custodian (likely one of the established crypto custodians like Coinbase Custody or BitGo). The ETF shares themselves settle through DTCC like any other security. You never touch the underlying Bitcoin. You never could.

Potential staking. Perhaps the most consequential detail for estate planners: the amended S-1 contemplates staking certain proof-of-stake assets held by the fund. If the fund holds Ethereum and stakes it, the staking rewards flow into the fund's NAV — and generate ordinary income that must be distributed to shareholders. This is not a hypothetical. It is a structural feature of the fund that has direct tax and trust planning consequences.

For context, the passive spot Bitcoin ETFs do none of this. IBIT holds Bitcoin in cold storage and does nothing else. The simplicity is the point. T. Rowe's product is designed for the investor who wants professional crypto management — the same pitch T. Rowe makes for its equity and fixed income funds. And for certain investors, that pitch is legitimate.

But estate planning is not about investment management. It's about ownership structure, control, and the legal framework governing how assets transfer at death and during life. And on those dimensions, the ETF wrapper creates real limitations.

The Core Estate Planning Divide

Here is the fundamental distinction that most advisors miss when recommending Bitcoin ETFs to high-net-worth clients:

ETF shares are securities. They sit in a brokerage account. They transfer at death via beneficiary designation or probate. They are governed by the securities regulatory framework. They cannot be self-custodied. They cannot be programmatically distributed. They are, in every legal sense, no different from shares of Apple or a Vanguard index fund.

Direct Bitcoin is bearer property. It sits wherever you put it — a hardware wallet, a multisig custody arrangement, a purpose-built custody architecture. It transfers via private key access. It can be placed into virtually any legal structure. It can be programmatically controlled via multisig governance. It is, in every legal sense, a unique asset class with unique planning opportunities.

Both get a step-up in cost basis at death under IRC §1014. Both are subject to estate tax above the applicable exemption amount ($15.06 million per person in 2026 under current TCJA provisions, with the scheduled sunset still under legislative debate). Both can be owned by trusts, LLCs, and other planning entities.

But the similarities end there. The practical differences in how each asset type interacts with advanced estate planning tools are dramatic — and they consistently favor direct ownership for families with substantial positions.

Step-Up in Basis: Same Rule, Different Flexibility

Both ETF shares and directly held Bitcoin receive a step-up in cost basis to fair market value at the date of death under IRC §1014. If you bought Bitcoin at $10,000 and die when it's at $75,000, your heirs inherit with a $75,000 basis. The $65,000 per coin in embedded capital gains vanishes. This is true whether the Bitcoin is held in IBIT, T. Rowe's new ETF, or a Coldcard in a safety deposit box.

So why does the ownership structure matter?

Because the step-up is only one piece of the estate planning equation. The other piece — often the more valuable piece for large estates — is what you do with the asset before death. And this is where ETF shares become constraining.

Consider a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT). A GRAT lets you transfer future appreciation to heirs at zero or near-zero gift tax cost. You fund the trust with an asset, receive annuity payments back over the trust term, and whatever growth exceeds the §7520 hurdle rate passes to your beneficiaries gift-tax-free. GRATs are most effective with volatile, high-growth assets — which makes Bitcoin an ideal GRAT funding asset.

You can fund a GRAT with ETF shares. But here's the operational reality: moving ETF shares out of a brokerage account into a GRAT requires coordination with the brokerage, reregistration of the shares, and ongoing custody through the brokerage in the trust's name. The brokerage may impose restrictions on trust accounts. The process adds friction and introduces counterparty dependency on the brokerage's willingness to support the structure.

Direct Bitcoin, by contrast, can be transferred to a GRAT-owned multisig wallet in a single transaction. The trust's custody is self-sovereign. There is no brokerage intermediary to negotiate with, no reregistration process, no account restrictions. The asset moves on-chain, and the trust holds it directly.

The same advantage applies to dynasty trusts, Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs), Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs), and virtually every irrevocable trust structure in the estate planner's toolkit. Direct Bitcoin is simply more portable, more flexible, and more compatible with advanced trust planning than ETF shares held at a brokerage.

The Custody Succession Problem

Here is a risk that almost never appears in ETF marketing materials: what happens when the fund closes?

ETFs can and do close. According to ETF.com, over 1,000 ETFs have been liquidated since the structure was introduced. When an ETF closes, shareholders receive the net asset value of their shares in cash. They do not receive the underlying assets. If T. Rowe Price decides in 2031 that its Active Crypto ETF is no longer commercially viable — insufficient AUM, regulatory headwinds, strategic repositioning — your heirs receive a cash distribution, not Bitcoin.

This is not a theoretical concern. It is the structural reality of pooled investment vehicles. You do not own Bitcoin inside an ETF. You own shares of a trust that owns Bitcoin. The distinction matters enormously when planning across decades or generations.

Consider a dynasty trust designed to hold Bitcoin for three or four generations. If the trust holds ETF shares and the fund liquidates in generation two, the trust receives cash that must be reinvested — triggering potential capital gains, requiring a new investment decision, and breaking the continuity of the Bitcoin position. The trustee must find a new vehicle, potentially at different expense ratios, with different custody arrangements, and with different tax characteristics.

If the dynasty trust holds Bitcoin directly via a properly structured multisig custody arrangement, none of this matters. The Bitcoin sits in addresses controlled by the trust's key holders. There is no fund sponsor to make a liquidation decision. There is no counterparty whose business viability affects your family's holdings. The Bitcoin is simply there, controlled by the trust's governance framework, for as long as the trust exists.

The counterparty stack for an ETF investor includes: the fund sponsor (T. Rowe Price), the custodian (whoever they selected), the authorized participants, the brokerage holding your shares, and DTCC. For a direct holder, the counterparty stack is: the Bitcoin network. That difference matters when you're planning in decades.

Privacy: A First-Principles Difference

ETF ownership is not private. Your brokerage knows your position. Your brokerage's custodian knows your position. The fund's transfer agent knows you're a shareholder. If you hold more than certain thresholds, SEC disclosure rules may apply. In divorce proceedings, brokerage statements are discoverable. In litigation, brokerage statements are discoverable. In bankruptcy, brokerage statements are discoverable.

Direct Bitcoin held in self-custody is private. Not anonymous — the blockchain is transparent, and any competent chain analysis firm can trace transactions if they know your addresses. But there is no brokerage statement. There is no transfer agent. There is no centralized record of your ownership that can be subpoenaed from a third party. Your ownership is known only to you and whoever you choose to tell.

For families concerned about asset protection, creditor claims, or simply maintaining financial privacy, this distinction is significant. A properly designed custody architecture with geographic key distribution and institutional-grade operational security provides a level of financial privacy that no brokerage-held ETF can match.

To be clear: privacy is not about tax evasion or hiding assets from legitimate legal processes. Directly held Bitcoin must still be disclosed on estate tax returns, gift tax returns, and in response to valid court orders. But the default state of direct custody is privacy, while the default state of ETF ownership is disclosure to multiple intermediaries, each with their own data security practices, breach risks, and regulatory reporting obligations.

Control and Sovereignty

When you hold ETF shares, you have zero control over:

  • Asset allocation decisions. T. Rowe's portfolio managers decide how much Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other assets the fund holds. If they decide to reduce Bitcoin exposure by 30% based on their macro view, your economic exposure to Bitcoin drops — and you had no say in the matter.
  • Fork participation. If Bitcoin undergoes a contentious hard fork (as it did in 2017 with Bitcoin Cash), the fund sponsor decides whether to claim the forked coins and how to handle them. You receive whatever the fund sponsor decides you receive.
  • Airdrop claims. Similar to forks — any airdrop or distribution related to assets held by the fund is managed by the sponsor, not by you.
  • Custody arrangements. If the fund's custodian experiences a breach, an operational failure, or a regulatory action, your exposure is determined by the fund's insurance and recovery processes — not by your own security architecture.
  • Lending. The fund's prospectus may or may not permit securities lending of the underlying crypto. Your assets may be lent to third parties for yield without your explicit consent.

Direct Bitcoin holders retain full sovereignty over every one of these decisions. You hold the keys. You decide the custody architecture. You claim forks and airdrops. You choose whether to lend, stake, or simply hold. For families building multi-generational wealth, this sovereignty is not a nice-to-have — it is the foundation of the entire planning strategy.

The Staking Problem

T. Rowe Price's amended S-1 contemplates staking proof-of-stake assets held by the fund. This deserves specific attention because it creates a tax problem that direct Bitcoin holders simply do not have.

If the fund stakes Ethereum or other PoS assets, the staking rewards constitute ordinary income. Under current IRS guidance (Revenue Ruling 2023-14), staking rewards are taxable as ordinary income at the time of receipt. Inside the ETF structure, this income flows through to shareholders — meaning you receive ordinary income distributions whether you want them or not.

This has three consequences for estate planning:

Trust income complications. If your shares are held in an irrevocable trust, the staking income distributions create Distributable Net Income (DNI) that must either be distributed to beneficiaries (potentially undermining asset protection) or retained by the trust and taxed at compressed trust income tax rates (37% on income above $15,450 in 2026). Neither outcome is desirable for a trust designed to accumulate and protect wealth.

Forced realization. You cannot defer or avoid the staking income. It is generated by the fund's investment decisions, not yours. The fund manager decided to stake, and you bear the tax consequences. This is the opposite of the control principle that should govern serious wealth planning.

Grantor trust mismatch. If the shares are in a grantor trust (where the grantor pays the trust's taxes), the staking income creates an annual tax liability for the grantor that was not part of the original planning design. The whole point of a GRAT or IDGT is to transfer appreciation efficiently — not to generate ongoing ordinary income that the grantor must pay taxes on.

Direct Bitcoin — actual Bitcoin, not Ethereum or other staking-eligible assets — generates zero income. It sits in custody and appreciates (or depreciates). There are no distributions, no ordinary income events, no DNI calculations, and no forced recognition. For trust planning purposes, this simplicity is a genuine structural advantage.

For Which Clients Does Each Approach Make Sense?

I've spent the last several sections explaining why direct ownership is superior for serious estate planning. Now let me be honest about when it's not — because intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that ETF wrappers genuinely serve certain client profiles better.

ETF Wrappers Make Sense When:

  • Total crypto allocation is under $500,000
  • Client has an existing estate plan built around beneficiary designations and TOD registrations
  • No appetite for trust planning beyond basic revocable trusts
  • Client or heirs are not technically capable of managing self-custody
  • Simplicity and advisor integration are the priority
  • Client wants professional active management (genuinely — not just defaulting to it)
  • Tax situation is simple enough that staking income distributions are manageable

Direct Ownership Is Superior When:

  • Bitcoin position exceeds $1 million
  • Client is oriented toward dynasty trust, GRAT, or IDGT planning
  • Privacy is a genuine concern (asset protection, creditor exposure, personal security)
  • Multi-generational holding horizon (20+ years)
  • Client wants maximum control over custody, forks, and protocol decisions
  • Tax planning requires avoiding forced income distributions
  • The family has or is willing to build proper custody infrastructure

The threshold is not arbitrary. At $500,000 or below, the cost and complexity of establishing self-custody infrastructure, creating irrevocable trust structures, and maintaining ongoing key management may exceed the estate planning benefits. The marginal value of a GRAT on a $300,000 Bitcoin position is modest. The privacy benefit exists but may not justify the operational burden.

Above $1 million — and certainly above the $5 million range where families start thinking seriously about dynasty trusts and GST exemption allocation — the calculus shifts dramatically. The estate tax savings from a well-executed GRAT or IDGT funded with directly held Bitcoin can be measured in millions of dollars over a multi-generational time horizon. The flexibility to structure custody governance, successor trustee key access, and programmatic distribution schedules is worth the operational investment.

The Hybrid Approach: Best of Both

For families in the $1–5 million range, or those who hold substantially more but want the convenience of partial ETF exposure, a hybrid strategy often makes the most sense:

Core position: direct self-custody in an irrevocable trust. This is the long-term, multi-generational holding. It goes into the dynasty trust or IDGT. It benefits from the full range of estate planning flexibility. It is custodied via multisig with geographic key distribution and documented succession procedures. This position is never sold — it is the family's permanent Bitcoin allocation.

Satellite position: ETF shares in a brokerage account with beneficiary designations. This is the liquidity layer. It has straightforward beneficiary designations for simple transfer at death. It can be sold or rebalanced without touching the core custody infrastructure. It satisfies the advisor's need for a familiar, reportable position. For many families, 20-30% of total Bitcoin exposure in ETF form provides adequate liquidity without compromising the estate planning strategy.

This hybrid approach gives the family the tax and trust planning advantages of direct ownership on the bulk of their position while maintaining operational simplicity on a portion. The key is getting the ratio right — and most advisors, trained in the brokerage world, instinctively lean too heavily toward the ETF side.

Case Study: The Hendersons and the "Simplicity" Trap

Case Study — Illustrative Example

Why Moving Everything to T. Rowe's ETF Would Be an Estate Planning Mistake

Robert and Diana Henderson are both 64. They've been accumulating Bitcoin since 2017. Their current holdings:

  • $3.2 million in BlackRock's IBIT — purchased at various prices, average cost basis approximately $18,000/BTC
  • $1.8 million in self-custody — held across two hardware wallets, average cost basis approximately $9,000/BTC
  • Total Bitcoin exposure: $5.0 million at current prices (~$75,000/BTC)

Their estate planning goals are clear: they want to establish a dynasty trust for their three children and seven grandchildren. They want to use their combined $30.12 million estate and gift tax exemption efficiently. They are long-term holders who have no intention of selling their Bitcoin position.

Their financial advisor — a well-meaning CFP at a large RIA — has recommended consolidating everything into T. Rowe Price's new Active Crypto ETF. His reasoning: "It's simpler. One position, one account, one beneficiary form. T. Rowe is a trusted name. And their active management might add alpha."

Here's what that recommendation actually costs the Hendersons:

1. It eliminates the dynasty trust opportunity. The Hendersons' self-custodied Bitcoin — $1.8 million with a ~$9,000 basis — is the ideal asset to transfer into a dynasty trust. If they contribute it now and Bitcoin continues to appreciate, all future growth occurs outside their taxable estate. With Bitcoin at $75,000 and a long-term target measured in hundreds of thousands, the estate tax savings could be enormous. Moving this position into an ETF wrapper and then trying to fund a dynasty trust with ETF shares adds unnecessary friction, brokerage dependency, and potential restrictions.

2. It creates forced income in the trust. If T. Rowe's ETF stakes Ethereum or other PoS assets, the staking income distributed to shareholders becomes DNI inside the dynasty trust. The trust must either distribute that income (undermining the accumulation strategy) or pay trust-level taxes at 37% on income above $15,450. The Hendersons' self-custodied Bitcoin generates zero income — which is exactly what you want inside an irrevocable accumulation trust.

3. It surrenders the step-up advantage. Both the IBIT shares and the self-custodied Bitcoin will receive a step-up in basis at death. But here's the subtlety: the Hendersons' self-custodied Bitcoin has a $9,000 basis. If they hold it until death at $75,000+, the step-up eliminates $66,000+ per coin in embedded gains. That's ideal for the position they keep in their estate (not the trust). Transferring everything to an ETF doesn't change the step-up math, but it removes the flexibility to strategically allocate low-basis Bitcoin to the trust (where growth escapes estate tax) and high-basis Bitcoin to positions held personally (where the step-up eliminates gains at death).

4. It introduces counterparty risk to a multi-generational plan. The Hendersons want their dynasty trust to hold Bitcoin for their grandchildren and potentially great-grandchildren. That's a 40-60 year time horizon. The probability that T. Rowe Price's Active Crypto ETF exists in its current form in 2066 is approximately zero. Fund mergers, closures, and reorganizations are routine in the asset management industry. Self-custodied Bitcoin with a properly documented key succession plan has no counterparty to fail.

5. It destroys privacy. Self-custodied Bitcoin held in a properly structured trust provides meaningful financial privacy. ETF shares in a brokerage account do not. For a family with $5 million in Bitcoin, privacy is not a luxury — it is a security consideration.

The better approach for the Hendersons:

  • Transfer the $1.8M self-custodied Bitcoin into a dynasty trust with multisig custody governed by the trust instrument
  • Use a portion of their exemption to shelter the transfer from gift tax
  • Keep the $3.2M IBIT position in their personal brokerage account for simplicity and liquidity, with beneficiary designations as a backstop
  • Consider a GRAT funded with a portion of the IBIT shares if they want to transfer additional appreciation to the dynasty trust
  • Do not move to T. Rowe's actively managed ETF unless there is a specific, documented investment thesis for active crypto management — and there almost certainly is not one that justifies the estate planning cost

The Advisor's Blind Spot

The Hendersons' advisor is not incompetent. He is optimizing for what he knows: brokerage-held securities with beneficiary designations. That framework works for stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. It does not work for Bitcoin at scale because it treats Bitcoin as just another financial asset when it is, structurally, something different.

Bitcoin's unique properties — bearer asset characteristics, self-custody capability, programmatic control via multisig, and zero forced income — make it the most trust-compatible asset in existence. No other asset can be transferred into a trust with a single on-chain transaction, custodied without any third-party intermediary, governed by a key management protocol written into the trust instrument, and held indefinitely with zero maintenance income or forced distributions.

Advisors who recommend consolidating large Bitcoin positions into ETF wrappers are, often unknowingly, stripping their clients of these advantages. They are converting an asset with extraordinary estate planning flexibility into a generic security with ordinary estate planning characteristics. For small positions, the trade-off is worth the simplicity. For large positions, it is not.

Action Steps in Light of T. Rowe's Filing

If you or your clients hold significant Bitcoin and are evaluating the new T. Rowe Price Active Crypto ETF — or any crypto ETF — against direct ownership, here is the framework:

  1. Audit your current ownership structure. What percentage of your Bitcoin is in ETF wrappers versus direct custody? Is the allocation intentional or accidental?
  2. Define your estate planning goals. Are you planning for simple beneficiary transfer, or are you building a multi-generational trust structure? The answer determines whether an ETF wrapper helps or hurts.
  3. Calculate the basis allocation. Which positions have the lowest cost basis? Those are your best candidates for irrevocable trust funding (where future appreciation escapes your estate). Which positions have the highest basis? Those may be better held personally for the step-up.
  4. Evaluate the staking income impact. If you're considering T. Rowe's active ETF specifically, model the impact of staking income distributions on your trust structure. If you're using grantor trusts, model the additional tax burden on the grantor.
  5. Assess your custody capability. Do you or your family have the technical infrastructure and institutional knowledge to manage self-custody across generations? If not, is building that capability worth the estate planning benefits? For positions over $1 million, the answer is almost always yes.
  6. Consider the hybrid approach. You do not have to choose one or the other. A core direct-custody position in a trust structure plus a satellite ETF position for liquidity may be the optimal architecture.
  7. Engage Bitcoin-specific counsel. General estate planning attorneys understand trusts and tax code. They may not understand multisig custody, key succession, or the operational mechanics of holding Bitcoin in a trust. You need both skill sets — and you need them working together.

The Bigger Picture

T. Rowe Price entering the crypto ETF market is, on balance, a positive signal. It normalizes Bitcoin ownership for institutional and retail investors. It creates another on-ramp for capital that would otherwise never touch digital assets. It adds another data point in the long march toward mainstream adoption.

But normalization through familiar wrappers comes with trade-offs. An ETF makes Bitcoin legible to the traditional financial system — and in doing so, it strips away many of the properties that make Bitcoin uniquely powerful for estate planning. The self-custody capability. The bearer asset characteristics. The zero-income profile. The counterparty-free existence. The privacy.

For families with modest positions who want simple exposure, T. Rowe's ETF — or IBIT, or FBTC — is perfectly fine. Buy the shares, set a beneficiary, move on with your life.

For families building serious, multi-generational Bitcoin wealth, the ETF is a convenience that costs more than it saves. Direct ownership, held in properly structured trusts with purpose-built custody architecture, remains the gold standard for Bitcoin estate planning. No pun intended.

The question is not whether to own Bitcoin. That question has been answered by $1.6 trillion asset managers filing S-1 registrations. The question is how to own Bitcoin — and for families with real wealth and real planning goals, the answer is still: directly, deliberately, and in structures that preserve every advantage the asset has to offer.
