Bitcoin Estate Planning in Pennsylvania

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Pennsylvania Est. 9 min read

Pennsylvania presents a distinctly challenging estate planning environment for Bitcoin families because of its inheritance tax — a transfer tax applied not to the estate itself, but to the beneficiary who receives assets at death, based on their relationship to the decedent. Unlike an estate tax (which is paid before heirs receive anything), Pennsylvania's inheritance tax is paid by the recipients: children and grandchildren pay 4.5%, siblings pay 12%, and all other beneficiaries (with very limited exceptions) pay 15%. Pennsylvania does not have a separate estate tax — but its inheritance tax applies to virtually every Bitcoin transfer at death, regardless of estate size.

For a Pennsylvania Bitcoin family passing Bitcoin to a child, a $1 million Bitcoin position generates $45,000 in Pennsylvania inheritance tax. For a $5 million position, the inheritance tax to direct descendants is $225,000. These are not hypothetical numbers — they are the actual cost of failing to plan proactively. Bitcoin estate planning in Pennsylvania requires advisors who understand both the inheritance tax structure and the trust and lifetime transfer strategies that can reduce or eliminate this obligation before it arises.

The Pennsylvania Inheritance Tax: What Bitcoin Families Must Know

Pennsylvania's inheritance tax is codified at 72 Pa. Stat. § 9101 et seq. It is imposed on the privilege of receiving property from a decedent who was a Pennsylvania resident at death. The tax rates by beneficiary class are:

  • Surviving spouse: 0% (exempt)
  • Children and grandchildren (lineal descendants): 4.5%
  • Siblings: 12%
  • All other beneficiaries: 15%

The inheritance tax applies to the gross value of assets passing at death — Bitcoin is no exception. Whether Bitcoin passes through a will, a revocable trust, a beneficiary designation, or by intestacy, it is subject to Pennsylvania inheritance tax at the applicable rate. There is no exemption based on the size of the estate, no exclusion for digital assets, and no basis step-up equivalent that reduces the taxable value for inheritance tax purposes.

Pennsylvania does allow a 5% discount if the inheritance tax is paid within three months of the decedent's death — an incentive to pay quickly that also provides modest planning value for estates with sufficient liquidity. But the core planning imperative is reducing the amount of Bitcoin subject to inheritance tax at all, through lifetime transfer strategies that move assets out of the estate before death.

Pennsylvania's inheritance tax applies to every Bitcoin transfer at death — to children, siblings, and other beneficiaries — regardless of estate size. There is no exemption threshold. Lifetime planning is the only way to reduce this burden permanently.

Lifetime Transfers: The Primary Strategy for Pennsylvania Bitcoin Families

The most effective strategy for reducing Pennsylvania inheritance tax is transferring Bitcoin out of the estate during the grantor's lifetime. Pennsylvania does not have a state gift tax — lifetime gifts are not subject to Pennsylvania inheritance tax (though gifts within one year of death may be included in the inheritance tax calculation under certain circumstances). The key strategies are:

Annual exclusion gifting: The federal annual exclusion ($18,000 per donee in 2024) allows gifts to heirs each year without federal gift tax or Pennsylvania inheritance tax consequences. Fractional LLC interests or direct Bitcoin positions can be gifted under the annual exclusion, systematically reducing the taxable estate at no transfer tax cost to either the donor or recipient.

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs): A GRAT transfers a Bitcoin position to an irrevocable trust while retaining annuity payments for a fixed term. If Bitcoin appreciates above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate during the GRAT term, the excess passes to heirs free of Pennsylvania inheritance tax and federal estate tax. Pennsylvania imposes no gift tax on the GRAT transfer. Appreciation that compounds within the trust for years after the GRAT term is fully outside the Pennsylvania inheritance tax base.

Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs) — Installment Sales: A sale of Bitcoin to an IDGT for a promissory note removes the Bitcoin and all future appreciation from the Pennsylvania inheritance tax base at the time of sale. The sale is not a taxable gift, so no Pennsylvania inheritance tax applies to the transfer. The grantor receives interest payments on the note — which are subject to Pennsylvania income tax but not inheritance tax. At the grantor's death, only the remaining note balance is subject to inheritance tax, not the full value of the Bitcoin held in the trust.

Irrevocable Trusts Funded With Lifetime Exemption: Gifts to irrevocable trusts using the federal lifetime exemption remove assets from the Pennsylvania inheritance tax base. Because Pennsylvania has no state gift tax, the cost is only the use of federal exemption — with no Pennsylvania-level transfer tax at the time of funding.

Pennsylvania Trust Law and Bitcoin Trust Structures

Pennsylvania trust law is codified at 20 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 7701 et seq. (the Pennsylvania Uniform Trust Act, effective 2006). Pennsylvania's UTC-based framework provides modern statutory authority for trust formation, directed trusts, modification, and decanting:

  • Directed trusts: Pennsylvania's trust act permits trust instruments to create separate investment director and administrative trustee roles. For Bitcoin trusts, this allows a family member or specialist to control custody decisions — including private key management and multi-signature architecture — while a corporate trustee handles distributions, tax reporting, and compliance.
  • Trust decanting: 20 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 7740.1 authorizes trustees with discretionary distribution authority to decant trust assets into a new trust with updated terms. Bitcoin trusts established before digital asset succession practices were developed can be decanted into modernized instruments without court approval in appropriate circumstances.
  • Perpetual trusts: Pennsylvania permits trusts to continue beyond the traditional Rule Against Perpetuities period. An inter vivos trust (created during the grantor's lifetime) can hold Bitcoin in perpetuity if the trust instrument opts out of the Rule Against Perpetuities under 20 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 6104. A Pennsylvania dynasty trust using the federal GST exemption can accumulate Bitcoin across generations without estate or inheritance tax at each generational transfer.

Digital Asset Succession Under Pennsylvania RUFADAA

Pennsylvania enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) at 20 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 3901 et seq. Pennsylvania RUFADAA provides executors, trustees, and attorneys-in-fact with legal authority to access digital assets — including Bitcoin — after death or incapacity. The priority hierarchy: online tool designations control first; governing instrument authority controls second; statutory defaults apply last.

As in every state, Pennsylvania RUFADAA provides legal authorization, not technical access. A fiduciary authorized by Pennsylvania law to access Bitcoin still needs the private keys, hardware wallet PINs, and multi-signature credentials to actually do so. Pennsylvania Bitcoin estate plans must pair RUFADAA authority in all governing documents with a separate, secured technical succession protocol — specifying the exact custody architecture and step-by-step recovery process — stored and maintained independently from the estate plan documents themselves.

Pennsylvania LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Pennsylvania LLCs (governed by the Pennsylvania Consolidated Statutes Title 15, § 8811 et seq.) provide effective holding vehicles for Bitcoin with inheritance tax planning advantages. Bitcoin held through a properly structured family LLC may qualify for minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts — typically 20–35% — when interests pass at death to heirs, reducing the Pennsylvania inheritance tax base on those interests. Minority LLC interests are valued at a discount to the underlying Bitcoin value, providing meaningful inheritance tax savings proportional to the discount applied.

More importantly, LLC interests gifted during the grantor's lifetime remove the underlying Bitcoin from the Pennsylvania inheritance tax base entirely — with the only cost being the consumption of federal annual exclusion and lifetime exemption amounts. A family LLC is therefore not merely an administrative structure; it is a central vehicle for the lifetime transfer program that reduces Pennsylvania inheritance tax over time.

Practical Priorities for Pennsylvania Bitcoin Families

  1. Understand the Pennsylvania inheritance tax rate structure applicable to your specific heirs — children pay 4.5%, siblings 12%, others 15% — and quantify the inheritance tax exposure based on your current Bitcoin position.
  2. Implement a lifetime gifting program immediately — Pennsylvania has no gift tax, so every dollar of Bitcoin transferred during life avoids the inheritance tax that would otherwise apply at death.
  3. Establish a Pennsylvania LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle, with an operating agreement governing custody and succession, and gift minority LLC interests to children or trusts under the annual exclusion and lifetime exemption.
  4. Consider GRAT and installment sale strategies for larger Bitcoin positions — both remove Bitcoin appreciation from the inheritance tax base at minimal or zero transfer tax cost.
  5. Document RUFADAA authority explicitly in all governing instruments, and maintain a separate, secured technical succession protocol for Bitcoin private key and custody access, coordinated with your multi-signature architecture.
