Bitcoin Estate Planning for Tech Executives: RSUs, ISOs, and the Complete 2026 Guide

You're earning $1.5M a year in RSUs, you're sitting on 25 BTC from 2019, and you have $200K in NQSOs with a $50 strike. Your estate attorney knows about the RSUs. Your accountant knows about the Bitcoin. Neither one has ever seen both together and built a coherent plan around the interaction.

Tech executives occupy a peculiar corner of the wealth management universe. You have not just one complex asset class — you have at least three simultaneously: Bitcoin (or other cryptocurrency), company equity in the form of RSUs, and a stack of stock options that may or may not have been exercised. Each of these three positions has completely different tax treatment at vesting, exercise, sale, and death. Each requires a different trust structure. Each has different liquidity characteristics. And the IRS has ensured that exercising them in the wrong sequence or the wrong year can trigger tax bills that bear no rational relationship to the actual cash you received.

This guide is for the senior tech professional who has accumulated meaningful Bitcoin alongside meaningful equity compensation and wants to stop treating those two things as separate problems. They aren't. The interaction between a large BTC position and a large ISO exercise in the same tax year can generate an Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) liability that genuinely shocks people. The charitable strategy that makes sense for low-basis Bitcoin is completely different from the charitable strategy that makes sense for RSUs that haven't vested yet. The trust structure that lets your heirs inherit Bitcoin with maximum flexibility is fundamentally incompatible with how unvested RSUs transfer — because they mostly don't.

We're going to walk through every significant planning decision at the intersection of Bitcoin and tech equity compensation. By the end, you'll have a complete mental map of how these pieces fit together — and a concrete checklist for the sequence of moves that actually need to happen.

The Dual Wealth Problem: Why "Just Diversify" Is Useless Advice

Standard financial advice treats concentration risk as a simple problem: you have too much of one thing, so sell some and buy a broad index. If you're a tech executive with Bitcoin, that advice misses the point by several miles.

You don't have one concentrated position. You have at least two, and they interact in ways that most advisors have never modeled:

  • RSUs are W-2 income at vest — taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37% federal, plus applicable state tax, plus FICA on the first ~$170K. There is no planning lever that changes the ordinary income treatment at vest. The only real choices are (a) time the vest to a lower-income year if possible, (b) donate shares immediately at vest, or (c) sell systematically under a 10b5-1 plan. The shares that vest sit on your balance sheet at a cost basis equal to the FMV on vest date.
  • Bitcoin held for more than a year is long-term capital gains — taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% federal depending on your income bracket, plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) if your MAGI exceeds $200K single / $250K married. A BTC position purchased in 2019 at roughly $7,000–$10,000 per coin now carries enormous embedded gain, entirely at long-term capital gains rates — the most favorable tax treatment available on investment assets.
  • ISOs are not taxed at exercise for regular income tax purposes, but they are an AMT preference item — meaning the spread at exercise counts for AMT even though it doesn't count for regular tax. NQSOs flip this: the spread at exercise is W-2 ordinary income, full stop, but subsequent appreciation is capital gains.

Consider the math for a senior VP at a large public tech company: $1.5M in RSUs vesting this year (after 37% federal withholding, the take-home is roughly $945K), plus $200K in NQSOs with a $50 strike price against a $200 stock price (exercise spread = $150 × shares, all W-2 income), plus a BTC position with $1.2M in unrealized long-term gains. The RSU and NQSO income will push this exec into the top federal bracket and the top California bracket simultaneously. Any Bitcoin sale on top of that adds to MAGI, triggering 3.8% NIIT on the Bitcoin gain. The marginal tax rate on the next dollar of Bitcoin gain in that year could approach 56–57% in a high-tax state. The solution is not to sell everything — it's to understand the stack, plan the sequence, and coordinate each position against the others.

RSU Estate Planning: The W-2 Problem You Can't Avoid

RSUs are simultaneously one of the most valuable compensation tools and one of the least flexible from an estate planning standpoint. Here's why.

At Vest: Always Ordinary Income

When RSUs vest, the fair market value of the shares on the vest date is ordinary income — reported on your W-2. There is no mechanism in the tax code to convert RSU vest income to capital gains, to defer it, or to exclude it. The only tax lever that exists is timing: if you can accelerate vesting into a year with lower other income (for example, a sabbatical year, or a year you took a lower salary to join an early-stage company), you reduce the marginal rate on that income. For most senior executives whose income is itself driven by RSU vesting, there is rarely a genuinely low-income year to accelerate into.

One exception worth noting: if you are the founder or co-founder of a company that later transitioned to RSUs (rather than restricted stock), you may have a brief window where sabbatical or leave-of-absence engineering could shift a vest tranche. But for most executives at established tech companies, the ordinary income treatment at vest is simply a cost of doing business.

The Donation Strategy: Donate RSUs at Vest

One technique that does work — and is dramatically underused — is donating RSU shares immediately at vest to a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) or directly to a qualified charity. Here's the tax math:

You still owe ordinary income tax on the FMV at vest (there is no way around that). But if you donate the shares immediately at vest instead of selling them, two things happen: (1) you get a charitable deduction equal to the full FMV on the date of donation (which is the same as the vest FMV), effectively neutralizing the income inclusion with a deduction of equal magnitude; and (2) you don't generate a capital gain on the donated shares, because your basis equals the FMV at vest and you're donating, not selling. For executives who have charitable intent, this can wipe out the tax on an entire vest tranche. The limitation is the standard 60% of AGI cap on cash charitable deductions (or 30% for appreciated property, though RSU shares donated at vest have a zero gain — basis = FMV — so the 60% limit applies).

10b5-1 Plans for RSU Sale Proceeds

Once RSUs vest and you hold the resulting shares, you face the classic insider trading problem: you can only sell during open trading windows, and those windows may be closed exactly when you want liquidity. The solution is a Rule 10b5-1 trading plan — a pre-scheduled, automatic selling program established when you are not in possession of material non-public information. Once established, the plan executes automatically, even if windows close.

For executives at public companies that hold Bitcoin on their balance sheet (increasingly common as of 2025–2026), this matters for Bitcoin too. If you are an executive at a company like MicroStrategy, Coinbase, or any other public company with significant BTC holdings, your BTC transactions may implicate insider trading rules if your company's Bitcoin position is material to its stock price. A properly structured 10b5-1 plan protects you on both the equity and the BTC side. Consult securities counsel before establishing one — the SEC tightened the rules in 2023 and imposed cooling-off periods (90 days for most officers; 30 days for directors).

Estate Planning: Unvested RSUs Don't Transfer

This is a critical planning point that shocks executives who haven't looked carefully at their grant agreements: unvested RSUs typically cannot be transferred to a trust, to heirs, or to anyone else during your lifetime or at death. The standard RSU grant agreement provides that unvested RSUs are forfeited at termination of employment, and death is a termination event unless the company's plan specifically provides otherwise.

Some plans include a "post-death vest" provision — allowing unvested RSUs to continue vesting for a limited period (often 12 months) after death for the benefit of the estate. But many do not. This means that a significant portion of what an executive thinks of as "their wealth" — the unvested RSU pipeline — may simply evaporate at death.

The implications for estate planning are significant:

  • Include your full vesting schedule in your estate inventory, but clearly annotate which tranches are unvested and therefore at risk of forfeiture. Do not allow your estate attorney to assume that vesting schedule = certain estate asset.
  • Acceleration clauses: Many RSU grant agreements include a double-trigger acceleration clause — if the company is acquired AND you are terminated within 24 months, all unvested RSUs vest immediately. This creates a sudden spike in estate value exactly at the moment of corporate transaction activity. It also creates a large ordinary income event in the estate that the executor needs to manage. If you have an acceleration clause, model it in your estate plan as a potential liquidity shock.
  • Life insurance gap: If a meaningful portion of your estate value is locked in unvested RSUs that vanish at death, the gap between your perceived estate value and your transferable estate value can be enormous. A properly sized life insurance policy in an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) can fill this gap — providing heirs with liquidity that offsets the unvested RSU forfeit.

ISO vs. NQSO Estate Planning: The Tax Characters Are Completely Different

Stock options are not a monolith. ISOs and NQSOs are treated entirely differently by the tax code, they behave differently during your lifetime, and they behave differently again at death. Getting this wrong — in either direction — is one of the most common and most costly mistakes in executive estate planning.

ISOs: The AMT Trap

Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) are attractive on paper: you pay no regular income tax when you exercise them. The spread at exercise — the difference between the strike price and the fair market value — is not W-2 income. If you hold the shares for at least two years from the grant date and at least one year from the exercise date, all gain is taxed as long-term capital gains on sale. That's an extraordinary tax benefit.

The catch is the Alternative Minimum Tax. The spread at exercise is an AMT preference item. In 2026, the AMT exemption is $137,000 for individuals and $220,000 for married filing jointly. If you exercise ISOs in a year when your regular income is already high, the AMT can hit hard — and it's payable even though you haven't sold the shares yet and haven't received any cash from the transaction.

The classic ISO disaster: an exec exercises $2M worth of ISOs in a year they also have $1.5M in RSU income. The regular income puts them deep into 37% territory. The ISO exercise adds $2M to the AMT base (not regular income base). AMT is calculated at 26–28% on income above the exemption, and then compared to regular tax. In this scenario, AMT likely bites for $150K–$300K in incremental tax — a real check written to the IRS on income you haven't received in cash.

The Bitcoin coordination strategy: Watch your Bitcoin position carefully when planning ISO exercises. If BTC is down significantly in a given year (reducing your anticipated capital gains distributions), that may create more AMT headroom. Exercise ISOs in years when your non-option income is lower, BTC gains are modest, and you are below the AMT phase-out threshold. The AMT exemption phases out at $206,700 (individual) / $310,050 (MFJ) for 2026 — at a rate of $0.25 per dollar of AMTI above these thresholds. For every $4 of ISO spread above the phase-out, you lose $1 of exemption. Model this precisely before exercising anything above $500K in spread.

You can recover AMT paid in prior years via the AMT credit, which converts to a refundable credit when your regular tax liability exceeds your AMT liability in a later year. This often happens in years after an ISO exercise when you haven't sold any of the underlying shares and your income is lower. But the credit is only useful if you have tax liability to offset it against — which brings us back to coordinating BTC sales and ISO exercises across multiple years.

NQSOs: Simple but Expensive

Non-Qualified Stock Options (NQSOs) are simpler: the spread at exercise (FMV minus strike price) is ordinary W-2 income, period. Your employer withholds taxes, it appears on your W-2, and you pay at your marginal rate. Subsequently, any appreciation in the shares from your exercise price to your ultimate sale price is capital gains — long-term if you hold more than a year, short-term if not.

For estate planning, NQSOs held by the decedent at death do receive a stepped-up basis to FMV on date of death — meaning the ordinary income component of the spread (from grant to FMV at death) is simply eliminated. However, the step-up only applies to the shares themselves if they've been exercised. Unexercised NQSOs received by an estate are generally subject to income in respect of a decedent (IRD) rules — the estate or beneficiary exercises them and recognizes the spread as ordinary income, though they may get an estate tax deduction for the IRD character.

What Happens to ISOs at Death

Here is the important estate planning wrinkle for ISOs that almost no one discusses clearly: ISOs automatically convert to NQSOs when the option holder dies. The beneficial holding period requirements that make ISOs valuable (2-year from grant / 1-year from exercise) cannot be inherited. Your heirs receive NQSOs, not ISOs — so any exercise after death creates ordinary income for the estate, not capital gains.

The silver lining: shares that were already exercised under ISO treatment and held by the decedent receive a stepped-up basis to FMV at date of death, just like other assets. So if you exercised ISOs, paid AMT on the spread, held the shares long enough to meet the LTCG holding period, and died while still holding them — your heirs inherit the shares at stepped-up basis with no embedded capital gain. The AMT you paid during life is effectively gone (not refunded, but no further tax is owed on the appreciation).

The practical implication: exercise ISOs and hold the shares yourself during your lifetime, especially if the shares have strong appreciation potential. Don't let unexercised ISOs sit in your estate to convert to NQSOs at death. If you exercise during life, meet the holding periods, and hold the shares in a trust that receives a step-up at death, your heirs get the full stepped-up basis at long-term capital gains rates with no IRD complications.

The §83(b) Election: The 30-Day Window That Cannot Be Reopened

The §83(b) election does not apply to RSUs — a common misconception. RSUs are specifically excluded from §83(b) treatment because they involve no forfeiture risk that §83(b) is designed to address (RSUs are simply a promise to deliver shares; there is no "property" transferred at grant). However, §83(b) applies critically to restricted stock — and restricted stock is increasingly common in two specific situations for tech executives:

  1. Pre-IPO and early-stage companies: Employees and executives joining companies before the first institutional round often receive restricted stock (not RSUs). The distinction matters enormously. With restricted stock, you receive actual shares immediately, subject to forfeiture conditions (vesting). With an §83(b) election, you can elect to be taxed immediately on the value at grant — typically near zero if the company is early-stage — rather than at the ordinary income rate when each tranche vests.
  2. Bitcoin mining company equity: If you are joining or founding a Bitcoin mining company as an executive, and you receive restricted stock rather than RSUs, the §83(b) election is the single most powerful tax tool available. Exercise it within 30 days of receiving the stock, pay ordinary income tax on (essentially) zero, and convert all future appreciation — including appreciation driven by Bitcoin's price increase — to long-term capital gains when you eventually sell.

The 30-day window is absolute. The IRS has been explicit: there are no extensions, no exceptions, and no late filings permitted after the 30-day deadline. Miss it, and every vest tranche is taxed as ordinary income at FMV on vest. This is a $0 tax decision with potentially millions of dollars of consequence — the kind of thing that gets lawyers fired when they forget to mention it.

The mechanical requirement: file the election with the IRS service center where you file your return, and also attach it to your return for the year of the transfer. Keep a copy with proof of mailing (certified mail with return receipt is the standard). The IRS loses paperwork. You want proof.

AMT Planning: The Bitcoin–ISO Collision

Let's be precise about the AMT calculation structure, because understanding it is the key to managing the ISO/BTC interaction.

Regular income tax is computed on regular taxable income. AMT is computed on Alternative Minimum Taxable Income (AMTI), which adds back certain "preference items" to regular income. The most significant preference item for tech executives is the ISO spread at exercise. AMTI is then reduced by the AMT exemption ($137,000 single / $220,000 MFJ for 2026), and tax is calculated at 26% on the first $220,700 of excess AMTI and 28% above that. You pay whichever is higher: regular tax or AMT.

Bitcoin capital gains are included in both regular income and AMTI — they don't get preferential treatment under either regime. What this means in practice is that large Bitcoin capital gains in a year can interact with ISO exercises in two damaging ways:

  1. Large BTC gains increase AMTI directly, reducing the relative benefit of any AMT exemption and pushing you further into AMT territory.
  2. Large BTC gains increase regular tax liability, which can paradoxically shield you from AMT in a specific scenario — if regular tax liability is high enough that it exceeds AMT, you're in regular tax territory and the ISO spread doesn't add incremental AMT cost.

The planning implication: in years when you have very large BTC gains (e.g., you sold 10 BTC at a $100K gain per coin = $1M LTCG), your regular tax liability may actually be high enough that modest ISO exercises don't trigger AMT. In years when you have no BTC sales and relatively low other income, AMT is more of a threat. The optimal strategy is to model your ISO exercise capacity against your BTC sale plan in every year — essentially solving for the maximum ISO exercise you can do before AMT exceeds your regular tax, across a multi-year projection.

For executives planning Roth conversions as part of a retirement and estate plan, the same dynamic applies: large Roth conversions increase AMTI, compressing the AMT headroom for ISO exercises in the same year. Sequence conversions in years with low ISO exercise activity and modest BTC gains. QSBS §1202: The $10 Million Wildcard

Most senior tech executives accumulate equity at their current employer — which is almost certainly not QSBS-eligible (publicly traded companies don't qualify, and most of the large private tech employers have grown past the gross assets threshold of $50M). But many executives also have angel investments, advisor equity, or early-stage startup holdings from a previous role — and those may qualify.

Section 1202 of the Internal Revenue Code allows non-corporate holders of Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) to exclude up to $10 million in capital gains (or 10x their invested basis, whichever is greater) from federal income tax on the sale of QSBS held for more than five years. The company must be a domestic C-corporation, must have had gross assets under $50 million at the time of issuance, and must be in an active trade or business (excluding certain service industries). Bitcoin and cryptocurrency do not qualify for QSBS treatment.

If you hold both QSBS equity from a prior startup and a significant Bitcoin position, the portfolio sequencing question is: which do you sell first, and how do you deploy the proceeds?

The answer is almost always: use §1202 for the equity gain (it's free money at the federal level), and use charitable vehicles for the BTC. Here's why:

  • The §1202 exclusion expires if unused at a sale — you cannot carry it forward or use it on other assets. Use it when you have the qualifying sale event.
  • Bitcoin donated to a DAF or CRT allows you to deduct the full FMV while eliminating the capital gain entirely. The charitable vehicle then sells the BTC with no tax owed. This is more valuable per dollar of gain eliminated than the §1202 exclusion, which provides a 0% federal rate but doesn't create a deduction.
  • Post-QSBS sale cash can be reinvested in diversified assets without any preferred tax treatment — so deploy it into a taxable portfolio, use it for Roth conversion planning, or fund an Irrevocable Trust for downstream estate planning.

Concentrated Position Risk: Two Assets, Double the Exposure

Standard diversification math assumes you have one large concentrated position. Tech executives with Bitcoin have two — and they don't correlate with each other in ways that reduce total portfolio risk. Both can fall simultaneously in a macro risk-off event. Both can be illiquid at once (trading windows closed on the stock; BTC in cold storage). This is double concentration, not diversification.

The tools available to reduce concentration risk on each side:

For Company Stock

  • Exchange funds: Pool your stock with other concentrated holders in a diversified fund structure. You receive a proportionate interest in a diversified portfolio without triggering a taxable sale. Eligibility requirements are strict (stock must be held for at least 7 years in the fund; management holds considerable discretion), and availability is limited to qualified investors with minimum contributions typically $1M+. But for executives with large single-stock RSU positions, it's worth evaluating.
  • Variable prepaid forward contracts: Sell the future upside of your stock in exchange for a lump sum today, while deferring the capital gain. The stock is used as collateral. These structures defer tax but do not eliminate it — the gain is recognized when the forward contract settles. Tax counsel is required; the IRS has challenged certain structures.
  • Protective puts / costless collars: Options strategies that lock in a price floor on company stock without triggering a constructive sale. Available during open trading windows; must be structured carefully to avoid the constructive sale rules of §1259.
  • Charitable remainder trust (CRT): Fund a CRT with appreciated company stock. The CRT sells the stock (no capital gains), invests the proceeds in a diversified portfolio, pays you an annuity income stream for life or a term of years, and the remainder passes to charity. The charitable deduction at funding is calculated based on the present value of the charitable remainder. Effective for executives with strong charitable intent and a desire for lifetime income diversification.

For Bitcoin

  • CME Bitcoin options (covered calls): If your BTC is already at a significant gain, writing covered calls on CME Bitcoin futures contracts generates income while providing partial downside protection. Be aware that CME futures are section 1256 contracts — marked to market at year-end with 60/40 long-term/short-term treatment — and structuring these with spot BTC holdings requires careful tax analysis.
  • Charitable strategies: As discussed, DAF or CRT contributions of appreciated BTC eliminate the capital gains entirely. The most tax-efficient way to reduce a large BTC position if you have charitable intent.
  • Systematic selling via 10b5-1 analog: While BTC doesn't require 10b5-1 compliance (it's not a security), structuring a regular, pre-committed selling schedule serves the same psychological and tax smoothing function. Selling equal tranches over 3–5 years spreads the capital gain across multiple years, potentially keeping you in the 15% LTCG bracket rather than the 20% + NIIT bracket. See our 2026 capital gains rate guide for the bracket thresholds.
  • Borrowing against BTC: The "buy-borrow-die" strategy allows executives to borrow against their Bitcoin position at custodial platforms (e.g., Coinbase Prime, Anchorage) without triggering a taxable sale. This provides liquidity for short-term needs while the BTC position remains intact. The risk is margin call if BTC falls significantly — suitable only for borrowing a modest fraction of position value.

GRATs: Timing the Pre-Exit Window

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is a zero-gift-tax wealth transfer vehicle: you fund it with an appreciating asset, take back an annuity for a fixed term, and any appreciation above the IRS §7520 hurdle rate passes to heirs gift-tax-free. For GRATs to work, the asset must appreciate more than the §7520 rate (currently in the 4–5% range as of 2026) over the trust term.

For tech executives, the optimal GRAT funding window is typically:

  • Before a catalytic event that will drive stock price appreciation: a product launch, earnings beat, acquisition announcement, or IPO lock-up expiration. Funding a GRAT before the value spike allows the appreciation above the §7520 rate to pass to heirs without gift tax.
  • In a Bitcoin correction: a GRAT funded with BTC at a temporary low locks in the low value as the starting base. If BTC recovers and appreciates above the §7520 rate over the trust term, the excess passes to heirs gift-tax-free.

GRATs can be funded with both company stock and Bitcoin — but they should be funded separately, not combined into one trust, because the GRAT structure requires precise annuity payments pegged to the initial funding value, and mixing assets with different volatility profiles complicates both the annuity calculation and the trustee's management obligations.

One important nuance: if you die during the GRAT term, the trust assets are included back in your estate. Short-duration "rolling GRATs" (2-year terms) reduce this mortality risk by cycling through multiple GRATs with short terms rather than one long-duration GRAT. Case Study: Jordan — VP of Product, $1.5M RSUs, 25 BTC, $200K NQSOs

Let's make this concrete. Jordan is a VP of Product at a large public tech company. Here's the snapshot:

  • RSUs: $1.5M vesting this year (4-year cliff with quarterly vesting). After federal and state withholding, Jordan nets roughly $870K in after-tax proceeds. The shares vest at an average price of $180 and are now trading at $185.
  • Bitcoin: 25 BTC purchased at an average of $9,200 in 2019. Current price: $92,000. Unrealized gain: ~$2.07M (25 × $82,800), all long-term.
  • NQSOs: $200,000 face value in options with a $50 strike, current stock price $185. Spread = $135 per option. If Jordan exercises all of them, the spread becomes ordinary income: $200K × ($135/$50 × shares) — let's say 5,000 options → $675,000 in W-2 income.
  • Marital status: Married filing jointly. Combined household income (including spouse) brings AGI to roughly $2.2M before any optional transactions.

The Immediate Problem: 2026 Tax Year Is Already Overloaded

Jordan's $1.5M RSU vest has already pushed the household into the 37% bracket at the federal level and the 13.3% bracket in California. Total marginal rate on the next dollar of ordinary income: approximately 50%. The NQSO spread is also ordinary income. If Jordan exercises the NQSOs in 2026, the $675,000 spread hits at 50% → $337,500 in incremental tax. After-tax proceeds from the exercise: ~$337,500. Not nothing, but the cost is enormous.

The Bitcoin position is LTCG. Jordan's AGI already far exceeds the 20% LTCG bracket threshold ($583,751 MFJ in 2026), so any BTC sale is taxed at 20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + California ordinary income rate (California taxes all capital gains as ordinary income) = approximately 36.8% combined. Every $1M in BTC sales costs $368,000 in tax.

Step 1: Defer the NQSO Exercise

Jordan should not exercise NQSOs in 2026 if they already have $1.5M in RSU income. Deferring to a year with lower W-2 income — for example, after a planned leave of absence, or if RSU grants decline in a future year — saves approximately $337,500 in federal/state income tax on the exercise spread. Options don't expire for 10 years from grant (typical); there is no urgency to exercise in 2026.

Exception: if the company's stock is likely to be acquired at a premium in the next 12–18 months, the calculation changes. Jordan needs to weigh the tax cost of early exercise against the risk of leaving intrinsic value on the table if options expire unexercised during a trading blackout or if employment terminates for any reason.

Step 2: Establish a 10b5-1 Plan for RSU Proceeds

Jordan should establish a 10b5-1 plan during the next open trading window. The plan should systematically sell vested RSU shares over 12–24 months, regardless of whether trading windows are open. This solves two problems: (1) it protects Jordan from insider trading risk on stock sales tied to knowledge of upcoming earnings or product launches; and (2) it forces systematic diversification rather than emotional holding of a single-stock position.

Step 3: Build the BTC Charitable Vehicle

Jordan's $2.07M in BTC gains is not going away. Selling all 25 BTC in one year creates a $760,000 federal/state tax bill. The alternative: donate 5–8 BTC to a Donor-Advised Fund. At $92,000 per BTC, donating 5 BTC creates:

  • $460,000 charitable deduction (reduces income dollar-for-dollar up to 30% of AGI)
  • $460,000 in capital gain permanently eliminated (the DAF sells the BTC tax-free)
  • The 30% AGI cap limits the current-year deduction to ~$660,000 (30% of $2.2M AGI), so the full deduction is available in year one. Any excess carries forward 5 years.

Combined effect: Jordan effectively "sells" 5 BTC at zero effective tax cost through the DAF, nets a meaningful charitable deduction, and retains 20 BTC for continued appreciation. The 20 remaining BTC can be sold systematically in future years — ideally in years with lower W-2 income when the LTCG rate remains at 20% + NIIT but total effective rate is lower.

Step 4: GRAT for the Remaining BTC Position

Jordan funds a 2-year rolling GRAT with 5 BTC at current value ($460,000). Over the 2-year term, if BTC appreciates to $140,000–$150,000 per coin, the appreciation above the §7520 hurdle rate (let's say 4.8%) passes to Jordan's heirs gift-tax-free. The annuity Jordan receives back from the GRAT effectively returns the $460,000 principal; the $230,000–$280,000 in appreciation transfers to the remainder beneficiary (typically a dynasty trust) without gift tax.

If BTC falls during the GRAT term, the trust fails and the assets return to Jordan — no estate tax loss, just no gift-tax benefit. Rolling GRATs every 2 years means you're perpetually putting new BTC into the vehicle as the old one matures.

Step 5: Update the Estate Plan to Reflect RSU Forfeit Risk

Jordan's total estate as of 2026:

  • Vested company stock (post-RSU): ~$2.1M (approximate after 2026 vest)
  • 20 remaining BTC: $1.84M
  • NQSOs (unexercised): $675K intrinsic value, but subject to forfeiture at termination
  • Unvested RSUs: approximately 3 years of $1.5M/year remaining = $4.5M projected, all at risk of forfeiture if Jordan dies before vesting
  • 401(k), home equity, other assets: let's say $1M

The actual transferable estate today is approximately $5.6M ($2.1M stock + $1.84M BTC + $1M other). The $4.5M in unvested RSUs is not transferable. Jordan needs to carry $4.5M in life insurance (held in an ILIT) to protect the family against the unvested RSU forfeiture gap. This is not "extra" insurance — it is precisely calibrated to cover the gap between perceived wealth and transferable wealth.

The Trust Structure Question: What Holds the Bitcoin?

RSUs cannot be held in a revocable living trust prior to vesting — the grant agreement controls, and it won't allow assignment. Vested shares, once in your possession, can be re-titled into a living trust or transferred to an irrevocable trust, subject to Rule 144 and insider trading restrictions if you're an affiliate. Company equity in an irrevocable trust counts toward your affiliate threshold — consult securities counsel.

Bitcoin is far more flexible. It can be held in:

  • Revocable living trust: Provides probate avoidance. The trust holds the BTC keys (or the custodial account) and transfers seamlessly at death without court involvement. Revocable trusts offer no asset protection and no estate tax benefits — everything is included in your taxable estate.
  • Irrevocable trust (SLAT, IDIT, GRAT): Removes BTC from your taxable estate if structured and funded properly. Requires giving up control — you cannot unilaterally pull the assets back. Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs) allow your spouse to benefit from the trust while removing the assets from your estate.
  • Family LLC / FLP: A family limited liability company can hold both Bitcoin and company stock (once vested), providing a management structure, valuation discounts for estate and gift tax purposes, and asset protection. The BTC is owned by the LLC; LLC membership interests are gifted to heirs or held in trust at discounted valuations.

One practical note on Bitcoin custody in trust: the trustee must have a defined protocol for managing private keys. If you are using self-custody (hardware wallet), the trust document should specify the key management procedure, backup locations, and successor trustee access protocol. If custody is delegated to a qualified custodian (Coinbase Prime, Anchorage, BitGo), the trust must be named as the account holder of record. Get this wrong and your heirs spend 18 months in probate court trying to access digital assets your trust was supposed to own. The technical details matter as much as the legal structure.

State Tax Considerations: California, New York, and Washington

Many tech executives are concentrated in three states with dramatically different tax profiles for equity compensation and Bitcoin:

  • California: No capital gains preference. All capital gains — including long-term Bitcoin gains and LTCG on stock — are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 13.3%. California also does not conform to §1202 QSBS, so all QSBS gains are taxable at the state level. RSU income and NQSO spread are also ordinary income. California has no estate tax. The state tax burden on tech equity and BTC is roughly equal.
  • New York: Capital gains taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 10.9% state + 3.876% NYC). RSU and option income also ordinary. No estate tax for amounts under $7.16M (2026 threshold); values exceeding the threshold may trigger New York estate tax on the entire estate (the "cliff" effect — exceeding the exemption by $1 causes the entire estate to be taxable, not just the excess).
  • Washington: No state income tax, but Washington enacted a 7% capital gains tax in 2021 on gains above $262,000/year (after exemptions). Bitcoin and stock gains above the threshold are subject to this tax. No estate tax below $2.193M; above that, the Washington estate tax applies on the excess at rates up to 20%.

For executives considering relocation to a lower-tax state (Nevada, Texas, Florida), the key test is establishing genuine domicile before any triggering event — RSU vest, option exercise, or BTC sale. California is aggressive about asserting continued residency for income sourced to California-based employment. Equity compensation earned while working in California retains California source income characteristics even if you've relocated — calculated based on the fraction of the vesting or holding period served in-state.

Working With Advisors Who Understand Both Assets

The fundamental challenge of planning at the intersection of tech equity and Bitcoin is that the experts are siloed. Your estate attorney understands RSUs and options from a securities law perspective but may have limited familiarity with Bitcoin custody, wallet mechanics, or the specific tax treatment of BTC under IRS Notice 2014-21 and Rev. Rul. 2023-14. Your CPA may understand AMT planning for ISOs but has never modeled the interaction of a large LTCG from Bitcoin with an ISO exercise year. Your financial advisor may have great Bitcoin conviction but limited experience with 10b5-1 plans and insider trading compliance.

You need a team that covers all three domains simultaneously, and you need them coordinating rather than working in isolation. Specifically:

  1. Estate attorney with tech executive equity experience who has drafted trusts holding digital assets
  2. CPA with AMT planning experience and familiarity with cryptocurrency tax reporting (Form 8949, Rev. Rul. 2023-14)
  3. Securities counsel for 10b5-1 plan establishment and insider trading compliance review
  4. Bitcoin-specific custodial or family office partner for custody architecture, wallet management within trust structures, and on-chain reporting

The planning conversation should happen as a coordinated team meeting, not as sequential siloed consultations. The whole point is the interaction between these assets — and that interaction only becomes visible when everyone is in the same room with the same numbers.

Final Thought: The Asymmetry Is in Your Favor — But Only if You Plan

Tech executives with Bitcoin are sitting in an extraordinary position. You have W-2 income that creates immediate liquidity and tax flexibility, company equity that builds wealth over a vesting schedule, and Bitcoin that has appreciated 10–20x in the past five years with no counterparty risk and a fixed supply. The combination is genuinely rare in the history of private wealth accumulation.

The risk is not that these assets lose value — it's that the tax interactions between them consume 40–50% of what could be transferred to your heirs, and that the estate planning structures you use fail to account for the peculiarities of each asset class. Unvested RSUs that forfeit at death. ISOs that convert to NQSOs for your heirs. AMT triggered by an ISO exercise in the same year you sold BTC to fund a GRAT.

None of these outcomes are inevitable. All of them are preventable with a coordinated plan executed in the right sequence. The checklist above is the starting point. The case study is the model.
