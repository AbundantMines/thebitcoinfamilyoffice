Bitcoin Community Property Estate Planning: The Double Step-Up Advantage Most Couples Miss

If you and your spouse are in California, Texas, Nevada, Washington, or one of six other community property states — and you've held Bitcoin since the early days — you may be sitting on one of the most powerful hidden tax advantages in estate planning. The double step-up in basis can eliminate every dollar of embedded capital gains in your Bitcoin at the first spouse's death. Most couples have never heard of it.

Educational Content Only: This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal, tax, or financial advice. Community property rules vary by state and individual circumstances. Consult a qualified estate planning attorney and tax advisor familiar with your state's community property law.

The Step-Up in Basis: A Quick Refresher

When someone dies holding appreciated Bitcoin, the IRS resets the cost basis of that Bitcoin to its fair market value on the date of death. This is called the stepped-up basis (IRC §1014). Heirs who inherit the Bitcoin and immediately sell it pay zero capital gains tax — all the appreciation that occurred during the decedent's lifetime is permanently eliminated.

In a common law state (where most Americans live), only the assets included in the decedent's estate receive the step-up. For a married couple who jointly own Bitcoin, this typically means: only the deceased spouse's half of the Bitcoin gets a stepped-up basis. The surviving spouse's half retains its original cost basis.

In a community property state, the rule is different — and dramatically more favorable.

The Double Step-Up: How Community Property Changes Everything

Under IRC §1014(b)(6), when community property passes at death, the entire community property receives a stepped-up basis — not just the deceased spouse's half. Both halves step up to the fair market value at the date of death.

This is the double step-up: the surviving spouse's share of community property Bitcoin receives a new basis at the date of the first spouse's death, even though the surviving spouse is still alive and still owns their half.

Double Step-Up: The Numbers

Couple in Texas. 20 BTC purchased during marriage at an average of $4,000/BTC. Total cost basis: $80,000. Current value: $1,420,000 (at $71,000/BTC). Embedded gain: $1,340,000.

Common law state (e.g., New York):

  • First spouse dies. Their 10 BTC steps up to $710,000. The other 10 BTC (surviving spouse's) retains $40,000 basis.
  • Surviving spouse sells all 20 BTC: gain on 10 BTC = $0 (stepped up); gain on other 10 BTC = $710,000 − $40,000 = $670,000.
  • Capital gains tax: $670,000 × 23.8% = $159,460.

Community property state (Texas):

  • First spouse dies. BOTH sets of 10 BTC step up to $710,000 each. Surviving spouse's entire 20 BTC now has $1,420,000 basis.
  • Surviving spouse sells all 20 BTC: gain = $1,420,000 − $1,420,000 = $0.
  • Capital gains tax: $0.

Difference: $159,460 saved — from the same Bitcoin, the same marriage, just a different state's property law.

For couples who bought Bitcoin at $1,000–$10,000 per coin and now hold millions in appreciation, the double step-up advantage compounds dramatically. A couple with 50 BTC purchased at an average of $5,000 ($250,000 total basis, now worth $3.55M) saves over $775,000 in capital gains tax compared to the same couple in a common law state — simply by virtue of living in a community property state and holding their Bitcoin as community property.

Non-Community Property States with Community Property Trust Options

If you live in a common law state, you're not necessarily locked out of the double step-up. Tennessee, Kentucky, and Alaska have enacted community property trust statutes that allow couples in those states — and potentially couples domiciled in any state — to hold assets in a specially structured trust that elects community property treatment. Assets held in these trusts may receive the double step-up under IRC §1014(b)(6).

This is an emerging planning strategy that estate planning attorneys in common law states are increasingly using for couples with highly appreciated assets — including Bitcoin. If you're in New York, Florida, Colorado, or another common law state with $1M+ in appreciated Bitcoin, ask your attorney whether a Tennessee or Alaska community property trust makes sense for your situation.

What Qualifies as Community Property Bitcoin?

Not all Bitcoin held by a married couple in a community property state automatically qualifies as community property. The characterization depends on when and how the Bitcoin was acquired:

Community Property (Double Step-Up Available)

  • Bitcoin purchased with wages or income earned during the marriage
  • Bitcoin purchased with proceeds from the sale of other community property
  • Bitcoin received as income from community property (e.g., mining income using community property equipment and funds)
  • Bitcoin purchased with community funds even if titled in one spouse's name only

Separate Property (Only One-Half Step-Up)

  • Bitcoin owned by one spouse before the marriage began
  • Bitcoin received as a gift or inheritance during marriage (even if received by both spouses together, if the donor intended to give to one)
  • Bitcoin purchased with separate property funds that have been clearly traced and not commingled
  • Bitcoin designated as separate property in a valid prenuptial or postnuptial agreement

The Commingling Problem

When community and separate funds are mixed — such as when pre-marital Bitcoin is held in the same wallet as Bitcoin purchased during the marriage — the entire commingled position may become community property under the laws of some states. This can be a feature (the entire position becomes eligible for the double step-up) or a problem (a prenup protecting pre-marital Bitcoin may be undermined).

For Bitcoin holders who accumulated significant pre-marital Bitcoin and later married in a community property state, maintaining clean separation between pre-marital Bitcoin (separate property) and post-marital Bitcoin (community property) through separate wallets or exchange accounts is critical for estate planning clarity.

The Community Property Revocable Trust: Best of Both Worlds

Most estate planning attorneys recommend that couples in community property states use a community property revocable trust — sometimes called a "community property trust" or "AB trust" — rather than joint tenancy or individual ownership for their Bitcoin.

A community property revocable trust:

  • Preserves community property character: Assets placed in the trust retain their community property status, preserving the double step-up benefit at the first spouse's death
  • Avoids probate: Assets pass through the trust to beneficiaries without going through the public probate process
  • Maintains flexibility: Either spouse can amend or revoke the trust during their joint lifetimes
  • Protects against accidental conversion: Holding Bitcoin in a properly titled community property trust prevents accidental conversion to separate property or joint tenancy (which can eliminate the double step-up in some states)

The key is titling: the trust must be explicitly structured as a community property trust, not just a joint revocable trust. In California, for example, holding assets as "joint tenants with right of survivorship" does NOT preserve community property character — it converts the property to a different ownership form that only gets one half stepped up at the first death. An experienced estate attorney in your state will ensure the trust is correctly structured.

The Interaction With Estate Taxes

The double step-up is a capital gains tax benefit — it's separate from estate tax. Let's be precise about how they interact for large Bitcoin positions:

  • Capital gains: Double step-up eliminates all embedded capital gains at the first spouse's death. If the surviving spouse sells immediately after inheriting, zero capital gains tax.
  • Estate tax: The deceased spouse's half of the community property is included in their taxable estate. The marital deduction (IRC §2056) generally allows the first spouse's estate to pass to the surviving spouse free of estate tax — no estate tax due at the first death regardless of size.
  • Second death: When the surviving spouse dies, their entire estate — including the Bitcoin, now with a double stepped-up basis — is included in their taxable estate. If it exceeds the estate tax exemption, estate tax applies. The double step-up has eliminated capital gains but not the estate tax on the second death.

For large estates ($20M+), the capital gains elimination at the first death is valuable — but the estate tax at the second death is still a planning concern. This is why the double step-up strategy is typically combined with other estate planning tools: lifetime gifting to dynasty trusts, GRATs, SLATs, or charitable bequests to manage the estate tax exposure at the second death.

Double Step-Up vs. Irrevocable Trust: The Trade-Off

Here's a tension Bitcoin-wealthy couples in community property states often face: irrevocable trusts (dynasty trusts, SLATs, GRATs) are powerful estate tax reduction tools — but transferring Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust removes it from the community property regime and potentially eliminates the double step-up.

Strategy Capital Gains at First Death Estate Tax at Second Death Best For
Hold as community property (revocable trust) $0 — full double step-up Full estate tax on surviving spouse's estate Estates near or below exemption; couples who plan to sell Bitcoin after first death
Transfer to irrevocable dynasty trust No step-up — carryover basis forever $0 — removed from both estates Large estates well above exemption; Bitcoin that will be held multi-generationally
Split strategy: hold some as CP, transfer some to trust Step-up on CP portion only Reduced — only trust portion outside estate Most common practical approach for large estates

The decision framework: if the estate is comfortably within the current exemption ($15M under OBBBA), the double step-up is maximally valuable — keep Bitcoin as community property, let it step up at the first death, surviving spouse sells with zero capital gains. If the estate is well above the exemption and estate tax is a serious concern, the dynasty trust may be worth foregoing the double step-up to eliminate the estate tax exposure.

For most Bitcoin-wealthy couples in the $3M–$15M range — which describes the majority of the BFO audience — the double step-up is the dominant strategy and dynasty trust transfers should be sized carefully to preserve the community property benefit on the core position.

Practical Steps for Bitcoin Couples in Community Property States

  1. Audit your Bitcoin ownership: For each Bitcoin position, determine: Was it acquired before or after marriage? With community or separate funds? Has it been commingled? This characterization determines whether you get one or two step-ups.
  2. Structure your trust correctly: If you don't already have a revocable trust, have an estate attorney in your state draft a community property revocable trust — not a joint tenancy, not a simple joint revocable trust, but specifically a community property trust that preserves community property character.
  3. Title your Bitcoin correctly: Move Bitcoin into the community property trust. The exchange account and any institutional custody account should be titled in the trust's name. For self-custody Bitcoin, execute an assignment agreement.
  4. Keep separate property separate: If you have pre-marital Bitcoin you want to protect as separate property (for divorce protection or other reasons), maintain it in a completely separate wallet and document its separate property character. Do not commingle with community property Bitcoin.
  5. Update your Letter of Instructions: Your digital asset letter of instructions should distinguish between community property Bitcoin and separate property Bitcoin — including which wallet addresses hold which.
  6. Model the trade-off: If your estate is approaching or above the exemption threshold, have your attorney and CPA model the double step-up vs. dynasty trust scenario side by side. The optimal answer depends on your specific numbers, your state, and your plans for the Bitcoin.
  7. Consider a community property trust if in a common law state: If you live in New York, Florida, Colorado, or another common law state, ask whether a Tennessee, Kentucky, or Alaska community property trust could extend the double step-up benefit to your situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the double step-up in basis for community property Bitcoin?

Under IRC §1014(b)(6), when community property passes at death, both halves receive a stepped-up basis to fair market value — not just the deceased spouse's half. For Bitcoin-wealthy couples in community property states, this means the surviving spouse can sell the entire Bitcoin position immediately after the first spouse's death with zero capital gains tax, regardless of how low the original cost basis was. In common law states, only the deceased spouse's half steps up.

Which states are community property states?

The nine community property states are: Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin. Alaska allows voluntary opt-in. Tennessee, Kentucky, and Alaska also have community property trust statutes that may allow couples in any state to hold assets in a qualifying trust to elect community property treatment — potentially extending the double step-up to common law state residents.

Is Bitcoin acquired during marriage automatically community property?

In community property states, assets acquired during marriage with marital funds are community property by default — including Bitcoin purchased with income earned during the marriage. Bitcoin owned before marriage, or received as a gift or inheritance, is generally separate property. Mining income and Bitcoin earned from community property mining equipment during marriage is typically community property. A prenuptial or postnuptial agreement can override these defaults.

Does holding Bitcoin as joint tenants preserve the double step-up?

No — this is a critical mistake. Holding Bitcoin as "joint tenants with right of survivorship" converts the property to a different ownership form in most community property states and eliminates the double step-up benefit. Only the deceased joint tenant's half steps up at death. To preserve the double step-up, Bitcoin must be held as community property — typically through a properly structured community property revocable trust. Have an estate attorney in your state review the titling.

Can we get the double step-up if we live in New York, Florida, or another common law state?

Potentially. Tennessee, Kentucky, and Alaska have community property trust statutes that may allow couples in any state to elect community property treatment for assets held in a qualifying trust. This is an emerging strategy — not universally accepted — and requires careful implementation by an attorney experienced with cross-state community property trusts. If you're in a common law state with $1M+ in appreciated Bitcoin, ask your attorney whether a community property trust election makes sense for your situation.

How does the double step-up interact with estate tax?

The double step-up eliminates capital gains — it doesn't eliminate estate tax. At the first death, the marital deduction typically allows assets to pass to the surviving spouse estate-tax-free regardless of size. At the second death, the entire estate — including Bitcoin with the double stepped-up basis — is subject to estate tax if it exceeds the exemption. The double step-up removes the capital gains burden; estate planning tools (dynasty trusts, GRATs, SLATs) address the estate tax at second death for large estates.
