Bitcoin Letter to Heirs: How to Write the Document That Saves Your Coins

Published March 16, 2026 · 20 min read

Your will says who gets the Bitcoin. The letter tells them how to actually get it — without losing everything in the process.

Here is the uncomfortable truth about Bitcoin estate planning: you can do everything right — set up a trust, fund it properly, name your beneficiaries, work with a qualified attorney — and your heirs can still lose every single satoshi.

Not because your legal documents were flawed. Not because the government seized anything. But because the people you love most had no idea what Bitcoin was, where yours was stored, how to access it, or what mistakes to avoid in the first 48 hours after your death.

The document that prevents this isn't your will. It isn't your trust agreement. It's a letter — a plain-language, deeply personal letter written directly to the people who will inherit your Bitcoin. A bitcoin letter to heirs is the single most important estate planning document most holders never write.

This guide will walk you through exactly what goes in that letter, what must never go in it, how it fits into a broader estate planning system, and — at the end — a complete template you can adapt and use today.

Why the Letter Matters More Than the Will

A will is a legal instrument. It tells a probate court who receives what. It satisfies legal requirements. It names executors and guardians and distributes property according to your wishes.

Here is what a will cannot do: it cannot teach your daughter what a hardware wallet is. It cannot explain to your son why the small black device in the safe deposit box is worth more than the house. It cannot tell your spouse to wait 90 days before making any decisions, to call the estate attorney before the CPA, and to absolutely, under no circumstances, type 24 words into any website that asks for them.

A will says: "I leave my Bitcoin holdings to my children in equal shares."

A bitcoin letter to heirs says: "Here is what Bitcoin is. Here is approximately how much I own. Here is where it is held. Here are the first three things you should do. Here are the five things you must never do. Here are the people to call, in this order. And here is where to find the technical document that gives you actual access."

Without the letter, the will is a legal formality pointing at an asset your heirs cannot touch. With the letter, everything changes.

This is especially critical for Bitcoin because of three properties that make it fundamentally different from every other inherited asset:

  1. Irreversibility. There is no customer service number. There is no "undo" button. If your heir sends Bitcoin to a wrong address, enters a seed phrase into a phishing site, or discards a hardware wallet — the loss is permanent and total.
  2. Opacity. Your heirs may not even know Bitcoin exists in your estate. Unlike a brokerage account that sends monthly statements to the house, self-custodied Bitcoin leaves no paper trail. If you don't tell them, no one will.
  3. Technical complexity. Even the simplest Bitcoin custody setup — a single hardware wallet with a 24-word seed phrase — requires knowledge that most people do not have. Multi-signature setups, multiple custodians, and mixed self-custody/exchange holdings multiply the complexity.

The letter bridges every one of these gaps. It is the translation layer between your carefully constructed Bitcoin estate plan and the real human beings who will execute it under the worst possible emotional conditions.

What Goes in the Letter — and What Absolutely Does Not

The bitcoin letter to heirs has a precise scope. Think of it as a high-level briefing document: enough information for your heirs to understand the situation, take the right immediate actions, avoid catastrophic mistakes, and connect with the professionals who will guide them through the rest.

What the letter DOES include

  • The fact that Bitcoin exists in your estate — and a rough sense of its significance. Your heirs need to know this is real and substantial, not a curiosity.
  • A plain-English explanation of what Bitcoin is — one paragraph, no jargon. Enough for someone who has never thought about cryptocurrency to understand why this matters.
  • Where the Bitcoin is held — which exchanges, which custodians, whether a hardware wallet exists and its general location. Not access credentials. Just the map.
  • Immediate steps to take — the first 48 hours. Who to call first. What to secure. What to do right now.
  • What NOT to do — a clear, explicit list of the mistakes that destroy inherited Bitcoin. This section may be the most valuable part of the entire letter.
  • Professional contacts — estate attorney, tax advisor, Bitcoin-knowledgeable financial advisor, and technical specialist if applicable. Names, phone numbers, emails.
  • A reference to the Technical Access Document — where it is, how to get it, and what it contains. The letter points to the keys without containing the keys.
  • Your personal wishes — an optional section where you explain what you hope your heirs will do with the Bitcoin. Hold? Spend? Donate? This is the emotional heart of the letter.

What the letter NEVER includes

This is non-negotiable. The following items must never appear in your bitcoin letter to heirs:

  • Seed phrases — the 12 or 24 words that control your Bitcoin. Never. Not under any circumstances.
  • Private keys — the raw cryptographic keys to any wallet.
  • PINs or passwords — to hardware wallets, exchange accounts, or anything else.
  • Exact balances — a general range is fine ("a significant portion of our net worth"). Exact numbers create security risks and go stale quickly.

Why this hard line? Because the letter may become semi-public. If it's stored with your will and the will goes through probate, court documents become part of the public record in most states. Even if the letter itself doesn't enter probate, it may be seen by attorneys, paralegals, accountants, family members you didn't intend, or anyone with access to the physical location where it's stored.

A letter that contains seed phrases isn't an estate planning document. It's a bearer instrument worth your entire Bitcoin stack, sitting in a filing cabinet.

The seed phrases, passwords, and detailed access instructions belong in a separate document — the Technical Access Document — stored in a different, more secure location. The letter references it. The letter does not replicate it.

The Three-Document System

The most effective bitcoin letter to heirs doesn't stand alone. It's one layer in a three-document system that separates information by sensitivity level and access requirements. This approach is used by serious cold storage estate planning practitioners and is the standard we recommend to every family we work with.

Document 1: The Letter of Instructions

This is the document this article is about. High-level, plain-language, written directly to your heirs. It orients them, gives them immediate action steps, and points them toward help.

Security level: Moderate. Can be stored with trust documents, in a home safe, or with your estate attorney. If someone unauthorized reads this letter, they learn that you own Bitcoin and who your advisors are — but they gain zero access to the Bitcoin itself.

Stored: With your trust or estate planning binder. A physical copy in your home safe or filing cabinet. A digital backup in an encrypted location your executor can access. This document is also closely related to a formal Letter of Instructions that may accompany your pour-over will.

Document 2: The Technical Access Document

This is the detailed custody roadmap. It contains exchange account names, hardware wallet models and serial numbers, which custodians hold which assets, the location of hardware wallets, and specific instructions for accessing each account. It may include login credentials for exchanges (or references to a password manager). For multi-signature setups, it identifies each keyholder and their role.

Security level: High. This document, combined with the seed phrases from Document 3, provides full access to the Bitcoin. It should be stored separately from both the Letter of Instructions and the seed phrases.

Stored: Attorney escrow, a second safe deposit box, or a secure location known only to your executor and one trusted backup person. Referenced by the Letter of Instructions but never co-located with it.

Document 3: The Seed Phrase Document

The actual keys. The 24 words. The thing that, if compromised, means everything is gone.

Security level: Maximum. For multi-signature setups, no single document should contain enough keys to move the Bitcoin — they should be distributed among multiple keyholders or locations. For single-signature wallets, this document alone is the master key.

Stored: The most secure location available. A bank vault. A fireproof safe in a separate location from the Technical Access Document. Never in the same place as Documents 1 or 2. Never on a computer. Never photographed. Never emailed.

The beauty of this system is that no single document — even if stolen or compromised — gives an attacker what they need. The Letter tells them Bitcoin exists but not how to get it. The Technical Access Document tells them where the Bitcoin lives but not the keys. The Seed Phrase Document contains the keys but, without the Technical Access Document, an attacker may not know which wallets they belong to or how the multi-sig is configured.

Three documents. Three locations. Three security levels. One system that works.

How to Explain Bitcoin to People Who Don't Understand It

This is where most bitcoin letters to heirs fail. The person writing the letter has been in Bitcoin for years — maybe a decade. They understand wallets and keys and UTXOs and block confirmations. Their heirs understand none of this. The gap is enormous, and the letter has to bridge it in about one page.

Here is a framework that works. Include a section at the top of your letter — before any instructions — that explains Bitcoin in language your heirs will actually understand:

"Bitcoin is digital money. It is not controlled by any bank or government. It exists on a global computer network, and the only way to access it is with a set of secret codes called 'keys.' Think of it like a safe deposit box that exists on the internet — but there is no bank that can open it for you. If you have the keys, you have the Bitcoin. If you lose the keys, the Bitcoin is gone forever. There is no customer service to call, no password reset, no one who can override the system. This is why the instructions in this letter matter so much. Please follow them carefully."

That's it. No talk of blockchain technology, decentralization, monetary policy, or hash rates. Your heirs don't need to understand how Bitcoin works. They need to understand three things: it's real, it's valuable, and it requires extreme care.

If you want to include a more detailed explanation — and for large holdings, we recommend it — attach a one-page "What Is Bitcoin and How Does It Work" appendix to the letter. Keep it under 500 words. Use analogies your specific family members will understand. If your daughter is an accountant, use ledger analogies. If your son is a contractor, use physical safe analogies. Write for your actual heirs, not for a generic audience.

The "Don't Sell Immediately" Paragraph

This section of your bitcoin letter to heirs may save your family more money than anything else you write.

When someone dies, the surviving family members are in crisis. They are grieving. They are overwhelmed by logistics — funeral arrangements, insurance claims, bank accounts, property, mail, subscriptions, bills. Into this chaos arrives an asset they don't understand, possibly worth a life-changing amount of money, that feels dangerous and confusing.

The natural instinct is to convert it to something familiar. Sell the Bitcoin. Get dollars. Put it in a bank account. Feel safe.

This instinct, applied to Bitcoin, is financially devastating for several reasons:

  • Tax implications. Inherited Bitcoin typically receives a stepped-up cost basis to the fair market value at the date of death. This is an enormous tax advantage — it effectively eliminates capital gains tax on all appreciation during the original holder's lifetime. But timing matters. Selling immediately, during a period of market volatility, could mean selling at a low point and crystallizing less benefit. Waiting for proper tax planning can save tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars.
  • Market timing. Bitcoin is volatile. The price on the day your heirs panic-sell might be a temporary low driven by the same news cycle that's overwhelming them. Three months later, the price might be meaningfully higher.
  • Irreversibility. Once Bitcoin is sold, the decision is permanent. You cannot un-sell it. You cannot get back in at the same price. The regret of a hasty liquidation compounds over years and decades.
  • Lost optionality. Your heirs may discover, once the grief subsides and they've spoken with advisors, that holding the Bitcoin aligns with their own financial goals. They lose that option if they've already sold.

Your letter should include an explicit, direct paragraph that says something like:

"I want to be very clear about this: please do not sell any of the Bitcoin for at least 90 days after my death. I know this asset may feel unfamiliar or even frightening. That is exactly why I am asking you to wait. The advisors listed below will help you understand the tax implications, the market conditions, and your options. There is absolutely no urgency to sell. The Bitcoin is not going anywhere. It will wait for you. Please give yourself the time to grieve and to make this decision with clear eyes and professional guidance."

Ninety days is a minimum. For large holdings, six months or even a year may be more appropriate. The point is to create a circuit breaker between grief and a financially irreversible decision.

The Mistakes That Destroy Inherited Bitcoin

Your bitcoin letter to heirs needs a "What NOT to Do" section, and it needs to be blunt. These are not theoretical risks. Every one of the following has happened to real families:

1. Posting about the inheritance on social media

"Dad left us a bunch of Bitcoin!" posted to Facebook or Instagram is an open invitation to scammers. Within hours, the heir will receive messages from "crypto recovery specialists," "tax advisors," and "Bitcoin experts" — all of them fraudulent, all of them after the keys. Your letter should say explicitly: Do not tell anyone outside the immediate family that you have inherited Bitcoin. Do not post about it. Do not discuss amounts with anyone except the professional advisors listed in this letter.

2. Sharing seed phrases with "helpers"

An heir who doesn't understand Bitcoin but knows they need to "do something" with 24 words may type them into a website, share them with a supposed expert found on Google, or hand them to a "crypto-savvy" friend. Every one of these actions can result in immediate, total loss. The letter must be explicit: The seed phrase — the 24 words — is the master key. Anyone who has these words can take all of the Bitcoin instantly and irreversibly. Never share them with anyone. Never type them into any website. Never photograph them. The only people who should see these words are you and the specific professional advisors named below.

3. Discarding hardware wallets

A hardware wallet looks like a USB drive or a small electronic device. To someone who doesn't know what it is, it looks like outdated tech — the kind of thing you throw away when cleaning out a loved one's belongings. Your letter should describe the device specifically: "There is a small black device called a [Brand] [Model] in [location]. It looks like [description]. This device is extremely important. Do not discard it, reset it, or give it away. Bring it to the estate attorney or the technical advisor listed below."

4. Guessing at PINs or passwords

Most hardware wallets have a limited number of PIN attempts before they wipe themselves. An heir who tries to "figure it out" by guessing PINs can permanently lock the device and destroy access to the Bitcoin. The letter should state clearly: Do not try to guess any passwords, PINs, or codes. The devices are designed to erase themselves after too many wrong attempts. Wait for professional help.

5. Trusting unsolicited "help"

After a death, scammers monitor obituaries and public records. They will contact your family offering Bitcoin recovery services, tax optimization, and inheritance management. Every single one is a scam. The letter should name the specific people your heirs should trust — and state that anyone not on the list should be treated with extreme skepticism.

Professional Contacts: Who to Call, in What Order

Your bitcoin letter to heirs should include a prioritized list of professional contacts — the people your heirs should reach out to, in order, upon your death. Don't just list names. Explain why each person matters and what they'll help with.

  1. Estate attorney. This is the first call. The estate attorney initiates the legal process, guides the executor through their duties, and coordinates with the other advisors. They may also hold a copy of the Technical Access Document. Include: name, firm, phone number, email, and a note like "[Name] has been briefed on our Bitcoin holdings and knows the overall estate plan. Call them first."
  2. Tax advisor (CPA). The second call. Before anyone sells, converts, or moves any Bitcoin, the tax implications need to be understood. The stepped-up cost basis, potential state taxes, and timing of any future sales all require professional tax guidance. Include: name, firm, phone number, email.
  3. Bitcoin-knowledgeable financial advisor or custodian. If you work with a financial advisor who understands Bitcoin — not just one who tolerates it — include their information. If you use an institutional custodian, include the custodian's estate/inheritance department contact. This person helps your heirs understand the actual Bitcoin holdings, current value, and options.
  4. Technical contact. If your custody setup is complex — multi-signature, multiple hardware wallets, distributed key storage — include a technical specialist who understands the specific configuration. This might be a Bitcoin consultant, a trusted friend with deep technical knowledge, or a firm that specializes in Bitcoin custody. Include: name, relationship, phone number, and a note explaining their role.

Review these contacts annually. People retire. Firms merge. Phone numbers change. An outdated contact list is almost as bad as no contact list. Date the letter every time you update it.

How to Update the Letter

A bitcoin letter to heirs is not a one-time document. It requires annual review, at minimum, and should be updated any time there's a material change in your Bitcoin holdings, custody arrangements, or advisory team.

Changes that trigger an update:

  • You move Bitcoin between custodians or wallets
  • You open or close an exchange account
  • You change your custody setup (e.g., moving from single-sig to multi-sig)
  • A professional contact changes — new attorney, new CPA, new advisor
  • Your family situation changes — new beneficiaries, divorce, marriage
  • Bitcoin's share of your overall estate changes significantly
  • You acquire a new hardware wallet or retire an old one

Every update should include a new date at the top of the letter. We recommend including a version number as well: "Version 3 — January 2027." This makes it immediately clear which version is current if multiple copies exist.

An outdated bitcoin letter to heirs can be worse than no letter at all. It might reference an exchange account you closed two years ago, a hardware wallet you replaced, or an advisor who retired. Your heirs, following outdated instructions in good faith, will hit dead ends at the worst possible time. If you're also managing this process for aging parents who hold Bitcoin, helping them establish this update habit is one of the most valuable things you can do.

Set a calendar reminder. Review the letter on the same day every year — your birthday, January 1st, or the anniversary of your first Bitcoin purchase. Whatever you'll actually remember.

Where to Store the Letter

Storage matters as much as content. The best bitcoin letter to heirs is useless if your heirs can't find it.

Where the letter SHOULD be stored

  • With your trust documents. If you have a revocable living trust, the letter should be stored with the trust binder. Your successor trustee will find it when they need it.
  • With your estate attorney. Your attorney's office can hold a copy and deliver it to your executor or trustee upon notification of your death.
  • In a home safe or secure filing cabinet. A physical copy in a location your family knows about and can access. Tell your executor and at least one heir where this is.
  • Digital backup. An encrypted copy in a location your executor can access — a password manager, an encrypted cloud folder, or a secure digital vault. Not as the only copy. As a backup.

Where the letter should NOT be stored

  • Inside the will. Wills go through probate. Probate documents become public record. If your bitcoin letter to heirs is an exhibit to or attachment of your will, it may become visible to anyone who searches the court records. The letter itself doesn't contain keys or passwords, but it does reveal that you hold Bitcoin and who your advisors are — information that should remain private.
  • Only on a computer. Computers die. Hard drives fail. Passwords are forgotten. A digital-only letter has a significant risk of being inaccessible precisely when it's needed.
  • In the same location as the seed phrases. The entire point of the three-document system is separation. If the letter and the keys are in the same safe, a single point of compromise or a single point of failure (fire, flood, theft) can expose or destroy both.

The ideal setup: physical copy with your trust binder at home, second physical copy with your estate attorney, encrypted digital backup accessible to your executor. Three copies, two locations, one consistent version.

Complete Template: Your Bitcoin Letter to Heirs

Below is a complete, usable template for a bitcoin letter to heirs. Adapt it to your specific situation, custody arrangements, and family. The sections in brackets require your personal information.

Date: [Month Day, Year]  |  Version: [Number]

A Letter to My Family Regarding Bitcoin

This letter is for: [Names of heirs/beneficiaries]

If you are reading this, I am no longer here, and I am sorry for that. This letter contains important information about an asset I own called Bitcoin. Please read it carefully and follow the instructions below. I have done my best to make this simple and clear. Take your time.

Section 1: What Bitcoin Is

Bitcoin is digital money. It is not controlled by any bank or government. It exists on a global computer network, and the only way to access it is with a set of secret codes called "keys." Think of it like a vault that exists on the internet — but there is no bank manager who can open it for you. If you have the keys, you have the Bitcoin. If the keys are lost, the Bitcoin is gone forever. There is no customer service, no password reset, and no override. This is why the instructions in this letter are so important.

Section 2: What I Own

I own Bitcoin held in the following locations:

  • [Exchange/Custodian Name] — an account in my name. This is a company that holds Bitcoin on my behalf, similar to a brokerage.
  • [Hardware Wallet Brand and Model] — a small physical device located in [general location, e.g., "the home safe" or "the safe deposit box at First National Bank"]. This device stores Bitcoin directly. It looks like [brief physical description]. Do NOT discard this device.
  • [Any other holdings — additional exchanges, custodians, retirement accounts with Bitcoin exposure, etc.]

These holdings represent [general description — e.g., "a significant portion of our family's wealth" or "an important asset worth protecting"]. I have not listed exact amounts here because they change with market conditions. The advisors listed below know the approximate scope.

Section 3: Immediate Steps (First 48 Hours)

  1. Call [Estate Attorney Name] at [phone number]. Tell them I have passed and that you have this letter. They know what to do and will guide you through the next steps. This is the most important phone call.
  2. Secure any physical devices. If you know the location of the hardware wallet described above, make sure it is in a safe place. Do not attempt to use it, unlock it, or guess any PINs.
  3. Do nothing else with the Bitcoin. Do not try to sell it, move it, or "figure it out" on your own. Professional help is coming. The Bitcoin is safe where it is.

Section 4: What NOT to Do

Please take this section seriously. These mistakes are irreversible.

  • Do NOT tell anyone outside the immediate family that you have inherited Bitcoin. Do not post about it on social media. Do not discuss amounts with friends, extended family, or anyone not listed in this letter. Scammers specifically target Bitcoin heirs.
  • Do NOT share the seed phrase (24 words) with anyone. If you find a set of 24 words — on paper, on metal, anywhere — those words are the master key to the Bitcoin. Anyone who has them can take everything instantly. Never type them into any website. Never share them with anyone. Never photograph them.
  • Do NOT discard any small electronic devices. Hardware wallets look like USB drives or small calculators. They may look worthless. They are not.
  • Do NOT try to guess PINs or passwords. Most hardware wallets will erase themselves permanently after too many wrong attempts. Wait for professional help.
  • Do NOT trust unsolicited offers of help. After my death, you may be contacted by people offering "Bitcoin recovery services" or "crypto tax optimization." These are scams. Only work with the specific people listed below.
  • Do NOT sell any Bitcoin for at least 90 days. I know this asset may feel unfamiliar. That is exactly why I ask you to wait. The advisors below will help you understand the tax implications and your options. There is no urgency. The Bitcoin will wait for you. Please give yourself time to grieve and to make this decision with clear eyes.

Section 5: Who to Call

These are the people I trust. Contact them in this order:

  1. Estate Attorney: [Name], [Firm]
    Phone: [Number]  |  Email: [Email]
    [Name] has been briefed on our Bitcoin holdings and knows the overall estate plan. Call them first.
  2. Tax Advisor (CPA): [Name], [Firm]
    Phone: [Number]  |  Email: [Email]
    Before selling, converting, or moving any Bitcoin, consult [Name] about tax implications — especially the stepped-up cost basis.
  3. Financial Advisor / Bitcoin Custodian: [Name], [Firm]
    Phone: [Number]  |  Email: [Email]
    [Name] understands Bitcoin specifically and can help you evaluate options for holding, selling, or managing the inheritance.
  4. Technical Contact: [Name]
    Phone: [Number]  |  Email: [Email]
    [Name] understands the specific technical setup of my Bitcoin storage. If the estate attorney or financial advisor needs help with the hardware wallet or custody configuration, [Name] can assist.

Section 6: Where to Find More Information

This letter is intentionally high-level. It does not contain passwords, codes, or the detailed instructions needed to access the Bitcoin. That information is in a separate document called the Technical Access Document.

The Technical Access Document is stored at: [location — e.g., "in attorney escrow with [Attorney Name]" or "in the safe deposit box at [Bank], Box #[Number]"].

Do not attempt to use the Technical Access Document without professional guidance from the people listed above. They will walk you through it.

Section 7: My Wishes

[This section is personal. Here are some options to consider:]

"I bought Bitcoin because I believe it is the best savings technology humanity has ever created. My hope is that you will take the time to learn about it before making any decisions. If, after careful thought and professional advice, you decide to sell — that is completely okay. But please don't sell out of fear or confusion. Give it time. You may come to see what I saw."

[Or: "I want this Bitcoin to provide financial security for your family. Use it wisely, consult the advisors I've listed, and don't rush into any decisions."]

[Or: "I'd like [X%] to be donated to [cause/organization]. The estate attorney knows this wish and can help execute it in a tax-efficient way."]

With all my love,

[Your Name]

Beyond the Template: Making the Letter Yours

The template above is a starting point. The letter that actually saves your family's Bitcoin will be personal, specific, and written in your voice — not in legal language, not in crypto jargon, but in the same tone you'd use sitting across the kitchen table from your spouse or your adult children.

A few principles for personalizing it:

Write for your specific heirs. If your primary heir is your spouse who has heard you talk about Bitcoin for years, the letter reads differently than if your heirs are adult children who have never expressed interest. Adjust the Bitcoin explanation, the level of urgency in the "what not to do" section, and the emotional tone accordingly.

Be specific about devices and locations. "The hardware wallet is in the safe" is good. "The black Coldcard Mk4 in the grey fabric case is in the bottom drawer of the home safe, behind the insurance documents" is better. Under stress, your heirs will be grateful for every specific detail that reduces ambiguity.

Don't over-explain Bitcoin. The letter is not an educational document. It's an emergency action guide. One paragraph explaining what Bitcoin is. One paragraph explaining why it matters. Then straight to the instructions. If you want to provide more education, write it as an appendix or direct them to your heir education resources.

Date it and sign it. Every time. The date tells your heirs how current the information is. If the letter is dated three months before your death, they can be relatively confident the information is current. If it's dated five years ago, they'll know to verify everything with the advisors.

Read it out loud. Does it sound like you? Would your family recognize your voice in it? If it reads like a legal document or a blog post, rewrite it. This is the last practical communication your family receives from you about this asset. It should sound like you.

The Letter You Write Today

Most Bitcoin holders reading this have not written a bitcoin letter to heirs. The reasons are always the same: it feels morbid, it's never urgent, the custody setup is "still being finalized," there's always something more pressing.

Here is the reality: if you died tomorrow, would your family know what to do with your Bitcoin? Would they know it exists? Would they know not to throw away the hardware wallet? Would they know who to call?

If the answer to any of those questions is no, then the most valuable thing you can do today — more valuable than checking the price, more valuable than optimizing your custody setup, more valuable than reading about the next halving — is to open a document and start writing.

It doesn't have to be perfect. The template above gives you a structure. Thirty minutes of writing today could save your family from losing everything. You can refine it next week. You can update it next month. But write the first version now.

Your Bitcoin is only as safe as your heirs' ability to access it without you. The letter is what makes that possible.
