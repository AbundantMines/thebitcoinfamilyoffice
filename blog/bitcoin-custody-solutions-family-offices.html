The Custody Spectrum

Bitcoin custody exists on a spectrum from full sovereignty (you hold all keys, no third parties) to full delegation (a custodian holds everything, you hold nothing). Every position on this spectrum involves tradeoffs between control, convenience, counterparty risk, and operational complexity.

Self-Custody: Hardware Wallets and Cold Storage

Self-custody means your family controls the private keys that authorize Bitcoin transactions. No third party can freeze, seize, or lose your funds. This is Bitcoin's core value proposition â€” monetary sovereignty â€” expressed at the operational level.

Hardware Wallets

A hardware wallet is a dedicated device that generates and stores private keys offline, signing transactions in an air-gapped or USB-connected environment. The leading options for family office use:

  • Coldcard: The gold standard for security-focused holders. Open-source firmware, air-gapped operation via microSD card, duress PINs, and a no-compromise design philosophy. Not user-friendly â€” but that's the point
  • Foundation Passport: Open-source, air-gapped, with a more polished user experience than Coldcard. QR-code based transaction signing. Gaining traction among sophisticated holders
  • Trezor Model T/Safe: Open-source, well-audited, with a touchscreen interface. Good balance of security and usability. Compatible with most multisig coordinators
  • Ledger: The most commercially successful hardware wallet. Closed-source secure element raises concerns among sovereignty-focused users, but offers the broadest ecosystem integration

For holdings above $1M, use at least two different hardware wallet brands in your custody setup. This protects against firmware vulnerabilities in any single manufacturer. Diversity of signing devices is a fundamental security principle.

Cold Storage Best Practices

  • Air-gapped signing: The signing device should never connect to the internet. Use QR codes or microSD cards to transfer unsigned and signed transactions
  • Seed phrase backup: Use metal seed phrase backups (Cryptosteel, Billfodl) stored in separate geographic locations. Paper degrades; metal survives fire and flood
  • Passphrase protection: Add a BIP39 passphrase to your seed phrase. This creates a "25th word" that makes the seed phrase alone insufficient to access funds
  • Verify receive addresses: Always verify addresses on the hardware wallet's screen before sending funds. Clipboard malware can replace addresses silently

Multi-Signature Custody

Multi-signature (multisig) is the most important advancement in Bitcoin custody for family offices. A multisig wallet requires M signatures from N total keys to authorize a transaction (e.g., 2-of-3, 3-of-5). This eliminates single points of failure while maintaining full sovereignty.

Why Multisig Matters for Families

  • No single point of failure: If one key is lost, stolen, or destroyed, funds are still accessible with the remaining keys
  • No single point of compromise: An attacker who obtains one key cannot steal funds
  • Succession built in: Keys distributed among family members and trusted parties enable natural succession
  • Governance enforcement: The M-of-N threshold mirrors committee-based decision making, requiring consensus to move funds

Common Configurations

2-of-3: The minimum viable multisig. Two keys are needed to sign. Good for individual holders wanting redundancy. For family offices, it's often too thin â€” a single compromise plus a single theft equals total loss.

3-of-5: The sweet spot for most family offices. Five keys can be distributed among family members, professional fiduciaries, and geographic locations. Any three can sign, providing excellent redundancy. Even if two keys are simultaneously compromised, funds are safe.

4-of-7: For ultra-high-net-worth families wanting maximum resilience. Seven keys distributed globally, with any four needed. This provides extraordinary redundancy but increases operational complexity significantly.

Key Distribution Strategy

Where you place keys matters as much as the threshold:

  1. Geographic distribution: Keys should be in different physical locations â€” ideally different cities or countries. A fire, flood, or regional disaster shouldn't be able to destroy enough keys to lock your funds
  2. Jurisdictional distribution: For large holdings, consider placing keys in different legal jurisdictions. A court order in one jurisdiction can't compel production of keys held in another
  3. Role-based distribution: Assign keys to roles (patriarch, successor trustee, attorney, custodian) rather than just individuals. When people change roles, keys transfer with the role

Collaborative Custody

Collaborative custody is the bridge between DIY multisig and full custodial delegation. A collaborative custody provider holds one key in a multisig arrangement while you hold the other keys. The provider can't move funds alone, but can assist with signing when needed.

Leading Providers

Unchained Capital: The pioneer of collaborative custody. Offers 2-of-3 multisig where you hold two keys and Unchained holds one. They provide inheritance planning, IRA custody, and concierge onboarding. Best for families wanting institutional support with self-sovereign control.

Casa: Offers multisig custody with a mobile-first experience. Their Premium tier provides 3-of-5 multisig, inheritance planning, and a dedicated advisor. Casa's key recovery service helps if you lose a key. Best for families wanting a more guided experience.

Nunchuk: A multisig coordinator that supports collaborative custody with multiple key agents. More flexible than Unchained or Casa but less hand-holding. Best for technically sophisticated families wanting customizable setups.

Advantages of Collaborative Custody

  • Professional support: Expert guidance on key management, transaction signing, and emergency procedures
  • Inheritance services: Structured succession protocols that activate upon verified death or incapacity
  • Operational simplicity: The provider handles the complexity of multisig coordination, health checks, and key rotation
  • Sovereignty preserved: You always hold enough keys to move funds without the provider's cooperation
The best custody solution is one that your family will actually use correctly. A perfect multisig setup that nobody maintains is worse than a simple collaborative custody arrangement that gets regular health checks.

Qualified Custodians

For family offices with regulatory requirements â€” especially those structured as registered investment advisors or with institutional LPs â€” qualified custodians may be mandatory. A qualified custodian is a bank, broker-dealer, or trust company that meets SEC requirements for safeguarding client assets.

Bitcoin-Capable Qualified Custodians

  • Coinbase Custody: The largest regulated Bitcoin custodian. SOC 2 Type II certified, $320M insurance policy, segregated cold storage. Minimum $500K
  • Fidelity Digital Assets: Backed by the $5 trillion asset manager. Institutional-grade cold storage with SOC 2 certification. Available to registered advisors and institutions
  • Anchorage Digital: Federally chartered digital asset bank (OCC). Offers custody, staking, trading, and lending. Growing institutional client base
  • BitGo: $700M insurance, multisig-based custody, and a Trust company charter. Popular with funds and family offices

The Custody Tradeoff

Using a qualified custodian means surrendering control of your private keys. This introduces counterparty risk â€” if the custodian is hacked, goes bankrupt, or is subject to government seizure, your Bitcoin may be at risk. The history of crypto custodial failures (Mt. Gox, FTX, Celsius, BlockFi) is a sobering reminder that "not your keys, not your coins" isn't just a slogan.

That said, qualified custodians operate under far more rigorous regulatory and audit requirements than exchanges. The risk is real but meaningfully lower than unregulated alternatives.

ETF Custody: The Easy Button

Bitcoin ETFs (iShares Bitcoin Trust, Fidelity Wise Origin, etc.) offer the simplest possible Bitcoin exposure: buy shares through any brokerage. The ETF issuer handles all custody through qualified custodians.

For family offices, ETFs make sense in specific contexts:

  • Retirement accounts: Bitcoin ETFs can be held in IRAs, 401(k)s, and other tax-advantaged accounts where direct Bitcoin custody isn't possible
  • Fiduciary compliance: Some fiduciaries may be more comfortable with ETF exposure than direct Bitcoin custody, given the regulatory clarity
  • Simplicity: For a small allocation (under 5% of portfolio), the operational overhead of direct custody may not be justified

However, ETFs carry significant disadvantages: annual fees (0.20-0.25%), no self-custody, no direct Bitcoin network access, counterparty risk to the ETF issuer and custodian, and no ability to use Bitcoin as collateral, in multisig, or in estate structures.

Our View

For a family office with Bitcoin as a core holding â€” not just an allocation â€” ETFs are insufficient. They provide price exposure but not the sovereignty, flexibility, and direct control that make Bitcoin unique as a multi-generational asset.

Key-Person Risk and Continuity

The most underrated risk in Bitcoin custody is key-person risk: what happens when the person who manages the keys is unavailable? This includes death, disability, extended travel, loss of mental capacity, or simply resignation from their role.

Mitigating Key-Person Risk

  1. Document everything: Custody architecture, key locations, device passcodes, and recovery procedures should be documented in a secure, accessible format
  2. Cross-train family members: At least two family members should be competent to execute a signing ceremony
  3. Use collaborative custody: A professional custody provider serves as institutional memory and can guide new keyholders through the process
  4. Regular health checks: Quarterly verification that all keys are accessible and all devices are functional. This catches problems before they become emergencies
  5. Succession testing: Periodically test the full succession process â€” including having the successor trustee execute a small transaction â€” to verify the plan works in practice

Insurance and Liability

Bitcoin custody insurance is an evolving market. The primary options:

  • Custodian insurance: Qualified custodians carry insurance against theft and internal fraud. Coverage varies from $100M to $700M+ in aggregate. Important: this is aggregate, not per-client
  • Direct crime insurance: Lloyd's of London and specialty insurers offer crypto-specific crime policies covering theft, hacking, and insider fraud. Premiums range from 0.5-2% of covered value annually
  • Self-insured (custody spread): Rather than paying premiums, some families distribute Bitcoin across multiple custody arrangements, accepting that any single failure would affect only a portion of holdings

For holdings above $10M, a combination of custodian insurance and direct crime coverage is prudent. For holdings above $100M, the insurance market becomes thin â€” self-insurance through diversified custody is often the practical approach.

The Hybrid Approach

The most sophisticated family offices don't use a single custody model â€” they use a layered architecture:

  • Core holdings (60-70%): Multi-signature cold storage, geographically distributed, with collaborative custody support. This is the family's long-term treasury â€” maximum security, low accessibility
  • Operational holdings (20-30%): Collaborative custody with faster signing procedures. Used for planned transactions, collateralization, and liquidity needs
  • Active allocation (5-10%): Qualified custodian or exchange, for trading, lending, or immediate liquidity. Treated as higher-risk â€” only what you can afford to lose to counterparty failure

This tiered approach mirrors how traditional institutions handle cash management â€” vault cash, operating accounts, and petty cash serve different functions with different security profiles.
