Bitcoin Cost Basis Methods: FIFO, HIFO, and Specific ID — Which One Saves the Most Tax?

Your choice of cost basis method can mean the difference between paying 23.8% or 0% on your Bitcoin gains. For high-net-worth families with long-held positions, the wrong default choice costs real money — and carries hidden estate planning consequences most advisors miss.

Educational Content Only: This article is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal, tax, or financial advice. Cost basis methods and their tax treatment are complex and vary by situation. Consult a qualified CPA or tax attorney before changing your cost basis method.

Why Cost Basis Method Is an Estate Planning Decision, Not Just a Tax Decision

Most Bitcoin holders think of cost basis as a tax filing detail — something their accountant handles once a year when calculating gains. For small holders with simple purchase histories, that's roughly accurate. For high-net-worth families with significant Bitcoin accumulated over years of purchases at very different prices, cost basis method selection is a multi-million-dollar estate planning decision.

Here's why: your cost basis method determines not just how much tax you pay when you sell, but also:

  • Which lots are available to receive the stepped-up basis reset at death
  • What basis transfers to heirs when you gift Bitcoin
  • What basis carries into an irrevocable trust — permanently, with no future step-up
  • How your self-custody Bitcoin is reported (or not) on Form 1099-DA
  • Whether your estate pays capital gains on trust distributions after your death

Choosing FIFO by default — which is what the IRS assumes if you don't elect otherwise — is often the highest-tax option for long-term Bitcoin families. And you can't retroactively change methods on lots you've already sold.

The Three Methods: How They Work

FIFO — First In, First Out

FIFO uses the oldest lots first. Every time you sell Bitcoin, the IRS calculates your gain using the cost basis of the Bitcoin you bought first.

Example: You bought 1 BTC at $5,000 in 2019, 1 BTC at $30,000 in 2021, and 1 BTC at $70,000 in 2024. You sell 1 BTC today at $71,000. Under FIFO, your gain is $71,000 − $5,000 = $66,000. Long-term rate: $66,000 × 23.8% (including NIIT) = $15,708 in tax.

When FIFO makes sense: Almost never, for long-term Bitcoin holders with appreciated positions. FIFO works in your favor only if you have high-basis recent lots you'd rather sell first — which is unusual for someone who bought aggressively during bear markets.

The estate planning trap: If you default to FIFO and sell your oldest, lowest-basis lots during your lifetime, you lose the stepped-up basis opportunity on those lots forever. You've paid tax on gains that would have been eliminated entirely if you'd held them to death. Those gains were a gift to the IRS that your heirs won't benefit from.

LIFO — Last In, First Out

LIFO uses the most recently acquired lots first. This works well if your recent purchases are at higher prices than earlier ones — you're selling higher-basis lots and recognizing smaller gains.

IRS status for Bitcoin: LIFO is not straightforwardly available for Bitcoin. The IRS has not explicitly approved LIFO for digital assets in the same way it has for certain inventory accounting. Most Bitcoin tax software defaults to FIFO or Specific ID. If you want LIFO-like results, Specific Identification is the cleaner implementation.

Specific Identification (Spec ID)

Spec ID lets you designate exactly which lots you're selling at the time of each transaction. You pick the lot, document the decision, and report it on your tax return. This requires real record-keeping — you need to know your acquisition date and cost for each lot, and you need to make the designation before or at the time of the sale, not retroactively.

Example (same scenario): You want to sell 1 BTC. Under Spec ID, you designate the 2024 lot (basis: $70,000). Your gain is $71,000 − $70,000 = $1,000. Tax: $1,000 × 23.8% = $238. You've just saved $15,470 compared to FIFO — while preserving your 2019 and 2021 lots intact for stepped-up basis planning at death.

Documentation requirement: The IRS requires that you identify the specific lot at or before the time of sale. A contemporaneous record — exchange confirmation, tax software lot selection screenshot, email to your accountant — is sufficient. You cannot retroactively elect Spec ID on lots you've already sold without documentation.

HIFO — Highest In, First Out

HIFO automatically selects the highest-cost lot each time you sell, minimizing your taxable gain without requiring you to manually choose each lot. It's essentially automated Specific Identification optimized for gain minimization.

IRS status: HIFO is not a standalone IRS-approved method — it's implemented via Specific Identification. If your exchange or tax software offers "HIFO" as a setting, it's applying Spec ID rules behind the scenes. The documentation and contemporaneous election requirements of Spec ID apply.

When HIFO is ideal: If you need to sell Bitcoin regularly — for distributions, lifestyle spending, or rebalancing — and you want to minimize current-year capital gains without managing each transaction manually, HIFO is the most practical approach. It doesn't require per-transaction decision-making but still outperforms FIFO significantly.

Method Comparison: $71,000 Sale, 3 Lots Available

Method Lot Used Basis Gain Tax (23.8%) Lots Preserved
FIFO 2019 lot $5,000 $66,000 $15,708 2021 ($30K), 2024 ($70K)
HIFO / Spec ID 2024 lot $70,000 $1,000 $238 2019 ($5K), 2021 ($30K)
LIFO (if available) 2024 lot $70,000 $1,000 $238 2019 ($5K), 2021 ($30K)

Tax savings from HIFO vs. FIFO: $15,470 per transaction. Over a lifetime of periodic sales, this compounds into seven-figure differences for large holders.

The Estate Planning Dimension: Which Lots to Preserve for Step-Up

Here's where cost basis strategy gets genuinely sophisticated — and where most generic Bitcoin tax guides stop. The estate planning question isn't just "which method minimizes current tax." It's "which lots should I sell now, and which should I hold to death for the step-up?"

The stepped-up basis resets your heirs' cost basis to the Bitcoin price on the date of your death. If you hold a lot with a $5,000 cost basis until death when Bitcoin is worth $200,000, your heir's basis becomes $200,000 — eliminating $195,000 of embedded gain permanently. No tax on 43 years of appreciation.

This creates a clear optimization framework:

The Bitcoin Lot Hierarchy for Estate Planning

  1. Sell first: Short-term lots (held <1 year) — taxed as ordinary income anyway, no long-term rate benefit, no estate planning upside from holding
  2. Sell next: High-basis lots near break-even — minimal gain, minimal step-up value at death
  3. Hold for life: Low-basis lots (purchased in early years at $1K–$30K) — maximum step-up value at death; selling them during life triggers the highest gains for the least estate planning benefit
  4. Donate to charity: Any appreciated lot — charity pays no capital gains, you get a deduction at full FMV. Best for your highest-appreciation lots if charitable giving is part of your plan.

Spec ID / HIFO accomplishes this naturally: it depletes high-basis lots first (minimizing current tax) while preserving low-basis lots intact (maximizing future step-up value). FIFO does the opposite — it depletes your most valuable lots for estate planning purposes first.

Worked Example: $5M Bitcoin Family, 10-Year Horizon

Assume a family with 50 BTC purchased across multiple years:

  • 10 BTC at average $3,000 (2018–2019) = $30,000 total basis
  • 15 BTC at average $15,000 (2020–2021) = $225,000 total basis
  • 25 BTC at average $50,000 (2022–2024) = $1,250,000 total basis

Current price: $71,000. Total portfolio: $3,550,000.

Family needs to sell 10 BTC over the next 3 years for distributions and lifestyle.

Scenario A: FIFO — Sell the 2018-2019 lots first (basis $3,000 avg). Gain = (10 × $71,000) − $30,000 = $680,000. Tax at 23.8% = $161,840. The family has now permanently depleted their lowest-basis lots — the ones worth the most for stepped-up basis planning. At death, heirs inherit only the $15K and $50K lots.

Scenario B: Spec ID / HIFO — Sell the 2022-2024 lots first (basis $50,000 avg). Gain = (10 × $71,000) − $500,000 = $210,000. Tax at 23.8% = $49,980. Tax savings vs. FIFO: $111,860. The family preserves all their low-basis 2018-2021 lots intact for stepped-up basis at death — where they eliminate potentially millions in embedded gains.

The Spec ID strategy saves $111,860 in current tax and preserves dramatically more value for heirs. Over a lifetime, the compounded benefit of this approach easily reaches seven figures for a family in this position.

Cost Basis and Irrevocable Trusts: The Permanent Decision

When Bitcoin is transferred to a revocable trust, the basis carries over — but when you die, the assets receive a stepped-up basis to current value, eliminating all embedded gains. This is the standard estate planning step-up.

When Bitcoin is transferred to an irrevocable trust — a dynasty trust, SLAT, or irrevocable asset protection trust — the basis carries over and stays there permanently. Irrevocable trusts generally do not receive a stepped-up basis at the grantor's death. The trust holds the original cost basis until it eventually sells the asset, at which point it pays capital gains tax.

This creates a critical planning question: which lots do you transfer to an irrevocable trust?

Lot Type Transfer to Irrevocable Trust? Why
Low basis (e.g., $3,000/BTC) ⚠️ Only if structure is compelling Locks in low basis forever; loses step-up opportunity if held in personal estate to death. But estate tax savings may outweigh basis loss for very large estates.
High basis (e.g., $65,000/BTC) ✅ Generally preferred Minimal embedded gain to carry; step-up at death on high-basis lots provides less benefit anyway; trust captures future appreciation outside estate.
Recently purchased (high basis, short-term) ✅ Ideal for irrevocable trusts Minimal gain if trust ever sells; removes future appreciation from estate; grantor trust swap power allows basis arbitrage later.

The grantor trust swap power offers a partial solution: in a properly drafted irrevocable grantor trust (like an IDGT), the grantor retains the power to substitute assets. This allows the grantor to swap a low-basis Bitcoin lot inside the trust for a high-basis asset of equivalent value — effectively moving the low-basis lot back into the personal estate (where it can receive a step-up at death) while keeping the trust funded with equivalent value.

This swap is a non-taxable event if assets of equal value are exchanged — and it lets you manage basis allocation between your personal estate and irrevocable trusts dynamically over time.

The 1099-DA Complication: Why Basis Records Matter More Than Ever

The IRS began requiring exchanges to issue Form 1099-DA in 2026 for the first time — but only for "covered" securities where the exchange has your basis information on file. Bitcoin purchased on an exchange after a certain date is "covered." Bitcoin purchased on an exchange before that date, or ever held in self-custody, is "noncovered."

Noncovered Bitcoin shows up on 1099-DA with the full sale proceeds but no basis. The IRS sees proceeds; the basis field is blank. If you don't document your actual basis separately — exchange purchase receipts, blockchain transaction records, cost basis software exports — the IRS's assumption may be basis = $0, making your entire proceeds a gain.

For high-net-worth families with complex purchase histories across multiple exchanges, wallets, and years, this is a documentation emergency. The basis records you need are:

  • Exchange transaction history (CSV exports) for every account, every year
  • For self-custody purchases: blockchain explorer records + payment confirmation
  • For mining income: fair market value at date received (this is your basis)
  • For gifted Bitcoin received: donor's original basis documentation
  • For inherited Bitcoin: date-of-death fair market value (your stepped-up basis)

This documentation doesn't just matter for current tax filing — it matters for your estate. When your heirs inherit Bitcoin and establish their stepped-up basis, they'll need records showing the FMV on your date of death. If the IRS audits years later, contemporary documentation is far more defensible than reconstructed estimates.

Choosing a Method: The Decision Framework

If You Plan to Hold Most Bitcoin Until Death

Your primary goal is to preserve low-basis lots for the stepped-up basis reset at death while selling only when necessary at minimum tax cost.

Best method: Specific Identification (or HIFO-configured software) — sell high-basis lots first, preserve low-basis lots for heirs. Pair this with a revocable trust as your primary holding structure to ensure clean stepped-up basis at death.

If You Make Regular Distributions or Sales

If you're selling Bitcoin regularly — for trust distributions, lifestyle spending, or portfolio rebalancing — manual Spec ID for each transaction is impractical. HIFO configured in your tax software automates the optimal lot selection.

Best method: HIFO (via Specific Identification settings in Koinly, CoinTracker, or TaxBit) — minimizes gain automatically on each sale while still preserving your lowest-basis lots.

If You're Funding an Irrevocable Trust This Year

You're transferring Bitcoin into a dynasty trust, SLAT, or GRAT. The basis you transfer is permanent — no future step-up.

Best approach: Use Spec ID to identify which lots you're transferring. Prefer high-basis lots (recently purchased, near break-even). Keep low-basis early lots in your personal revocable trust or estate where they can receive a step-up at death. Consider the grantor trust swap power for future basis management.

If You Have a Complex Multi-Wallet History

Multiple exchanges, hardware wallets, and years of purchase history with inconsistent records are unfortunately common for early Bitcoin adopters. In this situation, basis reconstruction becomes critical before you make any more sales.

Best approach: Engage a Bitcoin-specialized CPA (not a generalist) to do a full basis reconstruction using exchange APIs, blockchain analytics, and payment records. This should be done before your next significant sale — and certainly before transferring to any trust structure.

The Bottom Line: Default Is Expensive

The IRS default for Bitcoin is FIFO. FIFO sells your oldest, lowest-basis lots first — exactly the opposite of what a long-term estate plan requires. For a family with $3-5M in Bitcoin purchased over 5-10 years, defaulting to FIFO is a reliable way to pay hundreds of thousands of dollars more in tax over a lifetime, while simultaneously destroying the estate planning value of your most appreciated lots.

Switching to Specific Identification (or HIFO) requires nothing more than an election — typically done by configuring your tax software and having your CPA document the method change on your next tax return. It can be done retroactively on lots that haven't yet been sold (you cannot change the method on already-sold lots).

If you haven't reviewed your cost basis method in the last year, that review is overdue. Pair it with a conversation about which lots are earmarked for irrevocable trust transfers, which are reserved for stepped-up basis, and which are appropriate for charitable giving. Those decisions, made deliberately, are where the real value of sophisticated Bitcoin estate planning is created.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best cost basis method for Bitcoin?

For high-net-worth Bitcoin holders planning to hold long-term and pass Bitcoin to heirs, the best cost basis method is usually Specific Identification (Spec ID) configured as HIFO — sell highest-basis lots first, preserve lowest-basis lots for stepped-up basis at death. If you need to sell, Spec ID lets you designate which lots to use, minimizing current tax while protecting your most appreciated positions for your estate plan. FIFO is the IRS default but is typically the worst option for long-term Bitcoin families.

Does cost basis carry over when Bitcoin is transferred to a trust?

It depends on the trust type. Revocable trusts: basis carries over, and heirs receive a stepped-up basis at death — the best outcome. Irrevocable trusts (dynasty trust, SLAT, IDGT): basis carries over permanently, no step-up at death. The trust holds your original cost basis until it sells. This makes basis selection critical for irrevocable trust funding — prefer high-basis lots that go in, keep low-basis lots in your estate for the death step-up.

Is HIFO IRS-approved for Bitcoin?

HIFO is not a standalone IRS-approved method — it's implemented through Specific Identification, which IS IRS-approved. To use HIFO legitimately, you must apply Specific Identification rules: identify each lot at the time of sale and maintain contemporaneous records. Tax software that offers "HIFO" as a setting is applying Specific Identification behind the scenes. The documentation requirements are the same as Spec ID — you need acquisition date, cost, and sale price for each lot.

What happens to my Bitcoin cost basis when I give Bitcoin as a gift?

Gift recipients take your original cost basis (carryover basis). If your Bitcoin has a $5,000 basis and is worth $71,000, the recipient's basis is $5,000 — they owe tax on the full $66,000 gain when they sell. For charitable giving, the charity pays no capital gains, making high-appreciation Bitcoin an ideal charitable gift. For family members, gifting low-basis Bitcoin is estate-tax efficient (removes appreciation from your estate) but creates embedded gains for the recipient — plan accordingly.

How does Form 1099-DA affect Bitcoin cost basis tracking?

Form 1099-DA (first issued 2026) reports Bitcoin sales to the IRS — but only includes basis information for "covered" securities. Self-custody Bitcoin or Bitcoin held before exchange basis-tracking requirements may be reported as "noncovered" with no basis. If you don't provide your own basis documentation on your tax return, the IRS may assume zero basis, making your entire proceeds taxable. Contemporaneous records — exchange statements, blockchain records, purchase receipts — are essential.

Can I change my cost basis method after previous years?

You can change your cost basis method going forward, but you cannot retroactively change the method for lots you've already sold. If you've been using FIFO and switch to Spec ID, the change applies to future sales only. This makes early method selection important — the longer you've been defaulting to FIFO, the more you've potentially overpaid. Switch now for future sales, and document the method change with your CPA on your next tax return.
