Bitcoin Estate Planning in Oregon: Low $1M Estate Tax Threshold

Oregon imposes the lowest state estate tax Bitcoin family office minimum requirements in the United States. At today's Bitcoin prices, fewer than a dozen coins can trigger a state estate tax bill. Every Oregon Bitcoin holder must have a plan.

Oregon Estate Tax Bypass Trust

Oregon is, by a significant margin, the most dangerous state in the United States for Bitcoin estate planning. While most states either have no estate tax or set their exemptions high enough to exclude the majority of middle-class wealth, Oregon draws its line at just $1,000,000 — a threshold that Bitcoin's appreciation has already blown past for an enormous number of ordinary holders.

This is not a problem for the ultra-wealthy alone. It is a problem for the Portland software engineer who bought 15 BTC during the 2017 cycle. For the Eugene small business owner who stacked quietly during the bear market. For the Bend entrepreneur who received Bitcoin as early compensation from a startup. At today's prices, the Oregon estate tax is a problem for a very large and rapidly growing cohort of Bitcoin holders who may not even think of themselves as "wealthy" in the traditional sense.

This guide covers Oregon's estate tax structure, why married couples are particularly exposed, the critical role of bypass trusts, and how Bitcoin family office in Wyoming structures can provide planning that Oregon simply cannot offer on its own.

Oregon's Estate Tax: The Lowest Threshold in the Nation

Oregon is one of only twelve states (plus Washington D.C.) that impose a state estate tax separate from the federal estate tax. Most of those states set their exemptions at $2 million or higher. Oregon's $1 million exemption is the lowest anywhere in the country, matching only Massachusetts (which also uses $1M as of this writing).

The federal estate tax exemption in 2026 is approximately $13.6 million per individual. That means for federal purposes, the vast majority of Americans pay no estate tax. Oregon, however, starts taxing estates at just $1 million — a 13x lower threshold. There is no indexing for inflation. The exemption has remained at $1 million for years while Bitcoin and asset values have risen dramatically.

⚠ Bitcoin Price Math

With Bitcoin at approximately $95,000 per coin, an Oregon resident holding just 10.53 BTC has a Bitcoin estate worth exactly $1 million — right at the exemption threshold. Any holder with more than approximately 11 BTC is exposed to Oregon estate tax. This is not a planning problem for billionaires. It is a planning problem for many thousands of Oregon Bitcoin holders right now.

Oregon Estate Tax Rate Schedule

Oregon's estate tax is progressive, with rates starting at 10% and climbing to 16% for the largest estates. The rates apply to the taxable estate above the $1 million exemption:

Taxable Estate Value Oregon Estate Tax Rate
$1,000,000 – $2,000,000 10%
$2,000,000 – $3,000,000 10.25%
$3,000,000 – $5,000,000 10.5%
$5,000,000 – $7,000,000 11%
$7,000,000 – $9,000,000 12%
$9,000,000 – $10,000,000 13%
Over $10,000,000 16%

It is important to understand that this is a tax on the total estate value above $1 million, not just on Bitcoin. If an Oregon resident has $800,000 in Bitcoin plus $400,000 in other assets (home equity, retirement accounts, brokerage), their $1.2M taxable estate triggers Oregon estate tax on $200,000 at 10% — a $20,000 bill that could have been entirely avoided with proper planning.

The Portability Problem: Oregon's Non-Portable Exemption

Here is where Oregon's estate tax becomes particularly dangerous for married couples: Oregon's $1 million exemption is not portable between spouses.

At the federal level, when the first spouse dies, their unused estate tax exemption can be "ported" to the surviving spouse, effectively doubling the combined exemption to $27.2 million (in 2026). This portability election must be made on a timely filed estate tax return, but it is automatic and powerful.

Oregon does not offer portability. When the first spouse dies, their $1 million Oregon exemption is gone unless it was used — either to make lifetime gifts or to fund a specific type of trust known as a bypass trust (also called a credit shelter trust or family trust).

The Portability Trap for Oregon Couples

Without a bypass trust, here is what happens to an Oregon couple with a combined estate of $1,500,000:

• First spouse dies. Everything passes to the surviving spouse — which qualifies for the unlimited marital deduction, so no Oregon estate tax at the first death.

• Surviving spouse now holds the entire $1,500,000 estate. Their own Oregon exemption is $1 million. The remaining $500,000 is exposed to Oregon estate tax at 10% — a $50,000 bill.

• First spouse's $1 million exemption was completely wasted. The couple effectively had only one exemption instead of two.

With proper bypass trust planning, the couple can shelter up to $2 million from Oregon estate tax — saving $100,000 or more in tax.

The Bypass Trust: Oregon's Essential Planning Tool

For married Oregon Bitcoin holders, a bypass trust is not optional — it is mandatory for effective estate planning. Here is how it works:

When the first spouse dies, instead of all assets passing outright to the surviving spouse, the estate is split. The first $1 million (or up to the available exemption) flows into a bypass trust — a trust that is not included in the surviving spouse's taxable estate. The bypass trust can still benefit the surviving spouse (income, principal distributions for health, education, maintenance, and support), but it is not "owned" by them for estate tax purposes.

The remaining assets pass to the surviving spouse through the unlimited marital deduction, triggering no estate tax at the first death.

When the surviving spouse later dies, the bypass trust assets pass directly to the ultimate beneficiaries — typically children — outside the surviving spouse's taxable estate. Oregon estate tax applies only to the surviving spouse's own estate, which has already been reduced by the amount placed in the bypass trust.

Result: the couple has effectively doubled their Oregon estate tax exemption from $1 million to $2 million, potentially saving $100,000 or more in Oregon estate tax.

Oregon Capital Gains Tax: Another Layer of Exposure

Oregon's estate tax is only one dimension of the planning challenge. Oregon also taxes capital gains as ordinary income, with a top rate of 9.9%. This is the second-highest state capital gains rate in the nation, behind only California.

When Oregon Bitcoin holders sell appreciated Bitcoin during their lifetime, they face:

Tax Rate
Federal long-term capital gains 20%
Federal Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) 3.8%
Oregon state capital gains 9.9%
Combined effective rate ~33.7%

For an Oregon holder with $500,000 in Bitcoin gains, that 33.7% combined rate represents a $168,500 tax burden. Combined with potential estate tax exposure, Oregon's dual threat — capital gains during life, estate tax at death — makes planning urgent at every level of Bitcoin wealth.

Unlike California, Oregon is not a community property state. Oregon is a common law property state, which means only the deceased spouse's half of jointly held property receives a step-up in basis — not the surviving spouse's half. Oregon Bitcoin holders do not benefit from the double step-up advantage available in community property states like California or Bitcoin family office in Texas.

The Scenario: Portland Couple, 15 BTC

A Portland couple purchased 15 BTC in 2019 at $5,000 per coin — a total investment of $75,000. Those 15 BTC are now worth approximately $1,425,000 at $95,000 per coin.

Their total estate — Bitcoin plus home equity, retirement accounts, other savings — is approximately $2,000,000.

Without any planning:
At the first death, everything passes outright to the surviving spouse (marital deduction). No Oregon estate tax yet.
At the second death, the surviving spouse holds a $2,000,000 estate with a $1,000,000 Oregon exemption. Oregon estate tax applies to the $1,000,000 above the exemption at 10% = $100,000 in Oregon estate tax.

With a bypass trust:
At the first death, $1,000,000 flows into a bypass trust for the benefit of the surviving spouse and children. The remaining $1,000,000 passes outright to the surviving spouse.
At the second death, the surviving spouse's estate is $1,000,000 — exactly at the Oregon exemption threshold. Oregon estate tax = $0.
The bypass trust assets pass to children free of Oregon estate tax.

✓ Savings: $100,000 in Oregon estate tax eliminated through bypass trust planning alone.

Oregon Trust Law: Solid But Limited

Oregon adopted the Uniform Trust Code and offers a reasonable trust jurisdiction for standard planning purposes. Oregon trustees are well-regulated, and Oregon courts are experienced in trust administration matters.

However, Oregon's trust law has meaningful limitations compared to jurisdictions like Wyoming:

  • No Domestic Asset Protection Trusts (DAPTs) — Oregon does not allow self-settled asset protection trusts. Creditors can reach assets that you transfer to a trust in which you retain a beneficial interest.
  • Rule Against Perpetuities — Oregon limits trust duration to 90 years under the Uniform Statutory Rule Against Perpetuities, shorter than perpetual dynasty trusts available in Wyoming.
  • No tax-advantaged Bitcoin Trust Type Selector tools — Oregon lacks the purpose trust flexibility, directed trust provisions, and other advanced features that make Wyoming uniquely powerful.

Why Oregon Bitcoin Holders Should Consider Wyoming Trusts

Wyoming has become the gold standard for advanced Bitcoin trust planning, and Oregon holders have particularly strong reasons to look to Wyoming:

Asset Protection Through Wyoming DAPTs

A Wyoming Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) allows you to contribute assets to a trust in which you remain a beneficiary while protecting those assets from future creditors. Oregon creditors cannot easily reach assets in a properly structured Wyoming trust with a Wyoming trustee. The lookback period for fraudulent transfers in Wyoming is short, and the legal framework is sophisticated and well-tested.

Dynasty Trust Planning — Perpetual Duration

Wyoming abolished the Rule Against Perpetuities for properly drafted trusts. An Oregon family can establish a Wyoming dynasty trust to hold Bitcoin in perpetuity — across multiple generations — without the 90-year limitation that Oregon's law would otherwise impose. Given that many Oregon Bitcoin holders are relatively young (the Portland tech community skews toward younger professionals who adopted Bitcoin early), perpetual dynasty trust planning is particularly relevant.

Oregon-to-Wyoming Estate Tax Planning

Some Oregon families establish Wyoming trusts specifically to reduce Oregon estate tax exposure. This strategy — funding a Wyoming irrevocable trust with Bitcoin, reducing the Oregon taxable estate — can work when properly structured. The key requirements:

  • The trust must be genuinely irrevocable (you cannot retain control)
  • A Wyoming corporate trustee must be appointed and must exercise meaningful independent control
  • The transfer must be a completed gift for gift tax purposes
  • Any annual gift tax exclusion or lifetime exemption amounts must be properly tracked

Transfers that reduce the Oregon taxable estate below $1 million (or that protect the first spouse's exemption from being wasted) can eliminate Oregon estate tax entirely. The planning does require giving up control of the assets, which is why annual gifting programs and bypass trusts are often preferred first steps.

Oregon RUFADAA: Digital Asset Access for Fiduciaries

Oregon adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act through Oregon Revised Statutes §§ 114.255–114.295. This law grants fiduciaries — trustees, personal representatives, and agents under power of attorney — the legal authority to access and manage digital assets, including Bitcoin, when the account holder has expressly authorized such access.

For Oregon Bitcoin holders, this means your estate planning documents must include explicit digital asset authorization language. Without it, your executor or trustee may be legally blocked from accessing your wallets, exchange accounts, or multisig custody arrangements — even with the best intentions.

Every Oregon Bitcoin holder's revocable trust, will, and durable power of attorney should explicitly:

  • Authorize the fiduciary to access, manage, and transfer digital assets
  • Provide (or direct to) private key access, seed phrase storage, or custody instructions
  • Name a technically capable trustee or co-trustee who understands Bitcoin custody

Oregon has a notable Bitcoin community concentrated in its major cities. Bend's outdoor-industry and remote-work community includes a growing cohort of Bitcoin-forward professionals who relocated from California and other high-cost states. These are not people who thought they needed "estate planning." They are people who bought Bitcoin, held it, and were rewarded. Now the planning is urgent.

Annual Gifting to Reduce Oregon Taxable Estate

One of the simplest strategies for Oregon Bitcoin holders near the $1 million threshold is a systematic annual gifting program. The federal annual gift tax exclusion allows you to give $19,000 per recipient per year (2026) without using any lifetime exemption. Oregon does not have a gift tax.

For an Oregon holder with a $1.3M estate, gifting $300,000 in Bitcoin to adult children over three to four years (within annual exclusion amounts) could reduce the estate below $1 million and eliminate Oregon estate tax exposure entirely — at no gift tax cost.

This strategy requires careful documentation, especially for Bitcoin gifts: date of transfer, fair market value at time of gift, and proper reporting on the federal gift tax return (Form 709) if any individual gift exceeds the annual exclusion.

Should Oregon Bitcoin Holders Move to Another State?

For large Bitcoin holders in Oregon, the question of domicile change is increasingly relevant. Moving to Nevada, Wyoming, or Texas — all of which have no state estate tax and no state income tax — eliminates both Oregon capital gains tax and Oregon estate tax exposure.

Unlike California (where the Franchise Tax Board aggressively audits domicile changes), Oregon's Department of Revenue is less aggressive but still scrutinizes partial or clearly tax-motivated moves. The key is a genuine, permanent change: new home, new driver's license, new voter registration, and a meaningful shift in where you spend your time.

For holders with estates above $5 million — where Oregon estate tax rates reach 11% and above — the savings from a genuine domicile change can exceed $500,000. For those with estates between $1M and $2M, bypass trust planning and annual gifting are typically more practical than relocation.

Summary: The Oregon Bitcoin Planning Framework

Oregon's $1 million estate tax threshold makes it the most dangerous state in the US for Bitcoin estate planning. Here is the complete framework:

  1. Bypass trust — mandatory for married couples — Oregon's non-portable exemption means married couples must use a bypass trust to double their combined exemption from $1M to $2M. Without it, one exemption is wasted.
  2. Annual gifting program — Systematic annual gifts can reduce the taxable estate below $1M over time. Oregon has no gift tax. Up to $19,000 per recipient per year is federally excluded.
  3. Wyoming trust for asset protection and dynasty planning — Oregon offers neither DAPTs nor perpetual dynasty trusts. Wyoming provides both, and Oregon families can access Wyoming's advantages through a properly structured Wyoming trust with a Wyoming trustee.
  4. Capital gains planning — Oregon's 9.9% capital gains rate plus federal taxes creates a ~33.7% combined rate. CRT, charitable gifting, and Roth conversion strategies can reduce this burden.
  5. RUFADAA authorization — All estate planning documents must explicitly authorize fiduciaries to access and manage Bitcoin.
  6. Mining deductions — Bitcoin mining creates legitimate business deductions that can offset income and manage taxable estate growth.
  7. Consider domicile change for large estates — For holders above $5M, a genuine move to Nevada, Wyoming, or Texas eliminates both Oregon estate tax and capital gains tax.
