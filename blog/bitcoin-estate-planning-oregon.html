Bitcoin Estate Planning in Oregon

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Oregon is among the more challenging states in the country for Bitcoin estate planning. The combination of a top income tax rate of 9.9% that applies to capital gains without preference, a state estate tax with one of the lowest exemption thresholds in the nation — $1 million — and a trust taxation framework that can reach out-of-state trusts creates significant planning requirements for Oregon Bitcoin families. This is not a counsel of despair: there are specific, well-established strategies that address each layer of Oregon's tax burden. But they require proactive implementation, and the window to capture certain benefits closes as estates grow.

Oregon's 9.9% Income Tax on Bitcoin Capital Gains

Oregon imposes a graduated income tax with a top rate of 9.9% (Oregon Revised Statutes §316.037). Oregon does not distinguish between ordinary income and capital gains for state purposes — all income is subject to the same graduated rates. Long-term capital gains on Bitcoin, which benefit from federal preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%), receive no state-level capital gains preference in Oregon. A high-income Oregon resident realizing a $5 million Bitcoin gain pays Oregon income tax at 9.9% on that gain, in addition to the 23.8% federal rate — an effective combined rate approaching 33.7%.

Oregon added an additional income tax for high earners through the Multnomah County Preschool for All program (PFA Tax) — residents of Multnomah County (Portland metro) with income above $125,000 pay an additional 1.5% (income $125,001–$250,000) or 3% (income above $250,000). Portland and Multnomah County residents realizing large Bitcoin gains face a combined state and county income tax rate approaching 12.9% before federal tax is added — among the highest combined state/local income tax rates in the country.

Oregon's income tax applies to Oregon residents' worldwide income and to non-residents' Oregon-source income. Bitcoin gains are sourced to the taxpayer's state of domicile, not the location of any server or exchange — an Oregon resident who sells Bitcoin while traveling in Nevada still owes Oregon income tax on the gain.

Oregon's Estate Tax: Starting at $1 Million

Oregon's state estate tax (ORS Chapter 118) is one of the most aggressive in the country, with an exemption of $1 million — not indexed for inflation, not matching the federal exemption, and not increasing with standard-of-living adjustments. Every dollar of taxable estate above $1 million is subject to Oregon estate tax at rates ranging from 10% to 16%.

For Oregon Bitcoin families, the practical implication is stark: any estate worth more than $1 million — which includes any meaningful Bitcoin position plus home equity, retirement accounts, and other assets — faces Oregon estate tax. A family with a $3 million estate (modest by Bitcoin family office standards) may owe $200,000 or more in Oregon estate tax. A $10 million estate may owe $1.4 million in Oregon estate tax, in addition to any federal estate tax exposure above the $13.99 million federal threshold.

Oregon's estate tax exemption is $1 million — not indexed for inflation. For Bitcoin families, that threshold is alarmingly low. Proactive planning is not optional; it's urgent.

The Oregon estate tax is levied on the value of all property included in the Oregon gross estate, which generally follows federal gross estate rules under IRC §2031. Bitcoin held directly by an Oregon resident at death is included at fair market value. Bitcoin held in a revocable trust is included. Bitcoin held in an irrevocable trust properly structured during the grantor's lifetime may not be included — this is the primary estate tax planning opportunity for Oregon Bitcoin families.

Oregon Estate Tax Planning Strategies

  • Annual exclusion gifting: Oregon does not have a gift tax. Gifts of Bitcoin during lifetime are not subject to Oregon estate tax — they reduce the taxable estate dollar for dollar. A systematic gifting program using the federal annual exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2024) and larger amounts using the federal lifetime exemption can meaningfully reduce Oregon estate tax exposure over time.
  • Irrevocable trust funding: Properly structured irrevocable trusts — GRATs, SLATs, dynasty trusts — can remove Bitcoin from the Oregon gross estate if funded during the grantor's lifetime. Oregon has no gift tax, making lifetime transfers particularly valuable. The trust must be genuinely irrevocable and the grantor must relinquish the retained powers that would cause estate inclusion.
  • Oregon Qualified Terminable Interest Property (QTIP) trusts: At first death, assets passing to a surviving spouse qualify for the Oregon marital deduction — fully deferring Oregon estate tax until the second death. A QTIP trust for the surviving spouse defers the estate tax while allowing the surviving spouse to receive income. Combined with aggressive use of irrevocable trusts after first death, this can significantly reduce the overall Oregon estate tax burden.
  • Life insurance: An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) can provide liquidity to pay Oregon estate tax at death without requiring a forced Bitcoin sale. For families with estates near or above the $1 million threshold (which is essentially every meaningful Bitcoin family), life insurance planning deserves consideration alongside traditional estate planning.
  • Charitable planning: Charitable gifts reduce the Oregon taxable estate and can provide significant income tax deductions as well. Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) allow Bitcoin to be sold without immediate capital gains recognition while providing an income stream and charitable deduction.

Oregon Trust Taxation Rules

Oregon taxes trusts as resident trusts if the trust was created by an Oregon resident (ORS §316.283). This "creator rule" means that a trust established by an Oregon resident is potentially subject to Oregon income tax on all its income, even if the trustee is located in Wyoming and the beneficiaries live elsewhere. Oregon's reach extends to trusts created by Oregon residents regardless of trust situs.

However, Oregon's creator rule applies to undistributed trust income — income that accumulates in the trust. Distributions to non-Oregon beneficiaries may reduce Oregon's jurisdictional claim, and certain trust structures can limit Oregon's reach. The specifics depend on the trust's structure, beneficiary profile, and whether the trust qualifies as a grantor trust for Oregon purposes.

For Oregon residents considering a Wyoming or South Dakota trust situs, the analysis must include Oregon's creator rule and how it interacts with the chosen structure. An out-of-state trust situs alone does not eliminate Oregon income tax if Oregon created the trust through an Oregon-resident grantor. Consultation with a tax advisor experienced in Oregon trust taxation is essential before implementing any out-of-state trust strategy.

Oregon's Uniform Trust Code

Oregon adopted the Uniform Trust Code in 2005 (ORS Chapter 130), providing a modern statutory framework for trust administration, modification, and dispute resolution. Oregon's UTC includes directed trust provisions that allow trust instruments to separate investment authority from trustee administrative functions — applicable to Bitcoin trusts where a custody specialist should hold investment direction authority.

Oregon does not have a self-settled asset protection trust statute. Grantors cannot be discretionary beneficiaries of their own irrevocable trusts with creditor protection under Oregon law. For Oregon residents seeking asset protection trust structures, Nevada or South Dakota trust situs (with appropriate trustees in those jurisdictions) provides the framework Oregon lacks domestically — though Oregon's creator rule tax implications must still be analyzed.

Domicile Planning for Oregon Bitcoin Families

For Bitcoin families in Oregon facing both the 9.9% income tax and the $1 million estate tax threshold, domicile change is the most impactful available planning move — potentially eliminating both the income tax on Bitcoin gains and the estate tax simultaneously. Washington State, while having its own estate tax and capital gains tax issues, eliminates the income tax on gains below $262K. Nevada, Texas, Wyoming, Florida, and South Dakota eliminate both income tax and estate tax entirely.

A genuine domicile change from Oregon requires establishing a new state as the permanent home: registering to vote in the new state, obtaining a new state driver's license, changing the address on financial accounts and the IRS, spending more than 183 days in the new state, and generally moving the center of one's life to the new jurisdiction. Oregon does not have the same 10-year lookback aggressiveness as California, but the Department of Revenue does audit domicile changes that coincide with large income tax events — particularly Bitcoin sales shortly after a claimed domicile change.

Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
