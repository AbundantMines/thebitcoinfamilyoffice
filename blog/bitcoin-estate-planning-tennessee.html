Bitcoin Estate Planning in Tennessee

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Tennessee completed its transition to a true no-income-tax state on January 1, 2022, when the Hall Income Tax — a 6% tax on interest and dividends that had been the state's only individual income tax — was fully phased out and repealed. For Bitcoin families, the timing matters: Tennessee now joins Texas, Florida, Wyoming, Nevada, South Dakota, and Washington as states with zero state income tax on capital gains. Combined with no estate or inheritance tax, a recently enacted trust investment services framework, and a low overall cost structure, Tennessee has emerged as a legitimate option for Bitcoin families — particularly those in the Southeast.

No Income Tax on Bitcoin Gains: Post-Hall Tax Tennessee

The Hall Income Tax (Tennessee Code Annotated §67-2-101 et seq.) was Tennessee's longstanding narrow income tax on interest and dividend income. It explicitly did not tax wages, salaries, capital gains, or most other forms of income — a reflection of Article XI, Section 8 of the Tennessee Constitution, which prohibits a general income tax but was interpreted to allow the narrow Hall Tax on investment income.

The Hall Tax was phased out over five years: from 6% in 2016 to 5% (2017), 4% (2018), 3% (2019), 2% (2020), 1% (2021), and 0% effective January 1, 2022. Bitcoin capital gains — never covered by the Hall Tax since it applied only to interest and dividends, not capital gains — have been exempt from Tennessee income tax throughout. The repeal of the Hall Tax simply ensures that no residual income tax exposure exists for any investment income type.

Tennessee's constitution prohibits a general income tax on wages or salaries. While the constitutional provision has been the subject of legal debate regarding other types of income, the practical outcome for Bitcoin families is clear: capital gains on Bitcoin realized by Tennessee residents are subject only to federal tax. There is no state-level income tax bite, making Tennessee one of approximately eight states in the country with this advantage.

No State Estate or Inheritance Tax

Tennessee repealed its inheritance tax effective January 1, 2016, and had previously repealed its estate tax as well. Tennessee currently imposes neither a state estate tax nor an inheritance tax on beneficiaries. Tennessee residents' estates face only federal estate tax at the $13.99 million per-person exemption (2025). For families with estates below the federal threshold, the only death tax concern is federal — and for estates below $13.99 million per person, there is none.

This complete absence of state death taxation makes Tennessee one of the most favorable states in the country for building and transferring Bitcoin wealth across generations — the state creates no additional tax friction at death, during lifetime accumulation, or on distributions from trust structures.

Tennessee's constitution prohibits a general income tax, the Hall Tax on dividends and interest was fully repealed in 2022, and no estate tax exists. For Bitcoin families in the Southeast, that's a clean sweep.

Tennessee Investment Services Trust Act (TISTA)

Tennessee enacted the Tennessee Investment Services Trust Act (Tennessee Code Annotated §35-15-510 et seq.) — often referred to as TISTA — creating a framework for self-settled, asset-protected trusts with specific provisions designed for investment management purposes. TISTA allows a Tennessee resident to establish an irrevocable trust of which they can be a discretionary beneficiary while enjoying creditor protection for assets properly transferred to the trust.

Key features of Tennessee's TISTA framework:

  • Self-settled spendthrift protection: A Tennessee investment services trust can name the grantor as a discretionary beneficiary, protecting trust assets from the grantor's future creditors — a form of Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) mechanism.
  • Two-year fraudulent transfer limitation: Tennessee's fraudulent transfer lookback period for TISTA trusts is two years, similar to Nevada — among the shorter periods in any state with a DAPT statute.
  • Tennessee-qualified trustee requirement: The trust must have at least one Tennessee-qualified trustee — a Tennessee resident individual or a qualified institution with a place of business in Tennessee that maintains trust records and prepares or arranges for income tax filings.
  • Investment management flexibility: TISTA was specifically designed with investment management in mind, allowing co-trustees and investment advisors to share authority over the trust's investment portfolio — a directed trust-style framework that suits Bitcoin custody arrangements.

TISTA represents Tennessee's effort to compete with Nevada, South Dakota, and Wyoming for trust situs business. While Tennessee's framework is less established and has a shorter track record than Nevada's NAPT or South Dakota's dynasty trust regime, TISTA provides a meaningful asset protection vehicle for Tennessee residents who prefer to keep their trust infrastructure local.

Tennessee Trust Law Framework

Tennessee adopted the Uniform Trust Code (Tennessee Code Annotated §35-15-101 et seq.) providing a modern statutory foundation for trust administration, modification, and dispute resolution. The Tennessee UTC includes directed trust provisions that allow trust instruments to grant investment authority to parties other than the trustee — directly applicable to Bitcoin trusts where a custody specialist should hold investment direction authority.

Tennessee's Uniform Trust Code also provides for trust decanting (§35-15-816), allowing a trustee with absolute discretion over principal to decant assets into a new trust with updated terms — useful for modernizing Bitcoin trust custody provisions without triggering tax consequences.

Tennessee does not have a rule against perpetuities for trusts established under TISTA, allowing dynasty trust structures to persist for up to 360 years. This is substantially longer than most states' common law or statutory perpetuities periods, though it falls short of Wyoming's and South Dakota's true perpetuity authorization. For most practical planning horizons, 360 years is effectively unlimited.

Nashville, Memphis, and Tennessee's Growing Bitcoin Community

Nashville in particular has emerged as a growing hub for Bitcoin activity in the Southeast — a function of the city's significant in-migration from higher-cost states, its entrepreneurial culture, and several Bitcoin-focused conferences and events held there in recent years. This growing ecosystem translates into increasing availability of Bitcoin-aware legal, accounting, and advisory professionals in the Tennessee market, reducing the need to rely entirely on out-of-state specialists.

Tennessee vs. Wyoming for Trust Situs

Tennessee-resident Bitcoin families with large positions should compare TISTA and Tennessee's UTC provisions against Wyoming's more mature and Bitcoin-specific trust infrastructure. Wyoming's DPTA framework offers more explicit directed trust provisions, Wyoming's SPDI charter provides institutional Bitcoin custody with a dedicated regulatory framework, and Wyoming's complete abolition of the rule against perpetuities allows true perpetuity dynasty trusts rather than Tennessee's 360-year limit.

For Tennessee families who prefer their trust infrastructure to be geographically and legally closer to home, TISTA provides a reasonable alternative. For those seeking the most advanced Bitcoin-specific trust structure, a Wyoming-sited directed trust administered by a Wyoming trustee — with the family continuing to live in Tennessee — captures the best of both: Tennessee's favorable domicile environment and Wyoming's purpose-built trust law. Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
