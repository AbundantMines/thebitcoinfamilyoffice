Bitcoin Estate Planning in Maryland

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Maryland Est. 9 min read

Maryland holds the distinction of being the only state in the country that imposes both a state estate tax and a state inheritance tax. For Bitcoin families domiciled in Maryland, this creates a compounding transfer tax burden that demands immediate and comprehensive planning. A Bitcoin estate that exceeds Maryland's estate tax exemption (currently $5 million) faces a Maryland estate tax of up to 16%. And separately — independently — assets passing to siblings, nieces, nephews, and other collateral relatives face a Maryland inheritance tax of 10%, regardless of the estate's total size. These are not alternative taxes; they are cumulative. A large Bitcoin estate passing to a non-direct-heir can face both.

Maryland is, by this measure, one of the most demanding transfer tax environments in the country. Bitcoin families in Maryland who have not engaged in proactive lifetime transfer planning are at serious risk of losing a significant portion of their accumulated Bitcoin wealth to state transfer taxes at death — before the federal estate tax is even calculated. Bitcoin estate planning in Maryland requires advisors with deep fluency in both the state estate tax and the inheritance tax, and the full range of trust and lifetime transfer strategies that can systematically reduce both obligations.

Maryland's Dual Transfer Tax: Estate Tax and Inheritance Tax

The Maryland Estate Tax

Maryland's estate tax is codified at Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 7-301 et seq. As of 2024, the Maryland estate tax exemption is $5 million per individual. Maryland does not index this exemption for inflation and does not allow portability of a deceased spouse's unused exemption (unlike the federal system). The Maryland estate tax rate structure reaches up to 16% on the largest taxable amounts. For a Bitcoin family with an $8 million estate, the Maryland estate tax liability can approach $450,000 — in addition to any federal estate tax obligation.

Notably, Maryland does not have a state gift tax. This creates the same planning asymmetry as other no-gift-tax states: lifetime transfers reduce the Maryland estate tax base without triggering a state-level gift tax. Gifts that occur more than two years before death are generally excluded from the Maryland estate tax calculation, making early, consistent lifetime gifting an effective long-term strategy for reducing Maryland estate tax exposure.

The Maryland Inheritance Tax

Maryland's inheritance tax (Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 7-201 et seq.) is imposed on the privilege of receiving assets from a Maryland decedent, at a rate of 10%. Critically, the Maryland inheritance tax applies to assets received by collateral relatives — siblings, nieces, nephews, cousins, and non-family beneficiaries. Children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, and spouses are exempt from Maryland inheritance tax under Md. Code Ann., Tax-Gen. § 7-203. So for Bitcoin families passing wealth to children, the inheritance tax is not a concern — but for families with more complex family structures, or those including non-lineal heirs in their succession plans, the 10% inheritance tax applies to the full value of assets received.

The interaction of the estate tax and inheritance tax creates a trap for the unwary: a Bitcoin family with siblings or non-lineal beneficiaries designated in their estate plan may face both the Maryland estate tax (on the estate level) and the Maryland inheritance tax (on the recipient level) for the same assets. This double-layer must be addressed explicitly in every Maryland Bitcoin estate plan.

Maryland is the only state with both an estate tax and an inheritance tax. For Bitcoin families with non-lineal heirs or estates above $5 million, failing to plan means two separate layers of state transfer tax at death — in addition to the federal obligation.

Maryland Trust Code and Bitcoin Trust Structures

Maryland's trust law is codified at Md. Code Ann., Est. & Trusts § 14.5-101 et seq. (the Maryland Trust Act, effective 2015, based on the Uniform Trust Code). The Maryland Trust Act provides modern statutory authority for trust formation, directed trust arrangements, trust modification, and decanting. Key provisions for Bitcoin families include:

  • Directed trusts: The Maryland Trust Act permits trust instruments to separate investment direction authority from administrative trustee functions. For Bitcoin trusts, this allows the trust instrument to designate a Bitcoin-specialist or family member as investment director — controlling private key management, hardware wallet selection, and multi-signature architecture — while a corporate trustee handles administrative, distribution, and compliance functions. Neither role bears liability for the actions of the other when properly structured.
  • Trust protectors: Maryland permits trust protectors with powers enumerated in the trust instrument, including the power to modify trust terms to adapt to changed tax law or family circumstances, remove and replace trustees, and direct investment or custody decisions. Trust protectors are essential for Bitcoin trusts where custody technology and legal norms evolve faster than any static trust instrument can anticipate.
  • Decanting: Md. Code Ann., Est. & Trusts § 14.5-816.1 authorizes trustees with discretionary distribution authority to decant trust assets into a new trust with updated terms. Bitcoin trusts without adequate digital asset succession provisions can be modernized through decanting without court approval in appropriate circumstances.
  • Perpetual trusts: Maryland permits trusts to continue for up to 100 years (with certain exceptions for charitable trusts). While not truly perpetual, a 100-year Maryland dynasty trust can hold Bitcoin across three or four generations without estate tax at each generational transfer, using the federal GST exemption to protect against generation-skipping transfer tax.

Strategies for Reducing Maryland Estate Tax

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs): A GRAT is one of the most effective estate tax reduction strategies for Maryland Bitcoin families. GRAT funding produces zero taxable gift (the actuarial value of the retained annuity equals the contribution), meaning no Maryland gift tax is triggered. Appreciation above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate passes to heirs free of both Maryland estate tax and federal estate tax. Rolling GRAT programs can systematically transfer Bitcoin appreciation over time.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs): A SLAT removes assets from the Maryland estate tax base while preserving indirect spousal access through trust distributions. Maryland's lack of a gift tax means SLAT funding is subject only to federal gift tax analysis — no Maryland state gift tax applies. The SLAT corpus and all future appreciation are permanently outside the Maryland estate tax base after funding.

Installment Sales to IDGTs: A sale of Bitcoin to an intentionally defective grantor trust for a promissory note removes the Bitcoin and all future appreciation from the Maryland estate tax base at the time of sale, with no Maryland gift tax triggered. The grantor receives interest payments on the note — taxable as Maryland income but not subject to Maryland estate or inheritance tax. At death, only the outstanding note balance is included in the Maryland estate, not the full value of the Bitcoin held in the trust.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs): For Maryland Bitcoin holders with appreciated low-basis positions, a CRT allows transfer of Bitcoin, deferral of capital gains, and receipt of an income stream — while eliminating both Maryland estate tax and Maryland inheritance tax on the CRT remainder (which passes to charity and is deductible for both taxes). Maryland allows charitable deductions in computing the Maryland estate tax.

Strategies for Reducing Maryland Inheritance Tax

For Bitcoin families whose estate plans include siblings, nieces, nephews, or other collateral relatives, the Maryland inheritance tax demands specific attention:

Redirect beneficiary designations to exempt classes: Maryland inheritance tax does not apply to transfers to children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, and spouses. Restructuring a succession plan to pass assets primarily through lineal descendants — and directing assets to collateral relatives through lifetime gifts instead of at-death transfers — can significantly reduce inheritance tax exposure.

Use irrevocable trusts to change the character of the transfer: Assets held in a properly structured trust for the benefit of collateral relatives, funded during the grantor's lifetime, may avoid Maryland inheritance tax on those assets at death. The tax analysis depends on the specific trust structure and timing, and requires advice from Maryland estate counsel.

Life insurance in an ILIT: Life insurance proceeds paid to an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) pass outside the Maryland estate and inheritance tax bases. The ILIT can be structured to provide liquidity to pay Maryland transfer taxes without triggering additional tax on the insurance proceeds themselves.

Digital Asset Succession in Maryland

Maryland enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) at Md. Code Ann., Est. & Trusts § 17-101 et seq. Maryland RUFADAA provides fiduciaries — executors, trustees, and attorneys-in-fact — with legal authority to access digital assets, including Bitcoin, after death or incapacity. The standard RUFADAA priority hierarchy applies: online tool designations first, governing instrument authority second, statutory defaults last.

Maryland RUFADAA provides the legal framework but not the technical access. Maryland Bitcoin estate plans must combine explicit RUFADAA authority in all governing documents with a separate, secured technical succession protocol specifying the custody architecture and recovery procedures for Bitcoin private keys, hardware wallets, and multi-signature credentials. This protocol must be maintained separately from the estate plan documents — both for security reasons and because estate documents may become public through probate proceedings. Practical Priorities for Maryland Bitcoin Families

  1. Analyze both Maryland taxes simultaneously: the estate tax for estates above $5 million, and the inheritance tax for any assets passing to siblings or non-lineal beneficiaries. Both may apply, and both must be addressed in the plan.
  2. Implement a lifetime gifting program immediately — Maryland has no gift tax, so every dollar of Bitcoin transferred during life permanently reduces the Maryland estate tax base. Annual exclusion gifts, GRAT transfers, and installment sales are the primary tools.
  3. Establish a Maryland LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle, with an operating agreement governing custody, succession, and governance — and gift or sell minority LLC interests to heirs or trusts at discounted valuations.
  4. Review beneficiary designations to minimize inheritance tax exposure — assets passing to children and lineal descendants are exempt; assets passing to collateral relatives are taxed at 10%. Restructure where possible.
  5. Document RUFADAA authority explicitly in all governing instruments, and maintain a separate, secured technical succession protocol for Bitcoin private key and hardware wallet access, updated whenever the custody arrangement changes.
