Bitcoin QTIP Trust: Protecting a Surviving Spouse While Preserving Family Wealth

Hal Turner  ·  The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin QTIP Trust

Estate planning for married Bitcoin holders involves a tension that does not exist in the same way for most other asset classes: Bitcoin tends to be held with a conviction that transcends normal portfolio management. It is held for decades, across generations, with a long-term thesis that dwarfs ordinary investment time horizons. When one spouse dies, the question of who controls that Bitcoin — and where it ultimately goes — becomes acutely important.

The Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust, known as a QTIP, is one of the most powerful tools for resolving this tension. It allows a decedent's estate to claim the unlimited marital deduction — deferring estate tax entirely at the first spouse's death — while ensuring that the Bitcoin ultimately passes to the decedent's chosen remainder beneficiaries, not to whoever the surviving spouse may choose. For blended families, second marriages, or any family where the path of inheritance matters as much as the deferral of tax, the QTIP is an essential structure to understand.

What Is a QTIP Trust?

Definition

A Qualified Terminable Interest Property (QTIP) trust is a trust that qualifies for the federal estate tax marital deduction under IRC § 2056(b)(7). The trust provides the surviving spouse with a qualifying income interest for life — meaning all trust income must be distributed to the surviving spouse at least annually. In return, the decedent's executor makes a QTIP election on the estate tax return, which qualifies the trust assets for the unlimited marital deduction. The assets are included in the surviving spouse's gross estate at their death, at which point estate tax is assessed. The remainder passes to the beneficiaries chosen by the decedent — not the surviving spouse.

The QTIP structure solves a fundamental problem with outright marital bequests: leaving Bitcoin directly to a surviving spouse gives that spouse complete control over where the Bitcoin ultimately goes. If your spouse remarries, your Bitcoin could pass to a new partner's children rather than your own. In a blended family, the path of inheritance is rarely the same as the path of affection. The QTIP ensures that your Bitcoin reaches your intended beneficiaries — typically your children — regardless of what the surviving spouse chooses to do with their estate.

Why Bitcoin Holders Should Understand the QTIP

The unlimited marital deduction is one of the most powerful provisions in the federal estate tax code. It allows an unlimited amount of assets to pass from one spouse to another free of estate tax at the first spouse's death. For large Bitcoin positions — where the estate tax exposure at death could be tens of millions of dollars — the ability to defer estate tax entirely until the surviving spouse's death has enormous value. It preserves the full Bitcoin position for the surviving spouse's use, without a forced liquidation event to pay estate tax.

But the marital deduction in its simplest form — an outright bequest — comes with a significant trade-off: the surviving spouse takes full ownership and full control. They can spend it, give it away, invest it differently, or leave it to whoever they choose at their death. For Bitcoin holders who have built a significant position with a specific long-term thesis and specific intended beneficiaries, this loss of control is unacceptable.

The QTIP trust threads this needle. It qualifies for the unlimited marital deduction — so no estate tax is due at the first spouse's death — while locking in the decedent's choice of remainder beneficiaries. The surviving spouse receives the economic benefit of the trust (income and potentially principal distributions) for life, but they cannot change where the remainder goes at their death. That decision was made by the decedent, irrevocably, in the trust document.

The QTIP does not eliminate estate tax — it defers it to the surviving spouse's death. What it does do is preserve your Bitcoin for your children, not your spouse's next chapter.

How Bitcoin Is Held Inside a QTIP Trust

The mechanics of holding Bitcoin inside a QTIP trust require careful attention, because Bitcoin does not behave like the traditional trust assets the QTIP rules were designed for.

The Income Requirement

The QTIP rules require that all income of the trust be distributed to the surviving spouse at least annually. For traditional trust assets — bonds, dividend-paying stocks, real estate — "income" means interest, dividends, and rents. Bitcoin, held as a non-yielding asset, generates no income of this type. A trust funded entirely with Bitcoin would technically produce zero distributable income under traditional trust accounting rules, which creates a problem: if there is no income to distribute, does the trust still qualify for the QTIP election?

The answer lies in a feature of trust law that has become increasingly important for non-income-producing assets: the unitrust conversion. Under the Uniform Principal and Income Act (UPIA) and similar state statutes, a trustee may elect to treat a fixed percentage of the trust's fair market value — typically between 3% and 5% annually — as "income" for distribution purposes, regardless of whether the trust actually generates cash income. This is called a total return unitrust or UNITRUST conversion.

Applied to a Bitcoin QTIP, the unitrust conversion means the trustee annually distributes a set percentage of the trust's Bitcoin value to the surviving spouse as "income." If the trust holds $10 million in Bitcoin and the unitrust percentage is 4%, the trustee distributes $400,000 to the surviving spouse that year — either in cash (if the trustee sells enough Bitcoin to fund the distribution) or in Bitcoin itself if the trust document and applicable law permit in-kind distributions.

Important drafting note: Not all states have adopted unitrust conversion provisions, and the rules vary significantly among those that have. The trust document should explicitly address the trustee's authority to make a unitrust election and the applicable percentage. An estate planning attorney with experience in digital asset trusts should draft or review the QTIP provisions to ensure the income requirement is satisfied under applicable state law.

Trustee Custody of Bitcoin

The trustee of a QTIP trust is a fiduciary with duties to both the surviving spouse (as income beneficiary) and the remainder beneficiaries. For Bitcoin, this creates a specific tension: the trustee must balance the surviving spouse's interest in receiving distributions against the remainder beneficiaries' interest in trust appreciation. Selling Bitcoin to fund distributions reduces the position available for the remaindermen.

The trust document should address:

  • Custody architecture. The trustee holds the Bitcoin in custody — hardware wallet or institutional custodian — in their fiduciary capacity. Clear chain of custody documentation is essential.
  • Distribution funding mechanism. The trust should specify whether distributions may be made in-kind (Bitcoin) or must be in cash, and if in cash, the protocol for selling Bitcoin to fund distributions.
  • Investment authority. Can the trustee hold 100% of the trust in Bitcoin? Or does a prudent investor standard require diversification? The trust should address this explicitly, potentially specifying that Bitcoin is an acceptable concentrated holding consistent with the trust's investment policy.
  • Trustee succession. Custody of Bitcoin private keys must transfer cleanly when trustees change. A Letter of Instruction specifying key management, hardware wallet location, and seed phrase protocols is essential.

The Estate Tax at the Surviving Spouse's Death

The most important thing to understand about a QTIP trust is that it defers estate tax — it does not eliminate it. QTIP trust assets are included in the surviving spouse's gross estate at their death, at their then-current fair market value. Estate tax is assessed at that point, and the remainder passes to the designated beneficiaries after tax.

For Bitcoin, this deferral dynamic has significant implications. If a QTIP trust is funded with $5 million in Bitcoin at the first spouse's death, and Bitcoin appreciates to $50 million by the time the surviving spouse dies, the surviving spouse's estate includes $50 million from the QTIP. The estate tax on that appreciation — potentially $17 million at a 40% rate, above applicable exemptions — must be paid before the Bitcoin passes to the children.

This is not an argument against the QTIP — it is an argument for planning ahead. Several strategies can mitigate the estate tax at the surviving spouse's death:

  • Combining the QTIP with a dynasty trust for the remainder. The QTIP document can provide that, at the surviving spouse's death, the remainder passes into a dynasty trust for the benefit of children and grandchildren — with GST tax exemption allocated at that point. This defers generational taxes beyond the surviving spouse's death.
  • Life insurance in an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT). A survivorship life insurance policy held in an ILIT can provide liquidity to pay estate taxes at the surviving spouse's death without requiring Bitcoin to be sold.
  • Annual exclusion gifting by the surviving spouse. During the surviving spouse's lifetime, they can gift assets using the annual exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2024) to reduce their estate and the eventual estate tax burden — though they cannot make gifts from QTIP trust assets without specific authority.
  • Charitable remainder provisions. The QTIP can include provisions directing a charitable bequest from the trust remainder at the surviving spouse's death, creating an estate tax deduction at that time.

QTIP and Portability: A Critical Election Trade-Off

The interaction between the QTIP election and portability — the ability to carry a deceased spouse's unused estate tax exemption to the surviving spouse — is one of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of marital trust planning for Bitcoin holders.

Under current law, if a spouse dies with an unused estate tax exemption (because their estate is below the applicable exemption amount), the surviving spouse can "port" that unused exemption — adding it to their own exemption at their death. This is called the Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion (DSUE). For example, if the first spouse dies with a $14 million exemption and a $10 million estate, the $4 million in unused exemption can port to the surviving spouse, who then has their own $14 million exemption plus the $4 million DSUE.

The problem: making a QTIP election on assets that pass through the QTIP trust generally exhausts the marital deduction on those assets, and the interaction between QTIP elections and DSUE is complex. Specifically, a QTIP election means the first spouse's exemption is not applied to the QTIP assets (because the marital deduction is claimed instead), which may reduce the DSUE available. The surviving spouse does not "inherit" the unused exemption from the QTIP-elected assets in the same way they would from a bypass trust structure.

The strategic choice: An estate attorney must analyze whether electing QTIP treatment for Bitcoin assets is more valuable than using the first spouse's exemption to shield those assets in a bypass (credit shelter) trust — which would pass estate-tax-free at the surviving spouse's death and not be included in the surviving spouse's estate. For large Bitcoin positions expected to appreciate substantially, the bypass trust may ultimately be more tax-efficient than the QTIP deferral, depending on the family's specific facts. This is a fact-specific analysis that requires professional advice.

When a QTIP Is the Right Structure for Bitcoin

The QTIP is not the right structure for every Bitcoin holder. It involves trade-offs — estate tax deferral rather than elimination, inclusion in the surviving spouse's estate, and the income requirement complexity for a non-yielding asset. But it is the optimal structure in several specific circumstances:

Blended Families

The QTIP's most compelling use case is the blended family. If you have children from a prior marriage and your current spouse has no legal obligation to leave your Bitcoin to those children, the QTIP is often the only tool that simultaneously provides for your current spouse and ensures your Bitcoin ultimately reaches your children. Without a QTIP, an outright bequest to the surviving spouse — however well-intentioned — leaves your children dependent on goodwill that may not survive grief, remarriage, or estate planning by a new adviser.

Large Bitcoin Estates Where Deferral Has Value

For very large Bitcoin estates — those well above the federal exemption — the ability to defer estate tax until the surviving spouse's death can preserve enormous liquidity. If estate tax were due at the first spouse's death, it might require selling a significant portion of the Bitcoin position at that time. Deferring that tax event for decades, during which the Bitcoin continues to compound, may preserve far more wealth for the family overall — even accounting for the estate tax that will eventually be due.

Asset Protection for the Surviving Spouse

A QTIP trust provides the surviving spouse with economic access to the trust assets while protecting those assets from the surviving spouse's creditors (in most structures). The surviving spouse cannot be compelled to distribute trust principal to satisfy their personal debts, because they do not own the trust assets — they are merely the income beneficiary. For Bitcoin held by families with business or personal liability exposure, this protection layer has real value.

Control Over the Disposition of Bitcoin After the Surviving Spouse's Death

If you want certainty — not just an expression of wishes — about where your Bitcoin ultimately goes, the QTIP provides it. An outright bequest relies on the surviving spouse's future estate plan. A QTIP trust lock in the remainder disposition now, irrevocably. Your children receive the Bitcoin; the surviving spouse cannot redirect it.

Implementing a Bitcoin QTIP: What the Process Looks Like

A QTIP trust for Bitcoin is established as part of the overall estate plan — typically inside a revocable living trust as a "testamentary" trust that comes into existence at the first spouse's death. The key steps:

  1. Estate plan drafting. Your estate planning attorney drafts the revocable living trust with a QTIP sub-trust that activates at your death. The QTIP provisions specify the income distribution requirement (addressing the unitrust election for Bitcoin), trustee authority over Bitcoin custody, distribution standards for principal, and remainder beneficiary designations.
  2. Executor QTIP election. At the first spouse's death, the executor files an estate tax return (Form 706) and makes the QTIP election on the assets flowing into the QTIP trust. This qualifies those assets for the marital deduction. The election is irrevocable once made.
  3. Trust funding. The Bitcoin transfers to the QTIP trust. The trustee takes custody — hardware wallet, institutional custodian, or multisig structure — according to the trust's custody provisions.
  4. Annual income distributions. The trustee makes annual unitrust distributions to the surviving spouse, funded by selling Bitcoin as needed or by in-kind Bitcoin distributions if the document and applicable law permit.
  5. Administration through the surviving spouse's lifetime. The trustee administers the trust, maintains custody, and preserves the Bitcoin for the benefit of remainder beneficiaries.
  6. Distribution at the surviving spouse's death. The surviving spouse's estate includes the QTIP trust assets, estate tax is assessed, and the remainder passes to the designated beneficiaries (your children or a dynasty trust for their benefit).

Coordinating the QTIP with Your Overall Bitcoin Estate Plan

The QTIP is rarely the only structure in a comprehensive Bitcoin estate plan. It is most powerful when coordinated with other tools:

  • A bypass (credit shelter) trust funded with assets up to the first spouse's exemption, growing estate-tax-free during the surviving spouse's lifetime, while the QTIP trust holds the balance with the marital deduction.
  • A dynasty trust as the remainder beneficiary of the QTIP — so that when the surviving spouse dies, the Bitcoin passes into a perpetual structure that avoids estate tax at every subsequent generation.
  • A SLAT funded during the couple's joint lifetimes with the current elevated gift tax exemption, reducing the overall estate before death while the QTIP plan handles the residual at the first death.
  • Life insurance in an ILIT to provide liquidity for estate taxes at the surviving spouse's death, so Bitcoin does not need to be sold to fund the tax obligation.

The right combination of these structures depends on the size of the Bitcoin position, the family composition, the applicable exemptions, and the couple's priorities for access, control, and generational transfer. There is no universal answer — only a fact-specific analysis that requires qualified professional guidance.
