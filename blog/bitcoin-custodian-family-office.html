Bitcoin Custodians for Family Offices: A Rigorous Comparison for 2026

March 10, 2026  ·  16 min read  ·  The Bitcoin Family Office

Most family office Bitcoin content focuses on allocation: how much to hold, when to buy, what the macro case is. This guide ignores all of that. Allocation is the easy decision. Custody is the one that actually determines whether your family keeps the Bitcoin.

Custody decisions at the family office level are irreversible in both directions. A poorly designed custody arrangement can result in the total, permanent, unrecoverable loss of your position — through hack, operational failure, or the death of a key holder. An overly restrictive institutional arrangement can create regulatory friction, counterparty risk, and fees that compound against you for decades. Getting custody right requires understanding the full landscape of options, the regulatory context that constrains some choices, and the specific tradeoffs of each solution.

This guide gives you all of it — in the depth that a serious family office decision requires.

Why Custody Is the Family Office's Most Important Bitcoin Decision

Traditional family office assets — equities, bonds, real estate, private equity — have professional custody infrastructure built around them. The assets can't disappear. Transfers require legal documentation. If a custodian fails, SIPC or FDIC provides some backstop. Mistakes are costly but rarely catastrophic and usually reversible.

Bitcoin is structurally different. It is a bearer instrument: whoever controls the private keys controls the Bitcoin. There is no issuer, no registry, no recourse mechanism, no "forgot my password" recovery. A $10M Bitcoin position lost to a forgotten seed phrase, a house fire, or a compromised key is gone — permanently, with no legal remedy.

"Custody is not a back-office function for Bitcoin. It is the existential question. Everything else — allocation, tax, estate planning — is secondary to the question of whether you will still have the Bitcoin when you need it."

The other reason custody takes priority: it directly constrains your estate planning options. The legal structure you use to hold Bitcoin (irrevocable trust, LLC, FLP) must be designed around your custody arrangement. A trust where the trustee "holds Bitcoin" but has no operational custody plan is legally coherent but practically useless. The custody architecture must come first; the legal wrapper is built around it.

The Custodian Landscape: Three Distinct Categories

Bitcoin custodians fall into three meaningfully different categories, each with distinct security models, regulatory profiles, and use cases.

Self-Custody (Multisig)

The family holds its own private keys — typically in a multisig configuration (2-of-3 or 3-of-5) across geographically distributed hardware wallets. No third party is involved in the custody arrangement. Maximum sovereignty; maximum operational responsibility.

Self-custody via multisig is a legitimate and often superior option for single-family offices that are not registered as investment advisors and are managing only family assets. It is not appropriate for multi-family offices, registered investment advisors managing client assets, or families who lack the operational discipline to maintain a rigorous security protocol. See our analysis of multisig for family offices for implementation specifics.

Qualified Custodians

Regulated financial institutions — federally chartered banks, state-chartered trust companies, broker-dealers — that meet SEC standards for holding client assets. For Bitcoin, the leading qualified custodians are Anchorage Digital (federally chartered bank), BitGo Trust (state-chartered trust company), Fidelity Digital Assets (subsidiary of Fidelity Investments), and Coinbase Prime (state-chartered). These institutions can serve family offices with registered investment advisor relationships and provide the regulatory compliance infrastructure that self-custody cannot.

Hybrid Solutions (Collaborative Multisig)

Solutions like Unchained Capital and Casa occupy a middle ground: the family holds keys, the provider holds one key in a multisig arrangement, but the provider cannot spend funds without the family's participation. The family retains key sovereignty while gaining operational support, inheritance planning, and a recovery mechanism. These are not qualified custodians under most regulatory frameworks, but they provide significantly more security and operational support than pure self-custody.

The Regulatory Context: What "Qualified Custodian" Means for Family Offices

The qualified custodian requirement matters for family offices in specific regulatory contexts. Understanding when it applies — and when it doesn't — is critical for choosing the right custody solution.

SEC Investment Adviser Custody Rules

Under SEC regulations, registered investment advisers (RIAs) who have custody of client assets must generally maintain those assets with a qualified custodian. For single-family offices that qualify for the family office exemption from RIA registration under Dodd-Frank, this requirement does not apply — you can self-custody your own family's Bitcoin without a qualified custodian. For multi-family offices or RIAs serving external clients, the qualified custodian requirement is real and consequential.

What counts as a qualified custodian for Bitcoin? Under SEC guidance, the analysis focuses on whether the institution is a bank, broker-dealer, futures commission merchant, or foreign equivalent. Federally chartered banks (Anchorage Digital) and regulated trust companies (BitGo Trust, Fidelity Digital Assets) generally meet the standard. Standard cryptocurrency exchanges typically do not.

State Law and Trust Considerations

State-chartered trusts have their own custody requirements, which vary by state. If your family's Bitcoin is held in a trust, the trustee has fiduciary duties that may implicitly require the use of a regulated custodian — particularly in more conservative jurisdictions. Some states have enacted specific digital asset custody laws (Wyoming's DAST framework is the most developed). If your trust is domiciled in Wyoming, your trustee has clearer authority to use digital asset custody arrangements that might be more legally ambiguous elsewhere.

ERISA Considerations

If your family office manages assets subject to ERISA (pension plans, 401(k)s with Bitcoin exposure), the qualified custodian requirement is particularly strict. ERISA plan assets must be held in trust or by a qualified custodian. Current DOL guidance creates significant friction for Bitcoin in ERISA plans, and this remains an evolving regulatory area. Most family offices choose to keep Bitcoin outside of ERISA-governed accounts for this reason.

Comparison Framework: How to Evaluate a Bitcoin Custodian

Before the deep dives, here is the evaluation framework we use when assessing custodian options for family offices:

Key Evaluation Criteria

  • Security Model: Cold storage percentage, key management architecture, physical security, employee access controls, HSM usage, SOC 2 certification
  • Insurance: Coverage limits, what triggers coverage, named insurer, whether coverage applies to your specific arrangement
  • Key Recovery: What happens if the custodian fails? Can you recover Bitcoin independently? Is there a key escrow arrangement?
  • Reporting: Tax reporting, cost basis tracking, transaction history export, CPA-compatible formats
  • Pricing: Basis points on AUM, transaction fees, minimum fees, onboarding costs
  • Minimum AUM: Exclusivity thresholds and what they imply about service quality
  • Regulatory Standing: Qualified custodian status, state licensing, federal charter, audit history
  • Trust/LLC Compatibility: Can the custodian custody Bitcoin held by a trust, LLC, or FLP? What documentation do they require?

Deep Dives: The Leading Bitcoin Custodians for Family Offices

Collaborative Multisig · No Minimum

Unchained Capital

Unchained occupies a unique position in the custody landscape: it is neither a self-custody solution nor a traditional custodian. In Unchained's collaborative custody model, the family holds two keys in a 2-of-3 multisig arrangement, and Unchained holds one key. Unchained cannot spend funds without your participation. You can spend funds — in an emergency — without Unchained.

This architecture preserves the core Bitcoin sovereignty principle while providing meaningful operational support: key recovery assistance, inheritance planning through their vault product, transaction signing support, and a recovery mechanism if a family key is lost. For family offices that want to retain key sovereignty but need operational infrastructure, Unchained is frequently the best fit.

  • Security model: 2-of-3 multisig; client holds 2 keys, Unchained holds 1; client can always spend independently
  • Insurance: Crime coverage through a Lloyd's-backed policy; coverage details available upon request
  • Minimum AUM: No minimum for standard vaults; institutional pricing available at scale
  • Regulatory standing: Not a qualified custodian; appropriate for family offices not subject to RIA custody rules
  • Trust/LLC compatibility: Yes — Unchained works with trusts, LLCs, and other entities; has estate-planning-focused products specifically designed for inheritance
  • Best for: Self-sovereign families who want operational support, key recovery, and inheritance infrastructure without giving up key control
Concierge Multisig · Individual & Family Focus

Casa

Casa is the premium concierge multisig product for individuals and families with significant Bitcoin holdings. Their flagship offering is a 3-of-5 multisig with keys distributed between the client (multiple devices), Casa (one key, cannot spend independently), and optional mobile keys. The concierge service includes dedicated key recovery support, annual key health checks, and a protocol for inheritance that doesn't require heirs to be Bitcoin-technical.

Casa's differentiation is on service: white-glove onboarding, a dedicated security advisor, and a recovery process designed to work even when family members have minimal Bitcoin knowledge. This matters for estate planning — the custodian your heirs interact with after you're gone needs to be able to help them access funds without technical expertise.

  • Security model: 3-of-5 multisig for Platinum tier; client holds multiple keys, Casa holds one; emergency key recovery available
  • Insurance: Crime and theft coverage; coverage limits available upon inquiry
  • Minimum AUM: No minimum; subscription-based pricing (annual fee per vault tier)
  • Regulatory standing: Not a qualified custodian; appropriate for individual and single-family office use
  • Trust/LLC compatibility: Yes, with proper documentation
  • Best for: Families with significant holdings ($500K–$10M+) who prioritize concierge service, inheritance planning, and key sovereignty over institutional regulatory compliance
Institutional Custodian · SOC 2 · Insurance up to $700M

BitGo

BitGo is the dominant institutional Bitcoin custodian by AUM, with deep roots in the institutional market that pre-date most competitors. BitGo Trust Company is a South Dakota-chartered trust company — a qualified custodian for purposes of most SEC and state custody rules. Their security infrastructure is the most battle-tested in the industry: multi-approval workflows, HSM-based key management, SOC 2 Type II certification, and insurance coverage of up to $700M through a consortium of insurers including Lloyd's.

BitGo's reporting infrastructure is institutional grade: sub-accounts by entity, transaction-level cost basis tracking, and integration with major accounting systems. For family offices with multiple legal entities, this operational capability matters. The tradeoff is counterparty concentration: your Bitcoin is held by BitGo. Their failure — which has not occurred but remains theoretically possible — would trigger an insurance and legal recovery process rather than direct key access.

  • Security model: Multi-party approval, HSM-based key storage, 98%+ cold storage, SOC 2 Type II audited
  • Insurance: Up to $700M through Lloyd's consortium and others; crime and theft coverage
  • Minimum AUM: Typically $1M+; institutional pricing at $10M+ AUM
  • Regulatory standing: South Dakota-chartered trust company; qualified custodian for most purposes
  • Trust/LLC compatibility: Yes; full entity account structure available
  • Best for: Family offices requiring a qualified custodian for regulatory compliance, handling Bitcoin in irrevocable trusts with institutional trustees, or requiring insurance coverage above $10M
Federally Chartered Bank · Highest Regulatory Bar

Anchorage Digital

Anchorage Digital holds the only federal charter granted to a crypto-native institution: it is a national bank regulated by the OCC (Office of the Comptroller of the Currency). This is the highest regulatory bar in the US crypto custody landscape — a standard that no other crypto-native custodian has achieved. The OCC charter means Anchorage is subject to the same regulatory scrutiny as major national banks, with all the compliance infrastructure that implies.

For family offices with institutional investors, endowments, or pension fund relationships where the custodian must meet the strictest regulatory standard, Anchorage is frequently the only defensible choice. Their minimum AUM is high — typically $10M+ — and their onboarding process reflects the regulatory rigor of a federally chartered institution. Service is institutional: not concierge, but compliant in every dimension that matters at that level.

  • Security model: Biometric hardware security modules, multi-party authorization, institutionally-grade cold storage, OCC-supervised security framework
  • Insurance: Crime and professional liability coverage; details under NDA with institutional clients
  • Minimum AUM: ~$10M+; institutional onboarding process
  • Regulatory standing: OCC-chartered national bank — highest regulatory bar in US crypto custody
  • Trust/LLC compatibility: Yes; handles complex entity structures with regulatory documentation
  • Best for: Family offices where regulatory compliance is paramount — endowments, family foundations, multi-family offices with institutional LP relationships, or situations where OCC-level oversight is required or expected
Traditional Brokerage Integration · Institutional Bridge

Fidelity Digital Assets

Fidelity Digital Assets is the institutional Bitcoin custody and trading subsidiary of Fidelity Investments, and it occupies a distinct niche: it is the bridge for family offices that have existing Fidelity institutional relationships and want Bitcoin exposure without migrating to a crypto-native custodian. The custody infrastructure is enterprise-grade, the regulatory standing is unimpeachable (Fidelity is one of the most heavily regulated financial institutions in the US), and the reporting integration with Fidelity's broader wealth management platform is seamless.

The tradeoff is that Fidelity Digital Assets is Fidelity — a traditional financial institution that has built Bitcoin custody to serve existing institutional clients, not Bitcoin-native families who prioritize sovereignty. If you already run your family's portfolio on Fidelity's institutional platform and want to add a Bitcoin allocation without changing custodians, Fidelity Digital Assets is the natural fit. If you are a Bitcoin-native family building a custody architecture from first principles, it is rarely the best answer.

  • Security model: Cold storage infrastructure, institutional key management, enterprise-grade operational controls; specific architecture details proprietary
  • Insurance: Covered under Fidelity's institutional insurance programs; specific crypto coverage details available to institutional clients
  • Minimum AUM: Typically $1M+ for Digital Assets; relationship minimums may apply if accessing through broader Fidelity institutional
  • Regulatory standing: State-chartered trust company (New Hampshire), Fidelity institutional regulatory umbrella; qualified custodian
  • Trust/LLC compatibility: Yes; full entity account structure consistent with Fidelity institutional capabilities
  • Best for: Family offices with existing Fidelity institutional relationships wanting integrated Bitcoin custody; family office CFOs who need Bitcoin on the same platform as the rest of the portfolio

Self-Custody as a Legitimate Option: When Multisig Outperforms Institutional Custody

The custody industry's standard narrative is that institutional custodians provide superior security. This is not universally true, and for many family offices, a well-designed self-custody multisig arrangement is more secure and more appropriate than any institutional alternative.

The case for self-custody multisig at the family office level:

  • No counterparty risk. An institutional custodian's failure, insolvency, regulatory seizure, or operational failure can impair your access to Bitcoin even if the custodian is "insured." In self-custody multisig, you are the only counterparty that matters.
  • No minimum AUM friction. Institutional custodians require significant minimums and charge ongoing fees. A properly structured multisig has none of these frictions.
  • Key sovereignty aligns with Bitcoin's value proposition. Bitcoin was designed to be held this way. Using a custodian introduces the same counterparty risk Bitcoin was designed to eliminate.
  • Security scales with your investment. A $5M multisig arrangement with properly distributed keys, geographically separate storage, and documented operational procedures can be objectively more secure than many institutional custodians — which face their own operational risks, including insider theft.

The case for institutional custody:

  • Regulatory compliance for RIAs and multi-family offices
  • Insurance coverage at scale ($100M+)
  • Professional operations team — no family member needs to manage key security
  • Trust and estate integration with institutional documentation
  • Traditional financial infrastructure compatibility

The honest answer: if your family office is structured as a single-family office (not an RIA), if your family has the operational discipline to manage a rigorous security protocol, and if you are working with advisors who understand Bitcoin custody — self-custody multisig is frequently the right choice. If any of those conditions don't hold, an institutional custodian provides real value.

The 36-Question Due Diligence Framework

Before selecting any Bitcoin custodian, running rigorous due diligence is essential. The questions that matter are not the ones custodians put in their marketing materials — they are the operational, security, and recovery questions that reveal how the custodian actually works when something goes wrong.

The due diligence questions fall into five categories:

  1. Security architecture: What percentage of assets are in cold storage? How are keys generated? What hardware security modules are used? Who has access to keys, and what approval thresholds apply?
  2. Insurance and recovery: What is the total coverage limit? What triggers coverage? Is the policy named or blanket? What is the recovery process if the custodian becomes insolvent?
  3. Regulatory and compliance: What is the custodian's regulatory standing? When was the last external audit? What is their history with regulatory examinations? Are they subject to any pending enforcement actions?
  4. Operational resilience: What is the custodian's key recovery procedure? What happens to client assets if the custodian ceases operations? What is the SLA for transaction signing? What redundancy exists in their operational infrastructure?
  5. Family office fit: Can the custodian custody Bitcoin held in an irrevocable trust? An LLC? A family limited partnership? What documentation is required? What is the reporting output, and in what formats?

Structuring Custody Within an Irrevocable Trust or LLC

How you structure custody within a legal entity is not a question custodians typically help you answer — it sits at the intersection of legal, tax, and custody planning, and requires advisors who are competent in all three domains.

Bitcoin in an Irrevocable Trust

When Bitcoin is held in an irrevocable trust, the trustee has legal custody. The trustee's operational ability to manage the Bitcoin depends entirely on the custody arrangement. Three models work:

  • Institutional custodian as sub-custodian: The trustee opens an account with BitGo, Anchorage, or Fidelity Digital Assets in the name of the trust. The trustee authorizes transactions; the custodian holds keys. This is the most legally clean arrangement for institutional trustees.
  • Collaborative multisig with trust documentation: The trust's operating procedures specify that the trustee holds one key, an Unchained or Casa-type provider holds a second key, and a third key is held in escrow by the trust's attorney or a co-trustee. The trust document must explicitly authorize this arrangement and name successor key holders.
  • Self-custody multisig with documented procedures: The trustee manages keys directly per procedures attached to the trust. This requires a Bitcoin-competent trustee and very careful documentation. Rarely appropriate for institutional trustees; can work for directed trustees with a sophisticated protector.

See our complete guide to irrevocable trust for Bitcoin for the legal structure details.

Bitcoin in an LLC

An LLC provides the governance layer that allows custody procedures to be formalized. The LLC operating agreement should specify: the custody arrangement (which custodian, which keys, what signing threshold), who has authority to initiate transactions on behalf of the LLC, what happens if an authorized person is incapacitated, and how the custody arrangement can be changed. For multisig arrangements, the operating agreement is the governance document for the key holders — it specifies their responsibilities, their authority, and the procedures for key recovery and succession.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a qualified custodian for Bitcoin family offices?

A qualified custodian is a regulated financial institution that meets SEC standards for holding client assets — typically a bank, broker-dealer, or trust company. For Bitcoin specifically, federally chartered trust companies like Anchorage Digital and state-chartered custodians like BitGo Trust meet the qualified custodian standard. Standard cryptocurrency exchanges do not. Family offices that are registered as investment advisers may be required to use a qualified custodian for Bitcoin holdings — the family office exemption from RIA registration removes this requirement for most single-family offices managing only family assets.

Is self-custody a legitimate option for a family office?

Yes — for single-family offices that are not registered as investment advisers and are managing only family assets. A properly structured 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multisig setup with geographically distributed keys, documented security procedures, and regular operational testing can match or exceed institutional security while eliminating counterparty risk entirely. The qualification: your family must have the operational discipline and technical competency to maintain the arrangement reliably. If those conditions hold, self-custody multisig is frequently superior to institutional custody. If your regulatory structure requires a qualified custodian, that changes the calculus entirely.

How do I structure Bitcoin custody within an irrevocable trust?

The trust document should specify the custody arrangement with operational specificity — not just "the trustee shall hold digital assets," which is legally coherent but practically useless. Options: (1) designate an institutional custodian as sub-custodian with the trustee as authorized signer; (2) use collaborative multisig with keys distributed between the trustee, a service provider, and a co-trustee or attorney, with the operating procedure attached to the trust; (3) self-custody multisig where the trust document names key holders and their succession procedures. The trust's attorney must be Bitcoin-competent to draft this correctly. Generic digital asset language will not survive a contested trustee action.

What insurance coverage should a family office look for in a Bitcoin custodian?

Look for four things: (1) coverage limits that are meaningful relative to your holding size — BitGo offers up to $700M, which covers most family office positions; (2) a named insurer on a recognized policy, not "insurance from an undisclosed carrier"; (3) clarity on triggers — some policies exclude insider theft, key mismanagement, or specific attack vectors; (4) whether coverage applies to your specific custody arrangement — some policies only cover the custodian's cold storage, not keys held by the client in a collaborative multisig arrangement. Ask for the actual policy summary, not the marketing one-pager. A custodian that won't provide this isn't a custodian you should trust with significant assets.
