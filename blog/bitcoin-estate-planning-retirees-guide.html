Bitcoin Estate Planning for Retirees: Income Sequencing, RMDs, and the Complete 2026 Drawdown Guide

How retirees with Bitcoin navigate the drawdown phase — spending the right assets in the right order while preserving BTC's estate planning advantages for heirs.

Published March 16, 2026

You spent decades building wealth. Some of it ended up in a 401(k). Some in bonds. And at some point — maybe early, maybe recently — you bought Bitcoin. Now you're retired, or close to it, and the question isn't whether Bitcoin was a good investment. The question is what to do with it while you're drawing down everything else.

This is the part most Bitcoin content ignores entirely. The influencers talk about accumulation. The maximalists talk about never selling. But you're 68 or 72 or 77, and you have a mortgage-free house, Social Security checks hitting your account, required minimum distributions forcing money out of your IRA, Medicare premiums that fluctuate based on your income two years ago, and a Bitcoin position that has appreciated enormously — and every financial decision you make with it triggers a cascade of tax consequences that can cost tens of thousands of dollars if you get the sequencing wrong.

This guide is specifically for retirees in the 65–80 age range who hold meaningful Bitcoin positions alongside traditional retirement assets. We'll walk through the drawdown sequence, the tax traps, the Medicare land mines, and the estate planning strategies that determine whether your heirs receive your Bitcoin cleanly — or lose a significant portion to avoidable taxes.

The Retiree's Bitcoin Dilemma

Conventional retirement planning says to reduce portfolio risk as you age. Shift from equities to bonds. Prioritize capital preservation over growth. Bitcoin, with its 50–80% drawdowns, would seem to be the first thing a retiree should sell.

But the conventional framework misses something critical about Bitcoin and estate planning: Bitcoin held until death receives a stepped-up cost basis. If you bought 10 BTC at $5,000 each and they're worth $100,000 each when you die, your heirs inherit them at the $100,000 basis. The $950,000 in capital gains? Gone. Never taxed. That's a tax savings of $142,500 or more at federal long-term capital gains rates alone — potentially much more when state taxes apply.

Selling Bitcoin during retirement to "reduce risk" doesn't just trigger capital gains tax today. It permanently destroys the stepped-up basis opportunity — the single most powerful estate planning benefit available to Bitcoin holders. This is the core tension: volatility risk during your lifetime versus a massive tax benefit at death.

The resolution isn't to blindly hold or blindly sell. It's to build a drawdown sequence that spends other assets first, preserves Bitcoin for the estate, and manages the tax consequences of every dollar that moves.

The "Bitcoin Last" Withdrawal Sequence

For retirees with diversified holdings, the optimal drawdown order places Bitcoin at the very end. Here's the sequence and the logic behind each layer:

Layer 1: Taxable Bonds and Cash (Spend First)

Cash and short-term bonds generate minimal capital gains when sold. They're the lowest-tax-impact source of retirement income. Spending them first preserves higher-growth assets for longer, and the tax cost of liquidation is negligible. If you're holding Treasury bills, money market funds, or savings accounts, this is your first spending source after Social Security.

Layer 2: Required Minimum Distributions from Traditional IRAs (Forced)

You don't choose this one — the IRS does. Once you reach your required beginning date for RMDs, you must withdraw a percentage of your traditional IRA balance each year. These distributions are taxed as ordinary income. You can't avoid them (without penalty), but you can manage their impact, which we'll cover in detail below.

Layer 3: Additional Traditional IRA Withdrawals (Strategic)

In years where your income is unusually low — perhaps you delayed Social Security, had medical deductions, or are in a gap year — it may make sense to pull extra from the traditional IRA beyond the RMD. This accelerates the depletion of the tax-deferred account, reducing future RMDs and their cascading effects on Medicare premiums and Social Security taxation.

Layer 4: Roth IRA

Roth withdrawals are tax-free and don't count toward provisional income or MAGI. They're the ideal bridge if you need income without triggering any of the tax thresholds discussed below. Spend Roth funds after tax-deferred accounts but before Bitcoin.

Layer 5: Bitcoin (Spend Last — or Never)

Bitcoin should be the last asset you touch. Every year it remains in your estate unrealized, you preserve the stepped-up basis for your heirs. If your other assets can fund your retirement, Bitcoin passes to the next generation tax-free on the gains. The math on this is so compelling that for many retirees, the correct Bitcoin strategy is simply: don't sell it.

This sequencing isn't theoretical. It's the difference between your heirs receiving $1.5 million in Bitcoin with zero embedded tax liability versus receiving $1.2 million after you've sold some along the way and paid capital gains tax on the proceeds you already spent.

Social Security and Bitcoin: The Provisional Income Trap

Most retirees don't realize that their Bitcoin activity directly affects how much of their Social Security benefits get taxed. The mechanism is called provisional income, and it catches people every year.

Provisional income is calculated as:

  • Adjusted gross income (AGI)
  • Plus tax-exempt interest (municipal bonds)
  • Plus 50% of your Social Security benefits

If your provisional income exceeds $32,000 (married filing jointly), up to 50% of your Social Security benefits become taxable. Above $44,000, up to 85% becomes taxable.

Bitcoin capital gains count toward provisional income. A retiree who sells $80,000 worth of appreciated Bitcoin in a single year doesn't just owe capital gains tax on the Bitcoin sale — they may also push 85% of their Social Security benefits into taxable territory. On $36,000 of annual Social Security, that's an additional $30,600 of taxable income that wouldn't exist if they hadn't sold the Bitcoin that year.

The Coordination Strategy

If you absolutely must sell Bitcoin during retirement, time the sales for years when your other income is low:

  • Before Social Security begins: If you delay claiming to age 70, the years between 65 and 70 may have lower income — ideal for harvesting some Bitcoin gains.
  • Years with large medical deductions: A major medical expense that generates an itemized deduction can offset income, creating room for Bitcoin gains without crossing provisional income thresholds.
  • Avoid stacking: Never sell a large Bitcoin position in the same year you take a large IRA distribution or receive a pension lump sum. The compounding tax effect across provisional income thresholds, capital gains brackets, and IRMAA is brutal.

Medicare IRMAA: The Hidden $12,000 Bitcoin Tax

This one blindsides retirees more than anything else. IRMAA — Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount — is a surcharge on Medicare Part B and Part D premiums that kicks in when your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) exceeds certain thresholds.

For 2026, the base threshold for married filing jointly is approximately $206,000 in MAGI. Above that, your Medicare premiums jump in tiers. At the highest tier, you can pay over $500/month more per person for Part B alone — over $12,000/year in extra premiums for a couple.

Here's the critical detail: IRMAA is calculated based on your tax return from two years prior. A large Bitcoin sale in 2026 increases your 2028 Medicare premiums. And because capital gains are included in MAGI, a single Bitcoin liquidation event can trigger two full years of elevated premiums before resetting.

Real Cost Example

A retiree couple with $180,000 in regular income sells $100,000 of Bitcoin with $80,000 in gains. Their MAGI jumps to $260,000. They cross two IRMAA tiers, adding roughly $4,800/year in extra Medicare premiums — for two years. That's $9,600 in Medicare costs on top of the capital gains tax. The effective marginal tax rate on that Bitcoin sale isn't 15% or 20%. It's north of 30% when you include the IRMAA impact and potential Social Security taxation.

IRMAA Mitigation Strategies

  • Spread sales across years: Instead of selling $200,000 of Bitcoin in one year, sell $50,000 per year over four years. Stay below the IRMAA thresholds each year.
  • Use Qualified Charitable Distributions: QCDs from an IRA (if you're 70½+) satisfy RMDs without increasing MAGI. This creates room under IRMAA thresholds for Bitcoin sales if needed.
  • Roth conversions in early retirement: Converting traditional IRA funds to Roth before age 72 reduces future RMDs, which reduces future MAGI, which preserves more room under IRMAA thresholds for the rest of your life.
  • Life-changing event exception: If your IRMAA spike was caused by a one-time event (though a voluntary Bitcoin sale generally doesn't qualify), you can file SSA-44 with Medicare. Qualifying events include retirement from work, death of a spouse, or divorce — not investment sales.

The interplay between net investment income tax, MAGI calculations, and IRMAA makes Bitcoin liquidation planning one of the most complex areas of retirement tax management. Get this wrong and the cost compounds for years.

RMD and Bitcoin IRA Coordination

If you hold Bitcoin inside a traditional IRA — whether through a self-directed IRA, a Bitcoin IRA provider, or a spot Bitcoin ETF within your retirement account — RMDs force annual distributions starting at age 73 (under current SECURE 2.0 rules).

The problem with Bitcoin in an IRA is that RMDs are calculated based on the account balance as of December 31 of the prior year. If Bitcoin surged 80% during that year, your RMD is calculated on the inflated balance — and you must distribute that amount whether Bitcoin has since dropped or not.

Strategies for Bitcoin IRA RMDs

In-kind distribution: Some self-directed IRA custodians allow you to distribute Bitcoin itself rather than selling it first. The distribution is still taxed at ordinary income rates on the fair market value at the time of distribution, but you avoid selling during a potential dip and can hold the Bitcoin in a personal wallet afterward. From that point, any future appreciation builds a new cost basis.

QCD strategy (age 70½+): If you're charitably inclined, Qualified Charitable Distributions allow you to send up to $105,000 (2026 limit, indexed for inflation) directly from your IRA to a qualified charity. This satisfies your RMD, generates no taxable income, and doesn't increase your MAGI. For retirees with Bitcoin IRAs and charitable intent, QCDs are one of the most efficient moves available.

Roth conversion in down years: If Bitcoin drops 40% in a given year, your IRA balance is depressed. This is the ideal time to convert a portion to Roth — you pay tax on the lower value, and all future appreciation grows tax-free with no RMDs. This is a long-term play that reduces your RMD burden for every remaining year of your life.

Consolidate to reduce complexity: If you hold Bitcoin across multiple IRA accounts, consider consolidating. RMDs are calculated per account type, and having Bitcoin in one clearly managed account simplifies the annual distribution math considerably.

The "Collateral, Not Sale" Approach

For retirees who need liquidity but refuse to sacrifice the stepped-up basis benefit, borrowing against Bitcoin offers an alternative to selling. The concept is simple: pledge your Bitcoin as collateral, receive a loan in dollars, and spend the loan proceeds. Because a loan isn't income, there's no capital gains tax, no MAGI increase, no IRMAA trigger, and no provisional income impact.

Institutional Bitcoin lenders like Unchained and Ledn offer credit lines secured by Bitcoin, typically at 40–50% loan-to-value ratios. A retiree with 10 BTC worth $1 million could borrow $400,000–$500,000 without selling a single satoshi.

The Benefits

  • No taxable event — preserves the stepped-up basis for heirs
  • No impact on MAGI, provisional income, or Medicare premiums
  • Interest may be deductible if proceeds are used for investment purposes
  • You retain ownership of the Bitcoin — if it appreciates further, you benefit

The Risks

This is not a risk-free strategy. If Bitcoin's price drops significantly, you face a margin call — the lender requires additional collateral or partial loan repayment. For a retiree on a fixed income, a margin call during a 50% Bitcoin crash could force a sale at the worst possible time, realizing gains in a down market and potentially triggering all the tax consequences you were trying to avoid.

Mitigation: borrow conservatively (25–30% LTV instead of 50%), maintain a cash reserve specifically for margin calls, and use this strategy only with a portion of your Bitcoin, not the entire position. Retirees should treat Bitcoin-backed loans as a supplement to their drawdown plan, not the foundation of it.

Healthcare Planning and Bitcoin Assets

Long-term care is the financial risk that keeps retirement planners up at night. A private room in a nursing facility averages over $100,000 per year nationally, and costs in major metros can exceed $150,000. Medicare covers very limited long-term care. Medicaid covers it — but only after you've spent down nearly all of your assets.

Bitcoin complicates this in two ways:

First, Bitcoin is a countable asset for Medicaid eligibility. Medicaid's asset limits are strict (typically $2,000 for an individual, with some state variations), and Bitcoin held in a personal wallet absolutely counts. The Medicaid look-back period — typically 60 months — means that transferring Bitcoin to heirs within five years of applying for Medicaid triggers a penalty period during which you're ineligible for coverage.

Second, the appreciation complicates spend-down strategies. If you need to liquidate Bitcoin to pay for care, the capital gains tax on the sale reduces the amount available for actual care costs. A retiree liquidating $200,000 in Bitcoin with a $20,000 cost basis might owe $27,000+ in federal capital gains tax, leaving only $173,000 for care — roughly 18 months of coverage instead of 24.

Planning Approaches

  • Long-term care insurance: Purchased before retirement or in early retirement, this removes the need to liquidate Bitcoin for care costs. Hybrid policies that combine life insurance with LTC benefits are increasingly popular with high-net-worth families.
  • Irrevocable trust: Bitcoin placed in an irrevocable trust more than five years before a potential Medicaid application is removed from your countable assets. The trust must be properly structured — you cannot be a beneficiary — and the transfer is a completed gift for tax purposes. This is an aggressive strategy that requires experienced legal counsel.
  • Spend other assets for care first: If long-term care becomes necessary, spend taxable accounts, then IRA funds, then Roth funds for care costs. Keep Bitcoin intact as long as possible. If the care need is temporary (rehabilitation, for example), you may be able to fund it entirely from other sources without touching Bitcoin.

Gifting Bitcoin During Retirement

The annual gift tax exclusion for 2026 is $19,000 per person ($38,000 for a married couple using gift-splitting). Retirees can gift Bitcoin up to these amounts to any number of recipients each year without filing a gift tax return or using any lifetime exemption.

Why gift appreciated Bitcoin rather than cash? Because the recipient receives your original cost basis. If you bought Bitcoin at $5,000 and it's now worth $19,000, gifting the Bitcoin transfers the $5,000 basis to the recipient. When they eventually sell, they'll owe tax on the gain — but likely at a lower tax rate than yours, especially if the recipient is a younger family member in a lower bracket.

Meanwhile, you've removed $19,000 from your taxable estate without paying any gift tax, and without triggering any capital gains tax yourself.

Considerations for Retiree Gifting

  • Basis transfer vs. stepped-up basis: There's a tension here. Gifting transfers your low basis to the recipient (they'll owe tax when they sell). If you instead hold the Bitcoin until death, they'd receive a stepped-up basis (no tax on the gains). For large positions, holding to death is almost always better mathematically. Gifting makes more sense for smaller amounts, for recipients who need the funds now, or when reducing your estate below the federal exemption threshold is the priority.
  • 529 superfunding: You can front-load five years of annual exclusion gifts into a 529 education savings plan — $95,000 per beneficiary ($190,000 per couple). Sell Bitcoin, fund the 529 with cash, and the entire amount grows tax-free for education expenses. This triggers capital gains on the sale, so time it in a low-income year.
  • Custodial wallets: Gifting Bitcoin to minor grandchildren requires a custodial arrangement — either a UTMA account or a trust. Don't send Bitcoin directly to a minor's wallet without proper legal structure.

Charitable Giving Strategies for Bitcoin Retirees

Retirees who are charitably inclined have some of the most tax-efficient options available — especially with appreciated Bitcoin.

Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

If you're 70½ or older and hold Bitcoin in an IRA, QCDs allow you to distribute up to $105,000 directly to a qualified charity. The distribution satisfies your RMD, generates zero taxable income, and doesn't increase your MAGI. For retirees managing IRMAA thresholds and provisional income, QCDs are indispensable.

Note: the QCD must go directly from the IRA custodian to the charity. If the funds touch your personal account first, the tax benefit is lost.

Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Contributions

Contributing appreciated Bitcoin directly to a DAF avoids capital gains tax entirely and generates a charitable deduction up to 30% of AGI. For a retiree with 2 BTC at a $10,000 basis and $200,000 current value, a DAF contribution eliminates $190,000 in embedded capital gains and provides a $200,000 charitable deduction — which can be carried forward for five years if it exceeds the AGI limit in year one.

The DAF acts as a charitable checking account: you get the deduction immediately but can distribute grants to specific charities over time.

Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

A CRT is a powerful tool for retirees who want income from their Bitcoin position without triggering an immediate tax hit. You transfer Bitcoin to the CRT, the trust sells it (no capital gains to the trust), and the trust pays you an annual income stream for life or a term of years. At the end of the trust term, the remainder goes to charity.

Benefits: no upfront capital gains tax, an immediate partial charitable deduction, a steady income stream, and the Bitcoin is removed from your taxable estate. The income you receive from the CRT is taxable, but it's spread over many years rather than concentrated in a single sale year — dramatically reducing the IRMAA and provisional income impact.

The Clean Handoff: What to Do Now

All of the strategies above are meaningless if your heirs can't actually access your Bitcoin when you're gone. The clean handoff is the most important operational task for any retiree holding Bitcoin, and it needs to be addressed now — not later.

Letter of Instructions

Create a detailed letter of instructions — separate from your will — that tells your executor and heirs exactly where your Bitcoin is, how to access it, and who to contact for help. This letter should include:

  • Which wallets hold Bitcoin (hardware wallets, exchange accounts, IRA custodians)
  • Where seed phrases and private keys are stored (not in the letter itself — but directions to find them)
  • Names and contact information for your Bitcoin-knowledgeable advisors
  • Any multi-signature arrangements and the co-signers
  • Exchange account credentials and 2FA recovery codes (stored separately and securely)

Update this letter annually. Bitcoin custody arrangements change. Exchanges close. Hardware wallets get upgraded. A letter that was accurate in 2024 may be useless by 2027.

Successor Trustee Briefing

If your Bitcoin is held in a trust, your successor trustee must understand Bitcoin at a basic operational level — or know exactly who to call. Schedule an annual meeting with your successor trustee where you walk through the custody setup, demonstrate how the hardware wallet works, and confirm they have the contacts needed to execute the plan.

If your successor trustee is a bank or trust company, verify in writing that they have the capability and willingness to custody Bitcoin. Many traditional trust companies still cannot hold digital assets.

Estate Attorney Review

Your estate plan should have been reviewed within the last five years by an attorney who understands digital assets. Bitcoin creates unique estate planning issues — from the portability of private keys to the irreversibility of transactions to the interaction with state property laws — that traditional estate attorneys may not fully appreciate.

If your estate plan was drafted before you acquired Bitcoin, it almost certainly needs updating. Generic "digital assets" language in a will is not sufficient. Your plan should specifically address Bitcoin custody, transfer mechanisms, and the tax treatment of different transfer methods.

Seed Phrase Security

The most common point of failure in Bitcoin inheritance is lost seed phrases. Options for secure storage that balances access and security:

  • Multi-signature custody: A 2-of-3 multi-sig arrangement where you hold one key, an adult child holds one, and your attorney holds one. No single party can access the Bitcoin, but any two can — ensuring both security and survivorship access. Services like Unchained provide institutional-grade multi-sig infrastructure.
  • Attorney custody: Seed phrase in a sealed envelope held by your estate attorney, with instructions in the Letter of Instructions on when and how to open it.
  • Safe deposit box with dual access: A bank safe deposit box with your successor trustee listed as a joint renter — they can access it upon presenting a death certificate.

Whatever method you choose, test it. Have your successor trustee or executor walk through the recovery process with you present. A seed phrase is useless if nobody knows how to use it.

Case Study: Richard and Helen's 10-Year Drawdown Plan

Richard and Helen are 72. They hold 15 BTC (purchased at an average cost basis of $12,000 per coin), an $800,000 traditional IRA invested in a mix of equities and bonds, and receive $3,000/month ($36,000/year) in combined Social Security benefits. They own their home outright and have $60,000 in cash savings. They need approximately $90,000/year in after-tax income to maintain their lifestyle.

Their goals: maintain their lifestyle for 20+ years, minimize taxes across all income sources, protect Medicare premiums from IRMAA surcharges, and transfer the Bitcoin to their two adult children with maximum tax efficiency.

The Income Picture

At age 72, Richard must begin RMDs from the traditional IRA. Using the Uniform Lifetime Table, his first-year RMD factor is approximately 27.4, yielding an RMD of roughly $29,200. Combined with $36,000 in Social Security, that's $65,200 in gross income before touching any other asset.

After standard deduction ($32,300 for married filing jointly, both over 65), their taxable income from these sources is approximately $32,900 — comfortably in the 12% federal bracket. Their provisional income ($29,200 + 50% of $36,000 = $47,200) means about 85% of their Social Security is taxable, adding roughly $30,600 to taxable income. Total federal tax: approximately $7,600.

After taxes, they net approximately $57,600 from Social Security and RMDs. They need another $32,400 to reach their $90,000 target.

Years 72–75: The Bridge

Richard and Helen draw the $32,400 gap from their cash savings first ($60,000 covers almost two years), then from additional IRA withdrawals. They deliberately avoid touching Bitcoin.

In year 73, they begin strategic Roth conversions — converting $40,000–$50,000 of traditional IRA to Roth each year, filling up the 22% bracket but staying below the IRMAA threshold (~$206,000 MAGI). This is slightly painful now — they pay tax on the conversion — but it reduces future RMDs permanently. By age 76, they've converted roughly $170,000 to Roth and their remaining traditional IRA is closer to $500,000.

Years 76–80: Roth Draws Begin

With the traditional IRA reduced, RMDs are smaller — roughly $22,000/year at age 76. Social Security (with COLA adjustments) is closer to $40,000. Their combined income is around $62,000, well below IRMAA thresholds. They supplement from the Roth IRA — tax-free, no MAGI impact, no provisional income impact.

Bitcoin remains untouched. At current prices, the 15 BTC position continues to appreciate. The stepped-up basis opportunity grows larger every year.

Year 76: Annual Gifting Begins

Richard and Helen begin gifting 0.1 BTC to each of their two children and four grandchildren annually — six gifts of roughly $10,000 each (well under the $19,000 annual exclusion). Over five years, they transfer approximately 3 BTC to the next generation without using any lifetime exemption or triggering gift tax returns. The children receive the $12,000 basis — but they're in their 40s, in lower brackets, and can manage the eventual tax efficiently.

Year 78: Charitable Strategy

Helen, now 78, begins using QCDs to satisfy Richard's RMDs (from their jointly-managed finances). They direct $30,000/year to their church and alma mater via QCD. This satisfies most of the RMD, generates no taxable income, and keeps their MAGI low enough that Medicare premiums stay at the base rate.

The Estate at Age 82

After 10 years of disciplined drawdown, Richard and Helen's traditional IRA is largely depleted (converted to Roth or distributed via RMDs and QCDs). Their Roth IRA holds approximately $250,000. They've gifted 3 BTC to family. They still hold 12 BTC — now worth significantly more than when they started.

When they pass, those 12 BTC receive a stepped-up basis. Their children inherit Bitcoin with zero embedded capital gains tax. The Roth IRA passes income-tax-free. The total tax saved compared to a naive "sell Bitcoin for retirement income" approach: conservatively $200,000–$400,000, depending on Bitcoin's price at death.

That's not clever accounting. That's the difference between building a drawdown plan around Bitcoin's unique tax properties and treating it like just another investment to liquidate.

The Action Checklist for Bitcoin Retirees

Whether your situation mirrors Richard and Helen's or differs significantly, these are the concrete steps every Bitcoin-holding retiree should take:

  1. Map your drawdown sequence. List every income source and asset class. Assign each a position in the spending order. Bitcoin goes last.
  2. Calculate your provisional income threshold. Know exactly how much room you have before 85% of Social Security becomes taxable. Plan Bitcoin sales (if any) around this number.
  3. Check your IRMAA exposure. Pull your MAGI from two years ago. Determine how close you are to the next IRMAA tier. Every Bitcoin-related transaction should be evaluated against this threshold.
  4. Model Roth conversions. If you're between 65 and 73, there may be a window to convert traditional IRA funds at lower tax rates, reducing lifetime RMD burden.
  5. Evaluate QCD eligibility. If you're 70½+ and charitably inclined, QCDs should be your default RMD satisfaction strategy.
  6. Assess the borrow-not-sell option. If you need Bitcoin liquidity, get quotes from institutional Bitcoin lenders before considering a sale.
  7. Update your Letter of Instructions. Today. Not next month. If your heirs can't find or access your Bitcoin, every other strategy is irrelevant.
  8. Brief your successor trustee. Schedule the meeting. Walk through the custody setup. Confirm their capability.
  9. Review your estate plan. If it's more than five years old or predates your Bitcoin acquisition, it needs an update from a digital-asset-aware attorney.
  10. Consult a tax professional. The interactions between capital gains, RMDs, provisional income, IRMAA, and estate taxes are too complex for DIY planning at this scale. A fee-only advisor or CPA who understands both Bitcoin and retirement taxation is not optional — it's essential.

The Bottom Line

Bitcoin estate planning for retirees isn't about whether to hold or sell. It's about recognizing that you're playing a different game than you were at 45. The accumulation phase rewarded buying and holding. The drawdown phase rewards sequencing — spending the right assets in the right order, managing the tax interactions between Social Security, Medicare, RMDs, and capital gains, and positioning Bitcoin as the last asset standing when the estate transfers.

Get this right and Bitcoin becomes the most tax-efficient asset you pass to your heirs. Get it wrong and you pay capital gains tax, trigger IRMAA surcharges, push Social Security into taxable territory, and reduce the inheritance by hundreds of thousands of dollars — all from decisions that could have been avoided with proper planning.

The strategies in this guide aren't aggressive or exotic. They're the logical application of existing tax law to Bitcoin's unique properties. The stepped-up basis isn't a loophole — it's the law. QCDs aren't tricks — they're tools specifically designed for retirees. The "Bitcoin last" drawdown sequence isn't speculation — it's basic tax optimization that any competent advisor would recommend.

The only mistake is not planning at all.
