Bitcoin Valuation Discounts: How to Legally Value Large Positions Below Spot Price for Estate and Gift Tax

By The Bitcoin Family Office  |  Updated March 2026  |  Advanced Estate Planning  •  Valuation

The IRS values Bitcoin for estate and gift tax purposes at fair market value — defined as the price a willing buyer would pay a willing seller, neither under compulsion to act. For a 500-Bitcoin position, that "willing buyer" cannot simply purchase 500 BTC at the current spot price without moving the market. That economic reality is the foundation for the blockage discount — a legitimate, court-approved reduction to the spot-price valuation of a large Bitcoin position. Combined with minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts available through family limited partnership structures, large Bitcoin estates can be valued at 25–40% below spot for transfer tax purposes.

This is not a tax shelter. It is the application of standard valuation principles — embedded in the Treasury regulations since 1954 — to Bitcoin's unique market structure. The discounts are real because the economic constraints are real. A forced sale of 500 BTC at once would depress the price. A minority interest in a family LLC cannot be sold on an exchange. These facts produce lower fair market values, and lower fair market values produce lower transfer taxes.

Discount 1: The Blockage Discount

The blockage discount has its origins in estate tax cases involving large blocks of publicly traded stock. When an estate held millions of shares of a company, courts recognized that liquidating the entire block at once would depress the stock price — the "willing buyer" concept required accounting for this market impact. Treasury Regulation §20.2031-2(e) codified the blockage discount for publicly traded securities.

Bitcoin is increasingly being treated similarly. The argument: a single seller offering 500 BTC into the market cannot achieve the full spot price on every coin because the sale itself moves the price. The IRS has accepted blockage discount arguments for large Bitcoin positions in several private letter ruling discussions, and estate planning appraisers are now routinely analyzing Bitcoin's market depth to support blockage discount claims.

How the Discount Is Calculated

A qualified appraiser analyzes Bitcoin's market depth — the order book depth across major exchanges — and models the price impact of liquidating the entire position over a reasonable timeframe. The discount reflects the difference between the spot price on the valuation date and the estimated net proceeds from an orderly liquidation.

Key factors in the analysis:

  • Position size relative to daily trading volume. Bitcoin's average daily spot volume globally is approximately $15–25 billion. A 500 BTC position at $100,000/BTC ($50M) represents 0.2–0.3% of daily volume — unlikely to cause significant market impact. A 5,000 BTC position at $100,000/BTC ($500M) represents 2–3% of daily volume — meaningful market impact is supported.
  • Exchange-specific depth. OTC desks (Cumberland, Galaxy Digital) handle large blocks without full market exposure, but at a discount to spot. The OTC bid-ask spread for large blocks is documented and supports the discount.
  • Liquidation timeline. A "willing seller" is not required to dump everything at once. The appraiser models an orderly liquidation over a reasonable period (30, 60, or 90 days) and discounts for the time-value cost of gradual liquidation versus instant spot.

Typical blockage discount range for Bitcoin: 5–15% for positions in the 100–1,000 BTC range, based on current market depth analysis. Larger positions command larger discounts. Smaller positions may not support a material blockage discount argument.

Example: A 500 BTC estate position valued at spot = $50,000,000. Appraiser documents 8% blockage discount based on OTC market depth analysis and 60-day orderly liquidation model. Estate tax value: $46,000,000. Federal estate tax savings at 40%: $1,600,000 — from a single appraisal that costs $15,000–$30,000. ROI: 50–100x.

Discount 2: Minority Interest Discount

A minority interest discount applies when the transferred interest does not carry control of the underlying asset. The discount reflects the economic reality that a buyer of a 30% limited partnership interest cannot unilaterally access the underlying Bitcoin — they cannot force a liquidation, change the investment strategy, or compel distributions. These constraints reduce the interest's fair market value below 30% of the underlying asset's spot value.

The minority interest discount is accessed through a Family Limited Partnership (FLP) or Family LLC structure:

  1. The Bitcoin holder contributes Bitcoin to an FLP in exchange for a general partner (GP) interest (1–2%) and a limited partner (LP) interest (98–99%).
  2. The holder then gifts LP interests to children, grandchildren, or trusts — at a valuation that reflects the LP's lack of control and lack of marketability.
  3. The gift tax is calculated on the discounted value of the LP interest, not on the pro-rata share of underlying Bitcoin.

Typical minority interest discount for an LP interest in a Bitcoin FLP: 15–25%, based on comparable transactions in the private equity and limited partnership appraisal market. Courts have upheld minority interest discounts for FLPs holding liquid assets including marketable securities — the lack-of-control argument is independent of what the FLP holds.

Discount 3: Lack-of-Marketability Discount

A lack-of-marketability discount (LOMD) applies to interests that cannot be freely sold in an established market. An LP interest in a family FLP has no public market — there is no exchange where a minority LP can list their interest for sale. A buyer must negotiate privately, accept significant uncertainty about exit timing and price, and comply with transfer restrictions in the partnership agreement.

The LOMD is conceptually separate from the minority interest discount, though both arise from similar economic facts. Applied together, they produce a combined discount that is typically less than the sum of the parts — courts and appraisers apply them multiplicatively, not additively.

Typical LOMD for FLP interests: 10–20%, based on restricted stock studies and pre-IPO discount analyses used as benchmarks.

The Math: FLP Discounts in Action

A Bitcoin holder with a $10M position and a $15M lifetime exemption remaining wants to transfer $10M to a dynasty trust for children and grandchildren. Without any discounting:

  • Gift value: $10,000,000
  • Exemption consumed: $10,000,000
  • Remaining exemption: $5,000,000

With an FLP structure and a 35% combined discount:

  • Bitcoin contributed to FLP at $10,000,000
  • LP interests representing $10,000,000 of Bitcoin gifted to dynasty trust
  • Gift tax value of LP interests (35% discount): $6,500,000
  • Exemption consumed: $6,500,000
  • Remaining exemption: $8,500,000
  • Exemption saved: $3,500,000 — available for future gifts or estate reduction

Alternatively: a 35% discount allows the holder to transfer $15.4M of Bitcoin FMV using only the $10M exemption — instead of $10M of Bitcoin FMV. The discount effectively extends the reach of the lifetime exemption by 54%.

Section 2036: The IRS's Primary Weapon Against FLP Discounts

IRC §2036 is the estate planning attorney's nightmare for FLP structures. It provides that if a person transfers property to a trust or entity but retains the right to use, possess, or enjoy the property — or retains the right to designate who can use or enjoy it — the property is pulled back into the gross estate at death, regardless of the transfer.

The IRS applies §2036 aggressively to FLPs where:

  • The general partner (grantor) continues to use FLP assets for personal expenses
  • The grantor retains the economic benefit of the FLP without formal partnership distributions
  • The FLP was funded on the grantor's deathbed or within a short time before death
  • The FLP has no legitimate business purpose other than tax reduction
  • Non-tax reasons for the FLP are not documented contemporaneously

If §2036 applies, the entire FLP corpus is included in the gross estate — and all discounts are lost. The IRS's win rate on §2036 challenges is significant when FLPs lack a genuine non-tax business purpose documented at formation.

How to Avoid §2036 Inclusion

  • Establish genuine non-tax purposes. The FLP should have documented business purposes: investment management, asset protection, family governance, succession planning for a business, centralized management of a family Bitcoin treasury. These purposes must be real and documented in the partnership agreement and board minutes — not invented post-hoc.
  • Maintain separate finances. Never use FLP assets for personal expenses. Never commingle FLP Bitcoin with personal Bitcoin. The FLP must be operated as a genuine partnership.
  • Make proper distributions. Distributions to all partners (including limited partners) must follow the partnership agreement's terms — not just flow to the general partner/grantor.
  • Don't fund on your deathbed. FLPs funded when the grantor is in poor health or terminally ill are prime §2036 targets. Establish the FLP years before estate planning becomes urgent.
  • Retain adequate assets outside the FLP. The grantor should keep sufficient personal assets to meet living expenses without touching FLP assets. Courts have looked harshly at grantors who transferred virtually everything to an FLP and then lived off it.

⚠️ The "recycling" problem. If the grantor contributes Bitcoin to the FLP and the FLP immediately distributes most of it back to the grantor (in a different form), §2036 applies. The FLP must actually hold the Bitcoin — and the grantor must treat FLP assets as owned by the partnership, not as personal assets held in a different wrapper. Courts have seen every variation of this and applied §2036 accordingly.

Section 2704: The Threat That Never Materialized

In 2016, the Treasury proposed regulations under IRC §2704 that would have dramatically curtailed valuation discounts for family-controlled entities — effectively eliminating minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts when the family controlled the entity. The proposed regulations were withdrawn in 2017 following significant opposition.

As of 2026, §2704 remains the statute as originally enacted — it restricts certain "applicable restrictions" that lapse at death, but does not eliminate minority or LOMD discounts for FLPs with standard partnership agreement transfer restrictions. The proposed 2016 regulations that would have fundamentally changed the landscape are gone.

However, legislative risk remains. Congress could revive restrictions on valuation discounts for family entities. This is a known tail risk for long-term FLP planning — one reason to execute FLP transfers while the current favorable rules are in place.

The Blockage Discount for Direct Bitcoin: The Simpler Play

For holders who don't want to establish an FLP, a standalone blockage discount appraisal on a large direct Bitcoin holding is a lower-complexity starting point. No entity structure required. The appraiser analyzes the market and applies the discount to the position held directly.

The limitation: the blockage discount alone (5–15%) is smaller than the combined FLP discounts (25–40%). For large positions where the additional complexity of an FLP is justified by the tax savings, the FLP structure wins. For mid-sized positions or holders who want simpler documentation, a blockage discount appraisal on a direct holding may be sufficient.

Blockage discount appraisals are most valuable when:

  • The position exceeds 100 BTC (smaller positions have limited market impact arguments)
  • The estate is above or approaching the exemption threshold
  • The appraiser has specific cryptocurrency market expertise (not a generic business appraiser)
  • The appraisal is prepared well in advance of the estate return due date, not rushed at the last minute

The Appraisal Requirement

All valuation discounts must be supported by a "qualified appraisal" prepared by a "qualified appraiser" as defined under IRC §170(f)(11) and Treas. Reg. §1.170A-17. For gift tax purposes, a qualified appraisal must accompany Form 709. For estate tax, it must be attached to Form 706.

Requirements for a qualified appraisal:

  • Prepared by an appraiser with specific credentials (ASA, ABV, or equivalent) and cryptocurrency/digital asset valuation experience
  • Conducted no earlier than 60 days before the gift date and no later than the due date of the return
  • Must include the specific methodology for the blockage, minority interest, and LOMD discounts — not simply assert a percentage
  • Must reference market data supporting the discount conclusions (order book depth, OTC spread data, restricted stock studies, comparable transactions)

A generic business appraisal firm without cryptocurrency market expertise is unlikely to produce a defensible blockage discount analysis. The appraiser must be able to testify to their methodology if the IRS challenges the discount in examination or Tax Court.

Bitcoin Mining Assets: Additional Valuation Considerations

A Bitcoin mining company's value for estate tax purposes may include discounts for the company's equipment depreciation cycle, energy contract terms, and host dependency risk — in addition to discounts on any Bitcoin treasury held by the company. Mining-specific valuation expertise is essential for accurate estate planning.

Key Takeaways

  • A blockage discount of 5–15% can be applied to large Bitcoin positions (100+ BTC) based on market depth analysis showing that liquidating the full position at spot price is not achievable without market impact. This applies to direct holdings — no entity structure required.
  • A Family Limited Partnership structure unlocks minority interest (15–25%) and lack-of-marketability (10–20%) discounts on gifted LP interests — combined, 25–40% below the underlying Bitcoin's spot value for gift and estate tax purposes.
  • A 35% combined discount effectively extends the reach of a $15M lifetime exemption to cover $23M of Bitcoin FMV — or saves $3.5M of exemption on a $10M Bitcoin transfer compared to a direct gift.
  • IRC §2036 is the IRS's primary tool against abusive FLPs. A genuine business purpose, separate finances, proper distributions, and early establishment (years before death) are essential to avoid §2036 inclusion.
  • The proposed 2016 §2704 regulations that would have eliminated these discounts were withdrawn in 2017 and have not been revived — but legislative risk remains.
  • All discounts require a qualified appraisal from a credentialed appraiser with cryptocurrency market expertise, attached to the gift or estate tax return. A generic appraisal without market-specific methodology will not survive IRS scrutiny.
  • Discounts compound with other planning tools: GRAT, IDGT installment sale, dynasty trust, and annual exclusion gifts all benefit from the FLP's reduced valuation baseline.
