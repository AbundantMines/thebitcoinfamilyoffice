Bitcoin Estate Planning in Nebraska

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Nebraska Est. 9 min read

Nebraska is one of only six states that still imposes an inheritance tax — a tax that applies not to the estate itself, but to the beneficiaries who receive assets from it. For Bitcoin families domiciled in Nebraska, or for families outside Nebraska whose Bitcoin may be distributed to Nebraska-resident beneficiaries, this distinction is not academic. Inheritance tax planning for Bitcoin requires understanding who receives the Bitcoin, not just what the decedent owned. Bitcoin estate planning Nebraska must address this inheritance tax framework explicitly — alongside Nebraska's significant advantage of permitting dynasty trusts lasting up to 1,000 years.

Nebraska's Inheritance Tax: The Critical Distinction

Nebraska's inheritance tax (Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 77-2001 through 77-2037) is assessed against beneficiaries based on their relationship to the decedent and the amount they receive. The rate structure is as follows:

  • Class I — Immediate family: Spouse, children, grandchildren, parents, siblings, and other close relatives are generally exempt from Nebraska inheritance tax. Bitcoin passing to immediate family members is not subject to state inheritance tax under current law.
  • Class II — Remote relatives: Aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews, and other more distant relatives pay Nebraska inheritance tax at 1% on amounts received above $15,000. While this rate is low, it is not zero — and for large Bitcoin transfers to extended family members, the dollar exposure can be meaningful.
  • Class III — Non-relatives: Friends, business partners, non-related individuals, and most trusts for their benefit face Nebraska inheritance tax at 18% on amounts received above $10,000. This rate is among the highest inheritance tax rates in the country. A bequest of significant Bitcoin to a non-relative in Nebraska can trigger an 18% tax on the full value above $10,000.
Nebraska's 18% inheritance tax rate for non-relatives is one of the highest in the nation. Bitcoin families leaving assets to non-family beneficiaries — business partners, friends, charitable-adjacent individuals — face a serious planning imperative.

Nebraska substantially reformed its inheritance tax in 2023 (LB310), reducing rates for certain classes and adjusting exemption amounts. The reforms phased in reductions over several years. Families should confirm current rates and exemption thresholds with a Nebraska estate attorney, as the exact figures applicable at death depend on when the decedent dies during the phase-in period.

Planning to Minimize Nebraska Inheritance Tax on Bitcoin

The primary strategies for minimizing Nebraska inheritance tax on Bitcoin involve removing assets from the gross estate before death or directing transfers through structures that are not subject to the tax as applied to individual beneficiaries:

Irrevocable trust transfers during life: Bitcoin transferred to an irrevocable trust during the grantor's lifetime, with gifts that qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion or use of the lifetime exemption, is no longer part of the Nebraska taxable estate for inheritance tax purposes at death. For Class III beneficiaries, lifetime transfers avoid the 18% inheritance tax entirely — with the cost being the loss of basis step-up and the irrevocability of the transfer.

Charitable giving strategies: Bitcoin passing to a qualified charity is exempt from Nebraska inheritance tax regardless of class. Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) and Charitable Lead Trusts (CLTs) can be structured to benefit both family members and charitable organizations, reducing the inheritance tax-exposed portion of the estate while achieving income tax and estate tax benefits simultaneously.

Life insurance funding: An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) can provide estate liquidity equal to the expected Nebraska inheritance tax liability, ensuring that Bitcoin positions do not need to be liquidated at death to pay taxes — particularly important for Class III beneficiaries facing 18% rates.

Domicile of beneficiaries: Nebraska inheritance tax applies to real and tangible personal property located in Nebraska, and to all property (including Bitcoin) of a Nebraska decedent. If beneficiaries are Nebraska residents, the inheritance tax applies. Families with significant out-of-state beneficiaries should confirm the situs rules with their attorney.

Nebraska Dynasty Trusts: A Significant Advantage

Notwithstanding its inheritance tax, Nebraska offers a meaningful planning advantage for Bitcoin families committed to multigenerational stewardship: Nebraska permits dynasty trusts lasting up to 1,000 years. This is among the longest permissible trust durations in the United States, second only to states with perpetual (no-end-date) trust authority like Wyoming, South Dakota, and Nevada.

A Nebraska dynasty trust funded with Bitcoin can hold and appreciate that Bitcoin for effectively unlimited planning horizons. Combined with a multi-signature custody architecture that provides technical succession continuity across generations, a 1,000-year Nebraska dynasty trust is a serious multigenerational Bitcoin holding vehicle. The key requirements: the trust must be properly structured to avoid Nebraska inheritance tax on trust assets passing from generation to generation (this requires specific trust and distribution provisions), and the trust must include a robust key succession protocol that maintains technical access to the Bitcoin as trustees and beneficiaries change over decades and generations.

Nebraska Trust Code and Digital Assets

Nebraska has adopted the Uniform Trust Code, codified at Nebraska Revised Statutes Chapter 30, Article 38. The Nebraska UTC provides modern trust formation and administration infrastructure, including directed trust arrangements where investment direction and administrative trustee functions can be separated — essential for Bitcoin trusts where technical custody decisions require specialized expertise distinct from general trust administration.

Nebraska has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) at Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 30-3501 through 30-3526, providing fiduciaries statutory authority to access and administer digital assets of decedents. As always, RUFADAA provides legal access authority — technical access to Bitcoin requires a separate key succession protocol.

Practical Priorities for Nebraska Bitcoin Families

  1. Map your inheritance tax exposure: Identify who your Bitcoin beneficiaries are and their class (immediate family, remote relative, non-relative) under Nebraska law. For Class III beneficiaries, quantify the 18% inheritance tax exposure and model the alternatives.
  2. Establish an irrevocable trust for out-of-class beneficiaries: For any significant Bitcoin inheritance to non-relatives or remote relatives, evaluate whether a lifetime trust transfer eliminates or reduces the inheritance tax obligation.
  3. Consider a Nebraska dynasty trust: For family Bitcoin holdings, a properly structured dynasty trust lasting up to 1,000 years eliminates repeated inheritance tax exposure at each generational transfer while providing a long-duration holding vehicle for Bitcoin appreciation.
  4. Implement a key succession protocol: Multi-signature Bitcoin custody arrangements with documented succession of signing authority are essential for any trust structure — particularly one designed to operate across multiple generations.
  5. Review federal estate tax exposure: If your total estate approaches the federal exemption — including the potential post-2025 reduction — federal estate tax planning is as urgent as Nebraska inheritance tax planning.
