Bitcoin Estate Planning for Digital Nomads: Domicile, FBAR, Foreign Trusts, and the Complete 2026 Guide

March 16, 2026

You left the suburb. You left the 9-to-5. You probably left the country. But you didn't leave the IRS — and your estate plan needs to reflect that reality.

You're reading this from a café in Lisbon, a coworking space in Chiang Mai, or an Airbnb in Medellín. You hold Bitcoin — probably a meaningful amount. You earn in USD or stablecoins, spend in local currency, and haven't had a "permanent address" in years. Your parents' estate attorney back in Michigan has no idea what to do with you.

That's the problem. Generic estate planning advice assumes you live somewhere specific, bank somewhere specific, and die somewhere specific. Digital nomads break every one of those assumptions. And when you layer Bitcoin on top of a location-independent life, the estate planning complexity doesn't just increase — it fundamentally changes shape.

This guide is built for you. Not the person who owns 0.1 BTC in a Coinbase account and lives in Denver. The person who holds serious Bitcoin across self-custody wallets and multiple exchanges, earns income from three continents, hasn't filed a state tax return in years, and needs an estate plan that actually works when they die in a country they weren't born in.

We're going to cover domicile strategy, FBAR and FATCA obligations on foreign exchange accounts, the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion trap, trust structuring that won't trigger foreign trust classification, the exit tax for anyone considering renunciation, multi-country estate tax treaties, and practical custody architecture for someone who crosses borders with a hardware wallet.

None of this is theoretical. All of it is actionable.

The Domicile Problem: Where Do You "Live" When You Live Everywhere?

Estate and gift taxes don't care where you are right now. They care about your domicile — the jurisdiction you consider your permanent home, the place you intend to return to when your wandering stops.

For US citizens, this distinction matters less at the federal level. The US taxes its citizens on worldwide income and subjects their worldwide estates to federal estate tax regardless of where they live. You could spend 20 years in Portugal and never set foot in the US — the IRS still considers your global estate taxable at death. The current federal estate tax exemption sits at $13.99 million per person in 2026, but that number is politically contested and has been since the TCJA debates.

Where domicile becomes critical is at the state level. States with estate taxes (Massachusetts, Oregon, Washington, Connecticut, and others) impose their own death taxes with exemptions as low as $1 million. If a state can claim you as a domiciliary, it can tax your estate — including your Bitcoin. And states are aggressive about claiming domiciliaries, especially wealthy ones who haven't formally severed ties.

For non-US-citizen nomads, domicile is even more consequential. It determines which country's tax system governs your estate. A non-citizen, non-resident of the US gets only a $60,000 federal estate tax exemption on US-situs assets — a fraction of what citizens receive. If you're a non-US person holding Bitcoin on a US exchange, that Bitcoin may be considered US-situs property. Your domicile determines whether your home country's estate tax regime applies to your worldwide assets or only to assets within its borders.

The bottom line: even if you have no fixed address, the law will assign you a domicile. If you don't choose one deliberately, a state or country will choose one for you — usually at the worst possible time, which is after you're dead and can't argue about it.

Choosing a Nomad-Friendly Domicile State

If you're a US citizen nomad, you need a state domicile even if you never spend more than a few weeks there per year. The right state gives you three things: no state income tax, no state estate tax, and favorable trust law for your Bitcoin holdings.

Three states dominate this calculation:

South Dakota

No state income tax. No state estate or inheritance tax. Perpetual trust statute — meaning a dynasty trust established in South Dakota can last indefinitely, sheltering generational Bitcoin wealth from estate tax at every transfer. South Dakota also has among the strongest asset protection trust laws in the country, with a two-year statute of limitations for creditor claims against self-settled trusts. For Bitcoin holders, this is the gold standard domicile.

Nevada

Same core advantages: no income tax, no estate tax, perpetual trusts. Nevada adds strong spendthrift trust protections and a well-developed body of trust case law. The state has been actively courting trust business for decades, and its trust companies are experienced with digital asset custody arrangements.

Wyoming

Everything South Dakota and Nevada offer, plus Wyoming has been the most forward-thinking state on Bitcoin-specific legislation. Wyoming's Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI) charter created a legal framework for crypto-native banks. The state's Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO) LLC statute, while not directly relevant to estate planning, signals a legislative environment that understands digital assets. Wyoming also offers perpetual trusts and has no state income or estate tax.

How to Establish Domicile Without Living There

You don't need to live in South Dakota 183 days per year. Domicile is about intent, not physical presence. But intent needs to be backed by objective evidence. Here's the playbook:

  • Registered agent address: Establish a physical mailing address through a registered agent service. This becomes your legal address for all government filings.
  • Driver's license: Surrender your old state license and obtain one in your new domicile state. This is the single strongest piece of domicile evidence.
  • Voter registration: Register to vote in the new state. Even if you vote by absentee ballot from Bali, you're registered in South Dakota.
  • Bank account: Open a bank account at a local institution. Use it for some regular transactions.
  • Vehicle registration: If you own a vehicle (even one stored in the US), register it in the new state.
  • State tax returns: File a part-year or full-year resident return in the new state if required. More importantly, stop filing in your old state.
  • Estate planning documents: Execute your will, trust, and powers of attorney under the new state's law, referencing your domicile explicitly.

The key principle: sever every tie to your old state and create as many ties as possible to the new one. States like California and New York are notoriously aggressive about claiming former residents as domiciliaries. If you ever lived in either state, you need a clean, documented break — or they'll send your estate a bill.

Bitcoin and FBAR: The $10,000 Trap That Catches Nomads

If you hold Bitcoin at any foreign financial institution — Kraken's European entity, Bitfinex, Bitstamp, a local exchange in Thailand or Turkey — and the aggregate value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the calendar year, you are required to file FinCEN Form 114, the Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR).

Read that again. It's not $10,000 per account. It's $10,000 aggregate across all foreign accounts. It's not the year-end balance — it's the maximum value at any point during the year. If your foreign exchange account held $11,000 worth of Bitcoin for fifteen minutes on a Tuesday in March before you transferred it to self-custody, you have an FBAR obligation for the entire year.

The penalties are severe. Non-willful failure to file carries a penalty of up to $10,000 per account, per year. Willful failure can reach $100,000 or 50% of the account balance — whichever is greater. Criminal penalties are also on the table for willful violations.

For nomads, this is an especially dangerous area because:

  • You're more likely to use foreign exchanges out of convenience or necessity (local fiat on-ramps in your current country)
  • You may not realize that a European or Asian exchange qualifies as a "foreign financial account"
  • The $10,000 threshold is absurdly low — a single Bitcoin blows past it
  • You're filing US taxes from abroad, possibly with a preparer who doesn't ask about foreign accounts

The FBAR is filed separately from your tax return — it goes directly to FinCEN, not the IRS, with an April 15 deadline (automatic extension to October 15). It's electronic-only via the BSA E-Filing System. For detailed guidance on FBAR and related foreign reporting, see our complete guide to Form 3520 and foreign Bitcoin reporting.

Form 8938 and FATCA: The Other Foreign Reporting Obligation

FBAR isn't the only foreign reporting form nomads need to worry about. Form 8938, required under the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), applies to US taxpayers with specified foreign financial assets exceeding certain thresholds.

For taxpayers living abroad (which includes most nomads), the thresholds are higher than for domestic filers:

  • Single filers abroad: $200,000 at year-end, or $300,000 at any point during the year
  • Married filing jointly abroad: $400,000 at year-end, or $600,000 at any point during the year
  • For comparison, domestic filers: $50,000 at year-end, or $75,000 at any point

Bitcoin held at a foreign exchange qualifies as a specified foreign financial asset. Unlike FBAR, Form 8938 is filed with your income tax return (Form 1040). The penalties for failure to file start at $10,000 and can escalate to $60,000 for continued non-compliance after IRS notification.

Here's what catches nomads: FBAR and Form 8938 are separate obligations with separate thresholds. You might owe one, both, or neither depending on your balances. Many nomads unknowingly fail to file Form 8938 because they're unaware it exists separately from FBAR, or because they assume the higher thresholds for overseas filers protect them — not realizing that a single Bitcoin position can easily breach even the $200,000/$300,000 overseas threshold.

The overlap between FBAR and 8938 confuses even experienced tax preparers. The safe move: if you hold any Bitcoin at any foreign exchange and you're a US person, consult with a tax professional who specifically handles foreign asset reporting. The cost of professional preparation is trivial compared to the penalties for non-compliance.

The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion and Bitcoin: What Doesn't Qualify

The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) allows qualifying US citizens and residents living abroad to exclude up to $126,500 (2026) of foreign-earned income from US federal income tax. For nomads earning in USD while living in low-tax jurisdictions, the FEIE is one of the most powerful tax benefits available.

But here's the critical limitation that Bitcoin holders need to understand: Bitcoin capital gains do not qualify for the FEIE.

The FEIE applies only to earned income — wages, salaries, self-employment income, professional fees. Capital gains are passive investment income, and the FEIE explicitly excludes them. It doesn't matter that you were sitting in a zero-tax jurisdiction when you sold the Bitcoin. It doesn't matter that you meet the bona fide residence test or the physical presence test. Capital gains on Bitcoin are taxed by the US at the full applicable rate — short-term gains at ordinary income rates, long-term gains at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your total income.

This is the FEIE trap that catches nomads. You structure your life around the exclusion, move to Thailand or Georgia (the country, not the state), keep your earned income below the exclusion threshold, and think you're in the clear. Then you sell Bitcoin and discover that the $200,000 capital gain is fully taxable by the US, with no exclusion, no foreign tax credit (because you're in a zero-tax jurisdiction), and no offset.

The Bitcoin Mining Exception (Maybe)

There's one area where Bitcoin and the FEIE intersect in an interesting way: mining income. If a US citizen operates a Bitcoin mining business in a foreign country as a self-employed individual, the mining income might qualify as foreign earned income under the FEIE. The argument is that mining is an active business generating self-employment income, not passive investment income.

This is genuinely unsettled territory. The IRS has not issued specific guidance on whether mining income qualifies for the FEIE. The classification likely depends on the facts: is the nomad actively managing mining operations, or passively receiving mining rewards from a hosted arrangement? The more active the involvement, the stronger the FEIE argument.

Estate Plan Structure for Nomads: The Architecture That Works

With domicile established and reporting obligations understood, it's time to build the actual estate plan. For Bitcoin-holding nomads, the structure needs to be deliberately US-anchored — even if you spend 90% of your time outside the country.

The Revocable Trust as Primary Vehicle

A revocable living trust sited in South Dakota, Nevada, or Wyoming serves as the backbone of a nomad's estate plan. Unlike a will, which must go through probate in the state where you're domiciled (and possibly in every state where you own real property), a properly funded revocable trust passes assets outside of probate entirely.

For nomads, this has an additional advantage: it eliminates the question of which country's probate system applies. If you die in Portugal with a will that was drafted in South Dakota, Portuguese courts may assert jurisdiction over assets located in Portugal, creating a multi-jurisdictional probate nightmare. A revocable trust avoids this by removing assets from the probate estate altogether.

Key structural requirements for the nomad's trust:

  • US situs: The trust document should specify that it is governed by the law of your chosen domicile state (SD, NV, or WY). The trust's place of administration should be explicitly stated as that state.
  • US corporate trustee or trust company: Consider naming a South Dakota or Nevada trust company as co-trustee or successor trustee. This anchors the trust's administration in the US and eliminates any argument that it's being administered abroad.
  • US person as successor trustee: This is non-negotiable. If your successor trustee is a foreign national, the IRS may classify the trust as a foreign trust upon your death — triggering Form 3520/3520-A reporting and potentially adverse tax treatment for your beneficiaries. We'll cover this trap in detail below.

Beneficiary Designations on Exchange Accounts

US exchanges like Coinbase and Kraken US allow beneficiary designations on accounts. For assets held at these exchanges, beneficiary designations provide a direct transfer mechanism that bypasses probate entirely — similar to a payable-on-death designation at a bank.

For nomads, keep the following in mind:

  • Name the revocable trust as the beneficiary of exchange accounts to ensure assets flow into the trust's distribution framework
  • Alternatively, name individuals directly if you want specific exchange accounts to pass outside the trust
  • Review and update designations whenever you change exchanges, add new accounts, or experience life changes
  • Foreign exchange accounts may not offer beneficiary designations — which is another reason to consolidate holdings on US platforms or in self-custody

Hardware Wallet Custody: Accessible From Anywhere

For self-custodied Bitcoin, your estate plan is only as good as your heirs' ability to access the private keys. The nomad's custody challenge is unique: you need your seed phrase accessible from anywhere in the world, but secured against loss or theft across multiple jurisdictions.

A layered approach:

  • Primary custody: Hardware wallet (Coldcard, Trezor, Ledger) that travels with you
  • Seed phrase backup #1: Metal plate in a US safe deposit box in your domicile state — accessible to your successor trustee
  • Seed phrase backup #2: Encrypted digital backup stored in a cloud service, with the decryption key held separately by a trusted individual or in a sealed envelope with your estate attorney
  • Letter of instruction: A detailed, non-legal document stored with your trust that explains exactly where your Bitcoin is held, which wallets contain funds, how to access the hardware wallet, and the recovery process. Update this annually.

The cardinal rule: never store the seed phrase and the hardware wallet in the same location or with the same person. And never assume your heirs know how Bitcoin custody works. Write it down as if you're explaining it to someone who has never touched a hardware wallet — because that's probably who will be reading it.

The Foreign Trust Trap: How Nomads Accidentally Create Reporting Nightmares

This section alone could save you six figures in penalties. Pay attention.

Under IRC §§7701(a)(30)(E) and 7701(a)(31)(B), a trust is classified as a domestic trust only if two conditions are met:

  1. A US court is able to exercise primary supervision over the administration of the trust (the "court test")
  2. One or more US persons have the authority to control all substantial decisions of the trust (the "control test")

If either test fails, the trust is classified as a foreign trust. And foreign trusts trigger a cascade of reporting obligations and tax consequences that are wildly disproportionate to the actual economic substance of most situations:

  • Form 3520: Annual return filed by US persons who are treated as owners of, or who receive distributions from, a foreign trust. Penalty for failure to file: 35% of the gross reportable amount.
  • Form 3520-A: Annual information return filed by the foreign trust itself. If the trust doesn't file, the US owner is on the hook for a penalty equal to 5% of the trust's gross assets.
  • Throwback tax: Accumulation distributions from foreign trusts to US beneficiaries are taxed under a punitive throwback rule that applies the highest marginal rate plus an interest charge.

How do nomads accidentally trigger this? Several ways:

Foreign co-trustee. You name your partner (a non-US citizen living abroad) as co-trustee of your revocable trust. The control test now arguably fails — a non-US person has authority over substantial decisions. The IRS can reclassify the trust as foreign.

Foreign administration. Your trust is nominally governed by South Dakota law, but you (the trustee) are physically administering it from Lisbon for years. The court test may fail if the practical administration is occurring outside any US court's jurisdiction.

Death with a foreign successor trustee. Your trust was domestic during your lifetime because you, a US person, controlled it. But upon your death, if the successor trustee is a foreign person, the trust may be reclassified as foreign — triggering reporting obligations for your beneficiaries from day one.

The fix is structural and absolute: never name a foreign national as trustee, co-trustee, or successor trustee of a trust intended to be domestic. If your spouse or partner is not a US person, use a US trust company as co-trustee or successor trustee. The cost is a few thousand dollars per year. The penalty for getting this wrong is orders of magnitude greater. For a deeper dive on the mechanics, see our Form 3520 and foreign trust reporting guide.

The Exit Tax: What Happens When Nomads Consider Renouncing Citizenship

Some nomads eventually ask the question: why am I still a US citizen? The worldwide taxation, the FBAR filings, the FATCA reporting, the estate tax on global assets — for someone who hasn't lived in the US for a decade and has no intention of returning, renunciation starts to look rational.

It's not that simple. IRC §877A imposes an exit tax on "covered expatriates" — US citizens who renounce citizenship and long-term green card holders who surrender their cards. You're a covered expatriate if any of the following apply:

  • Your average annual net income tax for the five preceding years exceeds approximately $201,000 (2026, indexed)
  • Your net worth is $2 million or more on the date of expatriation
  • You fail to certify five years of tax compliance on Form 8854

If you're a covered expatriate, you're treated as if you sold all worldwide assets at fair market value on the day before expatriation. For Bitcoin holders, this means every satoshi of unrealized gain is recognized immediately. There's an exclusion amount (approximately $886,000 in 2026, indexed), but for anyone with significant Bitcoin holdings, the exit tax bill can be enormous.

Consider a nomad holding 50 BTC purchased at an average cost of $10,000 per coin. At a hypothetical price of $100,000 per coin, the deemed sale produces $4.5 million in gain. After the exclusion, approximately $3.6 million is taxable — at long-term capital gains rates if held more than a year, but that's still a tax bill approaching $720,000.

Planning strategies for those considering renunciation:

  • Charitable contributions before expatriation: Donating appreciated Bitcoin to a US public charity generates a deduction equal to fair market value with no capital gains recognition. This reduces both net worth and the eventual exit tax base.
  • Gifting to US-citizen family members: Transfers completed before expatriation are subject to normal gift tax rules (with the generous lifetime exemption), not the exit tax. Gifts made after expatriation to US persons are subject to a special transfer tax under §2801.
  • Timing: If you anticipate a significant increase in Bitcoin's price, renouncing sooner rather than later means a lower exit tax base. Conversely, if you expect prices to decline, waiting might reduce the tax.
  • Installment payment: Under §877A(b), you can elect to defer the exit tax on certain assets by posting adequate security with the IRS and paying interest. This is complex but can provide liquidity relief.

Renunciation is a one-way door. Once you're out, you cannot reclaim US citizenship (with narrow exceptions). The tax planning needs to happen before the expatriation date, not after. And the penalty for getting the exit tax calculation wrong is a permanent overpayment or an IRS audit of your final US tax return. Get professional help.

Multi-Country Estate Planning for Dual Citizens

Dual citizens — US + EU, US + Canada, US + UK, US + Israel — face an estate planning dimension that single-citizenship nomads don't: two countries may claim the right to tax the same estate.

The US imposes estate tax on the worldwide assets of US citizens. Many other countries impose their own inheritance or estate taxes. Without a treaty, double taxation is a real possibility.

The good news: the US has estate tax treaties with several countries, including Canada, the United Kingdom, France, Germany, Japan, and a handful of others. These treaties generally provide mechanisms to avoid double taxation through credits, exemptions, or allocation of taxing rights.

The bad news: most countries have no estate tax treaty with the US. If you're a dual citizen of the US and Portugal, the US and Thailand, the US and Mexico, or the US and virtually any Latin American or Southeast Asian country — there's no treaty to prevent double taxation. You're relying on the unilateral foreign death tax credit under IRC §2014, which is limited and doesn't always fully offset the double tax.

Bitcoin-Specific Issues for Dual Citizens

Bitcoin held on exchanges creates situs questions. If a US-Canadian dual citizen holds Bitcoin on a Canadian exchange, both countries may claim that asset is within their taxing jurisdiction. Canada may view it as Canadian-situs because the exchange is Canadian. The US taxes it regardless because the holder is a US citizen.

Self-custodied Bitcoin is even more ambiguous. Where is a Bitcoin wallet "located" for estate tax situs purposes? The IRS has not definitively ruled on this, and other countries' positions vary. Some practitioners argue that self-custodied Bitcoin follows the domicile of the holder. Others argue it's intangible property with no situs at all.

For dual citizens, the practical approach is:

  • Consolidate Bitcoin holdings on US exchanges or in self-custody rather than holding on exchanges in each country. This simplifies the situs analysis and keeps assets within a single country's clear jurisdiction.
  • Use the estate tax treaty if one exists between the US and your second country. Work with advisors in both countries who understand the treaty's specific provisions.
  • Structure the estate plan to maximize credits and exemptions in both countries. This often means coordinating the trust structure with the other country's succession laws.
  • Consider the forced heirship issue: Many civil law countries (France, Germany, much of Latin America) impose forced heirship rules that require a portion of the estate to pass to specific heirs regardless of the decedent's wishes. If your Bitcoin trust distributes assets in a way that violates your second country's forced heirship rules, the distribution may be challenged.

Practical Custody Architecture for the Nomad Lifestyle

Everything above — domicile, FBAR, trusts, treaties — is meaningless if your heirs can't actually access your Bitcoin. And for nomads, custody is uniquely challenging because you're mobile, your assets need to be portable, and you can't rely on a single physical location for backup.

Self-Custody Is Non-Negotiable for Nomads

Exchange accounts are jurisdiction-specific. If you die while your Bitcoin is on a Thai exchange, your US-based successor trustee may face months or years of bureaucratic friction trying to access those assets — assuming the exchange cooperates at all. Self-custody via a hardware wallet is the only truly portable, jurisdiction-neutral form of holding Bitcoin.

The nomad's custody stack should look like this:

Layer 1: Active spending. A small amount of Bitcoin in a mobile wallet (Phoenix, Muun, Blue Wallet) for daily transactions. This is your hot wallet — the equivalent of cash in your physical wallet. Keep it small enough that you wouldn't lose sleep over losing it.

Layer 2: Primary cold storage. The bulk of your holdings on a hardware wallet (Coldcard, Trezor Model T, Foundation Passport) that stays in your physical possession. This is what you carry across borders.

Layer 3: Multi-sig for large holdings. For amounts that represent generational wealth, a multi-sig arrangement with geographically distributed keyholders. A 2-of-3 multi-sig where one key is on your hardware wallet, one is held by a trusted family member in the US, and one is held by a collaborative custody provider like Unchained creates redundancy that no single point of failure can compromise.

Traveling With a Hardware Wallet

A hardware wallet is not a financial instrument for customs purposes — it's a piece of electronic equipment. There is no requirement to declare a hardware wallet when crossing borders, just as there's no requirement to declare the banking app on your phone. The Bitcoin isn't "in" the device; the device holds the keys to access Bitcoin on the blockchain.

That said, practical considerations apply:

  • Some countries' customs agents may not understand what a hardware wallet is. Carry it in a way that doesn't invite questions — in your electronics bag alongside a laptop and phone, not in a labeled case that says "CRYPTO WALLET."
  • If questioned, a hardware wallet is a "USB security device" or "authentication device." This is technically accurate.
  • The $10,000 currency reporting threshold for cross-border travel (FinCEN Form 105) applies to "monetary instruments" — cash, traveler's checks, money orders. Bitcoin on a hardware wallet is not a monetary instrument under current regulations, though this area continues to evolve.
  • Never travel with your seed phrase written on paper alongside your hardware wallet. If both are lost or confiscated, you've lost everything. The seed phrase backup should be in a separate, secure location — or encrypted and stored digitally.

Cloud-Encrypted Seed Phrase Backup

For nomads who can't rely on a single physical location, encrypted cloud backup of seed phrases provides global accessibility with strong security. Services like Unchained Vault offer institutional-grade seed phrase storage with multi-factor access controls.

If you prefer a DIY approach: encrypt your seed phrase with a strong passphrase using an open-source tool (GPG, VeraCrypt), store the encrypted file in multiple cloud services (iCloud, Google Drive, Proton Drive), and keep the decryption passphrase with your estate attorney and in your letter of instruction. The encrypted file is useless without the passphrase. The passphrase is useless without the file. Neither, alone, compromises your Bitcoin.

Putting It All Together: The Nomad's Estate Planning Checklist

This is the operational summary. Every item on this list should be completed, documented, and reviewed annually.

  1. Establish domicile in South Dakota, Nevada, or Wyoming. Obtain driver's license, voter registration, registered agent address, and bank account. Sever ties to your previous state of domicile completely.
  2. Create a revocable trust under your domicile state's law. Name a US person or US trust company as successor trustee. Never name a foreign national in any trustee role.
  3. Fund the trust with your Bitcoin holdings. Transfer exchange accounts into the trust's name where possible. For self-custodied Bitcoin, execute an assignment of the private keys to the trust (a written document assigning ownership of the specified wallet addresses to the trust).
  4. Execute supporting documents: pour-over will, durable power of attorney (naming a US person as agent), healthcare directive, and a letter of instruction covering all Bitcoin custody details.
  5. Consolidate exchange holdings on US-based platforms where possible to simplify situs analysis and reduce foreign reporting obligations.
  6. Audit foreign account reporting: file FBAR for any year where aggregate foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000. File Form 8938 if thresholds are met. If you're behind, consider the IRS Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures for delinquent filers living abroad — this program offers penalty relief for non-willful violations.
  7. Separate earned income from investment income for FEIE purposes. Never commingle Bitcoin capital gains with FEIE-eligible earned income on your return.
  8. Implement multi-sig custody for large holdings with geographically distributed keys.
  9. Store seed phrase backups in at least two locations: one physical (US safe deposit box) and one encrypted digital (cloud with separate decryption key held by a trusted person).
  10. Review annually. Laws change. Bitcoin's price changes. Your life changes. An estate plan that worked when you were single in Chiang Mai may not work when you're married with children in Lisbon. Review every twelve months with an attorney who understands both digital assets and international tax.

The Cost of Getting This Wrong

Let's be direct about the stakes. A nomad with significant Bitcoin who dies without an estate plan faces:

  • Intestate succession under a state or country they may not have chosen — distributing assets according to a default formula that may not reflect their wishes
  • Multi-country probate proceedings that can take years and consume six figures in legal fees
  • Potential double estate taxation with no treaty protection
  • Lost Bitcoin if heirs don't have access to private keys or don't even know the Bitcoin exists
  • FBAR and FATCA penalties accruing against the estate for prior years' non-compliance
  • Foreign trust reclassification triggering Form 3520 penalties for beneficiaries

The total exposure for a $5 million Bitcoin estate with these compounding failures can easily exceed $1 million — and that's before the emotional cost to your family of untangling the mess from a country they've never visited.

The cost of doing it right? A few thousand dollars per year for proper legal and tax counsel, plus the discipline to maintain your domicile, file your reports, and keep your custody documents updated. For someone who's built a multi-million dollar Bitcoin position while living on their own terms across the globe, this isn't a burden. It's the final piece of the architecture.

You Built a Borderless Life. Build a Borderless Estate Plan.

The digital nomad lifestyle is, at its core, an exercise in intentional design. You chose where to live, how to work, and what to own. Bitcoin fits that ethos perfectly — sovereign money for a sovereign life.

But sovereignty requires structure. The IRS doesn't care about your philosophy of freedom. Probate courts don't care that you haven't had a permanent address since 2019. Foreign exchange regulators don't care that you think reporting requirements are overreach.

What matters is whether you built the structure to protect what you've accumulated. A domicile in the right state. A trust that stays domestic. Reporting that's current. Custody that's accessible. An estate plan that works regardless of which country you're in when the plan gets activated.

This isn't generic advice for someone in Ohio. This is the blueprint for someone who already lives differently — and needs their estate plan to keep up.
