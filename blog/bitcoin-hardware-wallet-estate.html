Hardware Wallet Estate Planning: Ledger, Trezor, Coldcard — What Your Heirs Need to Know

By Hal Turner · The Bitcoin Family Office · February 25, 2026 · ~11 min read

Somewhere in your home there is a small device — roughly the size of a USB thumb drive — that controls access to your Bitcoin. When you die, your heirs will find it. What happens next depends entirely on how well you've prepared them.

Hardware wallets are the gold standard of Bitcoin self-custody. They keep your private keys offline, away from internet-connected devices and the malware that targets them. But they're also among the most misunderstood objects an heir can inherit. A Ledger Nano X is not like a safe-deposit box. There is no bank to call. There is no customer service line. There is no recovery process that bypasses the seed phrase.

If your heirs don't know what they're holding, don't have the right information, or — worse — make the wrong moves in the first hours after finding the device, they can permanently destroy access to the Bitcoin inside.

This guide explains what your heirs will encounter with the three most common hardware wallets, what they need to do (and not do), and what information you must leave behind right now — before it's too late to provide it.

The Three Hardware Wallets Your Heirs Are Most Likely to Encounter

The hardware wallet market is dominated by three products: Ledger, Trezor, and Coldcard. Each works on the same fundamental principle — your private keys never leave the device — but each has a different physical form, companion software, and recovery process. Your Letter of Instruction should specify exactly which one you use.

Ledger
Nano X · Nano S Plus
  • Connects via USB or Bluetooth (Nano X)
  • Companion app: Ledger Live
  • Recovery: restore 24-word seed to new device via Ledger Live
  • PIN required to unlock device
  • Heirs need: device + PIN, or seed phrase + new Ledger
Trezor
Model T · Model One
  • Connects via USB
  • Companion app: Trezor Suite (web-based at suite.trezor.io)
  • Open-source firmware, widely audited
  • Recovery: seed phrase entered directly on device screen
  • Heirs need: device + PIN, or seed phrase + new Trezor
Coldcard
Mk4 · Q
  • Air-gapped, no USB data mode required
  • Often paired with Sparrow Wallet or Electrum
  • More technical than Ledger or Trezor
  • Recovery: seed phrase into new Coldcard, then reconnect to watch-only wallet
  • Heirs likely need a Bitcoin custody consultant
Key Point

Each wallet has a different UI, a different recovery flow, and a different companion application. Your Letter of Instruction must state which wallet brand and model you use, where the device is stored, and which companion software was in use. "I have a crypto USB thing" is not sufficient information for an heir.

What Your Heir Actually Has to Do: Step by Step

Inheritance of a hardware wallet is not like inheriting a bank account. There is no institution to contact. The process is entirely technical and must be executed correctly. Here is what your heir will need to do, in order:

  1. Locate the hardware wallet device
    Your Letter of Instruction tells them where it is — desk drawer, safe, safety deposit box. Without this, they may never find it. Multiple devices may exist; each wallet device controls a separate set of keys.
  2. Download the correct companion software
    Ledger requires Ledger Live (desktop app). Trezor requires Trezor Suite (web browser at suite.trezor.io or desktop app). Coldcard is often used with Sparrow Wallet. Using the wrong software can cause confusion and errors.
  3. If the device is functional with PIN known: access directly
    Connect the device, enter the PIN, and the companion software shows the wallet and balance. Your heir can then transfer funds to their own wallet. This is the cleanest scenario.
  4. If the device is wiped, lost, or PIN unknown: use the seed phrase
    Your heir purchases a new hardware wallet of the same (or compatible) brand, initializes it in "restore from seed phrase" mode, enters the 12 or 24 seed words in the correct order, and the wallet is restored with the original Bitcoin intact.
  5. Confirm balance before moving anything
    Once restored, the heir should confirm the expected balance before initiating any transfers. A mismatch — especially a zero balance — is a warning sign that either the wrong seed was used, or a passphrase is in effect.
  6. Transfer to your own wallet using a test transaction first
    Never send the full balance in one transaction without first confirming a small test amount reaches the destination correctly. Bitcoin transactions are irreversible; a typo in the destination address cannot be undone.
Critical Distinction

The PIN is convenience, not security. It prevents someone who finds your device from accessing it immediately. But the seed phrase is the real key — it can restore your wallet on any compatible device, bypassing the PIN entirely. Security comes from keeping the seed phrase safe, not just the device.

The Passphrase Layer: The Most Common Reason Heirs Fail

This section may be the most important thing you read today. It is also the most commonly misunderstood aspect of hardware wallet estate planning.

⚠️
If a BIP39 Passphrase Is in Use, the Seed Phrase Alone Opens an Empty Wallet

A BIP39 passphrase (sometimes called the "25th word") creates a mathematically distinct wallet from the same seed phrase. The seed phrase without the passphrase opens a valid but empty wallet. The seed phrase with the passphrase opens the wallet containing the actual Bitcoin.

Here's why this matters so much for estate planning:

An heir finds the seed phrase. They restore it on a new Ledger. The wallet shows a zero balance. They conclude the Bitcoin is gone — maybe it was spent, maybe it was a mistake, maybe the device was already drained.

The Bitcoin is not gone. It is sitting in the passphrase-protected wallet, inaccessible because no one told them to enter the passphrase.

This scenario plays out regularly. Significant Bitcoin has been lost to estates because the holder used a passphrase for security and never informed their heirs — or never documented it in a way the heirs could find after death.

What Your Letter of Instruction Must State

Your Letter of Instruction must include one of these two explicit statements:

  • "A BIP39 passphrase IS used on this wallet." The passphrase is stored [location]. The wallet with the actual Bitcoin requires both the seed phrase AND this passphrase to access.
  • "A BIP39 passphrase IS NOT used on this wallet." The seed phrase alone restores the wallet with the full balance.

If you use a passphrase, the heir needs to know where it is stored — and that storage must be as secure as the seed phrase itself, while being accessible after your death. Consider storing the passphrase in a separate secure location from the seed phrase, with your estate attorney or trustee having access instructions for both.

Seed Phrase Backup Formats: What Your Heirs Will Find

The seed phrase backup is the most important document your heirs will search for. It comes in several forms, each with different durability and accessibility characteristics:

Format Description Risk Durability
Paper backup 12 or 24 words written or printed on paper; often stored in filing cabinet, desk drawer, or safe Fire & Water Risk Low — fragile, degrades
Metal backup Cryptosteel, Cryptotag, Bilodal, or similar steel plate with stamped/engraved words; typically in a safe Highly Durable High — survives fire and flooding
Encrypted digital file Seed phrase encrypted with tools like VeraCrypt or GPG; stored on USB or cloud Password Risk Medium — heir needs encryption password AND the file
Shamir Secret Sharing Seed split into multiple shares (e.g., 3-of-5); each share is useless alone; requires threshold to reconstruct Advanced High — but heir must locate the required number of shares

Whatever format you use, your Letter of Instruction must describe the format and tell the heir exactly where to find it. A metal backup in a fireproof safe is worthless to an heir who doesn't know the safe combination, the safe's location, or that a metal backup exists at all.

Note on Shamir Secret Sharing

SLIP39 Shamir Secret Sharing is supported natively by Trezor (Model T) and some other advanced wallets. If you've split your seed into shares and distributed them to multiple trusted parties, your Letter of Instruction must name each share holder and specify the minimum number of shares needed for reconstruction. If shares are lost or inaccessible, the Bitcoin is lost.

What NOT to Do When Inheriting a Hardware Wallet

The wrong moves in the first hours of inheriting a hardware wallet can be irreversible. Your heirs need to know what to avoid before they know what to do.

  • Don't repeatedly guess the PIN
    Most hardware wallets wipe themselves after 3–10 incorrect PIN attempts. A Ledger wipes after 3 wrong PINs. A Trezor wipes after increasing time delays followed by a wipe. Without the seed phrase, a wiped device means permanent loss of access.
  • Don't plug the device into an unknown computer
    A hardware wallet should only be connected to a computer you personally own and control. Plugging into a shared, public, or compromised computer while the device is unlocked could expose the wallet to malware — though this risk is lower for hardware wallets than software wallets.
  • Don't share the seed phrase with anyone claiming to be a "recovery specialist"
    Bitcoin inheritance fraud is a real and growing threat. Anyone who asks for your seed phrase — including people claiming to work for Ledger, Trezor, or any recovery service — is attempting to steal the Bitcoin. The seed phrase should only ever be entered directly into a hardware wallet device in your physical possession.
  • Don't rush
    The Bitcoin is not going anywhere. It is secured on the Bitcoin blockchain and can only be moved by whoever controls the private keys. A slow, careful, properly guided recovery is infinitely better than a rushed error that destroys access permanently.
  • Don't discard or donate the device before confirming access to all wallets
    If the device still has Bitcoin on it (perhaps in a passphrase-protected wallet the heir isn't aware of), discarding it eliminates the PIN-based access path. Keep the device secured until the estate is fully settled.

The Recommended Heir Action Sequence

If your heir finds a hardware wallet and doesn't know exactly what to do, this is the sequence to follow:

  1. Secure the device immediately — do not attempt access yet
    Place the hardware wallet, any seed phrase backups, and any associated paper documents in a secure, locked location. Do not plug anything in. Do not enter any PINs. Do not attempt any transactions.
  2. Find the Letter of Instruction
    Your Letter of Instruction is the roadmap for everything that follows. It should tell the heir which wallet brand and model it is, where the seed phrase backup is, whether a passphrase is in use, and who to contact for guidance. Without it, proceeding is dangerous.
  3. Engage a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney and, if needed, a Bitcoin custody consultant
    Before touching the device, heirs should contact an attorney familiar with digital assets. If technical recovery assistance is needed, hire a Bitcoin custody consultant through the attorney — not someone found online claiming to specialize in "crypto recovery."
  4. Purchase and set up your own new hardware wallet before restoring the inheritance wallet
    The heir should be comfortable with how a hardware wallet works before attempting to restore the inheritance. Buy a new device, initialize it, and back up the seed phrase. This builds familiarity with the process before any high-stakes recovery is attempted.
  5. Restore the inheritance wallet to your new device and confirm the balance
    Use the seed phrase (and passphrase if applicable) to restore the inherited wallet to the new device. Confirm the balance matches expectations. If the balance is zero and a passphrase might be in use, consult the Letter of Instruction and attorney before proceeding.
  6. Transfer to your own wallet using a small test transaction first
    Once you've confirmed access and the balance, transfer a small test amount to your own wallet first. Confirm it arrives. Then proceed with transferring the remaining balance. This is standard practice for any Bitcoin transaction involving large sums.
The Bottom Line

The single most important thing you can do for your heirs today is write a clear, complete Letter of Instruction that tells them which hardware wallet you use, where the seed phrase backup is stored, whether a passphrase is in use and where it is, and who to call for professional guidance. Everything else follows from that document.
