State Guide: Texas

Bitcoin Estate Planning in Texas: No State Taxes, Community Property, and Probate Alternatives

February 25, 2026 Hal Franklin 12 min read

Bitcoin family office in Texas has quietly become the most important state in the United States for Bitcoin holders. With no state estate tax, no state inheritance tax, no state income tax, and a regulatory posture that actively welcomes digital assets, the Lone Star State offers a financial environment that is nearly ideal for building, protecting, and passing on Bitcoin wealth. If you hold significant Bitcoin in Texas — or are considering relocating here — understanding how Texas law interacts with your estate plan is not optional. It is essential.

This guide covers everything a Texas Bitcoin holder needs to know: the state's extraordinary tax advantages, how community property rules affect Bitcoin ownership between spouses, Texas's uniquely streamlined probate options, trust planning strategies, and specific considerations for the growing cohort of Bitcoin miners who have chosen Texas for operations. We will also explain where Texas falls short — asset protection trusts — and what you should do instead.

Texas Tax Environment: Federal-Only Exposure

Let us start with the most important fact: Texas imposes no state estate tax, no state inheritance tax, and no state income tax. For Bitcoin holders, this is transformative.

Many of the wealthiest states — Massachusetts, Oregon, Washington, Maryland, Illinois — impose their own state-level estate taxes, sometimes beginning at estates as small as $1 million, with rates reaching 16–20%. New York stacks its own estate tax on top of federal exposure. California taxes capital gains on appreciated Bitcoin at up to 13.3% at the state level, on top of federal rates. Texas has none of this.

As a Texas resident, your estate planning exposure is limited entirely to federal estate tax, which currently applies at 40% on estates exceeding the federal exemption (approximaterially $13.99 million per individual as of 2026 under current law). Estate planning in Texas therefore focuses exclusively on federal mitigation strategies — GRATs, irrevocable trusts, charitable vehicles, annual gifting — rather than fighting a two-front war against both federal and state taxes simultaneously.

For Bitcoin holders with rapidly appreciating assets, this distinction is enormous. A Bitcoin position worth $5 million today could be worth $50 million within a decade. In California, that appreciation would carry both federal estate tax exposure and state-level capital gains on disposition. In Texas, the exposure is federal only — and with proper trust structuring, often significantly reduced.

Texas Tax Advantage: The Math

A Texas Bitcoin holder with a $20M estate faces federal estate tax exposure only. The same holder in Massachusetts would face Massachusetts estate tax of approximaterially $1.5M+ on top of federal exposure. Proper domicile in Texas is worth hundreds of thousands — sometimes millions — over a planning horizon.

Community Property and Bitcoin: What Texas Spouses Must Know

Texas is one of nine community property states in the United States, and this classification has profound implications for Bitcoin holders who are married. Understanding community property is not merely a legal nicety — it fundamentally affects who owns your Bitcoin, what happens to it when one spouse dies, and how it flows through your estate.

The Basic Community Property Rule

Under Texas law, property acquired during marriage using marital funds is presumed to be community property. This means that Bitcoin purchased with income earned during the marriage — regardless of which spouse made the purchase or whose name is on the wallet — is generally owned 50/50 by both spouses. Each spouse owns an undivided one-half interest in the community estate.

There are important exceptions. Bitcoin acquired before marriage remains the separate property of the acquiring spouse. Bitcoin received as a gift or inheritance (addressed to one spouse specifically) is also separate property. And if you can trace Bitcoin back to separate property funds — say, pre-marital savings used to buy Bitcoin — it retains its separate character, though the burden of proof is on you to demonstrate that tracing clearly.

When one spouse dies in Texas, the outcome depends on property classification:

  • The surviving spouse already owns their 50% community share outright — it does not pass through the deceased spouse's estate at all
  • The deceased spouse's 50% of community property passes through their estate, either by will or intestate succession if there is no will
  • Separate property of the deceased spouse passes through their estate entirely

This means that if you and your spouse jointly hold 10 Bitcoin as community property, and one of you dies, the surviving spouse automatically retains 5 BTC outright. The decedent's 5 BTC passes through the estate — subject to the will, probate, and potentially federal estate tax.

The Community Property Survivorship Agreement: Texas's Hidden Gem

Texas law provides a powerful planning tool that most married Bitcoin holders have never heard of: the Community Property Survivorship Agreement (CPSA). Under the Texas Estates Code, spouses can enter into a written agreement providing that on the death of either spouse, their share of community property passes automatically to the surviving spouse — without probate.

Think of this as the community property equivalent of joint tenancy with right of survivorship. If you and your spouse properly execute a CPSA covering your Bitcoin (including specific wallet or custody account designations, ideally), the entire community Bitcoin holding passes automatically to the survivor upon the first death. There is no probate. No court involvement. No waiting period.

For Bitcoin holders, the CPSA is an extraordinarily efficient tool. Combined with proper key management and a digital asset memorandum guiding the survivor on how to access the Bitcoin, a CPSA can ensure seamless transfer of community Bitcoin without the delays, costs, or public exposure of probate proceedings.

Key Action Item for Married Texas Bitcoin Holders

Discuss a Community Property Survivorship Agreement with your estate planning attorney. When combined with a clear digital asset access protocol, this can ensure your Bitcoin passes to your spouse instantly at death — entirely outside of probate. This is one of the most underutilized tools in Texas estate planning.

Texas Probate: Already Simpler Than Most States

Even if you do not use a survivorship agreement or a trust, Texas probate is substantially less burdensome than in most states. Texas has deliberately designed its probate system to minimize court involvement, lower costs, and speed up asset transfer. This matters for Bitcoin holders whose assets may be time-sensitive (particularly during market volatility) or who want to avoid the expense and delay of traditional probate.

Muniment of Title: Texas's Unique Shortcut

Texas offers a probate procedure called Muniment of Title that is unavailable in most states. If the decedent had a valid will and the estate has no debts (other than liens on real property, like a mortgage), heirs can present the will directly to the court and receive a court order confirming their title to estate assets — without opening a full estate administration.

The Muniment of Title process is faster, cheaper, and far simpler than traditional probate. For Bitcoin holders, this is significant: if the decedent's estate is will-based, debt-free, and relatively straightforward, heirs can potentially use this procedure to their right to the Bitcoin quickly, without the prolonged court administration that might apply in other states.

Note that Muniment of Title has limitations — it does not appoint a personal representative, so it may not be appropriate for complex estates or situations requiring active administration. But for a Bitcoin holder with a clean estate, a valid will, and no significant debts, it is an option worth understanding.

Independent Administration: Minimal Court Supervision

Texas also allows — and actively encourages — Independent Administration of estates. In most states, the executor (called a personal representative) must obtain court approval before selling assets, paying debts, or distributing property. This court supervision adds time and cost at every step.

Under Texas Independent Administration, the executor can act largely without court permission. Once appointed, they can sell, transfer, and distribute estate assets — including Bitcoin — without seeking court approval for each transaction. This makes Texas probate far more efficient than states that require ongoing judicial oversight.

For Bitcoin holders whose executors may need to move quickly (whether to transfer Bitcoin to heirs, liquidate to pay taxes, or interact with custodians), the flexibility of Independent Administration is a meaningful advantage.

Trust Planning in Texas: Still the Gold Standard

Despite Texas's relatively efficient probate system, a revocable living trust remains the preferred vehicle for most Bitcoin holders above a certain wealth Bitcoin family office minimum requirements. Here is why:

  • Privacy: Probate is a public process. A trust administration is private. For Bitcoin holders who prefer not to publicize their holdings, a trust is essential.
  • Continuity during incapacity: If you become incapacitated, a successor trustee can manage your Bitcoin immediately — no conservatorship, no court involvement.
  • Multi-state property: If you own real property in other states alongside Bitcoin, a trust avoids multiple probate proceedings in multiple jurisdictions.
  • Flexibility: A well-drafted trust can include detailed provisions for digital asset access, key management, and distribution instructions tailored to Bitcoin specifically.

The Texas Trust Code (Title 9, Property Code) is comprehensive and regularly updated. Texas recognizes directed trusts — where the trustee is separated from investment decision-making — which is particularly useful when you want a professional trustee for administration but wish to retain a Bitcoin-knowledgeable advisor directing investment decisions. Texas also recognizes purpose trusts and other specialized structures.

Texas Perpetuities: 300 Years

For Bitcoin holders who want to create a dynasty trust — a long-lasting trust designed to benefit multiple generations while minimizing estate tax exposure at each generation — Texas law is accommodating. For trusts created after September 1, 2021, Texas allows a trust to exist for up to 300 years before the rule against perpetuities terminates it.

A 300-year trust duration is sufficient for most dynasty planning purposes. Bitcoin placed in trust today could potentially benefit your great-great-grandchildren without incurring estate tax at each generation's death, thanks to the generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax exemption. Paired with Texas's zero state taxes, a Texas dynasty trust is a compelling planning vehicle.

RUFADAA: Texas Protects Digital Asset Access

Texas adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) as Chappropriater 2001 of the Texas Estates Code. Under this law, fiduciaries (executors, trustees, agents under power of attorney) have legal authority to access digital assets — including Bitcoin wallets, exchange accounts, and private keys — subject to the decedent's or grantor's prior directions.

For Bitcoin holders, this means your executor or trustee has a legal framework to access your Bitcoin after death or incapacity. This is why a carefully maintained digital asset memorandum — referencing wallet locations, seed phrases (or instructions for accessing them securely), exchange accounts, and hardware wallet locations — is so critical. RUFADAA provides the legal authority; your documentation provides the practical roadmap.

Asset Protection: What Texas Cannot Offer (and Bitcoin family office in Wyoming Can)

Texas has strong Bitcoin family office in Florida protection — the Texas Constitution exempts a homestead from forced sale by creditors, which is one of the most powerful individual asset protection tools in the US. However, when it comes to Domestic Asset Protection Trusts (DAPTs) — self-settled trusts where the grantor is also a discretionary beneficiary — Texas does not currently have enabling legislation.

If asset protection for your Bitcoin is a priority, you will need to look to a state like Wyoming or Nevada to establish your DAPT. Wyoming in particular has become the gold standard for Bitcoin holders: strong DAPT statutes, favorable LLC laws, no state taxes, and a legislature that has passed more Bitcoin-friendly legislation than any other state.

You can live in Texas and hold Bitcoin in a Wyoming DAPT. Your attorney will establish the Wyoming trust, appoint a Wyoming trustee or trust company, and transfer your Bitcoin into the trust. Texas courts will generally respect the Wyoming trust's asset protection provisions, though you should work with experienced counsel to structure this properly.

Texas + Wyoming: The Optimal Combination

Many sophisticated Texas Bitcoin holders use a two-state strategy: Texas domicile for zero state income and estate taxes, Wyoming trust for asset protection and dynasty planning. The two states complement each other exceptionally well.

Texas Bitcoin Communities: The Bitcoin Capital of America

Texas has arguably become the most Bitcoin-friendly state in the United States, and the concentration of Bitcoin-focused individuals, companies, and infrastructure here is unmatched outside of a few global cities.

Austin has emerged as the spiritual center of American Bitcoin culture. Bitcoin Magazine relocated to Austin. Swan Bitcoin, River Financial, and dozens of Bitcoin-native companies have established offices or moved headquarters to the Austin area. The Texas capital hosts major Bitcoin conferences and has attracted thousands of Bitcoin entrepreneurs, investors, and miners who relocated from higher-tax states. The Bitcoin culture in Austin is not a novelty — it is a serious, well-capitalized community that has materially shaped the industry.

Dallas and Houston are not far behind. Houston's energy industry has substantial overlap with Bitcoin mining, and the Dallas financial sector has seen significant Bitcoin adoption among family offices and wealth management firms. The Texas Legislature has repeatedly demonstrated legislative friendliness to digital assets, with bills addressing Bitcoin mining, digital asset custody, and property rights passed in recent sessions.

Bitcoin Mining in Texas: Estate Planning for Miners

West Texas has become one of the premier Bitcoin mining locations in the world. The combination of cheap ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas) wind and solar power, vast open land, a deregulated electricity market, and regulatory friendliness has attracted institutional miners ranging from publicly traded companies to family-owned operations. If you are a Bitcoin miner in Texas, your estate planning needs differ from a simple holder — and they require specific attention.

Business Structure for Texas Mining Operations

Bitcoin mining operations should almost universally be held in a legal entity — typically an LLC or C-corporation — rather than operated personally. This provides liability protection, separates business and personal assets, allows for a clear ownership structure that can be transferred at death, and enables proper tax planning (including bonus depreciation on mining equipment).

For estate planning purposes, the mining LLC or corporation itself becomes the estate planning asset. Rather than transferring individual machines or Bitcoin wallets at death, you transfer membership interests or shares in the entity — a far more manageable process. Interests in closely held mining businesses can also qualify for valuation discounts (minority and marketability discounts) that reduce the taxable estate value, subject to applicable tax rules.

Mining Tax Strategy and Depreciation

Texas Bitcoin miners have access to powerful federal tax strategies that can also serve estate planning purposes. Bonus depreciation on mining equipment — currently being phased back in under recent legislation — allows miners to deduct a substantial portion of equipment costs in the year of purchase. This reduces ordinary income, which in turn reduces the taxable estate over time.

⚡ Bitcoin Mining Tax Strategy for Texas Miners

Bitcoin mining operations offer some of the most powerful tax deductions available to high-income earners: bonus depreciation, equipment deductions, and operating expense write-offs that can dramatically reduce taxable income. For Texas miners whose estates could otherwise face significant federal estate tax exposure, mining's tax efficiency compounds over time.

ERCOT and Energy Contract Planning

Texas miners who have secured favorable ERCOT energy contracts or demand-response agreements hold assets with significant value beyond the mining equipment itself. These contracts should be carefully reviewed in the context of estate planning — are they assignable? Do they survive the death of an individual owner? If the mining operation is held in an LLC, contract continuity is generally preserved, which is another reason proper entity structure is essential.

Institutional miners — those with 1 MW or more of operational capacity — often have additional considerations, including financing arrangements, hosting contracts, and co-location agreements that all require coordination with estate planning counsel to ensure continuity across generational transitions.

Transfer on Death and Other Non-Probate Tools

Texas law offers several non-probate transfer mechanisms worth mentioning. While the Texas Transfer on Death Deed applies to real property rather than Bitcoin, its availability reflects the state's general attitude: Texas actively supports mechanisms that allow assets to transfer outside of probate, efficiently and without court involvement.

For Bitcoin specifically, the primary non-probate tools available in Texas are:

  • Community Property Survivorship Agreements (covered above) — for married couples
  • Revocable living trusts — for both individuals and married couples; assets held in trust do not go through probate
  • Beneficiary designations on exchange accounts — many custodians (Coinbase, Kraken, River) now accept TOD/beneficiary designations that function outside of probate; however, self-custodied Bitcoin requires trust or other planning
  • Joint ownership with right of survivorship — possible for some accounts; requires careful attention to tax consequences (no step-up basis on jointly owned property)

Self-custodied Bitcoin — Bitcoin held in hardware wallets, multisig setups, or cold storage — cannot designate a beneficiary the way an exchange account can. Estate planning for self-custodied Bitcoin requires either a trust that clearly owns the Bitcoin (with trustee access provisions), or an extremely well-documented set of instructions for heirs. RUFADAA provides legal authority for fiduciaries, but it cannot help if the keys are lost or inaccessible.

Putting It All Together: A Texas Bitcoin Estate Planning Checklist

  • ✓ Confirm Texas domicile is properly established (driver's license, voter registration, homestead filing)
  • ✓ Identify all Bitcoin as separate or community property
  • ✓ If married: consider a Community Property Survivorship Agreement for joint Bitcoin holdings
  • ✓ Establish a revocable living trust and fund it with Bitcoin holdings (or designate trust as beneficiary of exchange accounts)
  • ✓ Maintain a current digital asset memorandum (updated each time key management changes)
  • ✓ If asset protection is needed: establish Wyoming DAPT in addition to Texas revocable trust
  • ✓ For miners: ensure mining operations are held in a properly structured LLC or corporation
  • ✓ Review federal estate tax exposure annually as Bitcoin value changes
  • ✓ Consider dynasty trust provisions if estate may exceed federal exemption
  • ✓ Verify all fiduciaries (executors, trustees) understand Bitcoin and have access protocols

Summary: Texas Is Exceptional — Plan Accordingly

Texas is, by virtually every measure, one of the two or three best states in the United States for Bitcoin holders to reside. The complete absence of state-level estate, inheritance, and income taxes eliminates one of the two primary tax threats to Bitcoin complete guide to Bitcoin wealth transfer. The community property system, when properly utilized — particularly through survivorship agreements — provides efficient marital transfer without probate. And Texas's already-streamlined probate system means that even for those without trusts, estate administration is less burdensome than in most states.

At the same time, Texas is not a planning-free environment. Federal estate tax remains a very real threat for Bitcoin holders whose holdings are appreciating rapidly. Community property classification requires active management — married couples should document what is separate and what is community property, particularly if they hold Bitcoin from before marriage alongside Bitcoin acquired after. And for asset protection, Texas residents must look to Wyoming or Nevada.

The Bitcoin communities that have grown up in Austin, Dallas, and Houston represent a concentration of Bitcoin-native wealth that is unprecedented in American history. That wealth deserves estate planning that matches its sophistication. Texas law provides an extraordinary foundation — build on it thoughtfully.
