Bitcoin Estate Planning in Texas

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Texas has quietly become one of the most attractive jurisdictions in the United States for Bitcoin families building multigenerational wealth. The combination of no state income tax, no state estate tax, strong community property protections, and a modernized trust code creates a legal environment where Bitcoin can be held, grown, and transferred with significantly less friction than most states. For high-net-worth families already domiciled in Texas — or considering a change of domicile — the structural advantages deserve careful analysis.

No State Income Tax: The Bitcoin Compounding Advantage

Texas imposes no personal income tax, and its constitution prohibits one absent a voter referendum. This is not a minor benefit for Bitcoin holders — it is a compounding structural advantage. In states with high income taxes (California at 13.3%, New York at up to 10.9%), every realized gain from portfolio rebalancing, trust distributions, or entity-level dispositions carries a significant state tax cost. In Texas, that cost is zero.

For a Bitcoin family managing a concentrated position over decades, the absence of state capital gains tax creates meaningful optionality. Rebalancing into cold storage structures, moving Bitcoin between legal entities for estate planning purposes, or triggering gain to fund a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust — these transactions carry only federal tax consequences in Texas, not a layered state tax obligation on top.

Texas also imposes no inheritance tax and no state-level estate tax. The full federal estate tax exemption (currently $13.99 million per individual in 2025) is the binding constraint for most families — there is no additional Texas exemption threshold to navigate, no Texas "cliff effect" to avoid, and no state filing requirement at death separate from the federal return.

Texas Trust Law: The Uniform Trust Code Framework

Texas adopted the Uniform Trust Code in 2005 through the Texas Trust Code (Texas Property Code, Title 9, Chapter 112). The framework has been modernized through subsequent amendments and provides a solid statutory foundation for trust formation, administration, and modification. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families include:

  • Directed trust authority: Texas Property Code §114.0031 allows trust instruments to divide trustee duties — separating investment direction from administrative responsibilities. This is critical for Bitcoin trusts where a family member or specialist may direct custody and investment decisions while a corporate trustee handles compliance and distributions.
  • Trust decanting: Texas Property Code §112.072 permits trustees to "pour" trust assets from one trust to an updated trust with more favorable terms, without requiring court involvement or beneficiary consent in many circumstances. For Bitcoin trusts established years ago with outdated custody provisions, decanting offers a path to modernize without triggering a taxable event.
  • Virtual representation: Texas's Uniform Trust Code provisions allow living beneficiaries to represent interests of unborn or minor beneficiaries in trust modifications, simplifying the process of updating trust documents as family circumstances evolve.
  • Digital asset recognition: Texas Business & Commerce Code Chapter 20A (the Texas Uniform Electronic Transactions Act) and subsequent amendments recognize digital assets in commercial transactions. Texas HB 4474 (2021) explicitly recognized Bitcoin and digital assets in trust contexts, clarifying that trustees may hold digital assets and that custody arrangements for digital assets satisfy the prudent investor standard when properly structured.
Texas's constitutional prohibition on a state income tax is not a policy that can be reversed by a simple legislative vote. For Bitcoin families, that durability matters as much as the rate itself.

Community Property Considerations for Bitcoin

Texas is one of nine community property states, and the rules have significant implications for Bitcoin held by married couples. Under Texas Family Code §3.002, property acquired during marriage is presumed to be community property belonging equally to both spouses, regardless of which spouse's income purchased it. Bitcoin acquired with community funds during marriage is community property.

This creates both planning challenges and opportunities. On the challenge side: Bitcoin purchased with marital income is equally owned by both spouses, even if held in only one spouse's name or only one spouse's wallet. Estate planning documents must account for this — a revocable trust that doesn't address the community property character of Bitcoin may create ambiguity about what the trust actually holds.

On the opportunity side: Texas community property receives a full step-up in cost basis for both halves of the community estate at the first spouse's death (IRC §1014(b)(6)). This is a significant advantage over common law property states, where only the decedent's half receives the step-up. For a couple holding appreciated Bitcoin as community property, the surviving spouse inherits the entire position at a stepped-up basis, eliminating potentially decades of unrealized capital gains. The federal income tax savings can be substantial.

Married Bitcoin holders in Texas should consider a Community Property Agreement (Texas Family Code §4.002) to confirm the community property character of their Bitcoin holdings, or alternatively, a Partition and Exchange Agreement (§4.102) to convert community property to separate property where the full step-up advantage is less important than separate control and disposition.

Texas LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Texas LLCs (governed by the Texas Business Organizations Code, Title 3) provide an effective vehicle for holding Bitcoin as a family wealth structure. Key advantages include:

Charging order protection: Texas Business Organizations Code §101.112 provides that a judgment creditor of an LLC member's personal debt is limited to a charging order against distributions — they cannot seize the LLC interest or force a dissolution sale. For Bitcoin families concerned about liability exposure, this creates a meaningful barrier between personal creditors and Bitcoin held in the LLC.

Valuation discounts: Bitcoin held in a properly structured family LLC can qualify for minority interest discounts (typically 20-35%) and lack-of-control discounts when interests are transferred to heirs or trusts. This reduces the taxable value of gifts without reducing the economic benefit received by heirs — one of the most efficient estate planning tools available for concentrated Bitcoin positions.

Operational flexibility: An LLC operating agreement can specify exactly who has authority to sign Bitcoin transactions, how multi-signature custody is structured, and what approval thresholds apply to different types of transactions. The LLC becomes the governance framework for Bitcoin custody, not just a legal entity holding it.

Why Texas May Be Sufficient — and Where Wyoming Still Leads

Texas offers strong tax and legal foundations for Bitcoin estate planning, but families focused on the most sophisticated trust structures should understand where Wyoming still holds advantages. Wyoming's Directed Trust Act (Wyoming Statutes §4-10-710 et seq.) provides more explicit statutory authority for separating trustee roles in digital asset contexts. Wyoming's Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI) charter creates a purpose-built banking framework for Bitcoin custody. And Wyoming's DAO LLC statute (Wyoming Statutes §17-31-101 et seq.) offers governance structures with no clear Texas equivalent.

For families domiciled in Texas but seeking the most advanced trust situs, a Wyoming-sited trust administered by a Wyoming directed trustee — with the family continuing to live in Texas — captures the benefits of both: Texas domicile tax advantages and Wyoming's Bitcoin-specific legal infrastructure. Our detailed analysis of Wyoming trusts and LLCs for Bitcoin covers how this structure works in practice.

For families who prefer a single-jurisdiction approach, Texas provides an excellent foundation. The combination of no income or estate tax, strong community property rules, directed trust authority, and robust LLC protections covers the essential needs of most Bitcoin estate plans. The gap between Texas and Wyoming is narrowing as Texas continues to modernize its digital asset laws — and may continue to narrow in future legislative sessions.

Practical Steps for Texas Bitcoin Families

For Texas families building or updating a Bitcoin estate plan, the priorities are:

  1. Establish or update a revocable living trust that explicitly addresses Bitcoin custody authority, successor trustee technical requirements, and the interplay between community property character and trust ownership.
  2. Document the community property character of Bitcoin holdings — or make a deliberate election to partition — with proper agreements under Texas Family Code Chapter 4.
  3. Consider a Texas LLC for operational holding of Bitcoin, with an operating agreement that specifies multi-signature custody governance and transfer restrictions appropriate to your family's structure.
  4. Evaluate trust situs: for large positions, a Wyoming-sited directed trust alongside Texas domicile may offer the optimal combination of tax environment and legal infrastructure.
  5. Coordinate with a Bitcoin-specific custodian who can implement the multi-signature architecture that your legal structures require — legal documents without technical custody infrastructure are not an estate plan.

Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
