Texas Has No State Estate Tax

Texas imposes no state estate tax and no state inheritance tax. Like Bitcoin family office in Florida, Texas's estate tax was tied to the now-defunct federal state death tax credit (repealed federally in 2005), and Texas has not enacted a standalone estate tax to replace it. For Texas residents, the only estate tax exposure is the federal estate tax — applicable above the $13.99 million per-person exemption in 2025.

This means most Texas Bitcoin families owe zero estate tax at death. Even for those approaching the federal Bitcoin family office minimum requirements, the absence of a state layer simplifies planning considerably. Texas's favorable tax treatment extends to capital gains: Texas has no state income tax, which means no state-level capital gains tax on Bitcoin sales during life or on trust income distributions.

Texas Homestead Exemption

Texas provides one of the nation's strongest homestead protections under Article XVI, Section 50 of the Texas Constitution — unlimited in value for rural homesteads up to 200 acres, and up to 10 acres for urban homesteads. While this doesn't directly affect Bitcoin (which is personal property, not real estate), it illustrates Texas's strong asset protection culture and has indirect estate planning implications: families with a primary residence in Texas can hold substantial real estate wealth outside of protective Bitcoin Trust Type Selector tools without the same creditor risk they might face in other states.

For Bitcoin specifically, Texas law does not provide specific Bitcoin-related creditor protections equivalent to real estate's homestead exemption. Bitcoin in self-custody or exchange accounts is treated as general personal property for creditor purposes. Families seeking asset protection for Bitcoin in Texas typically use LLCs or irrevocable trust structures.

Community Property: The Critical Bitcoin Issue for Married Couples

Texas is a community property state — one of nine in the U.S. Under Texas Family Code §3.002, all property acquired by either spouse during the marriage is presumed to be community property, owned 50/50 by both spouses. Separate property (owned before marriage, or received as a gift or inheritance during marriage) retains its separate character if properly documented.

For Bitcoin holders, this has significant implications:

  • Bitcoin purchased during marriage with marital funds is community property. Even if only one spouse's name is on the exchange account or hardware wallet, the other spouse owns a 50% community interest. This affects what each spouse can do with the Bitcoin, how it is treated in the estate, and what happens at divorce.
  • Bitcoin purchased before marriage is separate property. But if separate-property Bitcoin is commingled with community-property funds, or if the tracing is not maintained, it can lose its separate character.
  • Community property Bitcoin receives a double step-up at the first spouse's death. In Texas (as in other community property states), when the first spouse dies, 100% of community property — including the surviving spouse's half — receives a step-up in basis to the date-of-death fair market value. This is a significant tax advantage over common law states, where only the decedent's half steps up.
In Texas, married couples hold Bitcoin as community property — meaning both spouses own it equally. This creates planning opportunities and potential complications that require deliberate structuring.

Texas Trust Code and Digital Asset Provisions

Texas adopted a version of the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) in 2017, codified at Texas Estates Code §§2001.001 et seq. This law gives fiduciaries — including successor trustees — legal authority to access and manage digital assets when explicitly authorized by the governing trust document or will.

The Texas Trust Code (Texas Property Code Chapter 112) governs trust formation and administration. Texas permits directed trusts, spendthrift trusts, and dynasty trusts (though Texas law limits perpetuities to 300 years, shorter than Bitcoin family office in Wyoming or South Dakota's perpetual trust provisions). For Bitcoin families with multigenerational planning horizons, the 300-year limitation is a consideration — some families how to set up a Bitcoin family office the operational trust in Wyoming while retaining Texas domicile.

Texas also adopted the Texas Uniform Electronic Wills Act in 2021, permitting electronic wills — a modernization relevant to families managing digital assets in digital environments.

What to Look for in a Texas Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney

Not all estate attorneys understand Bitcoin. In Texas — where the volume of Bitcoin wealth is large and the legal community is still catching up — finding the right attorney requires specific due diligence. Look for:

  • Demonstrated familiarity with digital asset fiduciary law. The attorney should know Texas's RUFADAA implementation, be able to draft express digital asset authority provisions, and understand why standard trust templates are inadequate for Bitcoin holders.
  • Understanding of custody mechanics. An attorney who doesn't understand the difference between self-custody and exchange custody — and the different legal treatment each requires — is not equipped to draft trust documents that work in practice.
  • Community property expertise. Texas community property rules are complex, and their intersection with Bitcoin planning (basis tracking, asset characterization, divorce exposure, step-up optimization) requires an attorney who handles community property regularly, not just theoretically.
  • Willingness to coordinate with a Bitcoin custody specialist. The best Bitcoin estate attorneys recognize that custody documentation and operational planning require expertise outside traditional law. They work with custody advisors rather than pretending to cover that ground themselves.
  • Experience with multi-jurisdiction planning. Many Texas Bitcoin families benefit from trust situs in Wyoming or South Dakota. An attorney who can navigate multi-state trust structures — and understand when a Wyoming trust makes sense for a Texas domiciliary — is more sophisticated than one operating entirely within Texas law.

Common Mistakes Texas Bitcoin Holders Make

  • Failing to document community property vs. separate property Bitcoin. Without clear records showing which Bitcoin was purchased before marriage (separate) vs. during marriage (community), disputes at death or divorce become difficult to resolve. Maintain timestamped purchase records and keep separate property Bitcoin in separate accounts.
  • Using a generic will or trust that ignores digital assets. Standard Texas will and trust templates drafted without Bitcoin provisions leave heirs without clear legal authority to access wallets. Texas's RUFADAA requires express authorization in the governing document.
  • Listing seed phrases in estate documents. Writing seed phrases in a will, trust, or any document that may enter probate or be seen by multiple parties is a security catastrophe. This mistake is unfortunately common among holders who DIY their estate planning.
  • Not planning for the community property step-up. Many Texas couples fail to structure their Bitcoin to take full advantage of the community property double step-up at the first spouse's death — one of the most valuable and underutilized tax benefits available to Texas Bitcoin families.

Key Planning Strategies for Texas Bitcoin Holders

Texas's favorable tax environment and community property rules create specific planning opportunities:

  • Use a revocable living trust with explicit digital asset and RUFADAA provisions to avoid Texas probate and maintain clear succession for Bitcoin holdings.
  • Document asset characterization carefully from the start — separate property vs. community property — and maintain that separation consistently in custody accounts.
  • Coordinate the community property double step-up with the overall estate plan to minimize capital gains tax at death.
  • For estates approaching the federal exemption, explore irrevocable structures — potentially sited in Wyoming — that remove Bitcoin from the taxable estate while preserving family access and control.
  • Establish a Letter of Instruction — separate from the trust document — containing the operational custody details that successors will need without creating security exposure in legal records.
