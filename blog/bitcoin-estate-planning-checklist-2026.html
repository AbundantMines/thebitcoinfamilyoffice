Bitcoin Estate Planning Checklist 2026: What to Do This Year

TCJA sunset uncertainty. Bitcoin near $95,000. State estate taxes already in effect. 2026 is not a year to defer your Bitcoin estate plan — it is a year to execute it.

By Hal Franklin
February 25, 2026
~10 min read

There is never a perfect time to review your estate plan. But 2026 is close. The convergence of federal tax uncertainty, Bitcoin trading near all-time highs, and active state estate taxes in more than a dozen states means that Bitcoin families who defer planning this year may be paying for that inaction for decades.

This is a narrative checklist — not an interactive tool (that exists separately at /tools/bitcoin-letter-of-instruction and /tools/bitcoin-wealth-dashboard). Think of this as your advisor-style briefing on what should happen in your Bitcoin estate plan before the end of 2026, organized by the quarter in which each item is most urgent.

Why 2026 Is a Critical Year for Bitcoin Estate Planning

Three forces are colliding in 2026 that make it unlike any prior planning year for Bitcoin holders.

1. TCJA Sunset Uncertainty

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 roughly doubled the federal lifetime gift and estate tax exemption. Without Congressional action, many of those provisions were set to expire at the end of 2025 and revert to pre-2018 levels. The political situation remains fluid as of early 2026 — further extensions, modifications, or expirations all remain possible depending on legislation.

What this means: if you have not yet used significant lifetime exemption to transfer Bitcoin out of your estate, the window of opportunity may be narrow. Every month of inaction carries a real cost — not just in potential estate tax, but in the compounding appreciation that occurs inside your estate instead of outside it.

2. Bitcoin at $95,000+

Bitcoin near $95,000 is both good news and a planning trigger. Good news: your Bitcoin is worth far more than you likely paid. Planning trigger: you may now be in an estate tax exposure tier you were not in two years ago. A family with 5 BTC at $10,000/BTC had a $50,000 Bitcoin position. That same family today has a $475,000 position. At 10 BTC, the number is $950,000. At 20 BTC, $1.9 million — well within state estate tax territory for residents of Massachusetts, Oregon, or Washington.

Your estate plan from 2022 or 2023 does not reflect your current exposure. That is the single most common problem we see.

3. State Estate Taxes Are Already in Effect

The federal estate tax gets most of the attention, but 12 states and the District of Columbia impose their own estate taxes — some with exemption Bitcoin family office minimum requirementss as low as $1 million. If you hold significant Bitcoin and live in one of these states, you may already be exposed to estate tax risk regardless of what happens federally:

  • Massachusetts & Oregon: $1 million exemption, not indexed for inflation
  • Washington: $2.193 million exemption (2024)
  • Illinois: $4 million exemption, not indexed
  • Minnesota: $3 million exemption
  • Rhode Island: $1.774 million, partially indexed
  • Hawaii & Maine: Higher thresholds, indexed for inflation

State estate planning is not optional for Bitcoin holders in high-tax states. It requires its own analysis, separate from federal planning.

Q1 2026 January – March: Get Your Foundation Right

The first quarter of the year is the highest-leverage window for estate planning reviews. Tax season forces attention on financial records, and the beginning of a new year is the natural reset point for recurring strategies like annual gifting. These five tasks should be completed or initiated before April.

  • 1
    Update or Create Your Letter of Instruction
    The Letter of Instruction (LOI) is the single most important document most Bitcoin holders don't have — or haven't updated in years. This is the document your executor, successor trustee, or surviving spouse will use to locate and access your Bitcoin after your death. It should include: every wallet or exchange where you hold Bitcoin, the custody structure for each (hardware wallet, multisig, custodial), seedphrase access instructions (by reference to secure storage — not in the LOI itself), and which trusted person holds each component.

    If your custody structure changed at any point in 2024 or 2025 — new hardware wallet, switched exchanges, moved to multisig, added a collaborative custody arrangement — your LOI is out of date. Outdated LOIs are not a minor compliance issue. They are a risk that your family never accesses your Bitcoin at all.
  • 2
    Run an Estate Tax Exposure Calculation at Current Bitcoin Price
    Pull your total Bitcoin holdings and apply the current price. Add other significant assets — real estate, retirement accounts, investment accounts. Compare the total to the relevant estate tax thresholds: federal and your state. If you are above the state threshold or within 20% of it, you are in active planning territory and should schedule an attorney review before mid-year. Use our Bitcoin estate tax calculator to run a quick estimate.
  • 3
    Review Beneficiary Designations on Exchange Accounts
    Major exchanges and custodians — Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, River, and others — now support beneficiary designations on custodial accounts. These designations are not automatically updated when your will changes, when you remarry, or when family circumstances change. A beneficiary designation on file overrides your will. If your ex-spouse is still listed, your Bitcoin goes to them — regardless of what your estate documents say. Verify every custodial account in Q1.
  • 4
    Confirm Your Successor Trustee or Executor Is Still the Right Choice
    People's circumstances change. The friend you named as executor five years ago may have moved abroad, developed health problems, or simply become less reliable. More specifically for Bitcoin: your chosen executor needs to be Bitcoin-literate — comfortable enough with digital assets to follow LOI instructions, interact with a hardware wallet, and coordinate with a custodian or attorney without losing the asset. Do a brief check-in: is your named executor still able and willing? Do they know they're named? Have they ever practiced the steps they'd need to take?
  • 5
    Check Whether Your State's Estate Tax Threshold Has Changed
    Some state estate tax exemptions are indexed to inflation and update annually. Others are fixed by statute and haven't moved in years, meaning inflation gradually pulls more estates into taxable territory. Hawaii and Maine adjust their thresholds periodically. Oregon, Massachusetts, Washington, Illinois, Minnesota, and Rhode Island do not — or adjust minimally. If you are near the threshold in a non-indexed state, run the numbers with current Bitcoin price. You may have crossed into a new planning tier since your last review.
Q2 2026 April – June: Deepen and Execute

With Q1 foundations in place, mid-year is the time for substantive planning work — meeting with advisors, updating formal documents, and stress-testing your current plan against a higher Bitcoin price and changing circumstances.

  • 1
    Annual Review Meeting With a Bitcoin-Literate Estate Attorney
    This is the non-negotiable item for 2026. Not all estate attorneys understand Bitcoin. A general practitioner may be excellent at trusts and wills but unfamiliar with UTXO basis tracking, multisig custody, or the mechanics of titling Bitcoin inside an irrevocable trust. Seek advisors who have worked with Bitcoin-holding clients before. At this meeting: review your exposure calculation from Q1, discuss whether lifetime exemption gifts are appropriate given current price and TCJA uncertainty, review your trust structures if any, and update any documents that have not been reviewed since 2023 or earlier.
  • 2
    Update Your Cost Basis Records
    Bitcoin cost basis is not automatically tracked by any single source of truth. Exchange records are fragmented. Wallets have no built-in cost basis. If you have been buying Bitcoin across multiple years on multiple platforms, your basis records may be incomplete, inconsistent, or simply missing for older lots. By mid-2026, you should have a clean, reconciled record of every lot: acquisition date, acquisition price (USD), number of BTC acquired, exchange or method. Your tax accountant needs this. Your executor needs this. Your beneficiaries need this. The absence of accurate basis records is one of the most common and costly mistakes we see in Bitcoin estate administration.
  • 3
    Review Your Investment Policy Statement (IPS) for Bitcoin
    A Bitcoin IPS is a written document that sets out your Bitcoin allocation strategies for HNW investors target, custody standards, distribution rules, and rebalancing criteria. It provides a framework for trustees, advisors, and family members who manage the Bitcoin position over time — including after your death. If your IPS was written when Bitcoin was $20,000, it may contain allocation targets, risk tolerances, or distribution rules that no longer reflect your intent. Review and update at current price levels.
  • 4
    Consider Whether Price Appreciation Has Moved You Into a New Planning Tier
    Bitcoin estate planning is tiered. Families with less than $1 million in total assets face different issues than those between $1–5 million, $5–13 million, or above. Price appreciation in 2024–2026 may have moved you from one tier to another without any action on your part. The strategies appropriate for each tier differ significantly: a family at $1.5 million total assets may need a basic LOI, will, and state estate planning. A family at $8 million may need an irrevocable trust, systematic gifting, and federal exemption planning before any potential sunset. Run the numbers again with current price and see which tier you're in.
  • 5
    Heir Education Check: Does Your Successor Know How to Access Your Bitcoin?
    This is often the most uncomfortable conversation on the checklist — but also one of the highest-leverage. Walk your primary heir or successor through the LOI. Not just "here is where the document is." Actually have them demonstrate, , that they could access your Bitcoin if they needed to. Where is the hardware wallet? How do they initiate access? Who do they call? What are the fail-safes if a device is lost? Institutional Bitcoin custody is only as good as the humans who can execute on it. A well-designed plan with an untrained executor is a plan that fails at the moment it matters most.

Estate Planning Triggers in 2026: Act When These Happen

Beyond the quarterly schedule, certain life and portfolio events require immediate estate plan attention — not a review at the end of the year. If any of these occur in 2026, treat them as triggers for an immediate LOI update and, depending on scale, an attorney consultation within 30 days.

  • →
    New wallet, multisig setup, or change in custody structure — LOI update required immediately. The old LOI no longer reflects how to access your Bitcoin.
  • →
    Bitcoin price crosses a threshold that triggers state estate tax exposure — Planning review required. You may have moved into taxable territory without any additional purchases.
  • →
    Marriage or divorce — Immediate update to will, trusts, and all beneficiary designations. Divorce revokes some provisions automatically in some states; do not assume.
  • →
    Birth or adoption of a child — Update beneficiary designations and trust documents to include the new family member. Review guardianship provisions.
  • →
    Death of a family member — If the deceased was named as executor, trustee, or beneficiary, immediate updates are required. Review the entire succession chain.
  • →
    New state of residence — State estate tax analysis required. Moving from Bitcoin family office in Texas to Massachusetts or Oregon is a major estate planning event for a Bitcoin holder.
  • →
    Large Bitcoin purchase that meaningfully changes total estate value — Re-run the estate tax exposure calculation. Update the IPS if allocation has changed significantly.

The #1 Mistake Bitcoin Holders Make in 2026

After reviewing dozens of Bitcoin estate plans, one failure pattern stands out above all others: failing to update the Letter of Instruction after moving Bitcoin or changing custody.

It happens like this. A family creates an estate plan in 2022 — will, trusts, LOI. The LOI accurately documents their Ledger hardware wallet, their Coinbase account, and their cold storage setup. In 2024, they move their Coinbase Bitcoin to River. In early 2025, they upgrade from a Ledger to a Coldcard and generate a new seed. Neither of these changes is reflected in the LOI.

The family feels covered. They have an estate plan. What they actually have is an estate plan pointing to accounts and devices that no longer hold their Bitcoin. When the executor follows the LOI instructions, they find empty accounts and deprecated devices. The Bitcoin — millions of dollars — is inaccessible.

The #1 Rule

Any time you move Bitcoin, change custody setup, or change any access credentials, update your LOI immediately — not at the next annual review. Treat it like a version-controlled document. Every significant custody change is a new version. Keep previous versions with the new ones, clearly labeled by date.

The LOI is not a "set it and forget it" document. It is a living document that must be maintained in sync with your actual custody structure. For families managing complex multisig setups or multiple custody layers, this maintenance should be a standing task — not an afterthought.

Your 2026 Bitcoin Estate Planning Summary

The checklist for 2026 is not complicated. It is a matter of execution. Here is the full list in brief:

Q1 (January – March):

  1. Update or create your Letter of Instruction
  2. Run an estate tax exposure calculation at current Bitcoin price
  3. Review beneficiary designations on all exchange accounts
  4. Confirm your executor and successor trustee are still the right people
  5. Check your state's estate tax threshold for 2026

Q2 (April – June):

  1. Annual review meeting with a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney
  2. Update cost basis records with 2024–2025 acquisition data
  3. Review and update your Investment Policy Statement
  4. Reassess which planning tier you're in based on current price
  5. Walk your primary heir through the actual LOI — don't just hand them the document

Bitcoin rewards long-term conviction. Your estate plan should reflect the same discipline. The families who treat estate planning as an annual practice — not a one-time event — are the ones who actually transfer their Bitcoin wealth to the next generation.
