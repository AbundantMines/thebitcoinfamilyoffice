Bitcoin Estate Planning in Georgia

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Georgia Est. 9 min read

Georgia imposes no state estate tax and no inheritance tax — a clear advantage for Bitcoin families domiciled in the Peach State. At death, Georgia Bitcoin holders face only the federal estate tax, which applies exclusively to estates exceeding the federal exemption of $13.99 million per individual in 2025. For most Bitcoin families, this means that state-level transfer taxes are simply not a factor — and planning energy can be directed entirely toward federal optimization, trust design, and the technical challenge of Bitcoin custody succession.

That said, the absence of a state estate tax does not reduce the urgency of estate planning — it simply removes one layer of complexity. Georgia Bitcoin families must still contend with the federal estate tax for larger positions, the risk of Bitcoin becoming inaccessible at the death or incapacity of the controlling holder, the absence of adequate digital asset succession instructions in most existing estate plans, and the need for governance structures that allow heirs to manage Bitcoin without forcing a premature sale. Bitcoin estate planning in Georgia demands the same rigor as any other state — with the advantage that less of the outcome is consumed by state tax.

Georgia's Estate Tax Advantage: No State-Level Transfer Tax

Georgia eliminated its state estate tax in 2014, when the state's estate tax was tied to the federal "state death tax credit" — a credit that was phased out by federal legislation in 2001–2005. Georgia did not reenact a standalone state estate tax, leaving Georgia residents with no state-level estate or inheritance tax obligation at death. Unlike neighboring states with more complex tax regimes, Georgia is a clean planning environment from a transfer tax standpoint.

The practical implication: a Georgia Bitcoin family with an $8 million estate — including a substantial Bitcoin position — owes $0 in Georgia estate tax. The same family in Illinois would owe approximately $600,000 in state estate tax. The same family in Maryland would owe both state estate tax and inheritance tax. Georgia's position on the spectrum of state transfer tax burden is among the most favorable in the country.

Georgia's no-estate-tax environment allows Bitcoin families to focus planning resources on generational wealth architecture rather than state tax mitigation — a significant structural advantage that many advisors underutilize.

Georgia Trust Code and Bitcoin Trust Structures

Georgia's trust law is codified at O.C.G.A. § 53-12-1 et seq. (the Georgia Trust Code, substantially revised in 2010). The Georgia Trust Code provides modern authority for trust formation, modification, and administration. For Bitcoin families, the key provisions are:

  • Directed trusts: Georgia's trust code permits trust instruments to create separate roles — a directing party controlling investment or custody decisions, and an administrative trustee handling operations, distributions, and compliance. For Bitcoin trusts, this allows a family member or specialist custodian to retain control of private keys and custody architecture without the corporate trustee bearing liability for those decisions.
  • Trust modification and decanting: Georgia courts have authority to modify trusts that fail to achieve the settlor's purposes due to changed circumstances. Additionally, trustee power to decant — transferring trust assets to a new trust with updated terms — is available under Georgia law. Bitcoin trusts drafted before digital asset succession practices were established can be modernized through decanting without court approval in appropriate circumstances.
  • Perpetual trusts: Georgia permits trusts to continue in perpetuity under O.C.G.A. § 44-6-200 et seq. (Rule Against Perpetuities modifications). A Georgia dynasty trust can hold Bitcoin indefinitely, accumulating value across generations without triggering estate tax at each generational transfer — capturing the full benefit of long-term Bitcoin appreciation free of transfer tax friction.
  • Spendthrift provisions: Georgia trust law enforces spendthrift provisions that protect trust assets from a beneficiary's creditors. For Bitcoin held in trust, this protection extends to the underlying Bitcoin — creditors of a beneficiary generally cannot reach trust assets until they are distributed, even in bankruptcy.

Digital Asset Succession Under Georgia RUFADAA

Georgia enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) at O.C.G.A. § 53-13-1 et seq. RUFADAA authorizes executors, trustees, and agents under durable powers of attorney to access and manage digital assets — including Bitcoin — after death or incapacity, subject to the priority hierarchy: online tool designations control first; governing instruments (will, trust, POA) control second; RUFADAA defaults apply last.

RUFADAA provides legal authority, not technical access. A fiduciary authorized by Georgia RUFADAA to manage Bitcoin still needs the actual private keys and custody credentials. Georgia Bitcoin estate plans must integrate: explicit RUFADAA authority in all governing documents, a separate and secured technical succession protocol specifying the custody architecture and key access procedures, and a tested recovery process that a non-technical fiduciary can execute without exposing the private keys to unintended parties. Federal Estate Planning Strategies for Georgia Bitcoin Families

Georgia's no-estate-tax environment means all transfer tax planning is focused on federal optimization. The core strategies for larger Bitcoin estates include:

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs): A GRAT allows a Bitcoin holder to transfer a position to an irrevocable trust while retaining annuity payments for a fixed term. If Bitcoin appreciates above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate during the GRAT term, the excess growth passes to heirs federal-estate-tax-free with no Georgia state tax consequence. Rolling GRATs — a series of short-term GRATs — can systematically shift Bitcoin appreciation to heirs over time.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs): Georgia Bitcoin holders with low-cost-basis positions can transfer appreciated Bitcoin to a CRT, defer capital gains recognition, receive an annuity or unitrust payment for a term, and pass the remainder to charity. Georgia conforms to federal income tax treatment of CRT distributions, making the state income tax consequences predictable and manageable.

Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts (SLATs): A SLAT removes Bitcoin from the federal taxable estate while preserving indirect spousal access through trust distributions. Georgia's trust code provides a solid framework, and the state's perpetual trust authorization allows a SLAT to continue indefinitely for the benefit of descendants after the spouse's death.

Intra-family loans and installment sales: Georgia Bitcoin holders can sell Bitcoin to an intentionally defective grantor trust (IDGT) for a promissory note at the applicable federal rate. The future appreciation inures to the trust (and ultimately to heirs) without estate or gift tax, while the seller receives interest payments and principal repayment. Georgia imposes no state gift or estate tax on this transaction.

Georgia LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Georgia LLCs (governed by the Georgia Limited Liability Company Act, O.C.G.A. § 14-11-100 et seq.) offer effective structures for Bitcoin holding with estate planning benefits. Bitcoin held through a family LLC can qualify for minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts — typically 20–35% — when interests are transferred to heirs or trusts, reducing the federal estate tax value of transferred interests.

Georgia LLC law provides charging order protection for LLC interests, preventing creditors from directly accessing LLC assets or forcing a dissolution. An LLC operating agreement for a Georgia Bitcoin holding company should address: multi-signature custody protocols and hardware wallet management, transaction authorization requirements, manager succession provisions, restrictions on voluntary member transfers, and coordination with the family's trust and estate plan documents. The operating agreement is the legal governance framework for Bitcoin custody decisions — not merely a tax planning vehicle.

Georgia Probate and Bitcoin Succession

Georgia's probate process is administered by the Probate Court in each county under O.C.G.A. § 53-5-1 et seq. Bitcoin held in the decedent's individual name is a probate asset subject to this process. For Georgia Bitcoin families, probate exposure creates two risks: the public nature of probate proceedings, which exposes the existence and approximate value of Bitcoin holdings; and the administrative delay of court proceedings while a successor attempts to access Bitcoin custody. Both risks are addressed by holding Bitcoin through a revocable living trust — the trust holds legal title to the Bitcoin (or to the LLC holding Bitcoin), and at death, the successor trustee steps in without court involvement or public disclosure.

Practical Priorities for Georgia Bitcoin Families

  1. Confirm Georgia's no-estate-tax baseline — your planning focus is federal optimization, trust architecture, and Bitcoin custody succession, not state tax mitigation.
  2. Establish a revocable living trust as the primary holding vehicle to avoid Georgia probate — critical for Bitcoin given the technical complexity of post-death custody access.
  3. Consider a Georgia dynasty trust using Georgia's perpetual trust authorization and the federal GST exemption for long-horizon Bitcoin positions intended to compound across multiple generations.
  4. Establish a Georgia LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle within the trust structure, with an operating agreement governing custody, succession, and governance.
  5. Implement RUFADAA authority explicitly in all governing instruments, and maintain a separate, secured technical succession protocol for Bitcoin private key and custody access.
