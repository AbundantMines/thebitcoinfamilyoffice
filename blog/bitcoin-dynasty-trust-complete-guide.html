Bitcoin Dynasty Trust: Complete 2026 Guide to Perpetual Bitcoin Wealth Transfer

By Hal Franklin · February 26, 2026 · 22 min read

Most Bitcoin estate planning conversations end at "put it in a trust." That answer isn't wrong — but it omits the most important word: which kind? A revocable living trust solves the probate problem. A dynasty trust solves the generational wealth problem. Those are not the same thing.

A Bitcoin dynasty trust removes your Bitcoin — and every satoshi of future appreciation — from your taxable estate, permanently, across every generation that follows you. If you hold a meaningful amount of Bitcoin and expect it to appreciate, this is the most powerful legal structure available to you. This guide explains exactly how it works, which states to use, how to fund it without triggering gift tax, how Bitcoin custody functions inside the trust, what it costs, and how it compares to every alternative.

⚠ Legal Notice

This guide is educational only. Dynasty trust planning involves complex federal and state tax law that varies by individual circumstance. Work with a licensed estate planning attorney and qualified tax advisor before implementing any strategy described here.

Section 1: What Is a Dynasty Trust? (Bitcoin-Specific)

A dynasty trust is an irrevocable trust designed to hold assets across multiple generations — potentially indefinitely — without triggering estate tax at each generational transfer. The term "dynasty" refers to its duration: unlike most trusts, which must terminate within a set period (historically limited by the "rule against perpetuities"), a dynasty trust in the right state can last forever.

The Rule Against Perpetuities — and Why It No Longer Applies

The rule against perpetuities (RAP) was a common-law doctrine requiring that property interests vest within 21 years after the death of the last relevant life in being at the time the trust was created. In plain English: trusts couldn't last forever. They had to end, and when they ended, the assets came back into someone's taxable estate.

Starting in the 1980s, a handful of states began repealing or modifying the RAP to attract trust business. Today, states including Wyoming, South Dakota, Nevada, Delaware, and Alaska have abolished the RAP entirely for trust assets. In these states, a properly drafted dynasty trust can exist in perpetuity — meaning your Bitcoin, and its future appreciation, can remain inside the trust structure indefinitely, passing to children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren, and beyond without triggering estate tax at each generation.

The Core Advantage: Locking Out Future Appreciation

This is the fundamental insight that makes a dynasty trust so powerful for Bitcoin specifically: you are not just removing today's value from your estate. You are removing every dollar of future appreciation from your estate.

When you fund a dynasty trust today with $5 million in Bitcoin, your estate is reduced by $5 million (using your lifetime exemption). But if that Bitcoin appreciates to $50 million, $100 million, or $500 million over the next 30 years — none of that appreciation ever touches your taxable estate. It was never in your estate to begin with. The appreciation belongs to the trust, which is a separate legal entity.

Compare this to holding Bitcoin personally: every dollar of appreciation compounds your Bitcoin inheritance tax exposure. At a 40% federal estate tax rate, a $50M estate generates a $20M tax bill. The same assets in a dynasty trust: $0 estate tax.

Dynasty Trust vs. Standard Irrevocable Trust

All dynasty trusts are irrevocable, but not all irrevocable trusts are dynasty trusts. The difference lies in duration and GST planning:

  • Standard irrevocable trust: May still be subject to the rule against perpetuities; often terminates and distributes assets to beneficiaries after a set period, at which point those assets return to taxable estates
  • Dynasty trust: Perpetual duration in the right state; assets stay inside the trust structure indefinitely; each generation receives benefits from the trust without ever "owning" the assets personally

Generation-Skipping Transfer (GST) Exemption

The Generation-Skipping Transfer tax is a separate federal tax (currently at the same 40% rate as the estate tax) applied to transfers that "skip" a generation — for example, transfers from grandparent directly to grandchild. Without GST planning, a dynasty trust could face GST tax each time assets flow to a grandchild or more remote descendant.

The solution: allocate your GST exemption to the dynasty trust when you fund it. In 2026, the federal lifetime exemption (which also covers GST exemption) is approximately $13.6 million per individual (subject to TCJA sunset provisions — confirm current law with your advisor). When you allocate GST exemption to the trust, all distributions to grandchildren, great-grandchildren, and beyond are exempt from GST tax forever. This is the "multi-generational" component of the dynasty trust strategy.

Section 2: How Bitcoin Dynasty Trusts Work (Mechanics)

Step 1: You Fund the Trust — and Lose Incidents of Ownership

The dynasty trust begins when you, the grantor, transfer assets into it. For Bitcoin, this means you irrevocably transfer title to the trust. To achieve estate exclusion, you must relinquish all "incidents of ownership" — the legal concept that determines whether you are treated as the owner of an asset for estate tax purposes.

Incidents of ownership include: the right to revoke or alter the trust, the right to direct distributions, a general power of appointment over trust assets, and certain retained economic interests. If you retain any of these, the trust assets may be pulled back into your taxable estate under IRC Section 2036 or 2038. Your estate planning attorney will draft the trust to avoid these traps.

You can fund the trust by gift (using your lifetime exemption) or by sale (via an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust structure, which avoids gift tax entirely — covered in detail in Section 6).

Income Beneficiary During Your Lifetime

You can be named as an income beneficiary or discretionary distribution beneficiary of the dynasty trust during your lifetime. This allows the trust to make distributions back to you — for example, from any income generated by trust assets. The key constraint: these distributions must be discretionary (controlled by an independent trustee), not automatic or guaranteed, to avoid incidents of ownership.

Assets Grow Inside the Trust — Forever Outside Your Estate

Once assets are in the dynasty trust, all future growth occurs inside the trust. The trust is a separate legal entity. Its assets don't belong to you, don't belong to your spouse, and don't belong to your children — they belong to the trust, for the benefit of your designated beneficiaries across all generations. When you die, the trust continues without interruption. There is no probate, no estate tax on trust assets, and no forced distribution.

Independent Trustee or Directed Trust Structure

The trust must be administered by a trustee who is sufficiently independent — you cannot be the sole trustee of a trust designed to remove assets from your estate (though you can serve as a co-trustee with limited powers in some structures, depending on state law and the specific powers retained).

The trustee manages the trust in accordance with its terms: investing assets, making discretionary distributions to beneficiaries, and maintaining trust records. For Bitcoin, trustee selection is critically important — they must have the technical competence to custody digital assets properly. Section 7 covers trustee selection in detail.

Trust Protector: Adapting to Law Changes

A trust protector is a person or entity (separate from the trustee) given specific powers over the trust — typically the ability to amend the trust, change its governing law, replace the trustee, or convert it from grantor trust to non-grantor trust status. For a trust designed to last indefinitely, a trust protector is essential: tax law will change many times over 50 or 100 years, and the trust needs the flexibility to adapt without requiring court approval for every amendment.

At Death: The Trust Continues

When you die, the dynasty trust does not terminate, distribute, or trigger estate tax. Your successor trustee steps in seamlessly. Assets remain inside the trust structure. No probate. No estate tax on any trust assets. The Bitcoin continues to grow inside the trust for the benefit of your children, grandchildren, and beyond — according to the terms you specified when you created the trust.

Section 3: Best States for Bitcoin Dynasty Trusts

You do not need to live in a favorable state to use its trust laws. You need only establish the trust under that state's law — typically by appointing a corporate trustee (trust company) licensed in that state, or by designating it as the governing law in the trust document. Here's how the top states compare:

Wyoming: The Best Overall Jurisdiction for Bitcoin

Wyoming is the premier jurisdiction for Bitcoin dynasty trusts, for reasons that go beyond trust law:

  • Perpetual trusts: Wyoming has abolished the rule against perpetuities entirely
  • Wyoming Trust Code: A comprehensive modern trust code modeled on the Uniform Trust Code with significant enhancements
  • Directed trust statute: Wyoming has an excellent directed trust statute, allowing full bifurcation of investment and distribution trustee roles — essential for sophisticated Bitcoin custody arrangements
  • Bitcoin-native trust companies: Wyoming has more Bitcoin-competent trust companies than any other state, a direct result of Wyoming's broader crypto-friendly regulatory framework (Wyoming SPDI charter, DAO LLC law, etc.)
  • No state income tax: Wyoming imposes no state income tax on trust income, even for trusts with non-Wyoming beneficiaries, if properly structured
  • Domestic Asset Protection: Wyoming offers strong DAPT (Domestic Asset Protection Trust) protections that can be layered with the dynasty trust structure

South Dakota: Best Domestic Asset Protection

South Dakota pioneered the modern dynasty trust and remains a close second to Wyoming:

  • Perpetual trusts (abolished RAP)
  • Best domestic asset protection trust statute in the country — 2-year statute of limitations for creditor challenges
  • No state income tax on trust income
  • Mature trust company industry with significant digital asset experience
  • Privacy provisions stronger than most states

Nevada: Privacy and DAPT

Nevada offers perpetual trusts, a strong DAPT statute, and exceptional privacy — Nevada does not require public disclosure of trust details. For high-profile individuals who prioritize confidentiality alongside estate protection, Nevada is worth evaluating.

Delaware: Depth of Legal Precedent

Delaware is the most legally mature trust jurisdiction. Its trust law is well-litigated and predictable. Delaware offers perpetual dynasty trusts and an extensive body of case law interpreting trust terms. For conservative planners who value legal certainty above cutting-edge innovation, Delaware remains a strong choice — though it lacks some of Wyoming's Bitcoin-specific infrastructure.

Alaska: DAPT Pioneer

Alaska was among the first states to allow self-settled asset protection trusts (DAPTs) and offers perpetual trust duration with no state income tax. Less Bitcoin-specific infrastructure than Wyoming, but a solid jurisdiction for combined asset protection and dynasty planning.

Your State of Residence Doesn't Determine Trust Domicile

This is a critical point: you can live in California, New York, or Texas and still domicile your dynasty trust in Wyoming. Trust domicile is determined by the governing law specified in the trust document and the location of the trustee. As long as you appoint a Wyoming trustee (or corporate trust company with Wyoming operations) and specify Wyoming law, your trust is a Wyoming trust — regardless of where you live. This is standard practice in trust planning.

Section 4: Dynasty Trust vs. Revocable Trust (Detailed Comparison)

Many Bitcoin holders set up a revocable living trust and believe they've addressed their estate planning. They've addressed probate — but they've left the estate tax problem entirely unsolved. Here's the complete comparison:

Revocable Trust: What It Does and Doesn't Do

A revocable living trust avoids probate. That's its primary function. Because you retain full control — you can change beneficiaries, amend terms, or revoke the trust entirely — all assets in a revocable trust are 100% in your taxable estate. The IRS treats a revocable trust as transparent: for estate tax purposes, it's as if the trust doesn't exist. Your assets pass to heirs efficiently, but they pass after estate tax has been applied.

Dynasty Trust: What It Does and Doesn't Do

A dynasty trust removes assets and all future appreciation from your taxable estate, permanently. You lose control — that's the price of removal. You cannot revoke the trust, unilaterally redirect distributions, or reclaim assets. In exchange, you get: estate tax elimination, creditor protection for beneficiaries, GST tax elimination (with proper exemption allocation), and generational wealth preservation without the forced liquidation that estate taxes often require.

When a Revocable Trust Is Sufficient

A revocable trust is the right tool when your estate is unlikely to exceed the federal exemption at death — currently around $13.6 million per individual (TCJA sunset could reduce this; confirm with your advisor). If you hold modest Bitcoin, have a simple family structure, and your primary concern is probate avoidance, a revocable trust with a Bitcoin rider and proper beneficiary designations may be entirely appropriate and far less expensive.

When a Dynasty Trust Is Essential

A dynasty trust becomes essential when: (1) your estate is likely to exceed the federal exemption at death, (2) you hold Bitcoin with significant appreciation potential, or (3) you want multi-generational Bitcoin wealth transfer without estate tax at each generation. Generally, if your Bitcoin position is worth $3M or more today, and you believe in Bitcoin's long-term appreciation thesis, the math increasingly favors a dynasty trust. At $5M+, it becomes very difficult to justify not having one.

Section 5: Dynasty Trust vs. Annual Gifting Strategy

Annual Gifting: Simple, Accessible, Limited

The annual gift tax exclusion allows you to give up to $18,000 per recipient per year ($36,000 for married couples using gift splitting) without using any of your lifetime exemption or owing gift tax. You can give to any number of people — your children, grandchildren, nieces, nephews, friends. Annual gifting is the simplest estate reduction strategy available and should be the foundation of every estate plan.

But annual gifting has real limitations for Bitcoin holders:

  • At $18K/year to two children, you remove $36K/year from your estate — a rounding error when your Bitcoin holding is worth millions
  • Once you gift Bitcoin, the recipient holds it personally — no creditor protection, no multi-generational structure, no GST planning
  • The recipient's holding becomes part of their estate — you've just moved the estate tax problem downstream one generation

Dynasty Trust: One Structure, Unlimited Future Appreciation

A dynasty trust, by contrast, removes potentially unlimited future appreciation in a single transaction. You fund the trust once (using your lifetime exemption), and every dollar of appreciation that occurs inside the trust for the next 100 years is permanently outside every taxable estate in your family. The lifetime exemption funds the trust; the trust generates compounding estate-tax-free returns indefinitely.

The Optimal Combination

These strategies are not mutually exclusive — they are complements. The optimal structure for a significant Bitcoin holder typically combines both:

  • Dynasty trust: Fund with your largest positions using lifetime exemption and/or IDGT sale; captures the bulk of future appreciation estate-tax-free
  • Annual gifting: Continue making annual exclusion gifts each year to further reduce your personal estate and provide current liquidity to family members

This layered approach maximizes estate reduction without requiring you to commit all assets to an irrevocable structure immediately.

Section 6: How to Fund a Bitcoin Dynasty Trust

Method 1: Direct Gift Using Lifetime Exemption

The simplest funding method: you transfer Bitcoin directly to the dynasty trust as a gift. The value of the gift is applied against your federal lifetime exemption (approximately $13.6 million per individual in 2026 — verify current amount, as the TCJA sunset may reduce this significantly). No gift tax is owed to the extent you have remaining exemption. The entire value of the Bitcoin, plus all future appreciation, immediately leaves your taxable estate.

The cost basis of gifted Bitcoin transfers to the trust (carryover basis from the grantor). This means future sales inside the trust will recognize gain from the original acquisition price — important for trust income tax planning.

Method 2: Sale to an IDGT — The Most Powerful Funding Mechanism

The Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) is the most powerful funding mechanism for high-value Bitcoin positions, because it allows you to transfer potentially unlimited appreciation with minimal gift tax and no capital gains tax recognition at the time of sale.

Here's how it works:

  1. Create the trust: Your attorney drafts the dynasty trust as a "grantor trust" for income tax purposes (by retaining certain powers that make the trust "defective" for income tax but not for estate tax)
  2. Fund with seed money: You gift approximately 10% of the intended trust assets as a seed gift (using your lifetime exemption) to establish the trust's net worth
  3. Sell Bitcoin to the trust: You sell the remaining Bitcoin to the trust in exchange for a promissory note. The note must bear interest at or above the IRS Applicable Federal Rate (AFR) for the term of the note
  4. No capital gains: Because the trust is a grantor trust for income tax purposes, the IRS treats you and the trust as the same entity — the sale is "transparent," and no capital gains tax is triggered
  5. No gift tax: It's a sale at fair market value — no gift, no gift tax, no exemption used (beyond the seed gift)
  6. Receive interest payments: The trust pays you interest on the note from its own assets (income generated by trust investments)
  7. All appreciation goes to the trust: Any appreciation above the AFR interest rate accrues inside the trust, permanently outside your estate

The IDGT sale is particularly powerful when the AFR is low (more appreciation escapes to the trust) and when the assets being transferred have high expected appreciation — both conditions that often apply to Bitcoin.

Method 3: Gradual Funding Over Multiple Years

Rather than funding the trust in a single transaction, some grantors prefer to fund it gradually — contributing Bitcoin over multiple years to spread their lifetime exemption usage and adapt to changing valuations. This approach also allows you to monitor the trust's operation and refine the custody arrangement before committing all assets.

Custody Mechanics at Funding

When you fund the trust with Bitcoin, the Bitcoin must actually move. This means:

  • Generating a new wallet or multi-sig address in the trust's name (or under the trustee's control)
  • Executing an on-chain transfer from your personal wallet to the trust's wallet
  • Documenting the transfer: trust valuation date, BTC price, cost basis of transferred lots, FMV at transfer
  • The trustee (or directed investment trustee) takes custodial control of the private keys

Work with your estate planning attorney and Bitcoin custody specialist to ensure the technical transfer is documented properly and that the chain of custody is clean and auditable.

Section 7: Trustee Selection for Bitcoin Dynasty Trusts

Trustee selection is arguably the most consequential decision in a dynasty trust structure. The trustee has fiduciary duty over assets that may exist for 50–100+ years. For Bitcoin specifically, the trustee must have both legal/fiduciary competence and Bitcoin technical competence — a rare combination.

Individual Trustee

A family member, trusted friend, or personal advisor can serve as trustee. The advantage: trust, familiarity, low or no fee. The disadvantage: individual trustees may lack Bitcoin technical capability, may die or become incapacitated, and may lack the institutional infrastructure for long-term key management and succession planning. Individual trustees are generally better suited to the distribution trustee role than the investment/custody trustee role in a Bitcoin dynasty trust.

Professional Trust Company

A corporate trust company (particularly Wyoming trust companies) offers institutional permanence, fiduciary infrastructure, and regulated operations. The best Wyoming trust companies have digital asset experience and can handle Bitcoin custody directly or in coordination with a qualified custodian. They have succession plans, insurance, compliance infrastructure, and the regulatory standing to serve as trustee indefinitely.

Directed Trust Structure: The Gold Standard for Bitcoin

The directed trust structure separates trustee roles into distinct parties, each with defined powers and responsibilities. This is the recommended structure for Bitcoin dynasty trusts:

  • Investment Trustee (or Investment Advisor): Holds and manages the Bitcoin. Has custody of keys or coordinates with the institutional custodian. Responsible for investment decisions, key management, and custody reporting. Can be a Bitcoin-specialist firm or knowledgeable individual
  • Distribution Trustee: Makes discretionary distribution decisions to beneficiaries. Often a family member, trusted advisor, or trust company. Has no access to or control over Bitcoin keys — focuses only on distribution governance
  • Trust Protector: Has the power to amend the trust, replace trustees, change governing law, and adapt the structure to future tax law changes. This role provides the flexibility that a perpetual trust needs to survive indefinitely

Under Wyoming's directed trust statute, each party is liable only for their own responsibilities — the distribution trustee is not liable for investment decisions, and the investment trustee is not liable for distribution decisions. This clear delineation of liability encourages best-in-class specialists to serve in each role.

The Key Question: Multi-Sig Key Distribution Between Trustee Roles

In a Bitcoin dynasty trust using multi-sig, the question of who holds each key — and how keys transfer when a trustee changes — must be documented in the trust administration procedures and the Letter of Instruction (separate from the trust document). A 2-of-3 multi-sig with one key held by the investment trustee, one key held by an independent co-signer, and one key held in geographically distributed cold storage is a common arrangement. This structure ensures no single trustee can unilaterally move Bitcoin, while maintaining practical operability.

Section 8: Bitcoin-Specific Custody in a Dynasty Trust

Bitcoin custody inside a dynasty trust raises unique challenges that traditional trust infrastructure was not designed to handle. Standard trust companies hold stocks, bonds, and real estate — assets with centralized registries, clearing houses, and standardized custodial infrastructure. Bitcoin has none of these. The trust holds whatever controls the private keys. Lose the keys, lose the Bitcoin. This makes custody planning arguably more important for Bitcoin trusts than for any other asset class.

Hardware Wallet in Trust Name

The simplest arrangement: the trustee holds one or more hardware wallets (Ledger, Coldcard, or similar) containing the trust's Bitcoin private keys. The hardware wallets are documented as trust property, stored in a secure location with access protocols, and maintained under the trustee's control. For small trusts or trusts with a competent individual trustee, this arrangement can work — but it introduces single points of failure and succession risk.

Multi-Signature Custody: The Recommended Standard

Multi-signature Bitcoin custody requires a threshold of keys (e.g., 2 of 3, or 3 of 5) to sign any transaction. No single key holder can move the Bitcoin unilaterally. This eliminates single points of failure and is essential for a structure designed to last 50–100+ years.

A typical 2-of-3 multi-sig arrangement for a Bitcoin dynasty trust:

  • Key 1: Held by the investment trustee (active management)
  • Key 2: Held by an independent co-signer (oversight and transaction approval)
  • Key 3: Stored in geographically secure, cold storage (recovery key; rarely accessed)

All three keys are documented in the trust administration procedures and the Letter of Instruction. The trust document specifies procedures for key replacement when a trustee changes, dies, or is removed — a process that must be executed carefully to avoid loss of access.

Key Succession Inside the Trust

When a trustee changes, the outgoing trustee's key must be replaced with a new key controlled by the successor trustee. This requires a multi-sig transaction: the existing quorum of key holders signs a transaction moving all trust Bitcoin to a new multi-sig address controlled by the new trustee configuration. This successor key transition must be planned in advance and tested — it cannot be improvised after a trustee has already left or died.

Letter of Instruction

The Letter of Instruction (LOI) is a separate, non-binding document that explains how to access and manage the trust's Bitcoin. Unlike the trust document (which is a legal contract), the LOI is operational: it describes the multi-sig setup, key locations, hardware wallet seed phrase storage, custody procedures, and the steps to take upon the trustee's death or incapacity. The LOI should be updated whenever the custody arrangement changes and reviewed annually. Store it separately from (but accessible to) the trustee.

Self-Custody vs. Institutional Custodian

Trust Bitcoin can be held in self-custody (trustee controls keys directly) or with an institutional custodian (Anchorage Digital, Coinbase Prime, Fidelity Digital Assets, etc.) in the trust's name. Institutional custody offers insurance, regulatory standing, and simplified trustee succession — the custodian handles key management, and the trust is simply a named account holder. The trade-off: counterparty risk (custodian failure), ongoing fees, and reduced Bitcoin-native sovereignty. For very large trusts ($10M+), institutional custody may be preferred for its reliability and insurance coverage.

Section 9: Costs of a Bitcoin Dynasty Trust

A dynasty trust is not cheap to establish. But relative to the estate tax it can eliminate, the cost-benefit math is almost always compelling for estates of meaningful size. Here's a complete cost breakdown:

Legal Setup: $15,000–$40,000

A Wyoming dynasty trust with Bitcoin-specific provisions — directed trust structure, digital asset custody language, trust protector provisions, proper GST exemption allocation — typically costs $15,000–$40,000 in attorney fees. The wide range reflects attorney experience, complexity of the trust structure, state of domicile, and whether an IDGT sale is being structured simultaneously. Do not use generic trust templates. Bitcoin-specific provisions and Wyoming-specific language require specialized drafting.

Trust Company Annual Fee: 0.25–1.0% AUM

A Wyoming trust company acting as trustee or administrative trustee typically charges 0.25–1.0% of assets under management annually. For a $5M trust, this is $12,500–$50,000/year. Some trust companies charge flat fees for smaller trusts. Fees decrease as a percentage as trust assets grow.

Investment Trustee Fee (if separate): 0.1–0.25% AUM

In a directed trust structure with a separate investment trustee managing Bitcoin custody, that party charges separately — typically 0.1–0.25% AUM for Bitcoin custody services. This is often substantially less than traditional investment management fees because Bitcoin does not require active portfolio management — it requires secure custody.

Annual Legal Review: $2,000–$5,000

Tax law changes. Bitcoin regulations evolve. The trust document should be reviewed annually by a qualified estate planning attorney to ensure it remains optimal and compliant. Budget $2,000–$5,000/year for this review.

The Break-Even: When the Trust Pays for Itself

Federal estate tax is 40% on assets above the exemption. At a 40% rate, a dynasty trust that removes $5M from your estate saves approximately $2M in estate tax (assuming the full $5M is above your exemption at death). Even at the high end of cost estimates, the trust pays for itself with significant margin — and that's before accounting for appreciation. If that $5M grows to $50M inside the trust, the estate tax savings are approximately $20M. The $400K in 10-year costs is less than 2% of the tax saved.

Section 10: Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bitcoin dynasty trust?

A bitcoin dynasty trust is an irrevocable trust structured to hold Bitcoin (and other assets) across multiple generations — potentially indefinitely — by taking advantage of states that have abolished the rule against perpetuities. Once funded, the trust assets (and all future appreciation) are permanently outside the grantor's taxable estate, shielded from creditors, and exempt from estate tax at each generational transfer when GST exemption is properly allocated.

How long can a dynasty trust last in Wyoming?

Wyoming has abolished the rule against perpetuities for trusts governed by Wyoming law. In practical terms, a Wyoming dynasty trust can last indefinitely — forever. There is no statutory maximum duration. This is one of the primary reasons Wyoming is the leading jurisdiction for Bitcoin dynasty trusts.

Can I still benefit from assets in a dynasty trust?

Yes, with important constraints. You can be named as an income beneficiary or discretionary distribution beneficiary of the dynasty trust during your lifetime. However, you cannot retain full control — no power to revoke, no general power of appointment over assets, and no retained right to the property that would pull it back into your taxable estate. The trust must be genuinely irrevocable to achieve estate-exclusion. Distributions to you are discretionary and controlled by the independent trustee.

What's the minimum for a dynasty trust to make economic sense?

As a general rule of thumb, a dynasty trust makes strong economic sense when: (1) your estate is likely to exceed the federal estate tax exemption at death, and (2) your Bitcoin holdings are large enough that future appreciation would generate meaningful estate tax. For most people, this threshold is roughly $3–5M or more in Bitcoin or total assets. Below that, simpler strategies (annual gifting, revocable trust with proper beneficiary designations) may be more cost-effective. Above $5M, the math increasingly favors the dynasty trust.

How does Bitcoin custody work inside a dynasty trust?

Bitcoin held in a dynasty trust must be held by or under the control of the trustee — not the grantor personally. Common arrangements include: (1) a professional trust company acting as custodian with institutional-grade cold storage; (2) a directed trust structure where an investment trustee (who may use a hardware wallet or multi-sig setup) holds the keys separately from the distribution trustee; or (3) an institutional custodian like Anchorage or Coinbase Prime holding Bitcoin in the trust's name. Multi-sig (2-of-3 or 3-of-5) is recommended to prevent single points of failure and to manage trustee succession cleanly.

Can I put Bitcoin in a dynasty trust without paying gift tax?

Yes — the most popular structure is a sale to an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT). You sell your Bitcoin to the dynasty trust in exchange for a promissory note bearing interest at the IRS Applicable Federal Rate (AFR). Because the trust is a "grantor trust" for income tax purposes, no capital gains tax is triggered on the sale. No gift tax is incurred because it's a sale at fair market value. Future appreciation accrues inside the trust, estate-tax-free, while you receive interest payments from the note.

What's the difference between a dynasty trust and a GRAT?

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is a shorter-term vehicle (typically 2–10 years) that transfers appreciation above the IRS hurdle rate (7520 rate) to heirs gift-tax-free. GRATs have a "mortality risk" — if the grantor dies during the GRAT term, assets return to the estate. A dynasty trust is permanent: once funded, assets stay outside the estate regardless of when the grantor dies, can last forever, and holds assets for multiple generations simultaneously. GRATs are tactical (near-term appreciation capture); dynasty trusts are strategic (permanent multi-generational wealth transfer). They can also be used together.

Do I need a Wyoming attorney to form a Wyoming dynasty trust?

Not necessarily, but it is strongly recommended that a Wyoming-licensed attorney be involved. A Wyoming dynasty trust must be governed by Wyoming law and have a Wyoming nexus (typically a Wyoming trustee or trust company). Any licensed attorney can draft the trust document, but you'll need either a Wyoming-licensed attorney or a Wyoming trust company to serve as trustee to establish proper state nexus. Many national estate planning attorneys work with Wyoming trust companies to set up these structures for clients in any state.
