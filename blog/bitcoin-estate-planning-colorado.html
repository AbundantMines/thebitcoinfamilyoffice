Bitcoin Estate Planning in Colorado

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Article Est. 8 min read

Colorado occupies an interesting position in the Bitcoin estate planning landscape: it is not a zero-tax jurisdiction like Wyoming, Nevada, or Florida, but its flat 4.4% income tax rate, complete absence of a state estate tax, growing Bitcoin community, and strong legal privacy culture make it meaningfully more favorable than California or New York. For Bitcoin families already resident in Colorado — particularly in the Boulder-Denver-Fort Collins corridor where much of the state's Bitcoin wealth is concentrated — understanding the specific legal and tax framework is essential for building a durable plan.

Colorado's Flat Income Tax: 4.4% on Bitcoin Gains

Colorado imposes a flat individual income tax rate of 4.4% (reduced from 4.55% by Proposition 121 in 2022). Unlike California's 13.3% top rate or New York's 10.9%, Colorado's flat tax applies uniformly regardless of income level — there are no higher brackets for large Bitcoin gains. A Colorado resident realizing a $50 million Bitcoin gain pays the same 4.4% state rate as one realizing $500,000. The effective combined federal and Colorado marginal rate on long-term capital gains is approximately 28.2% (23.8% federal + 4.4% state), a substantially lighter burden than high-tax states.

Colorado also allows a taxpayer who qualifies under the federal Taxpayer Bill of Rights (TABOR, Article X, Section 20 of the Colorado Constitution) to potentially receive refunds when state revenue exceeds spending limits — a structural constraint on future tax increases that provides some durability to the current rate. While not a constitutional prohibition like Nevada's income tax ban, TABOR creates meaningful political and structural resistance to significant income tax increases.

Colorado does not impose a special tax on capital gains — all income is subject to the same flat rate. Bitcoin gains held long-term receive the federal long-term capital gains preference (0%, 15%, or 20%) at the federal level; Colorado taxes the full gain at 4.4% regardless of holding period. This treatment is more favorable than California (which taxes capital gains as ordinary income at the state level) but less favorable than no-income-tax states.

No State Estate Tax

Colorado does not impose a state estate or inheritance tax. Like Texas and Florida, Colorado repealed its state estate tax when the federal state death tax credit (IRC §2011) was phased out after 2001. There is no separate Colorado estate tax, no inheritance tax on beneficiaries, and no state filing requirement at death beyond what the federal estate tax return requires. Colorado residents' estates are subject only to federal estate tax at the $13.99 million exemption level (2025).

For Bitcoin families whose estates are below the federal exemption, Colorado's absence of an estate tax means zero state death tax on their Bitcoin. For estates above the federal threshold, the only planning target is the federal estate tax — Colorado does not add a separate layer of complexity or cost.

Colorado Trust Code and Privacy Provisions

Colorado adopted the Uniform Trust Code (Colorado Revised Statutes Title 15, Article 5) providing a modern statutory framework for trust administration, modification, and dispute resolution. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families include directed trust authority (§15-5-808), trust decanting powers, and spendthrift trust protection (§15-5-502).

Colorado has a notable privacy protection for trust beneficiaries: the Colorado Uniform Trust Code does not require trustees to proactively disclose trust terms to discretionary beneficiaries who are not current distributees. A properly structured discretionary trust for future generations can maintain significant privacy regarding trust terms and holdings — relevant for Bitcoin families who prefer not to disclose the existence or magnitude of holdings to younger heirs before they reach appropriate maturity.

Colorado's flat 4.4% income tax means a Bitcoin family realizing a $50M gain pays the same rate as one realizing $500K — no punitive brackets, no surcharges. That predictability matters for long-term planning.

Colorado's Growing Bitcoin Infrastructure

Colorado has emerged as one of the more Bitcoin-friendly regulatory environments in the United States. The state became the first to accept Bitcoin as payment for state taxes and fees (via PayPal's conversion service) in 2022 — a largely symbolic but directionally significant policy. Colorado's tech-forward culture and significant presence of Bitcoin mining (particularly in the mountains where hydroelectric and renewable energy is available) has created a local ecosystem of Bitcoin-aware legal, accounting, and advisory professionals that is more developed than most inland states.

Colorado's Uniform Electronic Transactions Act and modernized business entity laws accommodate digital asset operations. The Colorado Secretary of State's office has been relatively forward-looking on business registration for Bitcoin-related entities.

Colorado LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Colorado LLCs (Colorado Limited Liability Company Act, CRS §§7-80-101 et seq.) provide standard charging order protection for multi-member entities. The Colorado Uniform Limited Liability Company Act, adopted in 2008, modernized the framework and provides a reasonable foundation for holding Bitcoin. However, Colorado LLC law does not offer the same level of single-member charging order protection as Wyoming or Nevada — a family LLC with a sole member in Colorado has more creditor exposure than one in Wyoming.

The practical guidance: Colorado-domiciled Bitcoin families who want strong asset protection should consider forming their Bitcoin holding LLC in Wyoming or Nevada and registering it as a foreign entity in Colorado. The Wyoming or Nevada formation provides stronger creditor protection, while Colorado registration allows the LLC to conduct business in Colorado without restriction. The annual foreign registration costs are modest relative to the structural protection gained.

Colorado vs. Wyoming for Trust Situs

Colorado's Uniform Trust Code is adequate for most trust administration needs, but Wyoming still holds significant advantages for Bitcoin-specific trust structures: the Wyoming DPTA's directed trust framework is more explicitly tailored to digital assets, Wyoming's SPDI charter creates a purpose-built custodian regulatory framework, and Wyoming's abolition of the rule against perpetuities allows longer dynasty trust horizons.

Colorado does not have a statutory rule against perpetuities for trusts (Colorado abolished the common law rule in favor of the Uniform Statutory Rule Against Perpetuities, which imposes a 90-year waiting period — shorter than Wyoming's true perpetuity authorization but much longer than traditional common law limits). For a dynasty trust intended to last multiple generations, Wyoming's true perpetuity authorization is preferable to Colorado's 90-year limit.

Colorado residents seeking the maximum trust law advantage while maintaining Colorado domicile should consider a Wyoming-sited directed dynasty trust administered by a Wyoming trustee — capturing Wyoming's legal infrastructure while living in Colorado's relatively favorable (if not zero) tax environment. Practical Steps for Colorado Bitcoin Families

  1. Establish a revocable living trust under Colorado law for probate avoidance, clearly specifying Bitcoin custody authority and successor trustee technical requirements.
  2. Form a Wyoming or Nevada LLC to hold Bitcoin, registered as a foreign LLC in Colorado — capturing stronger creditor protection without sacrificing Colorado operational flexibility.
  3. Evaluate Wyoming dynasty trust situs for long-horizon multigenerational planning — Colorado residents can access Wyoming's superior trust laws through a Wyoming-sited directed trust.
  4. Document community property status if married (Colorado is not a community property state — it is a common law property state — so the federal step-up at death applies only to the decedent's half of jointly held property, not both halves as in community property states).

Bitcoin Estate Planning Across States

State law matters significantly in Bitcoin estate planning. Wyoming and South Dakota stand out for trust-friendly statutes, no state estate tax, and perpetuity-exempt dynasty trust structures. Your state of domicile determines probate exposure, trust taxation, and available legal tools.
