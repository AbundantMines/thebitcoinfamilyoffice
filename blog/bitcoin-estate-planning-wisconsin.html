Bitcoin Estate Planning in Wisconsin

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Wisconsin Est. 9 min read

Wisconsin imposes no state estate tax and no inheritance tax — a meaningful planning advantage for Bitcoin families that removes an entire layer of transfer tax complexity. Wisconsin is also one of only a handful of states outside the traditional eight community property states to have adopted a community property system — the Wisconsin Marital Property Act — which creates unique estate planning considerations for married Bitcoin holders. Understanding how Wisconsin's marital property rules interact with Bitcoin ownership is essential for estate plans in the state.

This guide covers Wisconsin's no-estate-tax advantage, the Wisconsin Marital Property Act and its implications for Bitcoin ownership, trust and LLC structures for Wisconsin Bitcoin families, digital asset succession under Wisconsin law, and the federal-level strategies that allow Wisconsin families to protect and transfer Bitcoin across generations. Bitcoin estate planning Wisconsin requires advisors who understand both the marital property framework and the technical dimensions of Bitcoin custody succession.

Wisconsin's No-Estate-Tax Advantage

Wisconsin eliminated its state estate tax effective for deaths occurring on or after January 1, 2008, when the legislature declined to re-enact a standalone state estate tax after federal reform eliminated the state death tax credit. Wisconsin also imposes no inheritance tax. For Bitcoin families, this creates a clean planning baseline: only the federal estate tax — applicable to estates above $13.99 million per individual in 2025 — applies at death.

For Wisconsin families with Bitcoin holdings below the federal exemption, this means zero transfer tax at death when assets pass to heirs through a properly structured estate plan. Wisconsin Bitcoin holders do not face the state-level estate tax cliffs present in Massachusetts or Washington, nor the beneficiary-class calculations that burden families in New Jersey and Kentucky. The entire planning focus can be directed toward federal structures, marital property considerations, custody architecture, and succession planning.

Wisconsin's zero estate tax, combined with its unique marital property framework, creates a planning environment that is favorable but nuanced — particularly for married Bitcoin holders whose BTC acquired during marriage is classified as marital property by default.

The Wisconsin Marital Property Act and Bitcoin

Wisconsin's Marital Property Act (MPA), codified at Wis. Stat. §§ 766.001 et seq., adopted a modified community property system in 1986. Under the MPA, property acquired during marriage by either spouse through labor is classified as "marital property" — essentially community property — unless it meets specific criteria for "individual property" classification.

For Bitcoin families, the MPA creates the following default classifications:

  • Bitcoin acquired with earnings during marriage: Classified as marital property — both spouses own an undivided one-half interest.
  • Bitcoin acquired as a gift or inheritance and kept separate: Classified as individual property — owned by the recipient spouse alone.
  • Bitcoin owned before marriage: Individual property — but can become marital property through commingling or transmutation if not maintained separately.
  • Income earned on individual property Bitcoin: Classified as marital property under the MPA unless the trust instrument or property agreement specifies otherwise.

The MPA classification matters for estate planning because: (1) at the first spouse's death, the surviving spouse already owns one-half of the marital property Bitcoin, so only the decedent's one-half interest passes through the estate plan; (2) the federal estate tax step-up in basis applies to the decedent's one-half share at death; (3) estate planning documents must correctly identify which Bitcoin is marital property and which is individual property to avoid disputes and ensure correct tax treatment.

Marital Property Agreements for Wisconsin Bitcoin Families

Wisconsin couples can modify the MPA's default classification rules through a marital property agreement (MPA agreement) under Wis. Stat. § 766.58. For Bitcoin families, an MPA agreement can:

  • Reclassify Bitcoin from marital property to individual property — useful if one spouse acquired Bitcoin with premarital funds or wants to maintain a separate Bitcoin position within the marriage.
  • Reclassify Bitcoin from individual property to marital property — relevant if both spouses want a full step-up in basis on the entire Bitcoin position at the first death under the federal "community property step-up" rules (which apply to community property but not separate property).
  • Specify the management and control of Bitcoin within the marriage, including custody authority and transaction approval requirements.

The interaction between MPA property classification and the federal estate tax basis step-up is an important planning variable for Wisconsin Bitcoin families. Under federal law, community property (or Wisconsin marital property) receives a full step-up in basis on both halves at the first death — not just on the decedent's half. This can eliminate a significant capital gains tax liability on appreciated Bitcoin, making the marital property classification valuable for families with low-cost-basis positions who intend to sell after the first spouse's death.

Wisconsin's Trust Code Framework

Wisconsin enacted the Wisconsin Trust Code (WTC), codified at Wis. Stat. §§ 701.0101 et seq., effective July 1, 2014. The WTC is based on the Uniform Trust Code and provides modern statutory authority for trust structures that Wisconsin Bitcoin families require:

  • Trust protectors: The WTC expressly recognizes trust protector authority, allowing named individuals to hold specified powers over custody arrangements, trustee succession, and investment direction without full trustee fiduciary duties.
  • Directed trusts: Wisconsin trust instruments can designate investment trust advisors and distribution advisors with defined authority, enabling the functional separation of Bitcoin custody decisions from general trust administration.
  • Dynasty trusts: Wisconsin's rule against perpetuities permits trusts of personal property to last for up to the common law rule against perpetuities period. For families seeking longer perpetuity periods, Wyoming trust siting provides perpetual dynasty trust capability.
  • Decanting: The WTC includes decanting authority allowing trustees to transfer assets to a new trust with updated terms, enabling modernization of Bitcoin custody provisions without court proceedings.

Wisconsin LLCs for Bitcoin Holding

Wisconsin LLCs (governed by the Wisconsin Uniform Limited Liability Company Law, Wis. Stat. §§ 183.0101 et seq.) provide effective Bitcoin holding structures. Key planning points for Wisconsin Bitcoin families include:

MPA reclassification through LLC: Bitcoin contributed to a Wisconsin LLC may be reclassified from marital property to LLC membership interests under Wisconsin law. If both spouses contribute Bitcoin to the LLC, their interests in the LLC are marital property. If only one spouse contributes — and that Bitcoin was individual property — the resulting LLC interests may retain individual property status, depending on the structure. The interaction of MPA rules and LLC formation requires careful planning to achieve the desired classification.

Valuation discounts: Bitcoin held through a properly structured Wisconsin family LLC qualifies for minority interest and lack-of-marketability valuation discounts of 20–35% when membership interests are transferred to trusts or heirs, accelerating the pace at which Bitcoin value can be shifted to the next generation.

Operating agreement governance: The LLC operating agreement governs Bitcoin custody architecture, key-person succession, and membership transfer restrictions — creating the legal governance framework for Bitcoin custody decisions.

Charging order protection: Wisconsin LLC law provides charging order protection (Wis. Stat. § 183.0503) — creditors of a member cannot force dissolution or seize Bitcoin held by the LLC.

Digital Asset Succession in Wisconsin

Wisconsin enacted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), codified at Wis. Stat. §§ 711.01 et seq. Under Wisconsin's RUFADAA, authorized fiduciaries have legal authority to access, manage, and transfer digital assets — including Bitcoin — when the governing estate planning documents explicitly grant that authority. The three-tier priority structure applies: platform online tool first; governing document second; platform terms of service third.

Every Wisconsin Bitcoin estate plan must include explicit RUFADAA digital asset access authority in every governing instrument — trust agreements, durable powers of attorney, and wills. Generic property language is insufficient. A separate technical access document, maintained securely and updated regularly, must accompany the legal documents to provide the actual technical access credentials that RUFADAA authority enables. Wyoming Trust Situs for Wisconsin Families

Wisconsin families seeking perpetual dynasty trust capability or Wyoming's more comprehensive directed trust statute can establish a Wyoming-sited trust — with a Wyoming corporate trustee — while maintaining Wisconsin residency. Wisconsin's zero estate tax means there is no state tax penalty for out-of-state trust siting; the decision turns entirely on trust law quality and planning horizon. Practical Priorities for Wisconsin Bitcoin Families

  1. Classify your Bitcoin under the Wisconsin Marital Property Act — determine which Bitcoin is marital property and which is individual property, and evaluate whether an MPA agreement would optimize the tax and planning outcome, particularly the full step-up in basis benefit.
  2. Establish a Wisconsin LLC as the Bitcoin holding vehicle — with an operating agreement that governs custody architecture, key-person succession, and membership transfer restrictions.
  3. Fund irrevocable trust structures with discounted LLC interests — removing Bitcoin appreciation from the federal taxable estate over time through annual exclusion gifting or IDGT note sales.
  4. Incorporate explicit RUFADAA authority language in all governing instruments — Wisconsin's RUFADAA creates the legal framework, but every document must explicitly invoke it.
  5. Maintain a tested technical succession protocol — maintained separately from the estate plan documents — covering hardware wallet access, multi-signature key holder succession, and all custody service credentials.
  6. Consider Wyoming trust siting for the primary dynasty trust if a perpetual horizon is desired — capturing Wyoming's perpetuity and directed trust advantages while Wisconsin's zero estate tax removes any state tax concern from the siting decision.
