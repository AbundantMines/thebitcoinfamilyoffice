Bitcoin Prenuptial Agreement: Protecting Your Bitcoin Before Marriage

Without a prenup, your state's default marital property rules determine what happens to your Bitcoin in a divorce — and those rules were not written with Bitcoin in mind. In community property states, appreciation on pre-marital Bitcoin may become marital property. Mining income earned during marriage is almost certainly community property. A properly drafted Bitcoin prenuptial agreement is the single most important document a Bitcoin holder can sign before marriage.

By The Bitcoin Family Office Editorial Team  ·  Updated March 2026  ·  13 min read

Bitcoin creates prenuptial agreement issues that generic family law attorneys routinely miss. The volatility, the self-custody architecture, the mining income classification, the tracing challenges — Bitcoin doesn't fit neatly into the frameworks that prenups were designed around. A prenup drafted by an attorney who has never thought about digital assets may omit the provisions that matter most and include enforceability vulnerabilities that only surface years later in divorce court.

This guide covers what a Bitcoin prenup must include, how the key Bitcoin-specific issues are treated under different state laws, and how to structure the agreement to survive the enforceability challenges that family courts routinely apply.

Prenup vs. Postnup

A prenuptial agreement is signed before marriage. A postnuptial agreement serves the same function but is signed after the wedding. Both are valid in most states, but postnuptial agreements face higher scrutiny — courts are more suspicious of agreements signed after the relationship is formalized, since neither party can simply walk away from the engagement. If you are already married and don't have a prenup, a postnup is still worth doing; it is simply more complex to execute in a way that will withstand court challenge.

The Default Rules Without a Prenup

Before discussing what a prenup should say, it helps to understand what happens without one. The answer depends entirely on which type of state you live in:

Community Property States (9 States)

In California, Texas, Arizona, Nevada, Washington, Idaho, Louisiana, Wisconsin, and New Mexico, the default rule is: property acquired during the marriage with marital funds is community property, owned 50/50 by both spouses. Separate property (owned before marriage, inherited, or received as a gift) remains separate — but the line between separate and community property can blur quickly with Bitcoin.

Key community property problems for Bitcoin holders:

  • Appreciation on separate property: In California, appreciation on pre-marital Bitcoin remains separate property — but only if the Bitcoin itself remains separate and traceable. If you commingled pre-marital Bitcoin with Bitcoin purchased during marriage in the same wallet, tracing becomes a forensic accounting exercise.
  • Mining income during marriage: Bitcoin mining income earned during the marriage is community property in community property states — regardless of whether the mining equipment was owned before marriage. The income-producing labor during the marriage creates community property income.
  • Transmutation: If you add your spouse's name to a Bitcoin account, give them access to a wallet, or use separate Bitcoin to pay community expenses, you may have inadvertently transmuted separate property into community property.

Common Law / Equitable Distribution States (41 States)

In New York, Florida, Illinois, and most other states, the default is equitable distribution: marital property (acquired during the marriage) is divided equitably — not necessarily 50/50 — based on factors like length of marriage, contributions of each spouse, economic circumstances, and earning capacity. Separate property (owned before marriage) is generally excluded, but courts have discretion.

The equitable distribution problem for Bitcoin holders: judges have wide discretion, and "equitable" does not mean predictable. A judge who doesn't understand Bitcoin's asymmetric return profile may divide a Bitcoin position in ways that make no financial sense — ordering a forced sale at an inopportune time, for example, or assigning Bitcoin to the non-holder spouse without understanding that the other spouse's technical custody ability was the basis of the position's value.

What a Bitcoin Prenup Must Cover

1. Separate Property Schedule and Bitcoin Inventory

The foundation of any prenup is a clear statement of what each party owns at the time of marriage as separate property. For Bitcoin holders, this requires:

  • Specific Bitcoin holdings: Number of BTC held, approximate acquisition dates, acquisition cost basis, and current approximate value at time of signing. Do not use wallet addresses in the prenup itself — that creates a public record of your holdings.
  • Exchange accounts: List of exchange accounts holding Bitcoin, identified by institution (not account number) and approximate balance at signing.
  • Mining equipment: Hardware owned before marriage, described by type/quantity, current value, and location.
  • Mining companies or LLCs: Any business entity through which mining is conducted; the prenup should specify that the entity itself — not just the member interest — is separate property.
  • Related cryptocurrency: Any other digital assets held at the time of signing that should be treated as separate property.

The inventory should be attached as a schedule to the prenup — updated at signing with current values — and kept in a secure location (attorney's office, fireproof safe) for reference in any future dispute.

2. Appreciation on Separate Property

This is the most important and most frequently contested provision for Bitcoin holders. The issue: pre-marital Bitcoin that appreciates 10× during a 5-year marriage. Is the appreciation separate property (belonging to the original owner) or marital property (to be divided)?

The answer under default law varies by state:

State Type Default Rule on Appreciation Prenup Fix
California (CP) Passive appreciation on separate property stays separate if traceable; active appreciation (from marital efforts) may be community Define all appreciation on pre-marital Bitcoin as separate regardless of source
Texas (CP) Passive appreciation on separate property stays separate; income (mining) from separate property is community Define mining income during marriage as separate (or agreed share); confirm appreciation treatment
New York (equitable) Appreciation on separate property may be marital if marital efforts contributed (e.g., spouse managed the portfolio) Define all appreciation on pre-marital Bitcoin as separate; waive any claim to management contribution
Florida (equitable) Passive appreciation on separate property is generally non-marital; active appreciation may be marital Define appreciation as separate; include waiver of active management contribution claim
Nevada (CP) Passive appreciation is separate; income during marriage (mining) is community Define appreciation + mining income treatment explicitly

The prenup should explicitly state: "All appreciation, gains, income, and proceeds arising from [Party A]'s separate property Bitcoin holdings, as listed in Schedule A, whether such appreciation is characterized as passive or active, shall remain [Party A]'s separate property." Courts sometimes interpret appreciation provisions narrowly — the broader the language, the more protective.

3. Bitcoin Mining Income During Marriage

Bitcoin mining income earned during the marriage is one of the most contested areas in Bitcoin divorce cases. In community property states, income earned during the marriage is presumptively community property — regardless of whether the equipment was owned before marriage.

A Bitcoin prenup must explicitly address mining income:

  • Is mining income earned during the marriage separate or community/marital?
  • If community: what percentage is allocated to each spouse?
  • How is "mining income" defined — gross Bitcoin received, or net after operating costs?
  • Does the income characterization change if the non-mining spouse contributes labor to the mining operation?
  • How is income from future mining equipment purchased with marital funds treated?

For mining operators, this provision deserves careful negotiation. Designating all mining income as separate property requires the non-miner spouse to waive a significant economic right — courts will scrutinize this for fairness. A reasonable compromise: mining income from equipment owned before marriage is separate; mining income from equipment purchased during marriage with community funds is community.

4. Custody Architecture and Access During Marriage

Bitcoin self-custody creates a practical prenup issue that standard marital property agreements never address: who controls the Bitcoin during the marriage? A prenup that says "Bitcoin is separate property" is useless if the non-owning spouse has physical access to hardware wallets or seed phrases and initiates a transfer during a marital dispute.

The prenup should address:

  • Access rights during marriage: Does the non-owning spouse have any right to access wallets, seed phrases, or exchange accounts? If multisig is used, is the spouse a keyholder?
  • Transfer restrictions during marital dispute: A clause that prohibits either party from transferring Bitcoin out of the marital estate during a period of separation or pending divorce proceeding — preventing asset dissipation before division.
  • Discovery obligations: Both parties agree to disclose Bitcoin holdings and wallet addresses upon request in any divorce proceeding — eliminating the concealment problem that plagues Bitcoin divorce cases.
  • Temporary orders: Agreement that courts can issue temporary restraining orders on Bitcoin transfers during divorce pendency without requiring proof of imminent dissipation.
Security vs. Legal Clarity

There is inherent tension between Bitcoin security best practices (never disclose seed phrase locations; minimize the number of people who know about your holdings) and the full financial disclosure required for an enforceable prenup. The resolution: the prenup schedules describe holdings at a category level (quantity, approximate value, institution) — not seed phrase locations, wallet addresses, or access credentials. Security documentation is separate from legal documentation.

5. Future Bitcoin Acquisitions During Marriage

The prenup should specify how Bitcoin acquired during the marriage is treated. Options:

  • All Bitcoin acquired during marriage is marital/community property — default rule in most states; only specify if you want to confirm the default
  • Bitcoin acquired during marriage with separate property funds remains separate — requires tracing documentation; the prenup should specify record-keeping obligations
  • Bitcoin acquired as salary/bonus/income from marital employment is marital property; Bitcoin purchased with pre-marital funds that were kept separate is separate property — the most common hybrid approach
  • A fixed allocation — e.g., "50% of all Bitcoin acquired during marriage is marital property, 50% is [Party A]'s separate property" — simple but may not align with actual contribution patterns

6. Division Mechanism at Divorce

Standard prenups specify that property is divided in-kind or by sale. Bitcoin prenups need more specificity:

  • Bitcoin should be divided in-kind, not by forced sale — specify that any marital Bitcoin is divided by transferring a percentage of BTC directly, not by liquidating and dividing cash proceeds. Forced Bitcoin sales at divorce timing are unpredictable and may be tax-inefficient.
  • Valuation date — agree in advance on the valuation date for Bitcoin (date of separation? date of final decree? average of 30 days prior to date of separation?) — Bitcoin volatility makes this consequential.
  • Tax allocation — specify who bears the capital gains tax on any Bitcoin transferred incident to divorce. Under IRC §1041, transfers between spouses incident to divorce are generally not taxable events — but the receiving spouse takes the transferor's original cost basis. Clarify how embedded gain is factored into the division.
  • Exchange access transition — specify a timeline and process for transferring exchange account access, not just the Bitcoin balance.

7. Alimony / Spousal Support Waiver (If Applicable)

Many Bitcoin prenups include a waiver of spousal support (alimony) — particularly when one spouse has significant Bitcoin wealth and the other has comparable earning capacity. Courts scrutinize alimony waivers for unconscionability; a complete waiver is more vulnerable than a capped or time-limited provision. If alimony is addressed, include a "review trigger" — the parties agree to renegotiate the alimony provision if the financial circumstances at divorce are materially different from those at signing (e.g., if Bitcoin appreciates 50×, a blanket waiver may be struck as unconscionable).

Enforceability: What Makes a Bitcoin Prenup Hold Up in Court

A prenup that is not enforceable is worse than no prenup — it provides false confidence. Courts apply a consistent set of enforceability requirements that Bitcoin prenups must satisfy:

Full Financial Disclosure

Both parties must fully disclose their financial situation at the time of signing. For Bitcoin holders, this means disclosing the approximate value of Bitcoin holdings — including unrealized gains — even though this may feel uncomfortable from a security perspective. Failure to disclose can void the entire agreement. The disclosure goes into the prenup schedules; it does not need to include security-sensitive information like seed phrase locations.

Independent Legal Counsel

Both parties should have independent attorneys — ideally attorneys who have Bitcoin or digital asset experience. A prenup presented to the non-owning spouse by the owning spouse's attorney, without independent review, is a significant enforceability risk. Budget for both parties' legal fees; a well-advised counterpart makes the agreement more durable, not less.

Reasonable Time Before the Wedding

A prenup presented for signature three days before the wedding is presumptively coercive in many states. Courts have voided prenups signed under time pressure. Execute the prenup at least 30 days before the wedding; 60–90 days is better for complex Bitcoin provisions that require negotiation.

No Unconscionable Terms

Courts will not enforce prenup terms that are unconscionable — so one-sided that they shock the conscience. A prenup that designates all Bitcoin (pre-marital and marital) as separate property, waives all alimony, and requires the non-owning spouse to waive any financial claim in the event of divorce may be struck as unconscionable, particularly after a long marriage. The more extreme the provisions, the higher the risk. Include a "materiality threshold" — provisions only apply if the Bitcoin position at divorce is above a certain value, below which normal equitable distribution applies.

Voluntary Execution

Both parties must sign voluntarily, without duress. Document the voluntary nature: execute in the presence of both attorneys, include a recital in the agreement that each party had adequate time to review, sought independent counsel, and signs voluntarily.

The Postnuptial Agreement: If You're Already Married

Already married without a prenup? A postnuptial agreement covers the same ground but faces additional enforceability hurdles. Courts apply heightened scrutiny because:

  • Neither party can simply call off the wedding as leverage
  • The power dynamics within an established marriage may affect voluntariness
  • One spouse may have already contributed to the other's Bitcoin appreciation during the marriage

For a postnup to be enforceable, both parties should independently review it with separate counsel, both should receive meaningful consideration (not just a one-sided benefit), and both should execute it at a time of marital stability — not during conflict. A postnup executed during a period of marital crisis will not survive court challenge.

Despite the additional complexity, a postnup is far better than no agreement at all. If you are married and your Bitcoin has appreciated significantly since marriage, execute a postnup while the relationship is strong. Waiting until there is trouble is too late.

Prenup and Estate Plan Coordination

A Bitcoin prenup and a Bitcoin estate plan must be consistent. Common conflicts:

  • Prenup says Bitcoin is separate property; estate plan funds community trust with Bitcoin — if the prenup designates Bitcoin as separate but the estate plan treats it as jointly owned, the two documents conflict. Courts may use the conflict as grounds to challenge either.
  • Prenup waives survivor rights; state law gives surviving spouse elective share — most states allow a surviving spouse to claim an "elective share" of the estate regardless of what the will says. A prenup can waive the elective share — but must do so explicitly. If the prenup doesn't waive it, the surviving spouse can claim a statutory share of the estate even if excluded from the will.
  • Prenup is silent on death; estate plan is silent on divorce — the prenup governs divorce; the estate plan governs death. If the marriage ends in divorce, the prenup controls. If a spouse dies during marriage, the estate plan controls. Both documents need to address their respective scenarios — and be reviewed together by the same attorney team.

The Bitcoin Prenup Checklist

  1. ☐ Complete inventory of Bitcoin holdings at signing (quantity, approximate value, acquisition date) — attached as signed schedule
  2. ☐ Appreciation on pre-marital Bitcoin defined as separate property (explicitly including "active" appreciation)
  3. ☐ Mining income during marriage — characterization agreed and specified (separate, community, or percentage split)
  4. ☐ Future Bitcoin acquisitions during marriage — characterization specified by funding source
  5. ☐ Custody access provisions during marriage — who has access to what and on what terms
  6. ☐ Transfer restrictions during separation and divorce pendency
  7. ☐ Discovery and disclosure obligations for Bitcoin in divorce proceedings
  8. ☐ Division mechanism at divorce — in-kind transfer, not forced sale; valuation date specified
  9. ☐ Tax allocation for transfers incident to divorce (IRC §1041 treatment; basis carryover acknowledgment)
  10. ☐ Alimony/spousal support — addressed explicitly; review trigger included if extreme waiver
  11. ☐ Elective share waiver (if applicable to your state and desired)
  12. ☐ Full financial disclosure schedules completed and attached
  13. ☐ Both parties independently represented by attorneys with digital asset experience
  14. ☐ Executed at least 30 days before wedding; no time pressure
  15. ☐ Consistency review against existing estate plan documents

Common Mistakes in Bitcoin Prenuptial Agreements

Mistake 1: Using a Generic Template

Generic prenup templates do not address appreciation on volatile assets, mining income classification, self-custody access provisions, or in-kind division mechanics. A template prenup applied to Bitcoin holdings provides superficial protection — the provisions that matter most simply aren't there.

Mistake 2: Omitting the Inventory Schedule

A prenup that says "Bitcoin is separate property" without specifying which Bitcoin — how much, approximately when acquired, and where held — is extremely difficult to enforce. Courts need a baseline to determine whether specific Bitcoin at divorce is the same Bitcoin designated as separate in the prenup, or new Bitcoin acquired with community funds.

Mistake 3: Not Addressing Mining Income

Mining income during marriage is the most commonly overlooked Bitcoin prenup provision. In community property states, this is a significant default entitlement for the non-mining spouse. Even in common law states, income earned during marriage from marital labor is typically marital property. Silence on mining income means the default rules apply — which may not be what either party intends.

Mistake 4: Signing Under Time Pressure

A prenup presented in the week before the wedding — particularly one with complex Bitcoin provisions that the non-owning spouse hasn't had time to review with independent counsel — is exactly the kind of prenup that gets voided in court. Time pressure is coercive by definition. Start the prenup process 3–6 months before the wedding.

Mistake 5: Not Updating After Significant Bitcoin Appreciation

A prenup signed when Bitcoin was $10,000 may have included provisions that made sense at that valuation but are unconscionable at $100,000. If Bitcoin has appreciated significantly since signing, review the prenup with counsel and consider an amendment that updates the provisions to reflect current values. Courts assess unconscionability at the time of enforcement — what was fair at signing may not be fair 10 years later.
