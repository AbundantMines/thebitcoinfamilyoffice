Bitcoin Life Insurance Estate Planning: How to Pay a $2 Million Estate Tax Bill Without Selling a Single Satoshi

Federal estate tax is due nine months after death. There are no extensions for bear markets, no grace period because your Bitcoin is down 60%, no option to wait for a better price. If your estate owes $2 million in estate tax and your estate is primarily Bitcoin, one of three things happens: your heirs sell Bitcoin at whatever price happens to exist in those nine months, they borrow against it at potentially ruinous rates, or you planned ahead and have an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) that pays the bill with tax-free life insurance proceeds — and your heirs keep every satoshi.

Who Needs This: The Bitcoin Estate Tax Liquidity Problem

Federal estate tax applies to estates above the current exemption — $13.61 million per individual in 2024 (potentially modified by OBBBA legislation; verify current law). Below that threshold, no federal estate tax is owed. Above it, the tax rate is a flat 40% on the amount over the exemption.

State estate taxes have lower thresholds: Massachusetts and Oregon at approximately $2 million, Rhode Island at $1.7 million, Minnesota at $3 million. If you live in one of these states, the estate tax problem starts much sooner.

The liquidity problem is specifically acute for Bitcoin holders because:

  • Bitcoin has no income, no dividends, no coupon — it cannot fund its own estate tax bill the way a rental property or business generates cash flow
  • Bitcoin's price is highly volatile — a 60% bear market lasting 12–18 months can coincide exactly with the nine-month estate tax payment window
  • Bitcoin is psychologically difficult to liquidate for heirs who are also grieving — forced sales at emotionally and financially distressed times produce the worst outcomes
  • Bitcoin held in a dynasty trust or LLC may be illiquid even when the market is strong — accessing it requires trustee approval, exchange transfers, and custody processes that take time

The ILIT: How It Works

An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) is a trust that owns a life insurance policy on your life. Because the trust — not you — owns the policy, the death benefit is not included in your taxable estate. The trust receives the death benefit income-tax-free and estate-tax-free, and uses those proceeds to provide liquidity to your estate and heirs.

The Mechanics, Step by Step

  1. You establish the ILIT with a trustee (typically a professional trustee or trusted family member who is not you)
  2. The ILIT applies for and owns a life insurance policy on your life — you are the insured, the trust is the owner and beneficiary
  3. You fund annual premiums by making gifts to the ILIT — these gifts must go through Crummey notice procedures to qualify for the annual exclusion ($19,000/beneficiary/year)
  4. At your death, the insurance company pays the death benefit to the ILIT — income-tax-free under IRC §101; outside your taxable estate because you never owned the policy
  5. The trustee uses ILIT funds to provide estate liquidity — either by purchasing assets from your estate (giving the estate cash to pay taxes) or by making loans to your estate (repaid from other estate assets over time)
  6. Your heirs keep the Bitcoin — no forced sale, no bear-market liquidation, no nine-month panic
Why the ILIT must own the policy — not you: If you own a life insurance policy on your own life, the death benefit is included in your taxable estate (IRC §2042). A $3 million policy you own adds $3 million to your taxable estate — which then incurs 40% estate tax on the additional amount, requiring approximately $1.2 million MORE in insurance to cover the tax on the insurance. This circular problem is eliminated when the ILIT owns the policy — death benefits outside the estate, no circular tax problem.

Policy Types for Bitcoin Estate Planning

Second-to-Die (Survivorship) Life Insurance

The most commonly used policy for estate tax planning. A survivorship policy covers two lives (typically spouses) and pays the death benefit only after both insureds die. Because federal estate tax is generally not due until after the second spouse's death (the marital deduction defers estate tax for assets passing to a surviving spouse), the death benefit timing aligns perfectly with when the estate tax bill actually arrives.

Advantages for Bitcoin-holding couples:

  • Significantly lower premiums than two individual policies of the same total death benefit — because the insurer is betting on two lives, not one
  • One spouse being uninsurable does not disqualify the couple — the policy can often still be issued based on the other spouse's health if one is uninsurable
  • The death benefit arrives precisely when needed — after both spouses die and the estate tax becomes due

Individual Term and Permanent Policies

For unmarried Bitcoin holders, or for situations where estate tax exposure exists at the first death (not deferred by the marital deduction), individual policies are appropriate. Key considerations:

  • Universal life (UL) and indexed universal life (IUL) provide permanent coverage with flexible premiums and cash value accumulation — appropriate for estate tax planning where the need is permanent (the estate tax will eventually be due whenever you die)
  • Term life provides coverage for a specific period at lower cost — appropriate only if estate tax exposure is temporary (e.g., you plan to use exemption or gifting strategies that will reduce the taxable estate within the term period)
  • Whole life provides guaranteed death benefit, guaranteed cash value, and dividend participation — the most conservative choice; also the most expensive

Sizing the ILIT Policy: The Bitcoin Volatility Problem

Traditional estate tax ILIT sizing is straightforward: calculate the projected estate tax at death and buy a policy for that amount. For Bitcoin holders, the sizing problem is more complex because Bitcoin's value at death is unpredictable. Two approaches:

Scenario 1: Size for the Conservative Case

Assume Bitcoin's value at your death is lower than today — perhaps 50% of current price. Calculate estate tax on that conservative valuation. Buy a policy sized for that scenario. Rationale: if Bitcoin is higher, the estate has more liquidity and can better absorb estate tax without selling; the policy provides a floor for the worst case.

Scenario 2: Size for the Expected Growth Case

Assume Bitcoin appreciates significantly and model estate tax at a much higher Bitcoin valuation. Buy a larger policy. Rationale: estate tax is proportional to Bitcoin's value at death — if Bitcoin has appreciated 10× by then, estate tax may be 10× larger than today's projection. The policy must keep pace with projected Bitcoin appreciation to remain effective.

Most Bitcoin holders choose a middle path: size the policy conservatively today, with a rider or provision that allows increasing coverage without additional medical underwriting as Bitcoin appreciation warrants. This "guaranteed insurability" feature allows you to increase coverage over time without re-qualifying medically — important as Bitcoin's price (and therefore your estate tax exposure) grows.

Illustrative Sizing Example

Today: Bitcoin position worth $8M. Federal exemption $13.61M. No estate tax currently — but planning for growth.

10-year projection at moderate Bitcoin appreciation: Position worth $25M. Estate tax on $25M − $13.61M = $11.39M × 40% = $4.56M estate tax owed.

ILIT policy sized for: $5M second-to-die survivorship policy on the couple. Annual premium at ages 55/52 in good health: approximately $25,000–$40,000/year depending on insurer and policy type.

Without the ILIT: Heirs must sell Bitcoin worth $4.56M within 9 months of second parent's death — at whatever market price exists. If Bitcoin is in a bear cycle, they may sell 100,000+ sats at a 60% discount to fair value.

With the ILIT: Trustee receives $5M tax-free. Purchases assets from estate or loans cash to estate. Estate tax paid. Heirs keep the full Bitcoin position at its long-term appreciation value.

Crummey Notices for ILIT Premium Gifts

Gifts to the ILIT to fund insurance premiums must go through annual Crummey withdrawal notice procedures to qualify for the annual exclusion. Each beneficiary of the ILIT must receive written notice of their right to withdraw the gifted amount within a window (typically 30 days). After the window lapses, the funds are available for the trustee to use for premium payments.

For a couple with three children, the ILIT has five potential Crummey beneficiaries (two spouses cannot be beneficiaries of each other's ILIT, but children can be): $19,000 × 3 children = $57,000/year in premium gifts qualifying for the annual exclusion. For most second-to-die policies, this is sufficient to fund the entire premium without using lifetime exemption.

ILIT vs. Dynasty Trust: Coordination, Not Competition

Many Bitcoin holders have both a dynasty trust (holding Bitcoin) and an ILIT (holding life insurance). These structures serve different functions and should be coordinated:

Wyoming LLC + SD Dynasty TrustBitcoinLong-term wealth accumulation, estate exclusion, generational transfer0% SD trust income tax on accumulated gains; estate excluded if properly structured

The optimal integrated structure: Bitcoin grows in the dynasty trust (outside the estate, 0% trust income tax). At death, the estate tax bill is calculated on any Bitcoin remaining personally held (Bitcoin already in the dynasty trust is outside the estate). The ILIT pays the estate tax bill. Heirs receive: (1) Bitcoin already in dynasty trust (no estate tax, perpetual accumulation), (2) personally-held Bitcoin stepped up to current value (no capital gains tax on lifetime appreciation), and (3) ILIT life insurance proceeds to cover any estate tax owed.

State Estate Tax: Lower Thresholds Require Earlier Action

For residents of states with estate taxes at low thresholds — Rhode Island ($1.7M), Massachusetts ($2M), Oregon ($1M), Minnesota ($3M) — the ILIT planning conversation starts much sooner. A $2 million Bitcoin position in a Rhode Island domicile creates a potential $200,000–$400,000 state estate tax liability. An ILIT policy sized for the state estate tax (even if no federal estate tax is owed) provides the liquidity to pay the state bill without Bitcoin liquidation.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: The Three-Year Rule Violation

If you transfer an existing life insurance policy (one you already own) to an ILIT, the three-year rule applies: if you die within three years of the transfer, the death benefit is pulled back into your taxable estate. Always have the ILIT apply for and own the new policy from inception — never transfer an existing policy you own into an ILIT. If you die within three years of policy issuance, the insurer still pays the death benefit, but the IRS may include it in your estate. For new policies owned by the ILIT from the beginning, there is no three-year lookback.

Mistake 2: Incidents of Ownership

If you retain any "incidents of ownership" over the life insurance policy — the right to change beneficiaries, borrow against cash value, surrender the policy, or control any aspect of the policy — the death benefit is included in your estate regardless of trust ownership. The ILIT trustee must have independent control of the policy; you have no ongoing relationship with it other than funding the premiums through annual gifts.

Mistake 3: Failing to Update for Bitcoin Appreciation

A policy sized when Bitcoin was at $30,000 may be completely inadequate when Bitcoin is at $200,000 and your estate has grown from $10M to $50M. Annual reviews of ILIT coverage against current Bitcoin valuation and projected estate tax are essential. Many permanent life insurance policies allow increasing the death benefit (subject to underwriting) or purchasing additional paid-up insurance — use these features to keep coverage in line with Bitcoin appreciation.

Mistake 4: Wrong Policy Type for the Timeline

Term life insurance for estate planning purposes is rarely appropriate unless the estate tax exposure is temporary. If Bitcoin continues to appreciate, your estate tax exposure is a permanent feature of your estate — it doesn't go away after 20 years. A permanent policy (whole life, universal life, survivorship life) provides coverage for as long as you live and premiums are paid.

10-Item ILIT Checklist for Bitcoin Holders

  • Calculate current estate tax exposure — is your estate (including Bitcoin) above the federal exemption ($13.61M/individual) or any applicable state estate tax threshold?
  • Project Bitcoin appreciation — model your estate at 5× and 10× current Bitcoin price; size the ILIT policy for the growth scenario, not just today's valuation
  • Establish the ILIT before applying for insurance — the trust must exist and be the applicant/owner from day one; never transfer an existing personally-owned policy into the ILIT
  • Select a trustee who is not you or your spouse — a professional trustee or trusted adult child with no incidents of ownership issues
  • Apply for a second-to-die survivorship policy if married — lower premiums, timing aligned with when estate tax actually becomes due
  • Set up Crummey notice procedures — send written notice to all trust beneficiaries within 10 days of each premium gift; maintain copies permanently
  • Coordinate ILIT with dynasty trust — Bitcoin in the dynasty trust is outside your estate; ILIT covers estate tax on any Bitcoin still held personally at death
  • Request guaranteed insurability rider — allows increasing coverage without additional underwriting as Bitcoin appreciation warrants
  • Review annually — run estate tax projection with current Bitcoin price every year; adjust coverage if needed
  • Document coordination instructions for executor — the executor and ILIT trustee must know to coordinate at death: ILIT trustee purchases assets from estate or makes loans to fund estate tax payment

Bitcoin Mining: Reduce the Estate Tax Problem at the Source

The smaller your taxable estate at death, the smaller the estate tax bill — and the less ILIT coverage you need. Bitcoin mining bonus depreciation reduces ordinary income during your accumulation years. Charitable giving of appreciated Bitcoin reduces estate size. Mining tax strategy and estate planning work together to minimize the total estate tax exposure that the ILIT must cover.
