What the OBBBA Changed: The Bitcoin Tax Landscape in 2026

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 was a ten-year experiment with broad tax reform — most of its provisions were explicitly set to expire December 31, 2025. For Bitcoin families who built estate plans, business structures, and tax strategies around TCJA rules, that expiration date represented a potential cliff: higher estate tax rates, lower exemptions, reduced depreciation, and the elimination of the Section 199A deduction.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), enacted in 2025, averted that cliff in most respects. It extended, modified, and in some cases made permanent several TCJA provisions. For Bitcoin families, the relevant changes fall into three categories: estate and transfer tax, business income tax, and capital gains and investment income.

Understanding what changed — and specifically what the changes mean for Bitcoin — requires going provision by provision. The headline "TCJA extended" is accurate but insufficient. Several provisions changed in ways that create new planning opportunities, and the question of permanence versus further-extension risk means acting in 2026 rather than waiting is the right posture for most families.

The planning principle for 2026: Favorable tax law is not a reason to delay — it is a reason to act. Every year where the estate tax exemption is high, the gifting rules are generous, and the income tax environment is known is a year where permanent wealth transfers can be locked in at today's values and today's rules. Don't assume the current environment will persist indefinitely.

Change 1: The $15M Estate Tax Exemption

The single most significant OBBBA change for wealthy Bitcoin families is the estate tax exemption. Under pre-OBBBA TCJA, the federal estate tax exemption was approximately $13.99M per individual ($27.98M per couple with portability) — already historically high due to inflation indexing from the 2017 base of $11.18M. OBBBA increased this to approximately $15M per individual / $30M per couple, indexed for inflation going forward.

At the 40% estate tax rate, the difference between a $13.99M and $15M exemption represents $404,000 in additional estate tax shielded per individual — or $808,000 per couple. That is not the primary planning opportunity, however. The primary opportunity is the window itself: the combination of high exemption and current Bitcoin valuations creates conditions for permanent wealth transfer that have rarely existed simultaneously.

Why Bitcoin Price and Exemption Level Matter Together

Bitcoin is currently trading at approximately $70,000-$75,000 — roughly 44% below the $126,000 all-time high reached in early 2026. For estate planning purposes, this is a gift: transfers made at depressed values use less exemption for the same quantity of Bitcoin. A family transferring 100 BTC to a dynasty trust today uses approximately $7M-$7.5M of exemption. If Bitcoin returns to $126,000, those same 100 BTC are worth $12.6M — but the exemption used was $7M-$7.5M. The appreciation above the transfer value escapes all estate tax.

This is the core argument for acting in 2026 rather than waiting: high exemption + depressed Bitcoin price = maximum leverage per dollar of exemption used.

Change 2: Bonus Depreciation Restored

TCJA introduced 100% first-year bonus depreciation for qualified property placed in service after September 27, 2017. Starting in 2023, bonus depreciation began phasing down: 80% in 2023, 60% in 2024, 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026 (under TCJA's original schedule). Without OBBBA, bonus depreciation would have been entirely eliminated after 2026.

OBBBA addressed this phase-down. For Bitcoin mining operators and other capital-intensive businesses, this is the second most significant OBBBA change. The specific bonus depreciation rate in effect for 2026 under OBBBA should be confirmed with your CPA for the current tax year — the legislation modified the phase-down schedule, and the applicable rate depends on when property was placed in service.

The planning implication: if you have been deferring equipment purchases while waiting for legislative clarity on bonus depreciation, 2026 is the year to act. Mining operators who purchase ASIC hardware in 2026 under the restored (or modified) bonus depreciation rules can offset a substantial portion of current-year mining income in the year of acquisition — deferring taxes that would otherwise be due now.

The 2026 equipment window: If OBBBA restored higher bonus depreciation rates for 2026, purchasing mining equipment before December 31st captures that rate for the entire acquisition. Equipment placed in service on December 31, 2026 receives the same 2026 bonus rate as equipment placed in service January 1, 2026. Do not let year-end slip without confirming whether outstanding equipment purchase decisions should be accelerated.

Change 3: Section 199A QBI Deduction Extended

The Section 199A qualified business income deduction — the 20% deduction on pass-through business income — was explicitly set to expire December 31, 2025 under TCJA. For the approximately 25 million pass-through business owners in the United States, this was the largest single pending tax cliff event since the original TCJA sunset.

OBBBA extended the Section 199A deduction. Bitcoin mining operators who qualify — and most do, as mining is a trade or business not subject to the SSTB exclusion — can continue to deduct up to 20% of net qualified business income in 2026. At the top marginal rate of 37%, this represents an effective tax rate of approximately 29.6% on mining income rather than 37%.

For a mining operation generating $2M in net annual income, the QBI deduction is worth approximately $148,000 in annual federal tax savings. Extended over multiple years, this is one of the highest-value recurring tax provisions available to mining families.

Move 1: Use the Gifting Window Before It Narrows

The combination of the OBBBA $15M exemption and the current Bitcoin price creates a gifting window that may not persist. Several risk factors could narrow or close this window before year-end:

  • Bitcoin price recovery: If Bitcoin returns to $100,000+ before year-end, the same quantity of Bitcoin uses substantially more exemption for the same number of coins transferred. The window to transfer BTC at depressed prices is open now — it closes as price recovers.
  • Legislative risk: OBBBA extended the exemption, but future legislation could reduce it. While the near-term legislative calendar does not suggest a cut, the political environment is volatile. Transfers made now are protected by the anti-clawback regulations even if the exemption is later reduced.
  • GST exemption: The generation-skipping transfer (GST) exemption is unified with the estate tax exemption at $15M under OBBBA. Allocating GST exemption to dynasty trust transfers now locks in $15M of GST coverage at current values — if the exemption is later reduced, already-allocated GST exemption is not recaptured.

The Specific Gifting Actions for 2026

Annual exclusion gifts: The annual gift tax exclusion for 2026 is approximately $19,000 per recipient. A married couple can give $38,000 per recipient per year without touching lifetime exemption. For Bitcoin families with multiple children and grandchildren, this can move $100,000–$500,000 out of the estate annually at zero gift tax cost. These gifts must be made by December 31st — there is no retroactive gifting.

Superfunding a 529: Five-year front-loading of 529 contributions ($95,000 per beneficiary from a single contributor, or $190,000 from a married couple) can move significant assets out of the estate for education funding. Bitcoin families with grandchildren can make this transfer using other liquid assets, preserving Bitcoin within the estate plan for more tax-efficient vehicles.

Irrevocable trust funding: Contributions to a dynasty trust, GRAT, SLAT, or IDGT before December 31st use 2026 exemption at 2026 values. Trust funding completed in 2026 is protected — the transferred assets and their appreciation are permanently outside the estate regardless of future exemption changes.

Move 2: Execute Trust Transfers While Exemption Is High

The most powerful 2026 planning move for Bitcoin families with estates above $5M is executing or funding irrevocable trust structures that use the high exemption. Several options, ranked by complexity and asset transfer efficiency:

GRAT: Transfer Appreciation, Retain Control

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) transfers future Bitcoin appreciation out of the estate at minimal gift tax cost. You fund the GRAT with Bitcoin, receive an annuity stream back for a term of years, and the remaining appreciation passes to heirs tax-free. At current Bitcoin prices and historically low Section 7520 rates, even modest appreciation produces large tax-free transfers.

The 2026 planning angle: fund a new GRAT now while Bitcoin is at $70,000-$75,000. If Bitcoin recovers to prior highs within the 2-3 year GRAT term, the entire appreciation above the 7520 rate passes estate-tax-free to the remainder beneficiaries — using very little (or zero) lifetime exemption.

Dynasty Trust: Permanent Transfer, Multi-Generational

A dynasty trust funded in 2026 with an allocation of GST exemption creates a permanent, multi-generational tax shelter for the transferred Bitcoin and all its future appreciation. The $15M GST exemption under OBBBA can shelter up to $15M per grantor ($30M per couple) in today's dollars — and the entire appreciation above today's value is sheltered forever.

A family transferring 200 BTC ($14.4M) to a dynasty trust in March 2026 uses $14.4M of combined exemption. If Bitcoin reaches $300,000 in the next decade (a price level many serious analysts project based on halving cycles and institutional adoption), those 200 BTC are worth $60M — all sheltered. The $45.6M in appreciation above the transfer value never enters the estate.

IDGT Sale: Transfer Without Gift Tax

An Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) sale allows the transfer of Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust in exchange for an installment note at the Section 7520 rate — without gift tax, and without using exemption for the principal amount. Only the "seed" gift (typically 10% of the note amount) uses exemption. For families who have already used significant exemption, the IDGT sale is a way to transfer large amounts of Bitcoin without additional exemption usage.

Move 3: Equipment and Mining Decisions Before December 31st

For Bitcoin mining operators, the 2026 tax law creates specific year-end decisions around equipment and entity structure:

Equipment Purchase Timing

Under OBBBA's modified bonus depreciation rules, equipment placed in service in 2026 receives the applicable 2026 bonus depreciation rate. A decision made in Q4 2026 about whether to purchase new ASICs before or after December 31st carries significant tax implications. Confirm the applicable 2026 rate with your CPA before Q4, and model the after-tax impact of purchasing in 2026 versus 2027.

The general principle: if you intend to purchase equipment in the next 12 months and the 2026 bonus rate exceeds the expected 2027 rate, accelerating the purchase before December 31st captures the higher deduction. One year's difference in timing can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in tax deferral.

S-Corp Election Deadline

To elect S-Corporation status for tax year 2026, the election must generally be made by March 15, 2026 (for calendar-year taxpayers already in operation). If your mining LLC is not yet an S-Corp and you want S-Corp treatment to capture the QBI deduction and self-employment tax savings, the March 15th deadline is critical. If that has passed, the election can be made for tax year 2027 by March 15, 2027.

New LLCs formed in 2026 can still elect S-Corp status for the 2026 tax year if the election is filed within 75 days of formation. If you are forming a new mining entity in 2026, confirm the S-Corp election timing with your attorney.

Year-End Mining Income Management

December is the final opportunity to execute income-shifting strategies that reduce 2026 taxable mining income:

  • Tax-loss harvesting on personally held Bitcoin to offset 2026 mining income (no wash sale rule)
  • Charitable contributions of appreciated Bitcoin (eliminates capital gain, generates deduction at fair market value)
  • Section 179 elections on qualifying equipment to maximize current-year deductions
  • Roth IRA conversion planning — converting traditional IRA assets to Roth while ordinary income bracket room exists
  • Estimated tax payment review — ensure Q4 2026 estimated tax payment (due January 15, 2027) is sized correctly given actual mining income for the year

What to Watch in the Second Half of 2026

The 2026 tax environment is favorable but not guaranteed to remain so. Several developments could reshape planning priorities before year-end:

CLARITY Act and stablecoin legislation: The CLARITY Act and GENIUS Act stablecoin legislation are working through Congress. Passage would clarify digital asset custody rules — potentially affecting qualified custodian requirements for SDIRA holders and institutional trust structures. Monitor progress; passage before year-end could affect trust structure decisions.

IRS digital asset guidance: The IRS continues to issue guidance on cryptocurrency tax treatment, including Form 1099-DA reporting (now live for the first time in 2026 for broker transactions), staking income characterization, and DeFi protocols. New guidance could create planning opportunities or close existing strategies.

Bitcoin price volatility and estate planning reset points: Every significant Bitcoin price move — up or down — creates a potential planning decision. A sharp decline creates a gifting window (transfer more BTC for the same exemption usage). A sharp increase accelerates the urgency of trust transfers before further appreciation. Build "trigger" price levels into your estate plan — predetermined Bitcoin prices at which you will execute specific transfers — rather than making ad hoc decisions under market pressure.

OBBBA permanence versus expiration: OBBBA extended TCJA provisions, but political environments change. No current-law provision is guaranteed indefinitely. The families who act in 2026 are protecting themselves from future legislative risk, not just capturing today's opportunities.

Year-End Bitcoin Tax Planning Checklist for 2026

  • Calculate remaining lifetime exemption. Determine exactly how much of your $15M individual / $30M joint exemption has been used by prior taxable gifts. The unused balance is your 2026 planning capacity. This number drives every trust funding and gifting decision.
  • Model GRAT funding at current Bitcoin price. Run the GRAT analysis at today's BTC price to see how much appreciation a new GRAT would shelter at the current Section 7520 rate. If the projected benefit is material, initiate trust drafting now — waiting until Q4 compresses the drafting timeline unnecessarily.
  • Review annual exclusion gift list. Identify all family members (and 529 accounts) who can receive $19,000 gifts this year. Calculate total transferable amount. Schedule the transfers — Bitcoin or cash — before December 31st.
  • Confirm S-Corp election status for mining entity. If your mining LLC is not taxed as an S-Corp and your net mining income exceeds $200,000, model the 2026 QBI and self-employment tax savings from an S-Corp election. For 2027 elections, the filing deadline is March 15, 2027.
  • Plan equipment purchases before December 31st. If you intend to purchase mining equipment in the next 12 months, confirm the applicable 2026 bonus depreciation rate and model whether accelerating the purchase before year-end generates a material tax deferral advantage.
  • Execute tax-loss harvesting before December 31st. Review all Bitcoin positions held personally (outside SDIRAs). Any positions with unrealized losses can be harvested — sold and immediately repurchased, with no wash sale restriction — generating loss deductions that offset 2026 income.
  • Review and update beneficiary designations. OBBBA changes to estate tax exemption levels may affect the optimal allocation of assets between different beneficiary categories. Review IRA and life insurance beneficiary designations to confirm they align with your updated estate plan.
  • Schedule a year-end estate plan review with counsel. The 2026 tax changes are complex enough that a dedicated conversation with your estate planning attorney — not a brief mention in a tax return review — is warranted. Schedule it for Q3 2026 at the latest, not December, to allow adequate time for trust drafting and execution.

The Bottom Line

2026 is not a year to monitor — it is a year to act. The OBBBA-extended $15M exemption, the depressed Bitcoin price relative to its all-time high, the restored bonus depreciation for mining operators, and the preserved Section 199A QBI deduction create a confluence of favorable conditions that may not persist simultaneously in future years.

The families who look back on 2026 favorably will be the ones who funded dynasty trusts while Bitcoin was at $70,000, harvested tax losses during the correction, ran GRATs timed to capture the next price cycle, and locked in S-Corp structures to capture the QBI deduction going forward. These are not exotic strategies — they are established planning tools being applied at an unusually favorable moment.

The families who do nothing in 2026 may face a very different landscape in 2027 or 2028: higher Bitcoin prices (more exemption used per coin transferred), potentially revised legislation (lower exemptions or fewer deductions), and the accumulated cost of a year's worth of foregone tax-free compounding in dynasty trusts and GRATs that were never funded.

The window is open. The clock is running.
