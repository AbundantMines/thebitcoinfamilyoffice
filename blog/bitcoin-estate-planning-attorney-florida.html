Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney: What Florida Holders Need to Know

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  8 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning: Bitcoin family office in Florida Est. 8 min read

Florida has become a premier destination for high-net-worth Bitcoin families — and for good reason. No state income tax, no state estate tax, an unlimited homestead exemption, and a modern trust code that supports sophisticated digital asset planning create a legal environment that is genuinely favorable for Bitcoin wealth accumulation and transfer. But favorable conditions don't replace the need for deliberate planning. Finding a Bitcoin-literate estate attorney in Florida is essential — and not every estate attorney qualifies.

This guide explains Florida's specific legal landscape for Bitcoin holders and what to look for when selecting qualified legal counsel. This is educational content only — we do not recommend specific attorneys.

No State Estate Tax, No State Income Tax

Florida imposes no state estate tax. The state's estate tax was linked to the federal state death tax credit under IRC §2011, which was phased out in 2005. Florida has not enacted a standalone replacement, and its constitution makes it difficult to do so without voter approval. For Bitcoin families domiciled in Florida, the only estate tax exposure is federal — the $13.99 million per-person exemption in 2025, with portability for married couples.

Florida also has no state income tax, a protection enshrined in the Florida Constitution (Article VII, Section 5). This is not a statute that can be repealed by a simple legislative majority — a constitutional amendment requiring a 60% voter Bitcoin family office minimum requirements would be required. For Bitcoin holders realizing gains through sales, portfolio management, or trust distributions, there is no state capital gains tax layer in Florida. Federal rates apply, but the state adds nothing.

The combined effect: Florida Bitcoin families face only federal tax at both the income and estate levels, with no state overlay. This makes Florida one of the cleanest tax environments available to Bitcoin holders anywhere in the U.S.

Florida's Homestead Exemption and Asset Protection Culture

Florida's homestead exemption (Article X, Section 4 of the Florida Constitution) protects a Florida resident's primary residence from forced sale by creditors — without any dollar cap. A $10 million home is as protected as a $300,000 home, provided the resident meets the homestead requirements. This unlimited protection is among the strongest in any U.S. jurisdiction and reflects Florida's broader legal culture of robust asset protection.

For Bitcoin holders, this culture extends into trust law. Florida's Qualified Spendthrift Trust (Florida Statute §736.0505(3)) allows a grantor to be a discretionary beneficiary of their own irrevocable trust while maintaining spendthrift protection against creditors — a self-settled asset protection trust available within Florida. This structure requires a Florida-resident trustee and satisfies other conditions, but offers meaningful protection for grantors who fund the trust and retain beneficial access.

Tenancy by the entirety protection (Florida Statute §689.115) also allows married couples to hold property — including financial assets — jointly with strong creditor protection. For Bitcoin held in joint exchange accounts or jointly-owned LLCs, tenancy by the entirety may provide an additional protection layer for Florida married couples.

Florida Is NOT a Community Property State

This is a critical distinction for Florida Bitcoin holders, particularly those relocating from Bitcoin family office in Texas, California, Arizona, or other community property states. Florida is an equitable distribution state. Property acquired during marBitcoin family office vs. RIA comparisonge is not automatically co-owned 50/50 by both spouses. Instead, each spouse generally owns the property they individually acquire — and in a divorce, assets are divided "equitably" (not necessarily equally) based on the circumstances.

The practical implications for Bitcoin estate planning:

  • Bitcoin purchased by one spouse in Florida with their own funds is generally that spouse's separate property — not automatically half-owned by the other spouse as it would be in Texas or California.
  • Florida married couples do not get the "double step-up" in basis that community property states provide at the first spouse's death. Only the decedent's share of jointly held assets (if jointly held) receives a step-up — the surviving spouse's share does not.
  • If a couple relocates from a community property state to Florida, the community property character of assets acquired in the prior state generally follows them — but Florida law governs the treatment of assets acquired after domicile changes to Florida.

This difference in marital property law affects how Florida couples should title their Bitcoin, what Bitcoin Trust Type Selector tools make sense, and how basis planning should be coordinated. A Florida estate attorney who does not clearly understand the community property vs. equitable distribution distinction is not adequately equipped for this work.

Florida Trust Code and RUFADAA

Florida adopted its version of the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA) in 2016, codified at Florida Statutes §§740.001–740.10. Under Florida's RUFADAA, a trustee has legal authority to manage digital assets — including Bitcoin — when the governing trust document expressly grants that authority, or when the decedent authorized access through an online tool of a digital asset custodian.

For self-custodied Bitcoin held in a hardware wallet, the trust document's express grant of authority is the primary mechanism. Florida trust attorneys drafting documents for Bitcoin holders must include digital asset authority provisions that are explicit, not implied. Standard templates drafted before the digital asset era — or by attorneys unfamiliar with Bitcoin — frequently omit these provisions entirely.

The Florida Trust Code (Chapter 736, Florida Statutes) provides strong foundations for trust formation and administration. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin families include directed trust authority (§736.0807), trust decanting (§736.04117), and spendthrift protection (§736.0502). Florida's 360-year limit on trust duration (for trusts created after July 1, 2020) is more permissive than many states but still shorter than Bitcoin family office in Wyoming or South Dakota's perpetual trust provisions — a consideration for families with multigenerational horizons.

What to Look for in a Florida Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney

  • Explicit RUFADAA knowledge. The attorney should be able to draft express digital asset authority provisions and explain why Florida's statutory framework matters for trustee access to self-custodied Bitcoin.
  • Understanding of equitable distribution vs. community property. Florida's marital property rules differ from community property states in ways that directly affect Bitcoin titling, basis planning, and spousal rights. This is not an optional area of knowledge.
  • Familiarity with directed trust structures. Florida's directed trust statute allows investment direction authority to be separated from the trustee role. For Bitcoin families who want operational control over custody decisions, this structure is directly relevant — and the attorney should understand how to implement it.
  • Experience with out-of-state trust siting. Many Florida Bitcoin families benefit from trust situs in Wyoming or South Dakota — states with perpetual trust provisions, superior directed trust infrastructure, and a growing network of Bitcoin-native custodians. A Florida attorney capable of designing a multi-state trust structure (Florida domicile + Wyoming situs) is operating at the highest level of this practice.
  • Practical custody awareness. The attorney should understand the operational realities of Bitcoin custody — hardware wallets, multi-signature setups, exchange custody — and draft documents that work within those realities, not around them.

Key Planning Priorities for Florida Bitcoin Holders

  • Establish a revocable living trust with explicit digital asset and RUFADAA provisions. Florida probate can be time-consuming and expensive for estates without proper trust planning.
  • Understand the equitable distribution implications for your Bitcoin — particularly if relocating from a community property state where your Bitcoin may retain community property character.
  • Explore Qualified Spendthrift Trust structures if creditor protection is a priority alongside estate planning.
  • For estates approaching the federal exemption, evaluate irrevocable trust options — potentially sited in Wyoming — in coordination with your Florida attorney.
  • Create and maintain a Letter of Instruction that provides your successor trustee operational access to Bitcoin custody without exposing seed phrases in legal documents.
