Bitcoin Exchange Bankruptcy: What Happens to Your Heirs' BTC (and How to Plan Around It)

FTX proved that exchange-held Bitcoin is a credit claim, not property. Here's what that means for your estate plan — and how to protect your heirs from becoming unsecured creditors.

March 16, 2026

In November 2022, FTX collapsed in a matter of days. Over one million customers woke up to discover that the Bitcoin they thought they owned was gone — not stolen in the traditional sense, but legally inaccessible, frozen inside a bankruptcy estate controlled by a court-appointed trustee. The customers who held BTC at FTX didn't get their Bitcoin back. They received claims against an insolvent company. Three years later, those claims settled at approximately $1.17 per dollar of the November 2022 value — not the 2025 recovery price.

For most FTX customers, this was an expensive lesson in counterparty risk. For families engaged in Bitcoin estate planning, it should have been a fire alarm.

Because here's the question almost nobody asked during the FTX fallout: what happens when the person holding Bitcoin at a failed exchange is dead?

What happens when a Bitcoin holder passes away, their BTC sits at an exchange, and that exchange subsequently goes bankrupt? What rights do heirs have? What can an executor actually do? And does the entire estate plan — the trust, the beneficiary designations, the careful documentation — survive a bankruptcy proceeding?

The answers are sobering. And if your family holds significant Bitcoin at any exchange, they should reshape how you think about custody architecture within your estate plan.

The Credit Risk Most Bitcoin Holders Ignore

When you hold Bitcoin in your own wallet — a hardware device, a multi-signature setup, even a paper wallet — you hold actual Bitcoin. You control the private keys. The Bitcoin is yours in the most fundamental sense: no intermediary can prevent you from transacting with it.

When you hold Bitcoin at an exchange — Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, Binance, or any other — you do not hold Bitcoin. You hold a credit claim against that exchange. The exchange holds Bitcoin (or claims to), and you have an account balance that represents your share of whatever pool they maintain. The legal relationship is closer to a bank deposit than a safe deposit box, with one critical difference: bank deposits are insured by the FDIC. Exchange balances are not insured by anything comparable.

This distinction — property versus credit claim — is not a technicality. It is the single most important legal fact in Bitcoin estate planning, and it determines everything that happens when an exchange fails.

If you hold actual Bitcoin and you die, your executor accesses it using the credentials and instructions you've left behind. The Bitcoin transfers to your heirs. Full stop.

If you hold a credit claim against an exchange and you die, your executor must work with the exchange to transfer or liquidate the account. This is already more complex than self-custody. But if the exchange itself fails — before, during, or after the estate settlement process — the complexity becomes extraordinary.

What the FTX Bankruptcy Actually Taught Us

FTX filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on November 11, 2022, with an estimated $8 billion gap between customer deposits and available assets. The details that emerged were staggering: customer funds commingled with Alameda Research's trading operations, billions in unauthorized loans, and a governance structure that amounted to a single person making decisions with no oversight.

But for estate planning purposes, the mechanics of the fraud matter less than the mechanics of the resolution.

When FTX entered bankruptcy, every customer account was frozen. No withdrawals. No transfers. No ability to move Bitcoin to cold storage. Customers became unsecured creditors — a legal status that placed them behind secured creditors and administrative expenses in the bankruptcy priority waterfall.

The bankruptcy estate eventually recovered enough assets to pay customers approximately 118 cents on the dollar — based on the value of their holdings at the time of bankruptcy in November 2022, when Bitcoin traded around $16,000. By the time distributions began in 2025, Bitcoin had recovered to well above $80,000. A customer who held 10 BTC at FTX received a claim valued at roughly $160,000 — not the $800,000-plus those 10 BTC would have been worth on the open market.

The 118% recovery sounds generous until you realize the math: customers received dollars based on a bear market price while missing an entire bull cycle. The opportunity cost was enormous. And that was in a case where the bankruptcy estate performed unusually well. Most exchange failures don't end this cleanly.

Mt. Gox: The Decade-Long Case Study

If FTX was a three-year ordeal, Mt. Gox was a generational one.

Mt. Gox, once the world's largest Bitcoin exchange, filed for bankruptcy in February 2014 after announcing that approximately 850,000 BTC had been lost to theft. At the time, those coins were worth roughly $450 million. By the time creditors received partial distributions beginning in 2024 — ten years later — Bitcoin's price had multiplied by more than a hundred times.

The Mt. Gox creditors who eventually received BTC back did benefit from the appreciation. But the timeline tells the real story for estate planning:

  • Creditors who died during the decade-long bankruptcy process left their claims to heirs, who then had to navigate both probate and an international bankruptcy proceeding in Japanese courts
  • Some creditors sold their claims to distressed debt buyers at steep discounts during the wait, capturing only a fraction of eventual recovery
  • The legal costs of maintaining and eventually collecting on a bankruptcy claim across a ten-year timeline were substantial
  • Heirs of deceased creditors faced additional documentation requirements to prove succession rights in a foreign jurisdiction

The lesson from Mt. Gox isn't that exchange failures are always catastrophic. Some creditors did eventually receive valuable Bitcoin. The lesson is that the timeline of a bankruptcy proceeding — years, sometimes a decade — is fundamentally incompatible with the timeline of estate settlement and generational wealth transfer.

What Happens to Heirs When an Exchange Goes Bankrupt During Estate Settlement

Consider a specific scenario that no estate planning attorney wants to confront.

A Bitcoin holder — call him David — dies with 50 BTC held at a US-based exchange. His estate plan is well-drafted. His revocable trust names his two children as beneficiaries. His executor has documentation of the exchange account and login credentials. Everything looks manageable.

Six months into the estate settlement process, while the executor is still working through probate, the exchange announces insolvency and files for bankruptcy.

Here is what happens next:

The estate becomes a creditor in the bankruptcy. David's 50 BTC are no longer accessible as an account balance. The estate holds an unsecured claim against the bankruptcy estate of the exchange. The executor must file a proof of claim with the bankruptcy court, documenting the account balance and asserting the estate's right to recovery.

The executor cannot move the Bitcoin. From the moment the exchange enters bankruptcy, all customer assets are under the control of a bankruptcy trustee. The executor has no ability to withdraw, transfer, or move the BTC to cold storage. Even if the executor has full login credentials and had planned to transfer the Bitcoin to a hardware wallet, it's too late.

The claim may take years to resolve. Bankruptcy proceedings for financial services companies routinely take three to seven years. Mt. Gox took ten. During this entire period, the estate cannot distribute the Bitcoin or its equivalent value to the beneficiaries. The estate may need to remain open — incurring ongoing legal and administrative costs — until the bankruptcy claim resolves.

Heirs may receive dollars, not Bitcoin. Bankruptcy distributions are typically made in dollars, not in-kind Bitcoin. Even if the bankruptcy estate holds BTC, the trustee may liquidate it to simplify distribution. Heirs expecting to receive Bitcoin may instead receive a dollar amount calculated at the bankruptcy petition date value — potentially missing years of appreciation.

The estate plan's carefully structured tax treatment may be disrupted. If David's trust was designed to hold Bitcoin long-term and pass it to heirs with a stepped-up cost basis, receiving a cash distribution from a bankruptcy estate changes the tax analysis entirely. The heirs receive ordinary income or capital gains depending on the characterization of the bankruptcy distribution, not the favorable estate tax treatment of inherited Bitcoin.

Why Beneficiary Designations Don't Protect You

Many exchanges now offer beneficiary designation features, and some estate planning guides recommend using them as a simple way to transfer exchange-held Bitcoin to heirs outside of probate. In normal circumstances, these designations work similarly to beneficiary designations on brokerage or bank accounts — the named beneficiary contacts the exchange, provides a death certificate, and receives the account assets.

In bankruptcy, beneficiary designations may be worthless.

When an exchange enters bankruptcy, the automatic stay provisions of the Bankruptcy Code (11 U.S.C. § 362) freeze all assets. A bankruptcy trustee takes control of the exchange's assets — including all customer accounts. The trustee's obligation is to the bankruptcy estate and all creditors, not to individual account holders' beneficiary designations.

This is not theoretical. In every major exchange bankruptcy to date, customer assets have been pooled and distributed through the bankruptcy process, not through individual account-level designations. A beneficiary designation on a Coinbase account, for example, would not allow a named beneficiary to withdraw Bitcoin ahead of other creditors if Coinbase entered bankruptcy.

The same logic applies to Transfer on Death (TOD) designations. While TOD accounts are well-established for traditional brokerage accounts under the Uniform Transfer on Death Security Registration Act, their application to cryptocurrency exchange accounts has never been tested in a major US bankruptcy. There is no legal precedent establishing that a TOD designation on a crypto exchange account would survive a bankruptcy proceeding.

Similarly, if a revocable trust is the named owner of an exchange account, the trust's ownership claim converts to a creditor claim in bankruptcy. The trust doesn't have a senior position. It stands in line with every other unsecured creditor.

How Exchanges Protect Themselves — Not You

Read the terms of service of any major cryptocurrency exchange. Buried in the legal language, you will find provisions that explicitly establish the exchange's position in exactly these scenarios.

Common terms of service provisions include:

  • You are an unsecured creditor. Most exchanges' terms explicitly state that in the event of insolvency, customers' claims are treated as unsecured credit claims against the company. You do not have a property interest in specific Bitcoin held by the exchange.
  • Assets may be commingled. Many exchanges reserve the right to pool customer assets rather than maintaining segregated wallets for each customer. This means "your" Bitcoin is part of a general pool, and in bankruptcy, the pool is divided among all creditors — not returned to individual customers based on their account balances.
  • The exchange may use customer assets. Some exchanges' terms — FTX's infamously so — permit the company to use customer deposits for operational purposes, lending, or liquidity management. Even exchanges that don't explicitly claim this right may engage in rehypothecation of customer assets in ways that aren't transparent.
  • Limitation of liability. Virtually every exchange limits its liability for losses, including losses due to insolvency, to the amount of fees paid by the customer — not the value of the assets held.

These provisions are not accidental. They are drafted by attorneys who understand exactly how bankruptcy proceedings work, and they are designed to protect the exchange's equity holders and management — not customers.

The Insurance Gap: No SIPC, No FDIC, Limited Private Coverage

In traditional finance, customer assets at brokerages are protected by the Securities Investor Protection Corporation (SIPC), which covers up to $500,000 per customer if a brokerage fails. Bank deposits are protected by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) up to $250,000 per depositor.

Bitcoin held at cryptocurrency exchanges has neither protection.

SIPC coverage applies exclusively to securities — stocks, bonds, and other instruments registered under the Securities Exchange Act. Bitcoin is not a security (at least not under current SEC guidance for Bitcoin itself), and cryptocurrency exchanges are not SIPC member firms. If your exchange fails, SIPC doesn't answer the phone.

FDIC insurance applies to deposits at FDIC-insured banks. Cryptocurrency exchanges are not banks. Some exchanges have partnered with banks to offer FDIC-insured cash balances (the US dollar balance in your Coinbase account, for example, may be held at an FDIC-insured bank), but this coverage applies only to the cash balance — not to any Bitcoin or cryptocurrency.

Some exchanges carry private insurance policies, but these are generally limited:

Several critical points about exchange insurance:

  • Most policies cover theft and security breaches — not insolvency. If the exchange fails due to mismanagement, fraud, or market conditions, the insurance may not apply.
  • Coverage amounts are typically a fraction of total customer deposits. Coinbase's $255 million hot wallet policy covers a small percentage of the billions in customer assets on the platform.
  • Insurance policies are between the exchange and the insurer. Customers are not named insureds and generally cannot make direct claims against the policy.

For estate planning purposes, exchange insurance should be treated as a minor risk mitigant, not a safety net. No amount of private insurance replicates the protection that self-custody provides.

The Self-Custody Solution for Estate Planning

For families holding significant Bitcoin — the kind of positions that warrant formal estate planning — self-custody is the only approach that eliminates exchange counterparty risk entirely.

When Bitcoin is held in self-custody, there is no exchange that can go bankrupt. There is no terms-of-service agreement converting your property interest to a credit claim. There is no omnibus wallet commingling your Bitcoin with other customers' assets. There is no bankruptcy trustee who can freeze your account.

Your Bitcoin sits at an address controlled by your private keys (or a multi-signature arrangement you control), and when you die, your executor uses the instructions and credentials you've provided to transfer that Bitcoin directly to your heirs or into trust.

The estate planning infrastructure for self-custody Bitcoin is well-developed:

  • Letter of Instructions: A detailed, regularly updated document that tells your executor exactly where your Bitcoin is held, how to access it, and what to do with it. Stored securely — not in the will itself (which becomes public record) but referenced by the will and accessible to the executor.
  • Multi-signature arrangements: A 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 multi-sig setup ensures that no single point of failure (a lost seed phrase, a compromised device) can result in permanent loss of Bitcoin. It also allows for succession: one key held by the estate owner, one by a trusted family member or advisor, one by an institutional key holder.
  • Successor trustee briefing: The trustee of a Bitcoin-holding trust needs to understand — before the grantor's death — what Bitcoin is, how self-custody works, and what steps to take. This is not something to learn during the grief and urgency of estate settlement.
  • Hardware wallet documentation: Device locations, PIN codes (stored separately from devices), seed phrase backup locations, and firmware update history. All documented, all secured, all accessible to the right people at the right time.

Self-custody requires more upfront work than leaving Bitcoin at an exchange. But the work is finite and manageable. The risk of exchange bankruptcy during estate settlement is open-ended and potentially catastrophic.

When Exchange Custody Makes Sense Anyway

Self-custody isn't always the right answer. There are legitimate situations where exchange or institutional custody is the better choice, even accounting for counterparty risk:

Elderly holders who cannot manage private keys. A 90-year-old Bitcoin holder who has difficulty with technology should not be managing seed phrases and hardware wallets. The risk of self-custody errors — lost keys, forgotten PINs, phishing attacks — may exceed the risk of exchange insolvency. In these cases, a regulated US exchange with segregated custody (Fidelity Digital Assets, Coinbase Custody) is the appropriate choice.

Bitcoin IRAs and retirement accounts. Bitcoin held in an IRA must be held by a qualified custodian under IRS rules. Self-custody is not permitted for IRA assets. Families using Bitcoin IRAs should select custodians carefully, prioritizing those with segregated custody, regulatory compliance, and institutional insurance coverage.

Small positions where convenience outweighs risk. If someone holds 0.1 BTC at an exchange, the cost and complexity of setting up proper self-custody infrastructure may not be justified. The position is small enough that exchange risk, while real, represents a manageable loss scenario.

Active trading positions. Bitcoin that is actively being traded must be on an exchange. The solution here is to minimize the amount of time and the amount of Bitcoin that remains on-exchange, transferring to self-custody regularly.

For any of these scenarios, the estate planning approach should include:

  • Using regulated US exchanges with strong compliance records (Coinbase, Fidelity, Gemini)
  • Opting for segregated custody where available rather than omnibus accounts
  • Maintaining a documented plan for the executor to transfer or liquidate exchange positions immediately after death, before an exchange failure can trap the assets
  • Reviewing exchange financial health annually — audit reports, proof of reserves, regulatory actions

The Estate Planning Checklist for Exchange-Held Bitcoin

If your family holds any Bitcoin at exchanges — whether by choice or circumstance — this checklist should be part of your estate plan. It doesn't eliminate exchange counterparty risk, but it minimizes exposure and gives your executor the best possible chance of securing the assets before a failure occurs.

8-Step Executor Checklist for Exchange-Held BTC

  1. Maintain a complete exchange account inventory. Document every exchange where Bitcoin is held: account numbers, email addresses used, approximate balances, and whether two-factor authentication is enabled (and how to access the 2FA device). Update this inventory at least quarterly. Include it in your Letter of Instructions, not your will.
  2. Designate beneficiaries on each account. While beneficiary designations may not survive a bankruptcy proceeding, they streamline the transfer process in normal circumstances. Use them as a first layer of protection, not the only one.
  3. Document each exchange's custody model and insurance coverage. Know whether your exchange uses omnibus or segregated wallets. Know what insurance coverage exists and what it covers (theft? insolvency? both?). Record this information alongside the account inventory.
  4. Brief your executor on immediate post-death actions. The executor's first priority for exchange-held Bitcoin should be contacting each exchange, initiating the account transfer process, and moving Bitcoin to self-custody or an institutional custodian as quickly as possible. Time is the enemy: the longer Bitcoin sits at an exchange during estate settlement, the greater the exposure to an exchange failure.
  5. Establish a self-custody destination. Before the estate owner dies, set up a cold storage wallet or multi-signature arrangement that the executor can use as a destination for Bitcoin transferred off exchanges. Document this wallet's access procedures in the Letter of Instructions. The executor should not need to learn how to set up a hardware wallet during the estate settlement process.
  6. Consider moving significant positions off-exchange now. For positions above a family-determined threshold — $100,000 is a reasonable starting point, though many families use lower thresholds — the risk-adjusted value of self-custody exceeds the convenience of exchange custody. Move these positions to cold storage or an institutional custodian like Fidelity Digital Assets or Anchorage.
  7. Review exchange terms of service annually. Exchanges change their terms regularly. A term that previously guaranteed segregated custody may be amended. A new arbitration clause may limit recovery options. Read the terms once a year, or have your attorney review them. Flag any changes that affect your estate plan.
  8. Monitor exchange health signals. Proof of reserves reports, regulatory actions, executive departures, liquidity concerns, unusual withdrawal delays — these are the warning signs that preceded every major exchange failure. If you see them, move your Bitcoin immediately. Don't wait for confirmation. The FTX withdrawal halt went from "temporary pause" to "Chapter 11 filing" in four days.

The Bankruptcy Priority Problem

Understanding why exchange customers fare poorly in bankruptcy requires a basic understanding of bankruptcy priority — the order in which creditors get paid from whatever assets the bankruptcy estate can recover.

In a Chapter 11 bankruptcy, the typical priority waterfall is:

  1. Secured creditors — those with collateral pledged against their claims
  2. Administrative expenses — attorneys, accountants, and professionals running the bankruptcy process (these fees can consume tens or hundreds of millions of dollars)
  3. Priority unsecured claims — wages owed to employees, tax obligations, and certain other statutorily preferred claims
  4. General unsecured creditors — this is where exchange customers typically fall
  5. Equity holders — shareholders of the exchange, who typically receive nothing

Exchange customers are general unsecured creditors. They stand behind secured lenders, behind the army of attorneys and consultants billing $1,000+ per hour to administer the bankruptcy, and behind employee wage claims and tax authorities. Whatever is left gets divided among customers — pro rata, based on account balances at the petition date.

In FTX's case, an unusually large recovery from venture capital investments and other assets allowed for a better-than-expected distribution. But FTX is the exception. In most exchange failures, customers recover significantly less than 100 cents on the dollar. In cases involving outright fraud — and the history of cryptocurrency exchanges includes a disproportionate number of fraud cases — recovery can be minimal.

The Clawback Risk That Trust Owners Must Understand

Families who have transferred exchange-held Bitcoin into a trust face an additional risk that is often overlooked: the bankruptcy clawback.

Under the Bankruptcy Code, a trustee can "claw back" (reverse) transfers made by the debtor in the period before bankruptcy if those transfers are deemed preferential (within 90 days) or fraudulent (within two years, or longer under state law). While this provision typically targets transfers made by the exchange, not transfers of customer accounts, the legal lines are blurry when customer assets have been commingled.

If an exchange was commingling customer Bitcoin with its own assets — as FTX did — then a withdrawal made shortly before bankruptcy could theoretically be characterized as a preferential transfer. A customer (or trust) that withdrew Bitcoin in the weeks before a bankruptcy filing might be forced to return those assets to the bankruptcy estate.

This risk is low for customers who withdrew well before any signs of distress. But it is a real risk for anyone who withdraws during the warning period — which is precisely when most informed customers would try to move their Bitcoin to safety.

For trust-owned exchange accounts, the clawback risk adds another layer of uncertainty. Even if the trust "owns" the exchange account, the trust's claim in bankruptcy is only as strong as any other unsecured creditor's claim. And if the trust received Bitcoin from the exchange within the preference period, those transfers could be reversed.

A Note on Regulated Exchanges and State Trust Charters

Not all exchanges are created equal from a regulatory standpoint, and the regulatory structure does affect — though not eliminate — the risk profile for estate planning.

Gemini operates as a New York trust company, regulated by the New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS). This means Gemini is subject to capital reserve requirements, regular examinations, and fiduciary obligations that standard crypto exchanges are not. Customer assets at a NY trust company may receive different treatment in insolvency than assets at an unregulated exchange.

Fidelity Digital Assets operates within the Fidelity Investments ecosystem, one of the largest financial services companies in the world. While Fidelity Digital Assets' crypto custody is not covered by SIPC, Fidelity's institutional stability and regulatory compliance record provide a materially different risk profile than a standalone crypto exchange.

Coinbase, as a publicly traded company (NASDAQ: COIN), is subject to SEC reporting requirements, Sarbanes-Oxley compliance, and regular financial audits. This transparency doesn't prevent failure, but it makes the kind of hidden insolvency that characterized FTX and Mt. Gox significantly harder to conceal.

For families that must use exchange custody, these regulatory distinctions matter. A regulated US exchange with proof of reserves, audited financials, and a state trust charter is materially safer than an offshore exchange with opaque operations. But "safer" is not "safe." Regulation reduces the probability of failure; it does not eliminate the consequences.

The Bottom Line for Bitcoin Estate Planning

The synthesis of everything above reduces to a few clear principles:

Exchange-held Bitcoin is not your Bitcoin for estate planning purposes. It is a credit claim against a company. Your estate plan should treat it as such — with all the risk that implies.

Self-custody is the only way to guarantee that heirs receive actual Bitcoin. Not a claim. Not a bankruptcy distribution. Not dollars calculated at a filing-date price while the real value appreciates beyond reach. Actual Bitcoin, transferred directly. If your estate plan depends on heirs receiving Bitcoin, the Bitcoin needs to be in self-custody.

If exchange custody is necessary, choose regulated US institutions and have a transfer plan. The executor should know how to move Bitcoin off-exchange immediately after death. The estate plan should include a pre-configured self-custody destination. And the family should monitor exchange health signals as part of ongoing estate plan maintenance.

Beneficiary designations and TOD accounts are not bankruptcy-proof. Use them, but do not rely on them. They are a convenience feature, not a protection mechanism. In a bankruptcy, they may be overridden entirely.

Time is the critical variable. The longer Bitcoin sits at an exchange during estate settlement, the greater the exposure. Mt. Gox creditors waited a decade. FTX creditors waited three years. An executor who moves quickly — transferring Bitcoin to self-custody within days of the holder's death — dramatically reduces the window of vulnerability.

Bitcoin was designed to be held without intermediaries. Its entire value proposition rests on the elimination of trusted third parties. An estate plan that depends on a trusted third party to deliver Bitcoin to your heirs has reintroduced exactly the risk that Bitcoin was built to remove.

Plan accordingly.
