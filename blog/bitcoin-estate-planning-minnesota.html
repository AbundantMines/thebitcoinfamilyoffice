Bitcoin Estate Planning in Minnesota: The $3 Million Exemption and No Portability

Minnesota has the second-lowest estate tax exemption of any state — and a long track record of aggressive enforcement. For Bitcoin holders, the math is already alarming at current prices.

By Hal Turner
February 25, 2026
~11 min read

If you hold Bitcoin and live in Minnesota, the estate tax conversation starts at $3 million — not the $13.61 million federal threshold that most financial planning content focuses on. Minnesota's estate tax exemption is among the lowest of any state in the nation, and unlike the federal exemption, it has no inflation adjustment and no planned increases on the horizon. At $95,000 per Bitcoin, just 31 BTC crosses the Minnesota threshold on its own — before a house, retirement account, car, or any other asset enters the picture.

Minnesota is also known for diligent estate tax enforcement. The state's Department of Revenue has a dedicated estate tax program and actively pursues underfiled or undervalued estates. For Bitcoin holders in Minnesota, this is not a distant planning problem. It is a present and urgent one.

This guide covers everything Minnesota Bitcoin holders need to know about state estate tax — the mechanics, the math, and the planning strategies that can meaningfully reduce or eliminate exposure.

Minnesota Estate Tax: How It Works

The $3 Million Exemption

Minnesota exempts the first $3 million of a taxable estate from state estate tax. This exemption has been at $3 million since 2020 and is not indexed for inflation. There is no legislative plan to increase it further. As Bitcoin's price rises and general asset values continue to grow, the real value of this exemption erodes every year — catching more and more estates in its net without any change in the law.

Taxable estate for Minnesota purposes includes all assets owned at death: Bitcoin, real estate, bank accounts, brokerage accounts, retirement accounts (with some nuances), life insurance (if the deceased held incidents of ownership), business interests, and any other property. The starting point is gross estate value before any debts or expenses.

Minnesota Estate Tax Rates: 13% to 16%

Unlike the federal estate tax, which has a single 40% top rate, Minnesota uses a graduated rate structure on the amount above the $3 million exemption. The rates begin at 13% and increase to a top rate of 16% on amounts significantly above the threshold. These rates are not trivial — for a Bitcoin holder with $6 million in total assets, the Minnesota estate tax bill can easily reach $300,000 to $400,000 or more.

No Portability — Each Spouse Has a Separate $3 Million

This is the point that surprises most married Minnesota Bitcoin holders. At the federal level, married couples can effectively combine their exemptions through portability — if one spouse dies, the surviving spouse can elect to "inherit" the deceased spouse's unused federal exemption. A married couple can effectively protect $27.22 million from federal estate tax with proper planning.

Minnesota provides no such portability. Each spouse has their own $3 million exemption, period. If the first spouse to die leaves everything directly to the surviving spouse (using the unlimited marital deduction), the first spouse's $3 million exemption is permanently lost. The surviving spouse then dies with a single $3 million exemption — and the entire estate above that amount is subject to Minnesota estate tax.

For a married couple holding 60 BTC between them at $95,000 — a total value of $5.7 million — failing to plan around portability means the estate will face Minnesota estate tax on $2.7 million at the second spouse's death. That's a tax bill approaching $400,000 or more that proper credit shelter trust planning could largely or entirely eliminate.

Minnesota Has No Inheritance Tax

Minnesota imposes an estate tax, not an inheritance tax. The distinction is meaningful: the estate tax is paid by the estate before assets are distributed to heirs. Beneficiaries who receive Minnesota assets — including Bitcoin — do not pay an additional Minnesota tax on what they inherit. Federal capital gains rules apply to inherited assets based on stepped-up basis rules at the federal level, but that's a separate issue from Minnesota's estate tax.

Minnesota is a Common Law Equitable Distribution State

Minnesota follows common law property principles, not community property rules. Each spouse legally owns the assets titled in their name alone. Bitcoin held in a wallet or exchange account under one spouse's name and control is generally that spouse's separate property for estate purposes. This has planning implications: for credit shelter trust funding purposes, it matters who holds title to what assets, and Bitcoin ownership should be clearly documented and structured between spouses with estate planning in mind.

Minnesota has a long history of aggressive estate tax enforcement. The state's Department of Revenue actively matches death records against estate tax returns and pursues cases where estates that appear taxable — based on public records of property ownership, business registration, or investment accounts — fail to file. Bitcoin's pseudonymity does not create a planning exemption. If the estate is taxable, it must be reported.

For moderate Bitcoin holders — the early adopters who bought between 2015 and 2020, the employees who took Bitcoin compensation, the retail investors who accumulated steadily — the Minnesota threshold is already in range at current prices. Planning cannot wait for Bitcoin's next major price move.

Minnesota-Specific Bitcoin Estate Planning Strategies

1. Credit Shelter Trust (A/B Trust)

The credit shelter trust — also called a bypass trust or A/B trust — is the foundational tool for married Minnesota Bitcoin holders who want to preserve both spouses' $3 million exemptions.

The structure is straightforward: when the first spouse dies, assets up to the Minnesota exemption ($3 million) are placed into an irrevocable trust — the "B trust" or credit shelter trust — rather than passing directly to the surviving spouse. The surviving spouse can still benefit from the trust's income and assets, but the trust assets are not counted in the surviving spouse's taxable estate when they die.

The result: a married couple can shelter up to $6 million from Minnesota estate tax — $3 million for each spouse. Without the credit shelter trust, the couple is limited to $3 million total at the second death.

For Bitcoin held in the credit shelter trust, future appreciation also escapes the estate. If the Bitcoin in the trust doubles from $95,000 to $190,000 per coin between the first and second spouse's death, the entire appreciation accumulates inside the trust — outside the surviving spouse's taxable estate. The estate tax savings compound with Bitcoin's price appreciation.

2. South Dakota or Wyoming Dynasty Trust

Minnesota residents are not limited to Minnesota trust structures. A growing number of high-net-worth Minnesotans are establishing dynasty trusts in South Dakota or Wyoming — states with no state income tax, no rule against perpetuities, and uniquely favorable trust laws — to hold Bitcoin and other appreciating assets.

South Dakota in particular has become the dominant domestic trust jurisdiction. South Dakota has no trust income tax, no state capital gains tax, exceptional asset protection provisions, and allows self-settled trusts where the grantor can be a discretionary beneficiary. Wyoming offers similar advantages with additional specific protections for digital assets including cryptocurrency.

A Minnesota resident who transfers Bitcoin into a properly established South Dakota or Wyoming dynasty trust with an independent trustee removes those assets from their Minnesota taxable estate — permanently, for the life of the trust. South Dakota permits perpetual trusts with no mandatory termination date. The trust can hold Bitcoin for children, grandchildren, and future generations without triggering Minnesota estate tax at each generation.

The transfer to the dynasty trust is treated as a taxable gift for federal purposes (using federal lifetime exemption or potentially paying gift tax), but subsequent appreciation — potentially far larger — escapes both Minnesota estate tax and the generation-skipping transfer tax if structured properly.

3. Annual Gifting Program

Every person can give up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2024 IRS annual exclusion) completely free of gift tax and without using any lifetime exemption. These gifts are removed from the estate immediately and permanently.

For a married couple using gift-splitting, each recipient can receive $36,000 per year from the couple combined. A family with two adult children and four grandchildren can transfer $216,000 per year out of the estate — in Bitcoin, in cash, or in other assets — with zero gift tax and zero reduction in the Minnesota estate tax exemption.

Applied to Bitcoin specifically: gifting Bitcoin at today's prices removes not just the current value but all future appreciation from the estate. A gift of $18,000 in Bitcoin today might represent $90,000 to $180,000 in future estate value if Bitcoin appreciates 5x to 10x. The estate tax savings from a systematic gifting program compound over time.

Annual gifts should be documented. For Bitcoin, this means a contemporaneous record of the transfer, the wallet addresses involved, the date, the recipient, and the fair market value on the date of transfer. The IRS and Minnesota Department of Revenue both expect this level of documentation.

4. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

A GRAT is an estate freeze technique particularly suited to concentrated, high-appreciation positions like Bitcoin. You transfer Bitcoin into the GRAT, then receive fixed annuity payments back over a defined term — typically two to five years. At the end of the GRAT term, the remainder passes to heirs.

The "freeze" mechanics work as follows: the IRS assumes your Bitcoin will grow at a baseline interest rate (the Section 7520 rate, currently modest). Any actual appreciation above that assumed rate passes to your heirs completely gift-tax free — removed from your Minnesota estate with no additional tax cost. If Bitcoin significantly outperforms the IRS hurdle rate (which history suggests is likely over most multi-year periods), the GRAT transfers the excess appreciation at zero tax cost.

The risk profile of a GRAT is asymmetric in your favor: if Bitcoin underperforms, the assets come back to you and you're no worse off than before. If Bitcoin outperforms — as it has tended to do over multi-year periods — your heirs receive the excess appreciation free of Minnesota estate tax and federal estate tax.

Short-term GRATs (two years) are particularly popular because they reduce the risk of dying during the trust term (which would cause the assets to return to the estate) while still capturing near-term Bitcoin appreciation cycles.

Minnesota's History of Estate Tax Enforcement

Minnesota does not take a passive approach to estate tax collection. The Department of Revenue actively cross-references death records with county property records, business registration databases, and financial accounts to identify estates that appear taxable but have not filed a return. Minnesota estate tax returns are due nine months after the date of death and must include a full accounting of all estate assets.

For Bitcoin holders, this creates specific considerations:

  • Bitcoin is property for estate tax purposes. The IRS treats Bitcoin as property, and Minnesota follows federal classification for estate purposes. Bitcoin holdings must be reported and valued on the estate tax return.
  • Exchange records create a paper trail. If the deceased held Bitcoin on a centralized exchange (Coinbase, Kraken, etc.), those records will be subpoenaed or voluntarily provided to the estate's executor. Bitcoin on exchanges does not disappear at death.
  • Self-custody Bitcoin requires specific documentation planning. Hardware wallets and cold storage must be included in estate planning documents. Access instructions must be available to the executor. Bitcoin that heirs cannot access cannot be transferred — and the IRS's position is that it's still in the estate regardless of accessibility.

The practical implication: Minnesota Bitcoin holders must plan not just to minimize estate tax but to ensure their estates can comply with filing requirements and pay any tax due without forcing heirs into a distressed Bitcoin sale.

The Liquidity Problem: Paying the Tax

Minnesota estate tax is due within nine months of death. For an estate concentrated in Bitcoin, this creates a liquidity problem: if heirs lack cash to pay the estate tax, they may be forced to sell Bitcoin at whatever price exists at the time — regardless of whether that price reflects an optimal exit point.

Estate planning for Bitcoin holders should include a liquidity analysis. Options for ensuring estate tax liquidity include:

  • Maintaining a cash reserve within the estate to cover estimated tax liability without requiring an immediate Bitcoin sale.
  • Life insurance owned outside the estate (via an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust, or ILIT) to provide tax-free proceeds that can fund the estate tax bill.
  • Installment payment options for certain closely held business assets — though Bitcoin's classification here is more complex and not guaranteed.
  • Estate planning that reduces the taxable estate below the $3 million threshold through the strategies discussed above — eliminating the tax liability entirely.

The best liquidity plan is the one that doesn't require one: proper credit shelter trust planning and dynasty trust transfers that keep the estate below the threshold before death occurs.

Finding a Bitcoin-Literate Minnesota Estate Attorney

The combination of Minnesota-specific estate tax rules and Bitcoin's unique custodial and technical characteristics demands an attorney who understands both. Most estate attorneys are highly competent with Minnesota law; few have meaningful experience with Bitcoin's technical dimensions.

Questions to ask when evaluating a Minnesota estate attorney for Bitcoin planning:

  • Have you drafted trust documents that specifically address Bitcoin held in self-custody — hardware wallets, multisig, and cold storage? Standard trust language for "digital assets" may be insufficient for operational Bitcoin access planning.
  • Are you familiar with the credit shelter trust structure as calibrated for Minnesota's $3 million exemption — not the federal threshold? Some attorneys draft documents using federal triggers that don't capture the state-level planning need.
  • Have you worked with South Dakota or Wyoming dynasty trusts for Minnesota clients? Out-of-state situs planning requires understanding both jurisdictions — and the grantor's Minnesota obligations don't disappear just because the trust is in another state.
  • How do you approach Bitcoin valuation for estate and gift tax purposes? Defensible valuations — using the average of daily high and low on the valuation date from a major exchange — must be documented contemporaneously and retained.
  • Do you coordinate with CPAs who understand cryptocurrency taxation? Estate and income tax planning for Bitcoin holders are deeply intertwined. The attorney and accountant must work from the same facts.

Action Steps for Minnesota Bitcoin Holders

If you are a Minnesota resident with meaningful Bitcoin holdings, here is a practical starting point:

  1. Calculate your current total estate value. Include Bitcoin at today's market price, your home, retirement accounts, brokerage accounts, life insurance death benefits, and any business interests. Compare to the $3 million threshold.
  2. Model future price scenarios. Run the analysis at $150K, $250K, and $500K Bitcoin to understand how quickly your exposure escalates. Minnesota's flat exemption makes this exercise increasingly alarming at higher prices.
  3. Review your existing estate documents. Most wills and trusts drafted before 2020 for Minnesota clients do not reflect the current $3 million exemption correctly. Many use federal-trigger language that misses the state planning opportunity entirely.
  4. Implement a gifting program immediately. Annual gifting is the simplest, lowest-cost estate reduction strategy available. It requires no trust, no attorney — just consistent execution and proper documentation.
  5. Evaluate credit shelter trust planning with a Minnesota estate attorney. For married couples, this is often the single highest-leverage planning step available and should not be deferred.
  6. Explore dynasty trust siting in South Dakota or Wyoming. If your Bitcoin holdings significantly exceed the $3 million threshold — or are expected to — a dynasty trust is worth serious evaluation.

The Bottom Line for Minnesota Bitcoin Holders

Minnesota's estate tax is not a planning edge case. With a $3 million exemption — one of the lowest in the country — and no portability between spouses, even moderate Bitcoin holders face meaningful state estate tax exposure at current prices. The state's history of active enforcement means that failing to file is not a viable strategy. And Bitcoin's appreciation trajectory means that waiting to plan is itself a planning decision — one that becomes more expensive with each passing year.

The good news: every strategy discussed in this guide is available to Minnesota residents right now. Credit shelter trusts, South Dakota or Wyoming dynasty trusts, annual gifting programs, and GRATs are all established, legal, and effective. The difference between a $400,000 Minnesota estate tax bill and a substantially smaller one is almost entirely a function of whether you engaged a qualified attorney and implemented a plan.

Minnesota will collect its estate tax. The question is whether you've structured your affairs to minimize what's owed — or left that question unanswered for your heirs to deal with under deadline pressure and potential market duress.
