Bitcoin Estate Planning in Minnesota: $3M Estate Tax Threshold

Minnesota has one of the lowest state estate tax exemptions in the country — $3 million, not portable between spouses, with rates reaching 16%. At $95K per Bitcoin, just 32 BTC triggers Minnesota estate tax. Here is what every Minnesota Bitcoin holder must know.

By Hal Turner | February 25, 2026 | ~13 min read

If you hold Bitcoin in Minnesota, you are operating in one of the most estate-tax-hostile environments in the United States. Minnesota levies its own state estate tax with an exemption of just $3 million per person — not portable between spouses — and tax rates that reach 16% on amounts above the threshold. Pair that with Minnesota's income tax rate of up to 9.85%, one of the highest in the country, and you have a state where the government is aggressively positioned to capture a significant share of Bitcoin wealth at multiple points: when you sell, and when you die.

This is not a theoretical concern. At a Bitcoin price of $95,000, a Minnesota resident holding just 32 BTC — worth roughly $3.04 million — has a taxable estate. That figure catches a surprisingly broad range of holders: people who bought consistently through 2019, 2020, and 2021, healthcare professionals who allocated retirement savings to Bitcoin, technology workers who have been accumulating for a decade. The $3M threshold is low enough that estate planning is not just for the ultra-wealthy — it is a practical necessity for any serious Minnesota Bitcoin holder.

Minnesota Estate Tax: The Fundamentals

Minnesota imposes a state estate tax under Minn. Stat. § 291.03. The tax applies to the estates of Minnesota residents — regardless of where their assets are located — and to the Minnesota-sited property of non-residents. The current exemption is $3 million, and rates are graduated from 13% to 16% on the taxable amount above the threshold.

Unlike the federal estate tax, which has a high exemption ($13.61 million per person in 2024, $13.99 million in 2025) and allows portability between spouses, Minnesota's exemption is not portable. A deceased spouse's unused Minnesota exemption cannot be transferred to the surviving spouse. This is the most consequential feature of Minnesota estate tax for married couples — and the most frequently overlooked.

⚠ No Portability in Minnesota

A married couple in Minnesota cannot simply rely on the survivor's own $3M exemption after the first spouse dies. Without a bypass trust, the first spouse's $3M exemption is permanently lost if the estate passes outright to the survivor. Effective combined exemption without planning: $3M. With a properly funded bypass trust: $6M.

The $3M Math: Who Is Actually Exposed

Let us do the math explicitly, because the numbers are arresting.

At a Bitcoin price of $95,000:

  • 32 BTC = $3.04M — Minnesota estate tax kicks in. A single-person household with 32 BTC and modest other assets (home equity, retirement accounts, personal property) is well into Minnesota estate tax territory.
  • 50 BTC = $4.75M — Approximately $1.75M in taxable excess. At Minnesota's marginal rates in this range (approximately 13–14%), that is roughly $225,000–$245,000 in Minnesota estate tax.
  • 100 BTC = $9.5M — Approximately $6.5M in taxable excess. Minnesota estate tax at or approaching 16% on the upper portion — potentially $800,000–$1M+ in state tax alone, on top of any federal estate tax.

These are not edge cases. These are real positions held by real Minnesota families. Many have been accumulating for years and simply have not updated their estate plans to reflect how dramatically their net worth has grown. Bitcoin's asymmetric upside, which made these holders wealthy, also created an estate tax problem that many of them are only now beginning to appreciate.

⚡ The Accumulator's Trap

Long-term Bitcoin accumulators in Minnesota face a compounding problem: their basis is typically very low (purchased at $5K, $10K, $20K per coin), their current value is high, and their estate plan was written when they held far less. Every year without an updated estate plan is a year of potential compounded exposure.

The Bypass Trust: Essential for Every Minnesota Bitcoin Couple

Given Minnesota's non-portable exemption, the bypass trust — also called a credit shelter trust, family trust, or B trust — is the foundational tool for married Minnesota Bitcoin holders. This is not optional planning for the wealthy. At a $3 million threshold, it is essential planning for any married couple with meaningful Bitcoin holdings.

Here is how the bypass trust operates in a Minnesota context:

  1. First spouse dies. Instead of leaving everything to the surviving spouse outright (which forfeits the deceased's $3M exemption), assets up to $3 million are directed into the bypass trust.
  2. Surviving spouse benefits. The trust is typically structured to benefit the surviving spouse — they can receive income, and often principal for health, education, maintenance, and support — while keeping the assets outside their taxable estate.
  3. Second spouse dies. The bypass trust assets pass to heirs without being included in the surviving spouse's estate. The surviving spouse's estate separately uses its own $3M exemption. Combined effective exemption: $6 million.

For a Bitcoin-holding couple, the bypass trust might be funded with Bitcoin specifically — perhaps a specific number of BTC allocated to the trust at the first death. This requires careful attention to valuation (Bitcoin's price at date of death determines the value of assets available to fund the bypass), key access protocols for self-custodied coins, and trustee capabilities for managing a Bitcoin position over time.

Minnesota's Income Tax Problem: 9.85% on Bitcoin Gains

Minnesota's estate tax burden does not exist in isolation. The state also levies one of the highest income tax rates in the United States: 9.85% on taxable income above approximately $304,970 (for married couples, 2024). Bitcoin holders who sell in Minnesota pay this rate in addition to federal capital gains tax.

For a long-term Bitcoin holder selling at a significant gain, the combined federal and Minnesota tax bill can be severe:

  • Federal long-term capital gains: 20% (for high earners)
  • Federal net investment income tax (NIIT): 3.8%
  • Minnesota income tax: 9.85%
  • Combined marginal rate on Bitcoin gains: ~33.65%

This means a Minnesota Bitcoin holder selling $1 million in appreciated Bitcoin effectively sends approximately $336,500 to federal and state tax authorities. The remaining approximately $663,500 is what funds lifestyle, reinvestment, or philanthropy.

This income tax environment makes holding Bitcoin — rather than selling — even more valuable in Minnesota than in lower-tax states. It also creates significant planning opportunities around how and when Bitcoin is sold, and whether charitable giving (via charitable remainder trusts, donor-advised funds, or direct charitable transfers of appreciated Bitcoin) can reduce the income tax burden.

Minnesota Wealth Migration: The Domicile Change Option

For very large Bitcoin holders — those with positions of 100+ BTC or multi-million-dollar Bitcoin wealth — the tax calculus in Minnesota may be compelling enough to consider a genuine change of domicile. South Dakota and Wyoming are the two states most commonly targeted by high-earning Minnesotans:

  • Wyoming: No state income tax. No state estate tax. One of the best trust jurisdictions in the country (DAPT, directed trust, dynasty trust). Very favorable for Bitcoin holders specifically.
  • South Dakota: No state income tax. No state estate tax. Excellent trust law. Popular for dynasty trusts and offshore trust settlors relocating domestically.

A domicile change is not as simple as buying a house in Cheyenne or Sioux Falls. To successfully change domicile from Minnesota's perspective, the individual must:

  1. Establish a physical presence and residence in the new state
  2. Abandon Minnesota as a primary domicile (voter registration, driver's license, bank accounts, club memberships, social connections)
  3. Spend the appropriate number of days outside Minnesota
  4. Update estate planning documents, financial accounts, and professional registrations to reflect the new domicile

Minnesota aggressively audits claimed domicile changes, particularly for high-income taxpayers. A successful domicile change requires careful documentation and ideally a genuine lifestyle shift to the new state — not just a paper change. For someone with a $10M Bitcoin position facing potential $1M+ in Minnesota estate tax and $335K/year in state income tax on Bitcoin sales, the math on a genuine relocation can be extremely compelling.

That said, many Minnesota Bitcoin holders are deeply rooted in the state — professional practices, family, community ties — and relocation is not realistic. For them, the focus shifts to maximizing the effectiveness of planning within Minnesota's tax structure.

Minnesota Trust Law and RUFADAA

Minnesota has adopted a comprehensive trust code under Chapter 501C of the Minnesota Statutes, based on the Uniform Trust Code. Minnesota courts have a solid body of trust law precedent, and Minnesota is a competent trust jurisdiction for traditional trust planning purposes.

Minnesota has also adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), which governs how executors, trustees, and agents under powers of attorney can access digital assets — including Bitcoin — after the account holder's death or incapacity. Under Minnesota RUFADAA, fiduciary access to digital assets is governed by:

  • The explicit authorizations in the governing estate planning documents
  • The terms of service of any custodian (exchange, custody platform)
  • Whether the assets are custodied or self-custodied

For self-custodied Bitcoin, RUFADAA gives fiduciaries authority to act but does not create the technical ability to access private keys. Every Minnesota Bitcoin estate plan must address the practical question of key access: where are the keys stored, who has access, and what is the tested recovery protocol? Bitcoin inaccessible to heirs is Bitcoin lost.

No DAPT in Minnesota: Use Wyoming for Asset Protection

Minnesota does not have a Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) statute. Minnesota law does not permit a person to be a beneficiary of a self-settled spendthrift trust — meaning you cannot establish a trust in Minnesota, fund it with your own assets, name yourself as a beneficiary, and claim creditor protection for those assets.

For Minnesota Bitcoin holders who need asset protection — physicians, attorneys, business owners, executives — the solution is a Wyoming DAPT. Wyoming's DAPT statute is among the strongest in the country. A properly structured and funded Wyoming DAPT can provide significant creditor protection while also layering in estate planning efficiency through coordinated trust design.

The combination of a Wyoming DAPT for asset protection and a properly drafted revocable trust with bypass trust provisions for Minnesota estate tax creates a robust planning architecture for most Minnesota Bitcoin holders.

The GRAT: Transferring Bitcoin Appreciation Out of Your Estate

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is a federal transfer tax planning tool that works particularly well with appreciating assets like Bitcoin. The concept: you transfer Bitcoin into the GRAT, receive annuity payments back for a fixed term, and any appreciation above the IRS Section 7520 hurdle rate transfers to your heirs free of gift and estate tax at the end of the term.

Bitcoin's price volatility makes it genuinely well-suited for GRATs. If you can time a GRAT to capture a period of significant Bitcoin appreciation, you can transfer enormous wealth to heirs with zero transfer tax. Practical considerations:

  • Rolling GRATs: Instead of one large GRAT, establish multiple shorter-term GRATs sequentially to maximize the probability of capturing appreciation across market cycles.
  • Mortality risk: If the grantor dies during the GRAT term, the assets are pulled back into the estate. Shorter terms (2-year GRATs, for example) reduce this risk.
  • Zeroed-out GRATs: The GRAT can be structured so the annuity payments essentially return all principal to the grantor, with only the excess appreciation going to heirs. This minimizes gift tax regardless of the outcome.

For Minnesota residents, the GRAT reduces the federal taxable estate and also directly reduces the Minnesota taxable estate — both meaningful given the two overlapping tax systems.

Wyoming Dynasty Trust for Minnesota Holders

While Minnesota has no specific dynasty trust statute, Minnesota Bitcoin holders can establish a Wyoming dynasty trust to hold Bitcoin for multiple generations. Wyoming allows trusts to continue indefinitely (no rule against perpetuities for trusts) and provides the DAPT asset protection layer simultaneously.

A Wyoming dynasty trust funded with Bitcoin:

  • Can hold Bitcoin indefinitely across generations without triggering estate tax at each death
  • Provides creditor protection for beneficiaries (spendthrift provisions)
  • Allows the settlor to remain a discretionary beneficiary (DAPT feature)
  • Can be administered by a professional corporate trustee with Bitcoin custody expertise
  • Bypasses the Minnesota estate tax at both the funding generation's death and subsequent generations

The trust must be properly established in Wyoming with Wyoming-sited administration and a Wyoming trustee. It must have genuine economic substance. But for a Minnesota family with significant Bitcoin wealth, the combination of Minnesota estate tax avoidance and creditor protection makes the Wyoming dynasty trust one of the most powerful tools available.

Planning Priorities for Minnesota Bitcoin Holders

Given Minnesota's uniquely challenging tax environment — low estate tax exemption, non-portable, high income tax — here is the strategic priority order for Minnesota Bitcoin holders:

  1. Bypass Trust — Essential. If you are married and your combined estate may exceed $3 million (which includes all assets, not just Bitcoin), a bypass trust is your highest-priority planning action. This is the most reliable way to double your effective Minnesota exemption from $3M to $6M.
  2. Digital Asset Estate Plan Update. Ensure your estate planning documents explicitly authorize fiduciaries to access, manage, and transfer Bitcoin. Address key access for self-custodied Bitcoin with a tested, secure protocol.
  3. Wyoming DAPT (if creditor risk warrants). Healthcare professionals and business owners with liability exposure should consider a Wyoming self-settled trust as an asset protection vehicle, especially for Bitcoin held outside retirement accounts.
  4. Income Tax Planning. Work with a CPA experienced in cryptocurrency to manage the timing and character of Bitcoin dispositions, explore charitable giving strategies (donor-advised fund, charitable remainder trust with appreciated Bitcoin), and consider mining as a deduction source.
  5. GRAT for Larger Holdings. If your Bitcoin position is meaningfully above the $3M threshold, GRATs can transfer significant appreciation to heirs with zero transfer tax. Model this with a financial advisor familiar with cryptocurrency-specific GRAT mechanics.
  6. Domicile Change Analysis. If you hold 100+ BTC and are not deeply tied to Minnesota by professional or personal obligations, a genuine domicile change to Wyoming or South Dakota may be worth modeling. The tax savings can be extraordinary for large holders over time.
  7. Wyoming Dynasty Trust. For larger holdings intended for multiple generations, a Wyoming dynasty trust provides the combination of indefinite duration, creditor protection, and estate tax bypass that Minnesota law alone cannot offer.
