How to Avoid Estate Tax on Bitcoin: GRATs, Dynasty Trusts, and Gifting Strategies

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  12 min read  ·  2026-02-24

The federal estate tax is 40% on taxable estates above the exemption threshold. If you bought Bitcoin early and held it through several market cycles, a significant portion of your estate may be Bitcoin — and a significant portion of that Bitcoin could go to the IRS rather than your heirs without deliberate planning.

The good news: the U.S. tax code contains multiple legitimate, well-established vehicles for reducing estate tax exposure on appreciated assets. Bitcoin's volatility — which many investors find uncomfortable — is actually an advantage in several of these structures. High expected appreciation, high volatility, and a low interest rate environment are exactly the conditions where Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts perform best.

This guide covers the primary estate tax reduction strategies available to Bitcoin holders: GRATs, charitable remainder trusts, dynasty trusts, annual gifting, and entity-based valuation discounts. Each strategy has different trade-offs; the right combination depends on your estate size, income needs, charitable intent, and time horizon. Use this guide to frame the conversation with your estate attorney and CPA — it is not a substitute for professional advice on your specific situation.

Timing warning: The current federal estate and gift tax exemption ($13.99 million per individual in 2025) is scheduled to sunset on December 31, 2025, reverting to approximately $7 million (inflation-adjusted). Structures funded before the sunset lock in the higher exemption. If your estate is above $7 million, act before year-end 2025.

Step 1: Quantify Your Estate Tax Exposure

Step 1 of 6

Know the numbers before selecting a strategy

Estate tax planning without a current estate value is strategy without a target.

Your taxable estate includes:

  • All Bitcoin held in your personal name (valued at date of death)
  • Bitcoin held in revocable trusts (revocable = still in your estate)
  • Your share of Bitcoin held in LLCs, partnerships, or S-corps
  • Life insurance proceeds (if you are the owner)
  • Real estate, retirement accounts (IRAs are generally includible), business interests, and other assets

Subtract any amounts passing to a surviving spouse (the unlimited marital deduction) and charitable bequests. The remainder above the applicable exemption is taxed at 40%.

The challenge with Bitcoin: price volatility makes the calculation a moving target. At $50,000 per BTC, 100 BTC is $5 million. At $200,000, it's $20 million. Run the analysis at multiple Bitcoin price scenarios — current price, a conservative appreciation case, and an aggressive appreciation case. The strategies you implement today need to work across that range.

Step 2: Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

Step 2 of 6

Transfer Bitcoin appreciation to heirs gift-tax-free

A GRAT is one of the most effective tools for transferring appreciated Bitcoin out of your estate with minimal gift tax cost.

A GRAT works as follows:

  1. You transfer Bitcoin to an irrevocable trust (the GRAT)
  2. The trust pays you a fixed annuity each year for a term you specify (typically 2-10 years)
  3. The annuity payments are calculated to return your full contribution plus a modest IRS-prescribed interest rate (the Section 7520 rate) back to you over the term
  4. Any appreciation above that interest rate passes to your heirs at the end of the term — entirely gift- and estate-tax-free

The gift tax cost of a well-designed GRAT is close to zero (in a "zeroed-out" GRAT, the annuity payments precisely offset the contribution value at the 7520 rate). The strategy's effectiveness depends entirely on the asset outperforming the 7520 rate — and Bitcoin's historical appreciation rate has massively exceeded any plausible 7520 rate.

Key risks and mitigants:

  • Mortality risk: If you die during the GRAT term, the assets return to your estate. Use a shorter term (2 years) or hedge with life insurance.
  • Underperformance risk: If Bitcoin falls during the term, the GRAT fails — but the only cost is the legal fees. You get your assets back and can GRAT again. This asymmetry — zero downside other than costs, unlimited upside — makes GRATs particularly valuable for volatile assets.
  • Custody complexity: Bitcoin held in a GRAT must be managed by the trustee. Design the custody architecture carefully before funding.

Step 3: Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

Step 3 of 6

Liquidate appreciated Bitcoin, defer capital gains, and create an income stream

A CRT is ideal when you want to diversify a concentrated Bitcoin position, need income, and have charitable intent.

A CRT allows you to contribute appreciated Bitcoin to a charitable trust. The trust sells the Bitcoin without immediate capital gains tax (the trust is tax-exempt), reinvests the proceeds, and pays you (and potentially your spouse or other non-charitable beneficiaries) an income stream for a specified period or lifetime. At the end of the term, the remaining assets pass to designated charities.

Benefits of a Bitcoin CRT:

  • Capital gains deferral: If you contribute highly appreciated Bitcoin directly to a CRT, the trust sells it tax-free, preserving the full value for investment
  • Charitable deduction: You receive an immediate partial income tax deduction equal to the present value of the charitable remainder interest
  • Estate tax reduction: Assets in the CRT are removed from your taxable estate
  • Income stream: Provides a predictable income stream from a previously illiquid, non-yielding asset

The trade-off is permanence and charitable requirement: the assets in a CRT cannot be returned to you, and at least 10% of the initial value must ultimately pass to charity. If your goals are purely family wealth transfer with no charitable intent, a GRAT or dynasty trust is typically more appropriate. See the full Bitcoin CRT analysis for detailed modeling.

Step 4: Dynasty Trust in a Perpetual-Trust Jurisdiction

Step 4 of 6

Remove Bitcoin from the estate for generations

A dynasty trust eliminates estate tax at every generational transfer, allowing Bitcoin to compound across multiple generations.

Under the current federal tax code, assets transferred to a dynasty trust are subject to estate tax (or gift tax) once — when first contributed to the trust. Subsequent distributions to children, grandchildren, and great-grandchildren are not subject to estate or gift tax. The generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax exemption protects the trust's distributions across generations.

The math is compelling for a high-appreciation asset like Bitcoin:

ScenarioNo Dynasty TrustDynasty Trust
Initial Bitcoin value$5 million$5 million
Estate tax at death (Gen 1)$800,000 (40% on excess)$0 (GST exemption applied)
Value after 20 years (8% CAGR)$20.7M (on $4.2M base)$23.3M (on $5M base)
Estate tax at death (Gen 2)~$5.5M$0
Value to Gen 3~$14.8M$23.3M+

Key requirements:

  • Perpetual-trust jurisdiction: The trust must be established in a state that permits dynasty trusts of indefinite duration — Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska, Delaware, Nevada. Most states still have the Rule Against Perpetuities limiting trust duration.
  • GST tax exemption allocation: The trust must be properly structured and documented to allocate GST exemption to the contributions. Work with an estate attorney who has done this before.
  • Irrevocability: The trust is irrevocable. You give up control of the assets.

Wyoming is typically the preferred jurisdiction for Bitcoin dynasty trusts — see our analysis at Wyoming Trusts for Bitcoin. The generational wealth transfer framework covers the full multi-generational planning context: Passing Bitcoin Across Generations.

Step 5: Annual Gifting Strategy

Step 5 of 6

Transfer Bitcoin incrementally using the annual gift tax exclusion

The annual gift tax exclusion is a simple, powerful tool that requires no trust structure and no professional fees beyond the tax reporting.

In 2024, you can gift up to $18,000 per recipient per year without using any of your lifetime gift/estate tax exemption and without filing a gift tax return. Married couples can combine exclusions to gift $36,000 per recipient per year. With multiple children, grandchildren, and their spouses as recipients, an annual gifting program can move significant value out of your estate over time.

For Bitcoin specifically, annual gifting works best when:

  • Your Bitcoin position is large enough that small incremental transfers meaningfully reduce estate exposure over time
  • You want to introduce heirs to Bitcoin ownership gradually, with small amounts they must manage themselves
  • You want to start the clock on your heirs' holding period for long-term capital gains purposes

Important tax note: when you gift Bitcoin, the recipient takes your cost basis. If you gifted Bitcoin purchased at $10,000 when it's worth $100,000, the recipient owes capital gains tax on the full $90,000 gain when they sell. This is the key trade-off with gifting versus holding until death (where the heir receives a stepped-up basis). Work with your CPA to model the basis implications of your gifting strategy.

Step 6: Family LLC or LP with Valuation Discounts

Step 6 of 6

Use entity structure to reduce the taxable value of gifted interests

Transferring minority interests in a family LLC holding Bitcoin may create valuation discounts of 20-40%, reducing the effective gift or estate tax cost.

When Bitcoin is held in a family limited liability company (LLC) or limited partnership (LP), minority interests in that entity may be valued at a discount to the pro-rata underlying Bitcoin value. This discount reflects two real-world conditions:

  • Lack of control discount: A minority member cannot force a distribution, liquidation, or sale — so a 10% LLC interest is worth less than 10% of the LLC's Bitcoin
  • Lack of marketability discount: There is no established market for private LLC interests; a buyer would demand a discount for illiquidity

In practice, combined discounts of 20-40% are common for legitimately structured family LLCs, as established in IRS litigation and court decisions. If you gift a 20% LLC interest when the LLC holds $10 million in Bitcoin, the gift may be valued at $1.4-1.6 million (rather than $2 million) for gift tax purposes — a meaningful reduction.

This strategy requires genuine non-tax business purposes for the LLC, legitimate governance structure, and careful compliance with IRS rules regarding family entity discounts. It should be implemented only with experienced legal and tax counsel. The full framework for structuring Bitcoin through an LLC is covered in How to Structure Bitcoin for Family Wealth.

Most comprehensive estate plans for significant Bitcoin holders use a combination of these strategies — often a GRAT to transfer current holdings' growth, a dynasty trust for new accumulations, annual gifting for smaller transfers, and an LLC for asset protection and discount purposes. The strategies are not mutually exclusive.
