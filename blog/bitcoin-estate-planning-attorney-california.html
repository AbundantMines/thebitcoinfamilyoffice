Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney: What California Holders Need to Know

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  9 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning: California Est. 9 min read

California is home to an enormous concentration of early Bitcoin wealth — venture capitalists, early adopters, and technology founders who accumulated Bitcoin when prices were measured in hundreds, not tens of thousands, of dollars. For these holders, California's estate planning environment is a mixed picture: no state estate tax is favorable, but community property complexity, notoriously expensive probate, the highest state income tax in the nation, and serious trust taxation issues make California one of the most challenging states for Bitcoin estate planning.

This guide covers what California Bitcoin holders specifically need to understand about their estate planning environment and what to look for in a Bitcoin-literate attorney. This is educational content only — we do not recommend specific attorneys.

No California State Estate Tax — But Don't Stop There

California imposes no state estate tax. Like Bitcoin family office in Texas and Bitcoin family office in Florida, California's estate tax was linked to the federal state death tax credit (IRC §2011), repealed federally in 2005. California has not enacted a standalone replacement. Legislation has been proposed periodically, but no California estate tax is currently in force.

For Bitcoin families below the federal exemption ($13.99M per person in 2025), this means no estate tax at any level. For families above it, only the federal estate tax applies. This is genuinely favorable — but the absence of estate tax does not mean California is otherwise tax-neutral. The income tax picture is the opposite of favorable.

California Income Tax: The Trust Trap

California imposes income tax at rates up to 13.3% — the highest top marginal state income tax rate in the United States. This applies to capital gains (California does not distinguish between long-term and short-term capital gains — all gains are taxed as ordinary income at the full rate), trust income, and business income. For Bitcoin holders realizing gains in California, the combined federal + state marginal rate on capital gains can exceed 37% (20% federal long-term + 3.8% NIIT + 13.3% California).

Critically, California also taxes trust income if any of the following exist: the trust has a California-resident trustee, a California-resident noncontingent beneficiary, or the trust was created by a California testator. This means a California-resident Bitcoin holder cannot simply establish a Nevada or Wyoming trust and expect to eliminate California income tax on the trust's investment income — California will assert tax jurisdiction regardless of where the trust is nominally "sited" if a California resident is the trustee or a significant beneficiary.

California taxes trust income if any trustee or noncontingent beneficiary is a California resident — regardless of where the trust is legally formed. This is the critical planning challenge for California Bitcoin families using out-of-state Bitcoin Trust Type Selector tools.

There are legitimate structures that can reduce California's trust income tax reach, but they require careful implementation: removing all California-resident trustees, ensuring beneficiaries are not California residents, and satisfying California's specific nexus rules. These structures work — but they require an attorney who deeply understands California's trust income tax rules, not just the general proposition that "Wyoming trusts avoid state tax."

Community Property: Both an Opportunity and a Complexity

California is a community property state under California Family Code §760. This affects what each spouse can do with the Bitcoin, how it is treated in a divorce, what happens at death, and how the step-up in basis applies.

The community property step-up at death is a significant benefit: when the first California spouse dies, 100% of community property — including the surviving spouse's half — receives a step-up in basis to the date-of-death fair market value. For a couple holding 10 BTC purchased at an average of $5,000 each (total basis: $50,000) with a date-of-death value of $950,000, the entire $900,000 in appreciation is eliminated for both spouses' shares when the first spouse dies. This is a major tax advantage compared to common law states.

But community property also creates complications. Separate property Bitcoin (acquired before marriage, or received as a gift or inheritance) must be carefully traced and documented, or it risks being commingled and reclassified as community property. California's community property presumption is strong — any property acquired during marriage is presumed community property unless clearly established otherwise. Bitcoin holders who accumulated coins before marriage, or who acquired Bitcoin as separate property gifts, need an attorney who understands California community property tracing and can document the separate property character from the outset.

California Probate: The Strongest Argument for a Trust

California has one of the most expensive and time-consuming probate processes in the United States. California Probate Code §10810 sets statutory attorney fees and executor fees at a percentage of the gross estate value — not net value. On a $2 million gross estate, the statutory fee is $46,000 for the attorney alone, plus the same amount for the executor. On a $5 million estate, it rises to $108,000 — and these fees apply even if the estate is heavily leveraged.

California probate also takes time: a typical uncontested California probate takes 9–18 months Bitcoin family office minimum requirements. Complex estates — particularly those involving digital assets that the court must characterize and supervise — can take considerably longer. And California probate is a public proceeding: the will and all filings become part of the public record.

The combination of cost, time, and publicity makes the case for a revocable living trust in California stronger than in almost any other state. Assets held in a properly funded trust avoid California probate entirely. For Bitcoin holders, this means the trust must be properly funded — meaning the trust ownership documents reference the Bitcoin holdings explicitly, not just generic "personal property."

California and RUFADAA

California adopted RUFADAA (the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act) in 2016, effective January 1, 2017, codified at California Probate Code §§870–884. Under California's RUFADAA, a trustee has authority to manage digital assets including Bitcoin when the governing trust document expressly grants that authority. Without explicit authorization in the trust document, the trustee's authority over self-custodied Bitcoin is uncertain.

California's version of RUFADAA also permits the decedent to authorize fiduciary access through an online tool provided by the digital asset custodian — for example, Coinbase's account inheritance settings. For self-custodied Bitcoin, the trust document's express grant of authority is the only reliable mechanism.

Why California Bitcoin Holders Benefit from Wyoming or Nevada Trust Situs

Wyoming and Nevada offer superior trust infrastructure for Bitcoin families: perpetual dynasty trusts, robust directed trust statutes, no state income tax on trust income (if properly structured to eliminate California nexus), and a growing network of Bitcoin-native institutional custodians. California Bitcoin holders frequently establish trusts sited in Wyoming or Nevada to access these advantages.

The key planning requirement: the trust must be structured to eliminate California's basis for asserting income tax jurisdiction. This means no California-resident trustee (use a Wyoming or Nevada corporate trustee), no California-resident noncontingent beneficiaries (distributions may be discretionary rather than fixed to avoid triggering California nexus), and careful attention to California's 13-factor nexus analysis for trust taxation.

An attorney who advises California Bitcoin holders on Wyoming or Nevada trust structures without understanding California's trust income tax rules is providing dangerously incomplete advice. The out-of-state siting only works if the California nexus is deliberately and competently severed.

What to Look for in a California Bitcoin Estate Planning Attorney

  • Deep knowledge of California community property law as applied to Bitcoin. This includes acquisition tracing, commingling risks, marital property agreements (prenuptial or postnuptial), and the community property step-up at death.
  • Understanding of California's trust income tax rules. The attorney must be able to explain California's nexus rules for trusts and advise on whether an out-of-state trust situs will actually eliminate California income tax — or not.
  • Experience with California probate avoidance. A living trust is virtually mandatory for any California Bitcoin holder with meaningful assets. The attorney should routinely draft Bitcoin-specific trust provisions, not just add generic boilerplate.
  • RUFADAA fluency. California's RUFADAA implementation requires express authorization in trust documents for digital asset management. The attorney must know what language is necessary.
  • Multi-jurisdictional trust structuring experience. If the attorney recommends a Wyoming or Nevada trust, they must also understand how to prevent California from asserting tax jurisdiction over it — or they are setting up a structure that will fail.
