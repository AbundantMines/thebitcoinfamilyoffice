Bitcoin Estate Planning for Single Parents: Protecting Your Children's Future

You are the only safety net your children have. Here's how to make sure your Bitcoin — and your kids — are protected if something happens to you.

Published March 16, 2026 · 22 min read

Here's the uncomfortable truth that most estate planning guides dance around: if you're a single parent holding Bitcoin and you die without a plan, your children don't just lose access to your Bitcoin. They potentially lose the right to choose who raises them, the ability to access your wealth when they need it most, and the protection from receiving a life-changing sum of money before they're ready for it.

Married couples have a built-in safety net. When one spouse dies, the other steps in — manages the finances, raises the kids, keeps life moving. Single parents don't have that. You are the single point of failure in your children's lives, and Bitcoin's unique properties make this exponentially more urgent than it would be with a traditional brokerage account.

A brokerage account gets frozen at death and eventually distributed through probate. Annoying, but the assets don't disappear. Bitcoin stored in self-custody can become permanently inaccessible if no one knows how to reach it. The seed phrase written on a steel plate in your safe deposit box is worthless if nobody knows it exists — or if the person who finds it doesn't know what it is.

This guide walks through everything a single parent with Bitcoin needs to have in place. Not eventually. Now. Because the difference between "I'll get to it" and "it's done" is the difference between your children being protected and your children being at the mercy of a probate court.

The Single Parent's Unique Exposure

When a married parent dies without a will, the surviving spouse typically inherits everything and continues as the children's legal guardian. The system, imperfect as it is, has a default path that mostly works.

When a single parent dies without a will, there is no default path that works. Every single outcome requires a court to intervene.

Guardian appointment becomes a court decision. Without a will naming a guardian, a probate judge decides who raises your children. The court considers family relationships, stability, and the children's best interests — but "best interests" is subjective, and the judge doesn't know your family. Your sister who shares your values and parenting philosophy? She might lose out to the other parent who hasn't been involved in years, or to a grandparent you love but wouldn't choose as a primary caregiver.

Minor children cannot inherit property directly. This catches many parents off guard. Under every state's laws, minors lack the legal capacity to own property outright. If you die without a trust, your Bitcoin and other assets go into a court-supervised custodial arrangement. A judge appoints someone to manage those assets — and that person may not be who you'd pick. They'll manage your Bitcoin under court oversight until your child turns 18, at which point your child receives everything with zero restrictions.

Bitcoin adds a layer of complexity courts aren't equipped for. Probate courts handle bank accounts, real estate, and brokerage accounts every day. They are not equipped to handle hardware wallets, seed phrases, multi-signature setups, or the distinction between exchange-held and self-custodied Bitcoin. A court-appointed custodian who doesn't understand Bitcoin could make catastrophic mistakes — from losing access entirely to creating unnecessary tax events by liquidating at the wrong time.

The bottom line: as a single parent with Bitcoin, you have more exposure than almost any other estate planning scenario. No spouse to fall back on. An asset class that courts struggle with. And children who are counting on you to have this figured out.

Choosing a Guardian: The Most Important Decision You'll Make

Forget the trust structure, the tax optimization, and the key management protocol for a moment. The single most important decision in your estate plan is this: who raises your children if you can't?

This is the decision that keeps single parents up at night, and it's the one most likely to cause analysis paralysis. But here's the thing — an imperfect choice documented in a will is infinitely better than no choice at all, because no choice means a judge makes it for you.

Personal Guardian vs. Financial Guardian

Most people assume the guardian is the guardian — one person who handles everything. But you can (and often should) split the role:

  • Personal guardian: The person who raises your children day-to-day. Lives with them, makes parenting decisions, handles school and healthcare. This should be someone who shares your values and parenting approach.
  • Financial guardian (or trustee): The person who manages your children's inheritance. Makes investment decisions, approves distributions, files tax returns for the trust. This should be someone with financial literacy and ideally some understanding of Bitcoin.

These don't have to be — and often shouldn't be — the same person. Your brother might be the perfect person to raise your kids but terrible with money. Your financially savvy friend might be a great trustee but isn't the right fit as a daily caregiver. Splitting the roles creates a natural system of checks and balances, and it means you're choosing each person for what they're actually good at.

What Courts Consider

If your guardian nomination is ever challenged (which happens more often than people expect, especially by the other biological parent), courts evaluate:

  • The child's existing relationship with the proposed guardian
  • The guardian's stability — financial, emotional, geographic
  • The child's preference (if old enough to express one)
  • Proximity to the child's current school, community, and support network
  • The biological parent's rights (which are strong, even if they've been absent)

Document your reasoning. If you're choosing someone other than the obvious choice (a grandparent, the other biological parent), write a letter explaining why. This isn't legally binding on its own, but judges consider it heavily. "I chose my sister because she shares my approach to education and has been actively involved in my children's lives since birth" is far more persuasive to a court than a bare nomination with no context.

Backup Guardians and Contingency Planning

Name at least one successor guardian. If your first choice predeceases you, becomes incapacitated, or simply can't serve when the time comes, you need a backup. Without one, you're back to the court deciding.

Also consider: what if your children are different ages and have different needs? Some parents name the same guardian for all children to keep siblings together. Others make different arrangements if the age gap is significant. There's no universal right answer, but you need to think through it deliberately.

Why Minor Children Can't Inherit Bitcoin Directly

This is the piece that makes single-parent Bitcoin estate planning fundamentally different from just writing a will and calling it done.

Under U.S. law, minors cannot own property outright. They can't sign contracts, manage investments, or hold title to assets. This applies to everything — real estate, stocks, bank accounts — and it absolutely applies to Bitcoin.

So what happens if a single parent dies and their will says "I leave everything to my children"?

The assets don't go directly to the children. Instead, one of two things happens:

  1. Court-supervised custodianship: A court appoints a custodian to manage the assets until the child reaches the age of majority (18 in most states). The custodian must report to the court, get approval for significant transactions, and hand over everything — no strings attached — when the child turns 18.
  2. UTMA/UGMA custodial account: If the will directs assets into a Uniform Transfers to Minors Act account, a custodian manages them until the child reaches the UTMA age (18 or 21 depending on the state). Same result: full, unrestricted access at a fixed age.

Now think about what this means for Bitcoin specifically.

Your 8-year-old inherits $500,000 in Bitcoin. A court-appointed custodian — who may or may not understand cryptocurrency — manages it for 10 years under court oversight. Every significant decision requires court approval. The custodian might liquidate the Bitcoin to simplify management, triggering a massive capital gains event. Or they might hold it but have no idea how to properly secure self-custodied Bitcoin.

Then, at 18, your child receives the entire balance with absolutely no restrictions. No guidance, no conditions, no phased distributions. A teenager with potentially life-changing wealth and no structure around how to use it.

This is the scenario that a properly structured trust eliminates entirely.

The Minor's Trust: Your Children's Financial Safety Net

A trust designed for minor beneficiaries solves every problem that direct inheritance and UTMA accounts create. It gives you control over who manages the Bitcoin, how it's invested, when your children receive it, and under what conditions.

How It Works

You create a revocable living trust during your lifetime. You transfer your Bitcoin (and ideally your other significant assets) into the trust. You name a trustee to manage the trust assets if you die or become incapacitated. The trust document spells out exactly how assets should be managed and distributed.

While you're alive, you control everything. You can modify the trust, move Bitcoin in and out, change beneficiaries, or revoke it entirely. The trust becomes irrevocable — locked in — only at your death.

Distribution Schedules

This is where trusts dramatically outperform every alternative. Instead of handing your child everything at 18, you set a distribution schedule that reflects your values and your assessment of when your children will be ready:

A common structure for substantial Bitcoin holdings:

  • One-third at age 25: Old enough to have some adult experience, young enough to benefit from the remaining structure
  • One-third at age 30: By now, they've had five years of experience managing the first distribution
  • Final third at age 35: Full distribution of remaining trust assets

Some parents adjust these based on milestones rather than (or in addition to) ages — completion of a degree, demonstrated financial responsibility, or establishment of a career. Your attorney can build in whatever conditions matter to you.

Key Trust Provisions for Bitcoin

Spendthrift clause: Prevents creditors from reaching the trust assets and stops beneficiaries from pledging their future distributions as collateral. Essential protection, especially for young adults who might make impulsive financial commitments.

Trustee investment standards: Your trust should explicitly authorize the trustee to hold Bitcoin and other digital assets. Without this language, a trustee operating under the default "prudent investor" standard might feel legally obligated to sell the Bitcoin and invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks and bonds. If your intent is for the trustee to hold Bitcoin, say so explicitly in the trust document.

Education and health distributions: Even before the scheduled distribution ages, the trustee should have authority to use trust assets for your children's education, healthcare, and basic support. This ensures your kids are taken care of during the years before they receive direct distributions.

Successor trustee: If your chosen trustee can't serve (death, incapacity, resignation), who steps in? Name at least one successor. Consider a corporate trustee as a final backstop — they're not ideal for Bitcoin-specific management, but they won't die or become incapacitated.

UTMA vs. Trust: Why the Trust Almost Always Wins for Bitcoin

You'll hear about UTMA (Uniform Transfers to Minors Act) and UGMA (Uniform Gifts to Minors Act) accounts as simpler alternatives to trusts. And they are simpler — that's the problem.

Feature UTMA/UGMA Minor's Trust
Distribution age 18 or 21 (state-dependent, no flexibility) Any age(s) you choose
Distribution conditions None — full amount, no strings Custom conditions (education, milestones, phased)
Creditor protection Minimal Strong (spendthrift clause)
Investment flexibility Custodian has broad discretion but no specific Bitcoin authorization Can explicitly authorize Bitcoin holding and specific custody methods
Multiple beneficiaries Separate account per child Can pool or separate as needed, with spray provisions
Privacy Moderate Strong — trusts avoid probate and public record
Setup cost Minimal $2,000–$5,000+ for attorney-drafted trust

For a single parent with meaningful Bitcoin holdings — say, anything above $100,000 — a trust is almost always worth the additional cost and complexity. The ability to control distribution timing alone justifies it. Add creditor protection, explicit Bitcoin authorization for the trustee, and probate avoidance, and it's not even close.

UTMAs can work for smaller amounts or as a supplement, but they should never be the primary vehicle for transferring significant Bitcoin wealth to minor children.

Life Insurance: The Liquidity Bridge Your Plan Needs

Here's a scenario that catches even well-planned estates off guard: you die during a Bitcoin bear market. Your trust holds 10 BTC that was worth $1 million when you set up the plan, but it's worth $300,000 at the time of your death. Your children need support now — housing, education, daily expenses — and the trustee is forced to sell Bitcoin at the worst possible time to fund those needs.

Life insurance solves this. It provides immediate, guaranteed liquidity that doesn't depend on Bitcoin's price. The death benefit arrives in weeks, not months, and it's not subject to market timing.

How Single Parents Should Structure Life Insurance

Term life insurance is the foundation. It's affordable, straightforward, and provides a large death benefit relative to premium cost. A healthy 35-year-old single parent can typically get $1 million in 20-year term coverage for $50–$80 per month. That $1 million becomes the liquidity cushion that lets your trustee hold the Bitcoin through market cycles instead of panic-selling.

Name the trust as beneficiary, not the children. If you name minor children as life insurance beneficiaries, you're back to the same problem — minors can't receive property directly. The insurance company will require a court-appointed custodian, and the proceeds will be handed over at 18 with no conditions. Instead, name your revocable trust as the beneficiary. The proceeds flow into the trust and are governed by the same distribution schedule and conditions you've already established.

Consider an ILIT for larger estates. If your combined Bitcoin and other assets put your estate near or above the federal estate tax exemption (currently around $13.6 million per person, though this may change), an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) removes the life insurance proceeds from your taxable estate. The ILIT owns the policy, so the death benefit isn't counted as part of your estate for tax purposes. For single parents with substantial Bitcoin holdings in an appreciating market, this can save millions in estate taxes.

How Much Coverage?

The standard formula for single parents: enough to cover your children's living expenses, education costs, and any debts through their dependency years. A common benchmark is 15–20 times your annual expenses, but for Bitcoin holders, also factor in the possibility that you'd want your trustee to hold the Bitcoin long-term rather than liquidate for immediate needs.

Think of it this way: life insurance covers the short-term cash needs so your Bitcoin can remain a long-term asset in the trust.

Durable Power of Attorney and Healthcare Directive: Planning for Incapacity

Estate planning isn't just about death. It's about incapacity — the scenario where you're alive but unable to manage your affairs. For a single parent, this can be just as devastating as death, and in some ways more complex.

If you're in a coma, have a debilitating injury, or develop a condition that impairs your decision-making ability, who manages your Bitcoin? Who makes medical decisions for you? Who takes care of your children in the meantime?

Without the right documents, the answer is: whoever the court appoints after a lengthy, expensive guardianship proceeding.

Durable Financial Power of Attorney

A durable power of attorney (POA) designates someone — your "agent" — to manage your financial affairs if you become incapacitated. "Durable" means it remains effective even after you lose capacity (a regular POA terminates at incapacity, which is exactly when you need it most).

For Bitcoin holders, the POA must be specifically drafted to cover digital assets. A generic POA that mentions "bank accounts and investments" may not give your agent clear authority to access cryptocurrency exchanges, manage hardware wallets, or execute transactions on your behalf. Work with an attorney to include explicit digital asset language.

Your incapacity plan should address:

  • Authority to access and manage cryptocurrency exchange accounts
  • Authority to manage hardware wallets and execute Bitcoin transactions
  • Authority to manage multi-signature wallet arrangements
  • Instructions for maintaining (not liquidating) Bitcoin positions unless necessary for your care or your children's support
  • Specific language about digital assets that satisfies the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (RUFADAA), adopted in most states

Healthcare Directive / Healthcare Proxy

Separate from the financial POA, a healthcare directive (also called an advance directive or living will) specifies your medical treatment preferences and designates someone to make healthcare decisions if you can't. This person doesn't need to know anything about Bitcoin — they need to know your values about medical care.

For single parents, these two documents are non-negotiable. Without them, your family faces a court proceeding to establish guardianship over you and your affairs — a process that takes weeks or months while your children's lives are in limbo and your Bitcoin sits unmanaged.

The Letter of Instructions: Your Bitcoin Access Manual

This might be the most Bitcoin-specific document in your entire estate plan, and it's the one most people skip.

A letter of instructions is not a legal document. It's not binding. A court won't enforce it. But for Bitcoin holders, it's arguably the most critical piece of the puzzle because it's the document that tells your trustee how to actually access and manage your Bitcoin.

What the Letter Should Contain

  • Exchange accounts: Which exchanges hold Bitcoin (Coinbase, Kraken, etc.), approximate balances, and how to access them. Don't include passwords directly — reference where they're stored (password manager, sealed envelope in safe deposit box, etc.).
  • Hardware wallet inventory: Number of devices, makes/models, physical location. "Two Coldcard Mk4 devices — one in the home safe, one in the Chase safe deposit box at [branch address]."
  • Seed phrase access: Where seed phrases are stored, how to access them, and the format they're in. Steel plates, paper backups, encrypted digital backups — whatever your system is, document it completely. Include passphrases (the "25th word") if applicable.
  • Multi-signature details: If you use multi-sig, document the quorum requirement (2-of-3, 3-of-5, etc.), who holds each key, and the coordination wallet software. Multi-sig without documentation is a locked vault with no combination.
  • Trusted contacts: Technical contacts who can help your trustee navigate Bitcoin custody. A Bitcoin-savvy friend, a professional consultant, a specific service provider. People your trustee can call for help without risking the assets.
  • Personal messages: Many parents include letters to their children — not about Bitcoin, but about values, hopes, and guidance. These aren't legally necessary, but they matter.

Critical Rule: Never Put This in Your Will

Wills become public record when they go through probate. Anything in your will — including descriptions of where your Bitcoin is stored and how to access it — becomes accessible to anyone who requests the probate file. This is a security disaster.

The letter of instructions lives with the trust documents, stored securely, accessible only to the named trustee. Some attorneys hold a copy. Some families use secure storage services. The key is that it never enters the public record.

Update this letter at least annually, or whenever you change your custody setup, move Bitcoin between wallets, or change exchange accounts.

Digital Access Planning: Making Sure Your Bitcoin Is Actually Reachable

You can have a perfect trust, a brilliant trustee, and a comprehensive letter of instructions — and it all means nothing if your trustee can't physically access your Bitcoin.

Digital access planning bridges the gap between legal documents and practical reality. It's the operational layer of your estate plan.

The Self-Custody Challenge

Self-custody is the whole point of Bitcoin. You hold your keys, you control your wealth, no intermediary can freeze or seize your assets. But this strength becomes a vulnerability in estate planning because the same properties that protect your Bitcoin from third parties also protect it from your trustee.

If your trustee can't access your seed phrase, your Bitcoin is gone. Not "frozen in a legal dispute" gone — permanently, irreversibly gone. No court order can recover Bitcoin from a lost seed phrase.

Multi-Signature: The Gold Standard for Single-Parent Estate Planning

Multi-signature (multi-sig) wallet arrangements are the most robust solution for single parents who want both security during their lifetime and accessibility after death.

A common structure: a 2-of-3 multi-sig wallet where you hold two keys and your designated trustee holds one. During your lifetime, you control everything — you can transact with your two keys without the trustee's involvement. The trustee's key is inert until needed.

At your death or incapacity, the trustee uses their key plus one of your keys (documented in the letter of instructions) to access the Bitcoin. The trustee alone can't steal the Bitcoin during your lifetime (they only have 1 of 3 keys), and they can access it when needed without relying solely on finding a seed phrase in a safe.

Variations include:

  • 3-of-5: More redundancy, useful for larger holdings. Keys distributed among trustee, backup trustee, attorney, and two held by you.
  • Collaborative custody services: Companies like Unchained offer multi-sig vaults where they hold one key, you hold two, and the setup includes inheritance planning features.
  • Time-locked recovery: Some setups include a time-locked key that becomes active after a period of wallet inactivity, providing a backup access method.

Exchange-Held Bitcoin

Bitcoin held on exchanges is generally easier for trustees to access — it's similar to any other financial account. The trustee presents legal documentation (death certificate, trust documents, letters testamentary) and the exchange transfers the account or liquidates and distributes.

But exchange custody comes with its own risks: exchange insolvency, regulatory seizure, and loss of the account-holder's unique 2FA access. If you hold Bitcoin on exchanges, make sure your trustee has a documented path to access those accounts, including 2FA recovery methods.

The Practical Checklist

Before your estate plan is complete, verify that your trustee can answer "yes" to each of these:

  1. Do you know where the hardware wallets are physically located?
  2. Do you know how to access the seed phrases?
  3. Do you have (or know how to obtain) any required passphrases?
  4. Do you know which exchanges hold Bitcoin and how to contact them?
  5. Do you have access to the password manager or credential storage?
  6. Do you know who to call for technical help if needed?
  7. For multi-sig setups: do you have your key and know where to find the additional required key(s)?

If the answer to any of these is "no," your estate plan has a gap that could result in permanent loss of Bitcoin.

The 4-Document Minimum: Nothing Else Is Optional

Single parents need exactly four documents to have a baseline estate plan. Not a great plan — a baseline. The absolute minimum below which you are leaving your children exposed.

1. Last Will and Testament (with Guardian Nomination)

The will serves two critical functions for single parents: it nominates a guardian for your minor children, and it serves as a catch-all for any assets that aren't in the trust (a "pour-over" will directs them into the trust at death).

The guardian nomination is the most important sentence in this document. Courts give substantial weight to a parent's stated preference, and without it, they're guessing.

For Bitcoin holders: the will itself should not contain detailed information about your Bitcoin holdings, locations, or access methods. Wills are public documents after probate. Keep the Bitcoin details in the letter of instructions and trust documents.

2. Revocable Living Trust

This is the vehicle that holds your Bitcoin and other significant assets. It names the trustee who will manage them, establishes the distribution schedule for your children, sets the rules for how Bitcoin should be managed (hold vs. sell, custody standards, investment authority), and avoids probate entirely.

For single parents, the trust is the backbone of the plan. Without it, you're relying on probate courts and UTMA accounts — neither of which gives you meaningful control over how your Bitcoin is managed or when your children receive it.

3. Durable Power of Attorney (Financial)

Covers the incapacity scenario. Names your agent, grants them authority over your financial affairs including digital assets, and takes effect either immediately (with you still retaining control) or upon a triggering event like a physician's certification of incapacity.

For Bitcoin: must include explicit digital asset language. Generic financial POAs often fail to cover cryptocurrency exchanges and self-custody arrangements.

4. Healthcare Proxy / Advance Directive

Names your healthcare decision-maker and documents your treatment preferences. This has nothing to do with Bitcoin — but for a single parent, it's just as critical as the financial documents. If you're incapacitated and your children need someone making medical decisions for you and coordinating their care, this document makes that happen without court intervention.

The Supporting Documents

Beyond the four essentials, your complete Bitcoin estate planning package should also include:

  • Letter of instructions: The Bitcoin access manual described above
  • Beneficiary designation review: Ensure life insurance, retirement accounts, and any exchange accounts name the trust (not individuals) as beneficiary
  • Digital asset inventory: Updated annually, listing all Bitcoin holdings, custody methods, and access information
  • Guardian letter: Personal letter to the nominated guardian explaining your parenting values, children's needs, and any specific wishes

Putting It All Together: The Single Parent Action Plan

If you've read this far, you understand why this matters. Here's how to actually get it done, in order of priority:

Step 1: Choose your guardian and trustee. These are human decisions, not legal ones. Have the conversations. Make sure the people you're choosing are willing to serve. If you're splitting the roles (personal guardian and financial trustee), make sure both understand their scope.

Step 2: Hire an estate planning attorney. Not a general practitioner — an attorney who understands digital assets and has drafted trusts that hold Bitcoin. Ask specifically: "Have you drafted trust provisions authorizing the trustee to hold and manage cryptocurrency?" If the answer is no, keep looking.

Step 3: Execute the four documents. Will with guardian nomination, revocable trust, durable POA, healthcare directive. This is the minimum viable estate plan.

Step 4: Fund the trust. Transfer your Bitcoin into the trust's name (or, for self-custodied Bitcoin, document the trust's ownership of the wallets holding the Bitcoin). This step is frequently skipped — people create a trust but never transfer assets into it, which means the trust is an empty shell at death.

Step 5: Set up the digital access system. Write the letter of instructions. Establish the multi-sig arrangement with your trustee if applicable. Store seed phrase backups securely. Verify your trustee can actually access the Bitcoin by walking through the process together.

Step 6: Get life insurance. Term life at minimum. Name the trust as beneficiary. Consider an ILIT if your estate is large enough to face estate tax exposure.

Step 7: Review annually. Bitcoin holdings change. Custody setups evolve. Children grow. Trustees' circumstances shift. Review the entire plan at least once a year, and update the letter of instructions any time your Bitcoin custody changes.

Common Mistakes Single Parents Make

"I'll do it when I have more Bitcoin." The amount doesn't matter. If you have children and you have Bitcoin — any amount — you need at minimum a will with a guardian nomination. The trust becomes essential once holdings are significant enough that distribution timing matters, but the guardian decision is urgent at any wealth level.

Naming minor children as direct beneficiaries. On life insurance, retirement accounts, exchange accounts — anywhere you name a beneficiary, it should be the trust, not the children directly. Direct beneficiary designations to minors create the exact court-supervised custodial situation you're trying to avoid.

Telling no one about the Bitcoin. Privacy is important, but secrecy is dangerous. Your trustee needs to know the Bitcoin exists, approximately how much there is, and how to access it. You don't need to give them the keys today, but they need to know the keys exist and where to find them.

Using a will as the primary transfer mechanism. Wills go through probate. Probate is public, slow, expensive, and puts a court in charge. For Bitcoin, probate is also potentially dangerous — a court might order liquidation. Use the trust as the primary vehicle and the will as the backup catch-all.

Forgetting to update. An estate plan from three years ago that references a Trezor you no longer own and a Coinbase account you've closed is worse than useless — it sends your trustee on a wild goose chase while the actual Bitcoin sits undiscovered in a new Coldcard wallet.

The Cost of Doing Nothing

Let's be direct about what happens if a single parent with Bitcoin dies without an estate plan:

  1. A court decides who raises your children, potentially over the objections of the people you'd actually choose.
  2. Your Bitcoin enters probate — a public proceeding where your holdings become part of the court record.
  3. A court-appointed custodian manages your Bitcoin, possibly someone with no cryptocurrency experience.
  4. Your children receive everything at 18 with no conditions, no phased distributions, and no spendthrift protection.
  5. If your Bitcoin is in self-custody and no one knows how to access it, it may be lost permanently.
  6. Your children's financial future becomes a matter of luck rather than planning.

The cost of a proper estate plan — $3,000 to $7,000 for attorney fees, plus life insurance premiums — is trivial compared to the cost of not having one. And the time investment is measured in weeks, not months.

You're the only safety net your children have. Make sure the net holds.
