Bitcoin Estate Planning in Louisiana

The Bitcoin Family Office  ·  February 2026  ·  10 min read

Home › Research › Bitcoin Estate Planning in Louisiana Est. 10 min read

Louisiana stands alone among U.S. states in one critical respect: it is the only state that derives its legal system primarily from civil law rather than common law. Louisiana's legal heritage traces to French and Spanish civil law traditions, codified in the Louisiana Civil Code, rather than to the English common law system that underlies every other state's legal framework. For Bitcoin estate planning Louisiana, this distinction is not historical trivia — it shapes the entire estate planning environment in ways that require specialized expertise that most common law estate attorneys do not possess.

The most significant civil-law-derived planning constraint for Bitcoin families is forced heirship. Louisiana law requires that the "forced heirs" of a decedent — defined as children under the age of 24, and children of any age who are permanently incapacitated — receive a fixed portion of the estate (the "forced portion" or "légitime") regardless of the decedent's testamentary wishes. This is not a default rule that can be overridden by a will or trust. It is a substantive right of the forced heirs that cannot be disinherited except in narrow statutory circumstances. For Bitcoin families, this means a meaningful Bitcoin position may be legally required to pass to minor children even if the decedent's plan was to hold Bitcoin in trust for a different purpose or beneficiary set.

Forced Heirship: The Critical Louisiana Constraint

Louisiana's forced heirship provisions (Louisiana Civil Code Articles 1493–1505) provide forced heirs with a fixed share of the "disposable portion" — the portion of the estate not subject to forced heirship — varying by number of forced heirs:

  • One forced heir: The forced portion is one-quarter (25%) of the estate.
  • Two or more forced heirs: The forced portion is one-half (50%) of the estate.

The forced portion applies to the decedent's entire estate — including Bitcoin. A Louisiana Bitcoin holder with two minor children cannot, by will or trust, direct 100% of their Bitcoin to a spouse, a charitable organization, or a long-term dynasty trust without first satisfying the children's forced portion. The forced portion must pass to the forced heirs in ownership — not merely in trust or in usufruct — unless the forced heirs consent after reaching the age of majority.

Louisiana forced heirship is not a default that estate planning can avoid. It is a substantive right of minor children that attaches to the estate as a matter of law. Every Louisiana Bitcoin family with minor children must plan around it — not through it.

There are important nuances: the forced heirship rules apply to "successions" — property passing at death. Property that passes outside the succession — through jointly titled accounts, beneficiary designations, or certain inter vivos trusts properly structured under Louisiana law — may not be subject to the forced portion calculation. Bitcoin estate planning Louisiana must address whether and how Bitcoin transfers can be structured to minimize the forced heirship exposure without violating Louisiana's rules on simulation and simulation of donations.

Louisiana Trust Law: Not Common Law Trusts

Louisiana does have trust law — the Louisiana Trust Code (Louisiana Revised Statutes Title 9, §§ 1721–2252) — but it is a civil-law trust, not a common law trust. Louisiana trusts operate differently from trusts in every other state. Notably, Louisiana trusts cannot hold real property directly in some circumstances without specific provisions, and the interaction between trust law and forced heirship is complex and requires careful drafting by attorneys who practice Louisiana succession law specifically.

For Bitcoin families, the practical implications include:

  • Trusts do not automatically circumvent forced heirship: Inter vivos donations that are simulated or made in contemplation of death to defeat forced heirs can be subject to collation — a Louisiana doctrine requiring the donation to be brought back into the estate for forced heir calculations. Genuine inter vivos trusts funded well before death with no intent to circumvent forced heirship are on stronger footing, but require careful planning and documentation.
  • The trust instrument must conform to Louisiana Trust Code requirements: Common law trust templates used in other states are not appropriate for Louisiana trusts. An attorney licensed in Louisiana with specific expertise in the Louisiana Trust Code must draft any trust intended to hold Louisiana assets.
  • Usufruct: Louisiana law permits a surviving spouse to take a "usufruct" (similar to a life estate) over community property passing to children, which can provide a degree of planning flexibility that partially mitigates the immediate forced heirship impact on Bitcoin positions.

Community Property: Bitcoin Acquired During Marriage

Louisiana is a community property state under the Louisiana Civil Code. Bitcoin acquired during marriage with community funds is community property — each spouse owns an undivided one-half interest. This has significant estate planning implications: at the death of one spouse, only that spouse's one-half of the community Bitcoin is subject to succession. The other half already belongs to the surviving spouse. This community property regime interacts with forced heirship in complex ways that require analysis by a Louisiana succession attorney.

No State Estate or Inheritance Tax

Louisiana imposes no state estate tax and no state inheritance tax. This removes one complexity dimension — planning for Louisiana Bitcoin families is not complicated by a state wealth transfer tax layer. The only tax planning concern is federal: the federal estate tax for estates above the federal exemption (approximately $13.99 million per individual in 2025, with a projected reduction after 2025 if the TCJA provisions sunset).

Digital Asset Succession in Louisiana

Louisiana has adopted the Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (UFADAA, not the Revised version), codified at Louisiana Revised Statutes §§ 9:2271–9:2285. Louisiana's digital asset succession statute provides fiduciaries authority to access digital assets, subject to the decedent's express directives and platform terms of service. As in every state, the statute provides legal access authority — not technical access capability. Louisiana Bitcoin estate plans must include technical key succession protocols addressing private key access, multi-signature configurations, and hardware device locations.

Practical Priorities for Louisiana Bitcoin Families

  1. Engage a Louisiana succession attorney immediately — not a general estate attorney, and not an out-of-state attorney applying common law principles. Louisiana succession law is a distinct discipline. The forced heirship and community property analysis for your specific family situation requires someone who practices Louisiana civil law succession specifically.
  2. Map the forced heirship exposure: Identify who your forced heirs are (children under 24, permanently incapacitated children), calculate the forced portion based on number of forced heirs, and determine what portion of the Bitcoin position must be allocated to the forced portion regardless of other planning.
  3. Evaluate inter vivos trust strategies: Properly structured Louisiana trusts funded well before death — with genuine donative intent, not simulation — can provide planning flexibility while minimizing collation risk. This requires careful coordination with your Louisiana succession attorney.
  4. Address Bitcoin key succession separately: Implement a multi-signature custody architecture and maintain a technical succession protocol ensuring fiduciaries have both legal authority and practical key access capability.
  5. Consider domicile for planning purposes: For Louisiana families with very large Bitcoin positions and significant forced heirship exposure, a change of domicile to a state without forced heirship — before accumulation of the bulk of the Bitcoin estate — may be appropriate to evaluate. This is a significant personal decision with many non-financial dimensions, but it is worth modeling.
