Bitcoin Executor Guide: How to Handle a Bitcoin Estate in 12 Steps

By Hal Turner February 25, 2026 Executor Resource ~3,200 words

You've been named executor of an estate — or successor trustee of a trust — that includes Bitcoin. You have legal authority. What you may not have is the operational knowledge to act on it. This guide walks you through the next 60 days in 12 steps, starting with the single most important thing to do on day one (and what not to do).

Before You Begin: Three Things You Must Understand

Bitcoin is fundamentally different from every other asset you will manage as executor. Before you take any action, understand these three realities:

1. Legal Authority ≠ Operational Access

A court-issued Letters Testamentary makes you the legal executor of the estate. It gives you authority over bank accounts, brokerage accounts, real estate — and Bitcoin. But Bitcoin held in self-custody does not respond to court orders. The blockchain does not know you are the executor. Access to self-custodied Bitcoin requires the private keys or seed phrase — not a court document. You need both legal authority and operational access.

2. Irreversible Mistakes Are Possible

Sending Bitcoin to the wrong address is permanent. Attempting to access a hardware wallet with the wrong PIN repeatedly can permanently lock or wipe the device. Acting on bad advice from someone claiming to be a "Bitcoin recovery specialist" can result in theft of the entire estate. The first rule of Bitcoin estate administration is: when in doubt, do nothing and get qualified help.

3. The Step-Up in Basis Is Valuable — Don't Destroy It

When someone dies holding appreciated Bitcoin, their heirs receive a step-up in cost basis to the fair market value on the date of death. If Bitcoin was purchased at $5,000 and is worth $95,000 at death, the heirs' cost basis is $95,000 — not $5,000. This eliminates decades of capital gains. Do not sell the Bitcoin until you understand this rule and have confirmed the date-of-death value with a Bitcoin-literate CPA. Selling prematurely can cost heirs enormous sums in unnecessary taxes.

Phase 1 — Days 1–7: Secure and Document

Phase 1 · Days 1–7 · Secure and Document
Step 1
Locate and Photograph — Do Not Access

Find any hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor, Coldcard, or similar USB devices), any written documents that appear to be seed phrases (12 or 24 words), and any envelopes or documents labeled "Letter of Instruction," "Bitcoin Instructions," or similar. Photograph or document their location. Do not plug in hardware wallets. Do not attempt to enter any PINs. Do not photograph seed phrases (seed phrase photos are a security risk).

Secure these items physically — in a safe if available. If no safe, in a locked drawer or box. The goal of day one is preservation, not access.

Step 2
Find the Letter of Instruction

The Letter of Instruction (LOI) is a private document — not filed with a court, not part of the will — that the deceased should have prepared describing how to access their Bitcoin. It may contain: wallet locations, custody structure, who to contact for technical help, exchange account details, and the location (not the actual words) of seed phrase backups.

Search for the LOI in a home safe, fireproof file cabinet, with the estate attorney, with a designated "trusted contact," or in a sealed envelope stored with other important documents. If you find it, do not share it broadly — it is highly sensitive. If no LOI exists, proceed to the "If There Is No Letter of Instruction" section below.

Step 3
Record the Date-of-Death Value

The fair market value of Bitcoin on the exact date of death is critical for two reasons: (1) it establishes the gross estate value for estate tax purposes, and (2) it becomes the heirs' stepped-up cost basis. Record it immediately — look up the Bitcoin price on a credible source (CoinGecko, Kraken, Coinbase) for the specific date of death and note the USD value per BTC and the approximate number of BTC held. This is time-sensitive: estate tax returns require this figure.

If you do not yet know the exact amount of Bitcoin held, estimate based on any documents you've found. You will get the precise figure later from the wallet software, but the price on the date of death is fixed and needs to be captured now.

⚠ Scam Warning — Read Immediately

Within days of an obituary being published, scammers search for estates with Bitcoin. They will contact you claiming to be: "Bitcoin recovery specialists," "probate cryptocurrency consultants," forensic accountants who "specialize in digital assets," or even law enforcement. They will seem credible and offer to help you access the Bitcoin — for a fee or a percentage of the estate. No legitimate professional will contact you unsolicited. Every person who helps with this estate should be verified independently, hired through your estate attorney's referral, and have verifiable credentials. Do not share seed phrases, private keys, or wallet passwords with anyone you did not hire through proper channels.

Phase 2 — Days 8–21: Establish Legal Authority and Build Your Team

Phase 2 · Days 8–21 · Legal Authority + Team
Step 4
File for Letters Testamentary (or Begin Trust Administration)

If the deceased had a will, file for probate and obtain Letters Testamentary from the probate court. This document formally names you as executor and grants legal authority over estate assets. Some states have a waiting period; some have simplified small-estate affidavit procedures for estates under a threshold.

If the Bitcoin was held in a revocable trust (common for estates that planned ahead), the trust becomes irrevocable at death and you step in as successor trustee. No probate is required — the trust document itself is your authority. Obtain a certified copy of the trust.

Why this matters for Bitcoin: Exchanges will require documentation before allowing access to accounts. If the Bitcoin is on an exchange (Coinbase, Kraken, etc.), you cannot access it without legal authority documentation. If it's self-custodied, you technically have physical access before this step — but proceeding without legal authority creates personal liability.

Step 5
Engage a Bitcoin-Literate Estate Attorney

Your general estate attorney may be excellent but may not know what a seed phrase is. For Bitcoin estates of any meaningful size, you want an attorney who has handled digital asset estates before. Ask specifically: "Have you administered a self-custodied Bitcoin estate before?" and "Do you understand the difference between a hardware wallet and an exchange account?"

Your estate attorney will guide you through: probate or trust administration procedures in your state, exchange account succession (each exchange has its own deceased account process), required tax filings, and creditor notification requirements. Do not proceed to accessing or distributing Bitcoin without legal counsel.

Step 6
Engage a Bitcoin CPA

The tax implications of a Bitcoin estate are significant and specialized. A Bitcoin CPA will handle: the estate tax return (Form 706, required for estates above the federal exemption — verify current amount with your attorney), the final income tax return for the deceased, the step-up in basis documentation for heirs, and guidance on HIFO (Highest In, First Out) cost basis method to minimize future capital gains.

The CPA should also advise on whether Bitcoin should be distributed in-kind (Bitcoin transferred to heirs) or liquidated (sold for cash, then distributed). In most cases, distributing in-kind preserves the step-up in basis for heirs; the CPA will confirm based on the specific estate structure.

Step 7
Inventory All Bitcoin Holdings

With the Letter of Instruction and legal authority in hand, conduct a complete inventory. Look for: hardware wallets and the Bitcoin they hold, software wallets (Electrum, Sparrow, Bitcoin Core), exchange accounts (Coinbase, Kraken, Swan, River, Strike), Lightning Network wallets or nodes, and multi-signature wallet participation (requires coordination with co-signers).

For hardware wallets: connect to the appropriate software (Ledger Live for Ledger, Trezor Suite for Trezor) and record the balance without moving any Bitcoin. The goal of this step is inventory, not access for transfer.

For exchange accounts: contact the exchange's deceased account or estate team. Each exchange has a formal process requiring death certificate, Letters Testamentary or trust documentation, and sometimes notarized affidavits. Processing typically takes 2–6 weeks.

Step 8
Do Not Sell Bitcoin Yet

This step is about what not to do. The temptation to liquidate Bitcoin to simplify administration is understandable — but it may destroy significant value for heirs. The stepped-up cost basis means heirs who inherit Bitcoin and sell it immediately have zero capital gain. Selling during estate administration (before distribution to heirs) means the estate pays the gain, not the heirs at their stepped-up basis. Your CPA will advise on the specific structure, but the default is: do not sell.

There are exceptions: if the estate has insufficient liquid assets to pay debts and taxes, Bitcoin may need to be liquidated. If so, do this under your CPA's guidance, with full documentation of the forced-liquidation rationale.

Phase 3 — Days 22–60: Execute the Transfer

Phase 3 · Days 22–60 · Transfer and Close
Step 9
Establish New Wallets for Each Beneficiary

Before transferring Bitcoin to heirs, each heir should set up their own Bitcoin wallet and custody solution. You should not transfer Bitcoin to a wallet you control on their behalf — this creates a second custody hand-off and potential confusion. Ideally, each heir: sets up a hardware wallet (Ledger or Trezor are accessible for most heirs), backs up their seed phrase properly, and provides you with a receiving address.

If an heir is not Bitcoin-literate, this is the moment to engage a Bitcoin concierge or educator to walk them through the setup. Do not rush this step — a mis-configured wallet or a lost seed phrase at this stage means permanent loss of the inheritance.

Step 10
Execute the Transfer — With Verification

When transferring Bitcoin on-chain, always send a small test transaction first (0.001 BTC or less) and confirm the heir received it before sending the remainder. Bitcoin transactions are irreversible. A single character wrong in an address sends Bitcoin to an unrecoverable address permanently.

For multi-signature wallets: coordinate with all required co-signers. The co-signers may be attorneys, trusted individuals, or hardware security modules — the LOI or trust document will specify. Multi-sig transfers often require legal coordination in addition to technical coordination.

Document every transaction: timestamp, amount, sending address, receiving address, transaction ID (txid). This becomes part of your executor accounting record.

Step 11
File Required Tax Returns

Your CPA will coordinate the required filings, which typically include: the deceased's final Form 1040 (income through the date of death), Form 706 if the estate exceeds the federal exemption, state estate tax returns if applicable (Oregon, Washington, Massachusetts, Illinois, Minnesota, and others have state estate taxes), and K-1s if the estate operated as an estate entity during administration.

Estate tax returns are due 9 months from the date of death, with a 6-month extension available. Do not miss this deadline — there are significant penalties for late filing even if no tax is owed.

Step 12
Document Everything and Close the Estate

As executor, you have a fiduciary duty to beneficiaries. Maintain meticulous records: every decision made, every professional consulted, every transaction executed, every tax filing submitted. Prepare a final accounting for beneficiaries showing all estate assets, liabilities paid, administrative expenses, and distributions made.

For Bitcoin specifically: document the date-of-death value used for step-up basis, the method of determining the value, and all wallet addresses involved in transfers. This documentation protects you personally if a beneficiary later disputes the administration.

The Scam Warning Every Executor Needs (Expanded)

Bitcoin inheritance scams are sophisticated and growing. Here are the specific patterns to watch for:

The "Recovery Specialist" Scam

A "Bitcoin recovery specialist" contacts you offering to help access locked wallets — for 10-20% of the recovered Bitcoin. In reality, they will take your seed phrase or private key and steal the Bitcoin. Legitimate custody recovery (for genuinely lost keys) is extremely rare and almost never involves a cold call from someone who "found your contact information online."

The "Probate Court" Scam

Someone claiming to be from the probate court contacts you, saying there are "digital asset filings" needed or a "Bitcoin disclosure requirement." Probate courts do not proactively contact executors about cryptocurrency. All legitimate contacts from courts come through your attorney.

The Phishing Exchange Scam

You receive an email that appears to be from Coinbase, Kraken, or another exchange saying the deceased's account requires "verification" and asking you to click a link. Always navigate directly to exchange websites — never click links in unsolicited emails related to estate matters.

The Compromised Heir Scam

A scammer contacts one of the heirs, claiming to be a "Bitcoin estate specialist" who can get them their inheritance faster. The heir, eager to receive their share, shares information they shouldn't. Educate all beneficiaries: no one should contact them about the Bitcoin estate except the attorney and executor you hired.

If There Is No Letter of Instruction

This is the most difficult scenario. Without a Letter of Instruction, you know the deceased held Bitcoin but may not know: where it is, how much there is, what custody method was used, or how to access it. Your options, in order:

1. Search thoroughly. Check computers, phones, and paper files for anything related to Bitcoin: emails from Coinbase/Kraken/Swan/River, hardware wallet devices, printed QR codes, "seed" or "mnemonic" written on paper, files named wallet.dat, or metal backup devices (Cryptosteel, Bilodal).

2. Check email for exchange accounts. Search the deceased's email for account registration confirmations from Coinbase, Kraken, Swan Bitcoin, River Financial, Strike, Gemini, or similar. Each exchange has an estate/deceased account process.

3. Check tax returns. Bitcoin on exchanges appears on 1099s. Check the last 3 years of tax returns for Bitcoin-related 1099s, which will identify the exchange.

4. Engage a forensic accountant. For larger estates, a forensic accountant specializing in digital assets can analyze transaction records, blockchain data, and financial records to identify Bitcoin holdings. Hire through your estate attorney's referral.

5. Accept the loss. If the deceased used self-custody with no documentation and no seed phrase can be located, the Bitcoin may be permanently inaccessible. This is why estate planning documentation is so critical. There is no "Bitcoin recovery" without the private keys — no legal authority can compel the blockchain to release funds.

Building Your Professional Team

A Bitcoin estate of any meaningful size requires a three-person professional team:

Estate Attorney

Handles probate or trust administration, legal authority documentation, exchange account succession processes, creditor notifications, and court filings. For Bitcoin estates, specifically ask about their experience with digital asset estates and their familiarity with self-custody vs. exchange custody.

Bitcoin CPA

Handles the step-up in basis documentation, estate tax return (Form 706), final income tax return, HIFO cost basis elections, and guidance on in-kind vs. liquidation distribution. A general CPA may not understand the nuances of Bitcoin cost basis accounting — specifically request someone with Bitcoin estate experience.

Bitcoin Custody Consultant (for larger estates)

For estates above $500,000 in Bitcoin, consider engaging a Bitcoin custody consultant — someone who can physically examine hardware wallets, coordinate multi-signature wallet transfers, and advise on the technical aspects of the transfer process. This is distinct from a "recovery specialist" — you hire them through a referral, you verify their credentials, and they work alongside your attorney, not independently.
